At the moment your order is executed, nothing has yet changed hands. The trade is only a promise: you will have to pay, the seller will have to deliver — later. Between the two stretches a world the movies superbly ignore: confirmations, clearing houses, depositories, settlement. This plumbing never makes the headlines… except when it breaks, or when it dictates the script — as in January 2021, when it was the plumbing, and not a conspiracy, that made several brokers suspend purchases of GameStop. Time to take it apart.

## From trade to settlement

The life of a transaction plays out in four stages. **Execution**: the book matches buyer and seller, the price is set, the commitment is made. **Confirmation**: the back offices of the two parties check that they have recorded the same thing — direction, quantity, price, settlement date; at industrial scale, the slightest undetected disagreement is costly. **Clearing**: positions are netted — whoever bought 1 million shares and sold 900,000 settles only the balance — and, on organised markets, a clearing house steps in between the parties. **Settlement**: securities and cash change hands simultaneously in the books of the central depository, under the DvP principle (*delivery versus payment*: neither of the two legs moves without the other).

Between execution and settlement runs a conventional delay. The United States moved to T+1 — settlement the next day — at the end of May 2024; Europe still settles at T+2, with a switch to T+1 announced for October 2027 (a timetable announced by the European authorities, with the United Kingdom and Switzerland aligned). Why does this housekeeping detail deserve a paragraph? Because during that delay, your counterparty can default: the longer the window and the higher the volatility, the greater the risk carried — and the heavier the guarantees demanded by the clearing house. Shortening the cycle means less risk and fewer margins tied up; it also means tenser logistics — a European fund buying US equities now has to find its dollars within a few hours, time zones included.

## The clearing house: the buyer to every seller

The CCP (*central counterparty*) is the pivot of the system — it is the institution EMIR made unavoidable for derivatives (chapter 6). Its legal mechanism bears a name to know: **novation**. As soon as the trade is accepted for clearing, the contract between A and B is replaced by two contracts: the CCP becomes the buyer to the seller and the seller to the buyer. A and B are no longer exposed to each other — each now faces only the CCP, which from then on carries all the counterparty risk of the market.

To survive this role, it armours itself. **Initial margin** is a deposit required from each member, calibrated to cover the potential loss if its position had to be liquidated in degraded conditions — a few days of extreme moves. **Variation margin** settles every day, sometimes intraday, the running losses and gains, in cash: losses never accumulate. A member that fails to meet a margin call is declared in default; its position is liquidated or auctioned off, and the residual losses are absorbed in a precise order — the *default waterfall*, detailed in the box at the end of the chapter.

The system has proved itself: in 2008, the London clearing house (LCH) that cleared Lehman's interest rate swaps unwound a portfolio of around \$9tn of notional without even exhausting the defaulter's initial margin. But honesty obliges us to see the flip side: by concentrating everything on a handful of CCPs, we have created systemic nodes without precedent — a major CCP going down would make Lehman look like a dress rehearsal. "Too big to fail", openly assumed: regulators now impose recovery and resolution plans on them, hoping never to have to open them.

## Depositories and custody: the ultimate register

A modern share exists only as a book entry — paper has disappeared. At the top of the pyramid, the **central securities depository** (CSD) plays the notary: it keeps the ultimate register of who holds what, and it is in its books that settlement takes place. Europe has two of international stature: Euroclear in Brussels — around €40tn of assets under custody as of early 2026 — and Clearstream in Luxembourg, a subsidiary of Deutsche Börse. Below them, the **custodians** (BNP Paribas, CACEIS, State Street…) keep the securities accounts of institutionals and handle the daily life of portfolios: collecting coupons and dividends, corporate actions, exercising voting rights. Your PEA (the French equity savings plan) sits in the same pyramid: your broker keeps your account, and the chain runs all the way up to the central depository.

This plumbing is also the plumbing of collateral: securities placed on repo — the repo, which you will dissect in module 4, chapter 7 — circulate from account to account through these infrastructures, which offer so-called triparty services in which they manage the collateral baskets themselves. And custody has become a first-rank geopolitical subject: it is at Euroclear that close to €200bn of Russian assets have sat frozen since 2022. When people say post-trade is political, it is not a figure of speech.

## When the plumbing breaks

The ordinary incident is called the **settlement fail**: the seller does not deliver the securities on the scheduled date — securities lent out and not recalled in time, a cascading chain of settlements in which one link snapped. Benign in normal times, and now financially penalised in Europe by the settlement discipline regime: cash penalties in force since February 2022; the mandatory buy-in, initially planned, was suspended and then made conditional by the 2024 CSDR review — a last-resort tool, never triggered to date. In periods of stress, fails pile up and become a thermometer of tensions.

And then there is January 2021. GameStop, a struggling video game retail chain, is then the most shorted stock on the US market. Retail investors coordinated on social media rush in to buy; the share goes from around \$20 at the start of January to nearly \$500 intraday on the 28th. That morning, Robinhood and other brokers block purchases — sales remain allowed — at the peak of the fever. The explanation that immediately takes hold on social media: the hedge funds, in the middle of losing billions on their short positions, supposedly made a phone call to stop the massacre.

The real story plays out in post-trade. At the time, US equities settle at T+2: for two days, the clearing house for US equities (the NSCC, a subsidiary of the DTCC group) carries the risk that a broker fails to settle — a risk covered by guarantee deposits recalculated every night based on the volatility and concentration of positions. Millions of clients of a single broker, all buyers of the same stocks whose volatility had turned delirious: the formula explodes. On the morning of 28 January, the deposit requirement notified to Robinhood jumps from around \$700m the day before to \$3.7bn — several times the available cushion. The broker then does the only thing that mechanically reduces the requirement: suspend new purchases of the stocks concerned. Buying increases the exposure to be covered, selling reduces it — hence that asymmetry of blocked purchases / allowed sales, so shocking seen from the outside. The CCP then waives an exceptional supplement, the requirement falls back towards \$1.4bn, and Robinhood will nevertheless raise around \$3.4bn within a few days to rebuild its capital. The US congressional investigation documented no pressure from hedge funds — but an undercapitalised broker facing a regulatory margin call. A logical epilogue: the episode accelerated the American move to T+1, a risk window half as long meaning lighter margins.

If the topic comes up at the oral, the answer that sets you apart: the suspension of GameStop purchases was the mechanical consequence of the clearing house's margin calls under T+2 settlement — not a favour done to hedge funds. Knowing how to unroll the chain — extreme volatility → recalculated NSCC margins → insufficient broker capital → restriction of purchases — instantly puts you above the pack. Bonus: the same chain explains why sales, for their part, remained allowed (they reduce the exposure to be covered).
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="the default waterfall, step by step">
What happens when a CCP member defaults? Its portfolio is first taken in hand, hedged, then auctioned off to the surviving members. Any losses then follow a strict cascade. **One**: the defaulter's margins — its resources first. **Two**: its contribution to the default fund (a mutualised fund paid into by all the members). **Three**: a slice of the CCP's own capital, the *skin in the game*: the house must bleed before the survivors, to stay incentivised to calibrate its margins seriously. **Four**: the default fund of the surviving members — mutualisation proper. **Five**, beyond that: calls for additional contributions, or even last-resort tools such as haircutting the variation margins owed to winning members. The cascade rarely goes far down: for Lehman in 2008, everything was settled at the first step. Rarely does not mean never: in 2018, the default of an individual Norwegian trader on electricity price spreads burned through his margins and consumed around €100m of Nasdaq Clearing's mutualised fund — the surviving members paid for a single man. The whole philosophy of CCPs lies in this order of passage: the culprit's resources first, mutualisation second, the survival of the infrastructure as the north star.
</GoFurther>

The panorama is complete. You now know who trades, where and why, how prices form transaction by transaction, under what rules — and how securities and cash actually end up changing hands. The stage is set; the modules that follow bring on the instruments, one by one.
