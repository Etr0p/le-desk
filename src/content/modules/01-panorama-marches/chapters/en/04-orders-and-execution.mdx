In chapter 3, you met the buy-side trader and their obsession: executing without moving the price against yourself. This chapter opens up the machinery that makes that obsession rational. Behind every price displayed on a screen there is a precise object — the order book — and behind every execution, a very real cost that the displayed price does not show. This is the terrain of market microstructure: the discipline that studies how prices form, order by order.

## The order book

<Callout type="definition" titre="The order book">
The central register of an electronic market that gathers, at every instant, all the resting limit orders: on one side the *bids* (buy orders, ranked from the highest price to the lowest), on the other the *asks* (sell orders, from the lowest price to the highest). The best bid and the best ask form the **bid-ask spread**; their average is the **mid**, the best instantaneous estimate of "the" value of the security.
</Callout>

The book lives under a single rule, **price-time priority**: orders are served first by price (the most generous buyer, the least greedy seller), then, at equal price, by seniority — first come, first served. This simple rule is enough to organize millions of orders a day without human intervention.

There remains the economic reading of the spread: it is the **price of immediacy**. Whoever wants to buy *right now* pays the best ask; whoever wants to sell *right now* hits the best bid; the instant round trip therefore costs exactly the spread. The one who takes this toll road pays the one who collects — the market maker of chapter 3, paid to offer liquidity continuously. To compare securities that do not quote at the same level, the spread is expressed in basis points of the mid:

$$
\text{spread (bp)} = \frac{\text{ask} - \text{bid}}{\text{mid}} \times 10{,}000
$$

On a 99.95 / 100.05 quote: a spread of 10 cents, a mid of 100.00, i.e. 10bp — a very liquid security. A small cap can quote 50 or 100bp of spread: immediacy there is ten times more expensive.

## Order types

Every order arbitrates between two guarantees impossible to obtain together: being executed for certain, or at a price known in advance.

The **market order** says "execute now, at the best of what the book contains". Execution near-certain, price uncertain: it takes the best level, then the next, as far as its size requires — walking the book, exactly what the simulator just showed you.

The **limit order** says "buy at €99.93 or less" (or "sell at €100.07 or more"). Price guaranteed, execution uncertain: posted in the book, it waits for a counterparty to come to it — and can wait indefinitely if the market moves away. It is the order of the patient, and the raw material the book is made of.

The **stop order** sleeps as long as the market has not touched a threshold, then wakes up **as a market order**. Classic use: cutting a loss — "if the stock breaks €95, sell". Its **stop-limit** variant wakes up as a limit order: it bounds the exit price, at the risk of not exiting at all.

| Order type | Execution | Price | Typical use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Market | Near-guaranteed | No guarantee | Get out or in fast, reasonable size |
| Limit | No guarantee | Guaranteed (or better) | Work a price, provide liquidity |
| Stop | Near-guaranteed once triggered | No guarantee | Cut a loss, protect a gain |
| Stop-limit | No guarantee | Bounded by the limit | Cut a loss without selling at any price |

The choice boils down to one question: which costs you more — not being executed, or being executed at a bad price? The one who absolutely must sell before tonight takes a market order and pays for immediacy; the one who can wait posts a limit and gets paid by those in a hurry.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The stop guarantees NO price">
A sell stop at €95 does not promise an exit at €95: once the threshold is touched, the order becomes a **market** order, served at the prices available at that instant. If the stock opens in a *gap* at €88 after bad news — it never traded between 95 and 88 — the stop sells around €88. On 19 October 1987, stop sales triggered in cascade executed tens of percent below their thresholds and fed the fall; the *flash crash* of 6 May 2010 replayed the mechanics within minutes. The stop bounds the decision, never the price.
</Callout>

## The real cost of an execution

The displayed price is not the price you will pay. The full cost of an execution stacks three layers: the half-spread (buying at the ask when the value sits at the mid), the depth slippage (the next levels, if the order is large), and the commissions. Let us put numbers on it with the module's reference book — at the bid, €99.95; on the sell side: 300 shares at €100.05, 300 at €100.10, 500 at €100.20. Mid: €100.00. A market buy order for 800 shares consumes three levels:

$$
300 \times 100.05 + 300 \times 100.10 + 200 \times 100.20 = \text{€}80{,}085
$$

$$
\text{average price} = \frac{80{,}085}{800} = \text{€}100.10625
$$

Against the mid at €100.00, the extra cost is €0.10625 per share, i.e. €85 — decomposable into €40 of half-spread ($800 \times 0.05$) and €45 of depth slippage ($800 \times 0.05625$, the gap between the average price and the best displayed ask). Add the broker's commission, say 2bp with a €25 minimum: $80{,}085 \times 2/10{,}000 = \text{€}16.02$, below the minimum — so it is the minimum that bites, €25. Total cost of the friction:

$$
40 + 45 + 25 = \text{€}110 \approx 13.75 \text{ bp of notional}
$$

A "small" €80,000 execution leaves €110 on the table before the market has even moved. At the scale of a fund that turns its portfolio over several times a year, these basis points compound — that is why the buy-side trader of chapter 3 is a job in its own right.

Hence the professional answer to large orders: slicing them. A **VWAP** algorithm (*volume-weighted average price*) spreads the execution in proportion to the day's usual volumes, to obtain a price close to the volume-weighted average. A **TWAP** (*time-weighted average price*) slices into regular tranches over time. In both cases, the objective is the same: never cross several levels at once, even at the cost of carrying the risk that the market moves during the execution.

## Auctions and fixes

The continuous book is not the only mode of price formation. At the open and at the close, equity exchanges switch to **auction** mode (the fix): for a few minutes, orders accumulate without executing, then the system computes the single price that maximizes the executable volume, and everyone is served at that price. The auction aggregates dispersed information into one robust price — exactly what is needed when information has accumulated overnight (the open) or when an official price is required (the close).

The closing auction has taken on considerable importance: it concentrates a substantial share of European equity volumes — often estimated between a quarter and a third depending on the venue and the year, growing steadily. The reason is mechanical: indices are calculated on closing prices, funds are valued on them, so index funds — whose weight keeps growing — execute at the close to track their benchmark index exactly. Liquidity attracts liquidity: executing at the close is the guarantee of finding the volume on the other side, at the official price.

<GoFurther suffixe="price-time priority on a mini-example">
Three buy orders are waiting in the book: A, 300 shares at €99.98 (placed at 9:00 am); B, 200 shares at €99.99 (9:01 am); C, 400 shares at €99.98 (9:02 am). A market sell order for 600 shares arrives. First served: B, in full — its price is the best for the seller, never mind that it arrived after A. That leaves 400 shares to serve at the €99.98 level, where A and C are tied on price: seniority decides. A, arrived at 9:00 am, is served in full (300 shares); C receives only the remaining 100, and its last 300 shares keep waiting. The moral, in two parts: improving your price by one cent puts you ahead of everyone; at equal price, every second of head start counts — these queues are what high-frequency traders fight over in microseconds.
</GoFurther>

You now know what happens between the moment an order leaves and the moment it is executed — and what the journey costs. Spread, depth, slippage: this microstructure vocabulary is exactly what a jury expects when it asks "what happens when I buy a share?". There remains the question this book leaves open — who supplies the liquidity your orders consume, and why? That is the subject of the next chapter.
