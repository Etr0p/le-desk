Market makers quoting in microseconds, dark pools, ten venues for the same security: chapter 5 described a sophisticated playing field. It needs a referee. Regulation is not a bureaucratic layer laid on top of the markets — it is a condition of their existence: without imposed transparency and without cheating being punished, nobody would entrust their savings to strangers. This chapter maps who regulates what, then walks through the three bodies of rules you will meet from your very first day on the floor — MiFID II, EMIR, market abuse — before finishing behind the Chinese wall.

## Who regulates what

First reflex to acquire: distinguish two logics. **Conduct and markets** regulation watches the way people trade — transparency, disclosure, abuse, client protection. **Prudential** supervision watches the soundness of those who trade — capital, liquidity, balance-sheet risks. In France, the two heads are separate: the AMF (Autorité des marchés financiers, the French markets regulator) for markets, the ACPR (Autorité de contrôle prudentiel et de résolution) for the prudential side.

| Authority | Scope | To remember |
| --- | --- | --- |
| AMF (France) | Markets and conduct: issuer disclosure, asset management, market abuse | France's stock-market watchdog |
| ACPR (France) | Prudential: solvency of banks and insurers | Backed by the Banque de France |
| ESMA (EU) | Coordination of the market regulators of the Twenty-Seven; direct supervision of rating agencies | Based in Paris |
| ECB — SSM (euro area) | Direct prudential supervision of the euro area's largest banks | Since 2014, around a hundred groups |
| SEC (United States) | Securities markets: equities, bonds | Created in 1934, after the 1929 crash |
| CFTC (United States) | Derivatives: futures, options on futures, swaps | Heir to Chicago's agricultural markets |
| FCA (United Kingdom) | Markets and conduct across the Channel | A framework now autonomous since Brexit |

Two curiosities for the oral. First, the American split does not separate conduct from prudential but **securities and derivatives**: a future on the S&P 500 falls under the CFTC, the stocks making up the index fall under the SEC — a historical legacy (futures were born on wheat in Chicago) that always surprises. Second, the French dual oversight: a bank that mistreats its investor clients answers to the AMF; the same bank, undercapitalised, answers to the ACPR — and, if it is large, directly to the ECB via the Single Supervisory Mechanism.

## MiFID II: the great overhaul

The first Markets in Financial Instruments Directive (2007) had opened competition between execution venues — it is the one that gave birth to the fragmentation of chapter 5. MiFID II, applicable since January 2018, drew the lessons and overhauled the daily workings of trading floors. Four concrete worksites.

**Best execution, hardened.** Promising the best result is no longer enough: you need a written execution policy, controls, and the ability to demonstrate, order by order, that the result obtained (price, costs, speed, likelihood of execution) was the best reasonably available. The smart order routing of chapter 5 is not a technological luxury: it is the materialisation of a regulatory obligation.

**Pre- and post-trade transparency.** Publication of quotes and transactions, extended beyond equities alone, and caps imposed on trading without transparency — this is where the market share of dark pools was reined in.

**Research paid for separately** (*unbundling*). Before 2018, brokers' research was "offered" to managers, implicitly funded by execution commissions. MiFID II required separate invoicing, to make the costs visible and the conflicts less comfortable. The effect, real and debated: compressed research budgets, fewer analysts, impoverished coverage of small caps — to the point that the European legislator has since reopened the file and relaxed the rule.

**Investor protection.** Product governance (who is this product meant for?), detailed cost disclosure, and the recording of communications: yes, on a desk, professional telephone conversations are recorded and archived. It is not a movie myth, it is an obligation — and a reflex to have before joking on the phone.

## EMIR: the lesson of 2008

Before 2008, over-the-counter derivatives formed a web of bilateral contracts that nobody had a map of. When Lehman Brothers falls in September 2008, neither the banks nor the regulators can say who is exposed to whom, or for how much; at the same moment, the insurer AIG, which had sold credit protection on a massive scale without posting collateral, owes its survival to public money alone. The detail of the autopsy waits for module 11 — here, remember the regulatory response. At the Pittsburgh G20 (2009), the major economies commit; Europe translates in 2012 with the EMIR regulation: **mandatory central clearing** of standardised OTC derivatives (interest rate swaps, CDS indices) through clearing houses, **reporting** of all derivatives to trade repositories, and **margin exchange** for what remains bilateral. Concretely: two banks entering a swap no longer face each other — a clearing house steps in between and calls margin, in cash, every day. The exact mechanics of these clearing houses — novation, margins, default waterfall — is the heart of chapter 7.

## Market abuse

The European Market Abuse Regulation (MAR), adopted in 2014 and applicable since July 2016, organises the subject into two families of offences.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Inside information">
A piece of information is inside information if it combines three features: it is **precise** (not a mere corridor rumour), **non-public**, and **likely to have a significant effect on the price** if it were known. Using it to trade, passing it to a third party, or recommending buying or selling on its basis: all three are prohibited, as soon as one knows — or ought to know — its inside nature.
</Callout>

**Insider dealing** does not only target the M&A banker who would buy shares in the target he advises. It also catches the executive who sells before a profit warning, the IT contractor who saw the press release before publication — and the brother-in-law tipped off at the family dinner, along with the one who tipped him off.

<Callout type="piege" titre="No need to have made money">
The insider-dealing breach is constituted by the **use of the information**, not by the profit. Selling to avoid a loss is just as reprehensible as buying to gain — a loss avoided is an advantage. And if the market ends up moving the other way, the absence of gain erases nothing: the offence is judged on the information held at the moment of the order, never on the outcome of the trade. At the oral, this is an immediate marker of legal rigour.
</Callout>

**Price manipulation**, for its part, consists of creating a false appearance of supply or demand. Its electronic variants bear names that have become famous: *spoofing* (displaying large orders with no intention of executing them, to push others to trade, then cancelling them) and *layering* (the millefeuille version: several layers of fictitious orders at staggered prices). The anecdote that fixes the ideas: for years, Navinder Sarao practised spoofing on S&P 500 futures from his parents' house in Hounslow, in the western suburbs of London, accumulating around \$40m. Arrested in 2015, he was accused by the US authorities of having contributed to the flash crash of 6 May 2010 — the one from chapter 5. His real responsibility for the crash remains debated (one man alone in a suburban house against the deepest market in the world…), but the case showed that manipulation is not the preserve of large institutions, and that regulators know how to trace their way back to a bedroom screen.

On the sanctions side, the AMF has an enforcement committee (commission des sanctions) that can impose fines of up to €100m or ten times the benefit derived. In practice, routine cases run to hundreds of thousands or millions of euros; the heaviest to tens of millions — the record, handed down in 2023 against the asset manager H2O AM, is around €75m. Decisions are published with names attached, which is often the most costly sanction; and the gravest cases tip over into criminal proceedings, where professional bans and prison sentences are added.

## The Chinese wall: living with conflicts of interest

An investment bank houses under the same roof businesses that are structurally in conflict. The M&A team knows about deals months before they are announced — inside information in its purest form. Research publishes opinions that are supposed to be independent. Trading executes. If information circulated freely between the three, the bank would be an insider-dealing machine. Hence the **Chinese wall** (renamed "information barrier" in the rulebooks): physical separation — separate floors, badges, compartmentalised IT access — and procedural separation. Every confidential deal has its named insider list. Compliance keeps a confidential *watch list* of sensitive names, to monitor staff dealing; when the conflict becomes public, the name moves to the *restricted list* and research stops publishing on it. Bringing an analyst over to the confidential side on a one-off basis — *wall-crossing* — follows a formalised, dated, signed procedure.

That is why research and M&A do not lunch together: an analyst who learns at the coffee machine that a takeover bid is in the works becomes an insider — he can no longer publish on the stock, and the bank must be able to prove to the regulator that the wall held. Compliance is not decorative: it is what protects the licence to operate.

<GoFurther suffixe="the Volcker rule and the end of bank prop trading">
In the United States, the great legislative response to 2008 — the Dodd-Frank Act, passed in 2010 — contains a provision that has remained attached to the name of its inspirer, former Fed chairman Paul Volcker: banning banks that collect insured deposits from proprietary trading (speculating with the bank's balance sheet, unconnected to any service rendered to a client). The idea fits in one sentence: taxpayers' money, which guarantees the deposits, must not fund the house poker table. Direct consequence: the prop trading desks of the big American banks closed, and their teams scattered to hedge funds. The boundary remains delicate to draw — the market making of chapter 5 requires carrying positions: where does client service end, where does the house bet begin? Years of implementing texts have tried to draw the line. Europe explored the same path (the Liikanen report in 2012, the French banking separation law of 2013, a European project ultimately abandoned) with a far more modest result: the activities actually separated proved marginal. The spirit survives differently on the continent — heavier capital requirements on market activities and reinforced supervision: the continental version of the same suspicion.
</GoFurther>

The rules are written, the referee installed. What remains is the housekeeping, invisible and decisive: how, once the trade is done, securities and money actually change hands. That is post-trade — and it is post-trade that wrote the script of the GameStop saga. Chapter 7.
