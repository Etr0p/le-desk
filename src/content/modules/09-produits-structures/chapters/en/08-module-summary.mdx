This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the essentials chapter by chapter, the formula sheet, the products and their DNA, the risk map, the traps and the reflexes. To reread the day before an oral — and to complete with the jury mode of the practice section. The guiding thread fits in one sentence: a structured product is a made-to-measure promise assembled out of standard building blocks — a zero-coupon that guarantees, options that promise, a margin that helps itself along the way — and desk pricing never consists of choosing the headline number in the shop window, but of *solving* for it so that the sum of the blocks, margin included, comes to exactly 100.

## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **The promise in building blocks (ch. 1).** A structured product is bank **debt** whose repayment follows a formula: under the hood, rates (a zero-coupon) and optionality (options), nothing else. The central constraint: $\text{budget} = 100 - \text{ZC} - \text{margin}$ — at $r = 3\%$ over 5 years, the ZC costs $100 \cdot e^{-0.15} = 86.07$, the margin takes 1, and **12.93** is left for the entire conditional promise. The bank is not on the other side of the client: it buys the blocks, delta-hedges, and its margin is locked in on day 1, indifferent to the scenario.
2. **The capital-protected note (ch. 2).** ZC + ATM calls, and the shop-window number is a division: $p = \text{budget}/\text{call price}$ — $12.93/24.33 = 53.1\%$ of the upside. Rates ↑ ⇒ ZC ↓ ⇒ budget ↑ ⇒ participation ↑ (72.5% at $r = 5\%$; 7.8% at $r = 0.5\%$ — the product's disappearance in the zero-rate decade, its return in 2023). The client is a *buyer* of volatility: at constant budget, participation falls from 62.9% to 40.4% when vol moves from 15% to 30%. Four patches to dress up the ratio: a cap (call spread, "118%" of a capped upside), Asian averaging, partial protection, a longer maturity — every one of them buys a degraded option.
3. **The reverse convertible (ch. 3).** The client lends 100 (ZC at 95.12) and **sells an ATM put** (premium 5.57) without knowing it: 10.45 available, compounded for one year, coupon **10.99%**. The decomposition that says it all: $10.99 = 5.13 + 5.86$ — the risk-free rate compounded plus the put premium; any coupon above the risk-free rate is the price of a risk sold. Gain capped at 10.99, breakeven at 89, delivery in shares below the strike: module 8's insurer, repackaged. The barrier version softens and pays less: down-and-in put at 70 ⇒ premium 1.45 ⇒ coupon 6.7%.
4. **The autocallable (ch. 4).** The Athena, the world's best-seller: annual observations, automatic call if $S_i \geq S_0$ with a memory coupon ($100 + c \times i$), a 60% protection barrier observed at maturity only — a down-and-in put sold by the client. The coupon is **solved for**, not chosen: at $r = 3\%$ and 20% vol, the fair coupon is 8.65% (6.1% at 15% vol, 11.6% at 25% — and the probability of loss follows: 2.9 / 7.9 / 13%). Duration is uncertain and perverse: 52% of calls as early as year 1, average life 2.4 years — short when the market rises, long when it falls.
5. **Barriers, worst-of, correlation (ch. 5).** The DIP pays less than the vanilla on every path, so it is worth less: 5.57 for the canonical vanilla, 1.45 at a 70 barrier, 0.24 at a 60 barrier — a small premium, but a conditional loss of ~40%: catastrophe insurance. A barrier = a level + a frequency: the same DIP is worth **73% more** observed daily rather than at expiry only (0.236 versus 0.136). The worst-of pays the minimum: the call falls from 10.43 ($\rho = 1$, the vanilla recovered) to 3.31 ($\rho = 0$), and the worst-of DIP doubles at $\rho = 0.5$ (0.47 versus 0.24) — the client sells dispersion, the desk is **short correlation** and buys it back to hedge. In a crisis, correlations rise towards 1: the basket's diversification disappears exactly when it is needed.
6. **Monte Carlo (ch. 6).** The principle never changes — price = discounted risk-neutral expectation — but as soon as the payoff depends on the path, the closed-form formula dies: simulate, compute the payoff, average, discount. The lognormal step $S \cdot \exp[(r - \sigma^2/2)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,z]$: drift $r$ (risk-neutral world) docked by $\sigma^2/2$ (the convexity correction — without it, the asset would beat the risk-free rate). The machine is locked onto its analytical limits (the canonical call 10.4506 recovered at 1 million draws); the error shrinks as $\sigma_{\text{payoff}}/\sqrt{n}$ — dividing the error by 10 costs a hundred times more simulations; frozen seeds make the pricing auditable, and the delta is bumped with common random numbers (0.644 versus pure noise between 0.33 and 0.83 otherwise).
7. **Term sheet, margin, accidents (ch. 7).** Read in the desk's order: issuer, underlyings (worst-of?), barriers and their observation, call mechanics, formula, fees. At issue $100 = \text{ZC} + \text{options} + \text{margin}$: the product is typically worth **97 to 99** on the day it is paid 100 — 0.5 to 1% per year all in, published in the KID (PRIIPs, 2018). Three accidents, one per invisible risk: Lehman 2008 (the guarantee is worth the signature), Korea 2015-2016 (concentrated HSCEI barriers: the hedges feed the fall), March 2020 (dividends cancelled: the desk, long dividends by construction, buckles — Natixis pulls out).

## The formula sheet

| Formula | When to use it |
| --- | --- |
| $\text{ZC} = 100 \cdot e^{-rT}$ | The building block of the guarantee — and of the bank's funding: 86.07 at 3% over 5 years, 77.88 at 5%, 97.53 at 0.5% — the whole history of the market in one exponent |
| $\text{budget} = 100 - \text{ZC} - \text{margin}$ | The central constraint of the business: $100 - 86.07 - 1 = 12.93$ — everything the formula will promise has to fit inside it |
| $p = \dfrac{\text{budget}}{\text{ATM call price}}$ | THE division of the capital-protected note: $12.93/24.33 = 53.1\%$ — if the inverted ratio gives more than 100% on a thin budget, that is the trap |
| $\text{redemption} = 100 + p \times \max\!\left(\dfrac{S_T - S_0}{S_0},\ 0\right) \times 100$ | The capital-protected payoff: index up 40%, $100 + 0.531 \times 40 = 121.26$; index down, a flat 100 |
| $\text{coupon} = \dfrac{100 - \text{ZC} + \text{put premium}}{e^{-rT} \cdot T}$ | The reverse convertible dismantled: $(100 - 95.12 + 5.57)/0.9512 = 10.99\%$ — day-1 available cash, compounded and paid back out |
| $\text{coupon} = \text{compounded risk-free rate} + \text{compounded premium}$ | The universal reading grid: $10.99 = 5.13 + 5.86$ — zero premium ⇒ coupon = risk-free rate; the rest is a risk sold |
| $\text{RC breakeven} = 100 - \text{coupon}$ | ≈ 89 on the canonical: as long as the stock loses less than the coupon, the client wins; below that, every euro of decline is his |
| $\text{DIP} = \max(K - S_T,\ 0) \times \mathbb{1}(\text{barrier hit})$ | The put that has to be born: payoff less than or equal to the vanilla everywhere, hence a lower price — 1.45 at a 70 barrier, 0.24 at a 60 barrier, versus 5.57 vanilla |
| $\text{DIP} + \text{DOP} = \text{vanilla put}$ | The in + out parity: every path activates exactly one of the two — a pricer consistency check, like barrier = strike ⇒ DIP = vanilla |
| $\text{worst-of payoff} = 100 \times \max\!\left(\min\!\left(\dfrac{S_T^{(1)}}{S_0^{(1)}},\ \dfrac{S_T^{(2)}}{S_0^{(2)}}\right) - 1,\ 0\right)$ | Paid on the worst performer: 10.43 at $\rho = 1$, 3.31 at $\rho = 0$ — dispersion drives the minimum down, and the price with it |
| $z_2 = \rho\, z_1 + \sqrt{1 - \rho^2}\,\varepsilon$ | Cholesky in dimension 2: a share $\rho$ of common shock, a share $\sqrt{1 - \rho^2}$ of idiosyncratic shock — $\rho = 1$ gives back the single underlying |
| $\text{price} = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[ e^{-r\tau} \times \text{cash flow}_\tau \right]$ | The principle unchanged since module 8: discounted risk-neutral expectation — Monte Carlo merely estimates it when the closed form dies |
| $S_{t+\Delta t} = S_t \times \exp\!\left[\left(r - \dfrac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,z\right]$ | The simulation step: drift $r$ (risk-neutral, no forecast) minus $\sigma^2/2$ (the lognormal's convexity — without it, manufactured arbitrage) |
| $\text{standard error} = \dfrac{\sigma_{\text{payoff}}}{\sqrt{n}}$ | The bill for precision: 0.15 at ten thousand draws, 0.015 at a million — dividing the error by 10 demands a hundred times more simulations |
| $\Delta \approx \dfrac{\hat{V}(S_0 + h) - \hat{V}(S_0)}{h}$, common seeds | Bump and reprice: with common random numbers the noise cancels in the subtraction (0.644 for a true 0.637) — bump the parameter, never the randomness |
| $F = S\,e^{(r - q)T}$ | The hedge is run in forwards: dividends ↑ ⇒ forward ↓ ⇒ barrier more threatened ⇒ DIP more expensive ⇒ bigger coupon — and the desk keeps the risk |

## The products and their DNA

| Product | The building blocks | Who sells what | Client risk | When it sells |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Capital-protected note | ZC + ATM calls bought | The client BUYS options | Opportunity cost (five years paid zero) + issuer signature | High rates (fat budget) and low vol (cheap calls) — 2023, not 2018 |
| Reverse convertible | ZC + vanilla put sold by the client | The client SELLS an ATM put | The entire downside below the strike, upside capped at the coupon | Calm markets hunting for yield — so vol sold at the bottom |
| Barrier RC | ZC + down-and-in put sold | The client sells a conditional put (cheaper) | Cliff: protected as long as the barrier holds, full loss otherwise | The commercial dial: low barrier = likely protection = melted coupon |
| Autocallable (Athena) | ZC + memory digitals + DIP sold | The client sells the barrier put AND the upside beyond the coupon | Perverse duration (long when it falls), cliff at 60%, no guaranteed coupon | The permanent best-seller: wins in everything except disaster — and that is priced |
| Worst-of autocallable | Same, on the worst performer of a basket | The client additionally sells correlation (dispersion) | The worst underlying decides alone; in a crisis the basket falls as one | When single-name no longer pays enough: the DIP doubles at $\rho = 0.5$, the coupon follows |

## Who carries what: the risk map

| Parameter | The client | The desk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Volatility | Sells it without knowing (the embedded put) — at the bottom of the cycle, when the product sells well | Receives it and recycles it in its book, delta-hedged — plus the wild greeks near barriers (barrier shift) |
| Correlation | Sells it via the worst-of: the coupon top-up is the premium of dispersion risk | Short correlation by construction: its worst-of puts lose when $\rho$ rises — and $\rho$ rises in a crisis; it buys correlation to hedge |
| Dividends | Gives them up (price index): that renunciation funds part of the coupon | Long dividends via its forward hedges — March 2020: cancellations, hundreds of millions lost per major house |
| Issuer credit | Carries it in full: the ZC is a senior unsecured claim — Lehman 2008 | "Sells" it: the more expensive its funding, the prettier its formulas — the paradox of the fragile signature |

## The classic traps

1. **"The bank wins when the client loses"** (ch. 1): false — the blocks it bought pay whatever the formula promises, the desk delta-hedges, the margin is locked in on day 1, indifferent to the scenario. The real conflict lies elsewhere: the margin follows volumes, and the incentive is to sell formulas that look attractive in the window and whose risk is hard to read.
2. **Comparing two participation rates without reading the formula** (ch. 2): "118% of the upside" is obtained by degrading the option — a cap, Asian averaging, protection at 90, a longer maturity. And "protected" carries two asterisks: at maturity only (before that, the product quotes its blocks — selling back at 85 is nothing abnormal), and only if the issuer survives.
3. **"Guaranteed coupon" read as "guaranteed return"** (ch. 3): the coupon IS paid, but at $S_T = 70$ the client receives 80.99 — the coupon was the *price* of the risk that has just materialised, not a consolation on top. "I lose less than the shareholder" is true, and that is the anaesthetic: the shareholder had not capped his upside.
4. **"It is a 5-year investment"** (ch. 4): neither likely (52% die at one year, average life 2.4 years), nor guaranteed (22% go the full distance). The clean formulation: a random duration between 1 and 5 years — short if the market rises, long if it falls, never the one you would want.
5. **"Barrier at −40%, so nearly riskless"** (ch. 5): the 60 barrier is hit only 0.62% of the time, but conditional on the hit the average loss is around 40% of the nominal — catastrophe insurance, not a small risk. Twin trap: the worst-of's "diversification" — the basket never protects someone who only receives its minimum, and in a crisis all correlations go to 1.
6. **Reading Monte Carlo noise as signal** (ch. 6): a price without its standard error means nothing (10.50 ± 0.09), comparing two runs on independent seeds is reading randomness, and a delta bumped without common random numbers comes out anywhere between 0.33 and 0.83 for a true 0.64. And a million paths do not repair a model with no smile: Monte Carlo solves the computation, never the model.
7. **Forgetting the "issuer risk" line** (ch. 7): "capital protected" is never a property of the product, it is a property of the signature — the zero-coupon is a bond, with all of module 5's credit risk. A decomposition that omits it is wrong, and Lehman put a number on it: a few tens of cents on the dollar, years later.

## What the jury asks most

| Standard question | The one-line reflex |
| --- | --- |
| "Decompose an autocallable for me" | By the flows: a ZC for the nominal, memory digitals for the coupons, a down-and-in put **sold by the client** whose premium pays the coupon — plus the two risks outside the formula: the signature, and the margin (issue-date value 97 to 99) |
| "The product offers 11% when the savings account pays 5%: where do the 6 points come from?" | From the premium of the put sold: $10.99 = 5.13 + 5.86$ — any coupon above the risk-free rate is the price of a risk sold, and the question is "which option have I sold without knowing it?" |
| "Why does the coupon rise with volatility?" | Because it is solved for, not chosen: vol ↑ ⇒ the put sold by the client is worth more ⇒ fair coupon 6.1 / 8.65 / 11.6% at 15 / 20 / 25% vol — and the probability of loss follows, 2.9 / 7.9 / 13%: a thermometer, not generosity |
| "What is the duration of this 5-year product?" | Nobody knows it: 52% called at year 1, average life 2.4 years, 22% go the distance — short when the market rises (you have to reinvest higher up), long when it falls (stuck without a coupon) |
| "Why Monte Carlo rather than Black-Scholes?" | The principle has not moved — discounted risk-neutral expectation — but the payoff depends on the path (minimum, call dates, correlated joint law): the closed form dies, so you simulate and average, with an error in $1/\sqrt{n}$ announced alongside the price |
| "Why did capital-protected notes come back in 2023?" | Because $\text{ZC} = 100 \cdot e^{-rT}$: the rise in rates knocked it down from 98 towards 87, and everything no longer tied up becomes option budget again — the generosity of the formulas is a thermometer of long rates, not a feat of engineering |

## The twelve reflexes for the oral

1. In front of any term sheet, dismantle: $100 = \text{ZC} + \text{options} + \text{margin}$ — the formula IS the product, and pricing consists of solving for the displayed parameter.
2. Any coupon above the risk-free rate is the price of a risk sold: look for the option, it is rarely on the brochure.
3. First reading reflex: who buys and who sells the optionality — capital-protected, the client buys (bounded risk); RC and autocallable, he sells (an insurer's profile).
4. The macro chain to recite: rates ↑ ⇒ ZC ↓ ⇒ budget ↑ ⇒ participation ↑ — fifteen years of market history in one arrow.
5. The buyer of a capital-protected note buys volatility (expensive vol = thin participation); the RC seller sells it — at the bottom, exactly when the product sells best.
6. Never compare two percentages, compare two formulas: every patch (cap, Asian, partial protection, maturity) dresses up the ratio by trimming the payoff.
7. The coupon is not chosen, it is solved for so that price + margin = 100: a market quote dressed up as a commercial promise — when it rises, it is the risk that has become more expensive.
8. A barrier is not a level, it is a level plus an observation frequency: +73% between expiry-only and daily — at equal coupon, the continuous barrier hides a greater risk.
9. A small premium does not mean a small risk, but an improbable one: 0.24 of premium, a conditional loss of 40% — module 8's catastrophe insurance, more concentrated.
10. Worst-of = correlation sold by the client; the desk, short correlation, buys it back — and in a crisis $\rho$ rises towards 1: the dispersion paid out as coupon disappears at the worst moment.
11. A Monte Carlo price is announced with its standard error, compared on frozen seeds, and differentiated with common random numbers: bump the parameter, never the randomness.
12. Always end a decomposition with the two lines outside the formula: issuer risk (the guarantee is worth the signature) and the margin (0.5 to 1% per year, issue-date value 97-99, published in the KID).

## Bridges to the rest of the course

- **Module 1 — markets and regulation.** The issuer-distributor-client chain recycles microstructure: the structured product's margin is the market maker's spread, in a more opaque form. And chapter 7's regulation — KID, PRIIPs, MiFID II and its policed retrocessions — is module 1's, born of the scandals: Bénéfic, Doubl'ô, Lehman.
- **Module 2 — probability.** The module's entire pricing runs on module 2: the expectation (the price), the lognormal and its convexity correction ($-\sigma^2/2$: the typical path does worse than the average), the central limit theorem (the error in $1/\sqrt{n}$ and the confidence interval around the price), and the independence fallacy the worst-of exploits — "three indices, improbability cubed" is false precisely in a crisis, when correlations go to 1.
- **Module 4 — discounting.** The zero-coupon IS module 4 in production: $100 \cdot e^{-rT}$, the building block of every guarantee, whose price wires monetary policy into the shop window. And duration explains the asterisk: before maturity, a "protected" note quotes its blocks, and five years of residual maturity make it a long product.
- **Module 7 — the firm derivatives.** The desk hedges in forwards, not in spot: $F = S\,e^{(r - q)T}$, cash and carry — hence the dividend risk it keeps, and the structurers' taste for high-dividend underlyings. The margin locked in at issue is the twin of the swap's.
- **Module 8 — options.** The entire module is module 8 taken off the desk and put in the window: the participation's ATM call, the RC's sold put (the client-as-insurer), the delta-hedging that neutralises the bank, the vega that makes the coupons breathe, the parity hiding in the RC's available cash (at $S = K$, it is worth exactly the call: 10.45), and the risk-neutral world where everything is priced.

Each one-line answer is an entry point, not a complete answer: train yourself to develop them over two minutes in jury mode, and to put numbers on them with the module's exercises. One last word for the road: this module made you change chairs — from the trader pricing an isolated option to the structurer assembling promises, then to the client who has to guess what he has sold without knowing it. You now know that no coupon is free, that no barrier is a rampart, and that the product's most important building block is its most discreet: that zero-coupon whose price, $100 \cdot e^{-rT}$, commands all the generosity of the shop window. And that $r$ is not a fact of nature: the rates that manufacture the zero-coupon are steered, meeting after meeting, by central banks — the ones whose two points of hikes resurrected the capital-protected note, and whose decade of zero rates had pushed all of savings towards autocallables.
