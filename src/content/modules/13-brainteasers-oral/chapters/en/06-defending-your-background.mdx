For five chapters you have sharpened the technical side of the oral: bounding, decomposing, going through the complement, quoting a game. One blind spot remains, and it is enormous: half of a competitive oral does not test finance. It tests whether a jury of professionals wants to work with you — to hand you a task at 7 a.m., to sit you next to a client, to read your figures without re-checking everything. And that half has a property candidates systematically underestimate: it is prepared exactly like a pricing. A structure, verifiable facts, calibrated versions rehearsed out loud. The candidate who improvises his introduction after three months spent on the Greeks has misallocated his working time: this chapter's questions come up at *every* oral, with a probability of 100% — no brainteaser in chapter 4 can say as much.

## The pitch: 90 seconds, four blocks, one rule

<Callout type="definition" titre="The 90-second pitch">
The **pitch** is the prepared answer to "tell us about yourself", and its canonical structure fits in four blocks: **where I come from** (the education, in one sentence — the jury has your CV in front of them, no point reading it to them), **the trigger** (the precise moment markets became your subject), **what I have built** (internships, projects, work: the evidence), **why here** (what this competition or this desk has that is specific in your eyes). Four blocks, 90 seconds, and one writing rule that governs every sentence: one **verifiable fact** per sentence, never an adjective.
</Callout>

The verifiable-fact rule is the heart of the chapter. "I am passionate about finance" is not information: it is what the day's forty candidates will say, and nothing allows it to be verified — the sentence slides off the jury without leaving a trace. "I backtested a momentum strategy on ten years of data" is a fact: it can be dated, discussed, and above all it *invites questions* — which data, what survivorship bias, what Sharpe ratio (module 12)? That is exactly what you want: a successful pitch writes the jury's first five questions in your place, on ground you have prepared. So run your draft through the sieve: every sentence containing "passionate", "rigorous" or "motivated" without a fact behind it gets rewritten or deleted. The adjective is a conclusion the jury must draw for itself; your job is to supply the premises.

Then prepare three cuts of the *same* pitch — same blocks, same facts, only the depth changes:

| Length | Content | Situation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **30 seconds** | The trigger, one piece of evidence, why here | Quick round of the table, "introduce yourself in a word" |
| **90 seconds** | The four blocks, one piece of evidence per block | The canonical version, by default |
| **3 minutes** | Each piece of evidence unfolded into a numbered example | "Take your time", one-on-one interview |

The length is not your choice: it is read in the instruction and in the jury's body language — and knowing how to cut it short is already a desk signal, where you answer a trader between two quotes, not a lecture hall.

## "Why financial markets?": the moment, then the evidence

Three answers doom you from the start. **Money**: the jury knows the junior pay scale better than you do, and a purely pecuniary motive announces someone who will leave the moment a bigger number shows up elsewhere. **Prestige**: it confesses that you are responding to the image of the job, not the job. **"The adrenaline"**: the worst of the three — a desk looks for people who are *calm* under pressure, and module 11 showed what those who come looking for thrills cost; announcing that you love the rush is describing yourself as an operational risk.

The mechanics of a good answer come in two beats: **a precise moment**, then **what you did next** — proof through action. The moment alone is not enough (everyone "discovered finance" somewhere); it is the reaction that informs: having read such a book, built such a spreadsheet, taken such a course, dissected such a product. And this is where the content of the twelve modules becomes an oral argument: citing a *mechanism* that hooked you — the way a CDS separates credit risk from the bond that carries it (module 5), or covered interest rate parity locking a forward exchange rate by pure arbitrage (module 6) — is infinitely more credible than a vague theme like "markets" or "investing". The precision of the mechanism cited is the best proxy for the reality of your curiosity.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The same answer, before and after">
**Before**: "I have always been passionate about markets, I love adrenaline and pushing myself, and I want to grow in a stimulating environment." Three adjectives, zero facts, no possible question — the jury moves on to the next candidate.

**After**: "During an internship, I saw a client turn down a capital-guaranteed product because the coupon seemed too low to him. I wanted to understand where that coupon came from: I reconstructed the structure — the zero-coupon carrying the guarantee, the remaining options budget — and I realised that the level of interest rates manufactured the generosity of the formula. It is that link between a central bank's decision and the shop window of a savings product that made up my mind." A dated moment, a verifiable action, an exact mechanism (modules 9 and 10) — and the jury wants only one thing: to dig, on your ground.
</Callout>

## "Why this desk?": know the chairs

The question assumes you know what distinguishes the chairs of a trading floor — module 1, chapter 3, mapped them, and the oral is where that map pays off. **Flow trading** manages an inventory and quotes continuously: the job of the spread and the hedge, where you are paid to absorb clients' risk for a few hours. **Structuring** assembles bespoke — zero-coupon plus options, module 9 — and lives off product expertise. **Sales** translates in both directions: the client's needs towards the desk, the desk's capabilities towards the client; the relationship job, armed with technique. **Research** argues and publishes: convincing through analysis, without a position. Answering "why this desk" requires naming what the targeted chair has that is specific *and* what you bring to it — not reciting the map, but placing yourself on it with a fact from your background for each chair set aside.

Then comes the trap question: "and if we put you somewhere else?". The two losing answers are symmetric: "it doesn't matter, everything interests me" (nobody believes a candidate without a preference, and the jury reads a lack of reflection) and rigidity ("it's this desk or nothing" — nobody wants a junior who negotiates before having produced). The winning answer owns an **argued preference** and a **genuine curiosity**: "my preference goes to structuring, for this precise reason; but a stint in sales would teach me what clients refuse and why — that is the structurer's raw material". You show that you know the system of chairs, not only your own.

## The four questions that always come back

- **"Why you and not someone else?"** Answer with **complementarity**, never with superiority: you do not know the other candidates, so any direct comparison is a false precision — the capital sin of the oral. The form that works: "I do not know the others; here are the two things I bring, and their combination is rare" — two facts, drawn from the pitch, whose intersection singles you out.
- **"A failure?"** The three-beat structure: the **fact** (precise, dated, real), **your share** (what was down to you, without diluting it in the context), **what you changed** (the observable behaviour adopted since). The classic trap is the fake failure — "I work too much", "I am too much of a perfectionist": the jury reads it as a refusal to answer, that is, as the inability to do what the desk does all day long — look its errors in the face (module 11).
- **"A market view?"** The expected format: the **thesis** (one sentence), the **mechanism** carrying it (linked to what you know: monetary transmission, the credit cycle, valuation), and the **level or event that would make you change your mind**. That third element is what the jury really marks: a view without an invalidation condition is not a view, it is an opinion — this whole course has taught you to reason in falsifiable mechanisms, and this question checks that the method has taken hold.
- **"Any questions for us?"** Always **two**, prepared, specific to the interviewer — one about the job seen from their chair ("what has changed most in the way you quote over the past five years?"), one about what they expect from a junior. "No, everything is clear" is a non-answer that costs: it says the conversation did not interest you enough to want more of it.

<GoFurther suffixe="building a falsifiable market view, step by step">
Take a deliberately simple thesis: "the yield curve should re-steepen within a year". On its own, it is worth zero — it is a coin flip. Add the mechanism: "the policy-rate cutting cycle anchors the short end downwards, while long-dated bond supply — deficits to finance, central banks in QT no longer absorbing the paper (module 10) — keeps the pressure on the long end; the 2-10 year slope should widen — and I give its current level in basis points". Finally add the invalidation: "if inflation re-accelerates to the point of repricing hikes, the short end climbs back and the thesis breaks; I would change my mind if the money market stopped pricing cuts over twelve months". Three storeys: the thesis can be debated, the mechanism can be checked, the invalidation can be monitored. Note that the *direction* matters less than the architecture: a jury prefers a debatable, falsifiable thesis to a recited consensus — it does not mark your view, it marks your method. One last refinement: give the current level of the key variable (the slope, the policy rate, the spread). A view without the starting figure is a map without a scale.
</GoFurther>

## The news: three topics in depth, not ten headlines

The jury is not testing your press review: it is testing your ability to *reason* about a live subject. Hence the **three-topic** method: master three in depth rather than ten superficially, chosen to cover three registers:

- **a rates topic** — the central bank cycle, the slope of the curve, a recent move: the terrain where module 10 becomes news;
- **a credit or equities topic** — a sector under pressure, an issuer in difficulty, a move in spreads or valuations (modules 3 and 5);
- **a structural topic** — regulation, the geopolitics of commodities, tokenisation, ESG: the topic that shows you look further than the screen.

For each, three layers to know: the **figures** (the current level and the move — a candidate who says "rates went up" without saying from where to where has said nothing), the **mechanism** (linking the topic to the modules: monetary transmission, the credit cycle, valuation), and the **two camps** — the arguments of those who think the opposite of you. Being able to lay out the opposing thesis honestly is the clearest maturity signal a candidate can send.

To stay informed without drowning: one short daily source (a market brief, ten minutes, for the levels), one weekly source that argues (for the two camps), and the **primary sources** for your three topics — central bank statements and press conferences (module 10), issuers' reports, regulators' texts. The primary source is your competitive edge: most candidates repeat the journalistic layer; the one who quotes the statement itself speaks with a different voice.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The four ways to lose the non-technical oral">
**Reciting your CV instead of telling it**: the jury has it in front of them; the chronology read out is dead time — every line must become a fact with a lesson. **Overselling**: the jury *always* tests the weakest line of the CV — the two-week internship inflated into "trading experience", the skimmed certification: every word on the CV must withstand three questions of depth, otherwise remove it. **Asking nothing at the end**: the non-question signals non-interest. **Criticising your school or your previous internship**: the jury does not hear the criticism, it hears what you will say about them next — disloyalty is the one flaw no competence compensates for.
</Callout>

The substance is ready: a pitch in three lengths, motivations proven through action, structured answers to the ritual questions, three news topics in three layers. What remains is what turns this preparation into points on the day: the method of the oral itself — reformulating, unrolling, staying silent, handling the blank and the error in front of the jury. That is chapter 7, the last of the course.
