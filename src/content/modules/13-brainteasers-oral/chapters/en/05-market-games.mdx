Chapter 4's brainteasers tested your logic; at some point in the interview, the jury changes register and pulls out a die, a deck of cards or a jar of coins. "Make me a price on this die." This is no longer a riddle: it is a desk simulation. In one sentence, you are being asked to redo exactly what module 1's market maker does all day long — estimate a value, show a two-way price, manage the risk of whoever trades against you. It is the moment the whole course becomes a game: module 2's expected value, module 1's spread, module 8's option, module 5's implied probability and module 12's sizing will parade past in ten minutes. This chapter gives you the rules.

## The building block: expected value, before opening your mouth

<Callout type="definition" titre="The price of a game is its expected value">
The **expected value** of a discrete game is the average of the payoffs weighted by their probabilities: $E = \sum p_i \, g_i$ (`esperanceJeu` in the module's library). For a die with $f$ faces, it has a closed form: $E = (f+1)/2$ — that is, $3.5$ for a d6, $50.5$ for a d100 (`esperanceLancerDe`). It is the **value** around which everything else is organised: a fair entry price, a bid, an ask, odds. Until you have it, you have nothing.
</Callout>

The first, non-negotiable reflex: **compute $E$ before speaking**. The candidate who announces a price and then computes has already disqualified himself — he quoted at random, which no desk forgives. The calculation is almost always trivial (deliberately so: the jury tests the reflex, not the arithmetic): "you win the face of the die in euros" is worth €3.50; "you win €10 if the card is red" is worth €5. And beware the "middle of the faces" trap: the middle of 1-6 read too fast gives 3; the true expected value is $(6+1)/2 = 3.5$.

## Quoting a bid/ask: market making in miniature

The jury does not ask "how much is this die worth?" but "**quote me** this die" — and the nuance is the whole job. To quote is to show two prices: a **bid** (the price at which you buy the outcome of the roll) and an **ask** (the one at which you sell it). The value is 3.5; a good quote looks like **3.3 / 3.7**:

- **Symmetric around the expected value**: the bid below $E$, the ask above. An off-centre spread tells the jury you have a directional view — on a die, that is absurd.
- **Of justified width**: the spread is your compensation for the risk and your margin for estimation error. Recall module 1's lesson: the market maker *lives off the spread* — that is exactly what you are doing here. Know how to defend it: on ONE roll, the standard deviation of a d6 is about 1.71; your 0.2 margin per trade is tiny against that noise, and only becomes an income when repeated — the market maker's job is a law of large numbers.
- **Alive**: the jury is going to trade, and watch what you do *next*.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="'I lift your ask' — the moment of truth">
You quote 3.3 / 3.7; the jury says "I buy from you at 3.7". You have just sold at 3.7 a flow worth 3.5: +0.2 of expected value, very good. But the right reaction does not stop there: *why* did he buy? If he trades again, and again, you must requote higher — maybe your price was too low, maybe he knows something (a loaded die?). It is module 1's **adverse selection** in embryo: whoever trades against you may be better informed. The candidate who shifts his quote to 3.5 / 3.9 after being lifted twice shows he has understood the job; the one who stays nailed to 3.3 / 3.7 gets eaten. What the jury is testing is less your first price than your second.
</Callout>

## The die with a re-roll: the hidden option

A jury favourite: "you roll a d6; you may keep the result, or re-roll once — the second roll is final. How much is this game worth?" Reason backwards, as for any option: the second roll is worth $E = 3.5$; on the first roll you therefore keep any result that beats 3.5 — that is, 4, 5 or 6 (average 5) — and you re-roll 1, 2 or 3:

$$
E = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 5 + \tfrac{1}{2} \times 3.5 = 4.25
$$

The lesson to state explicitly, because it is the one the jury is waiting for: the right to re-roll **is worth 0.75** — an option, even one never exercised at a loss, always has positive value (module 8: the right without the obligation). And prepare for the jury's follow-up, near-systematic: "and with *two* re-rolls?" The naive calculation chains on: ½ × 5 + ½ × 4.25 = 4.625. But it misses a refinement: with two re-rolls in hand, the continuation value at the first roll is 4.25 — you should therefore keep only 5 or 6 (average 5.5, probability 1/3):

$$
E = \tfrac{1}{3} \times 5.5 + \tfrac{2}{3} \times 4.25 = 4.666667
$$

The keep threshold *rises* with the number of re-rolls remaining — the richer your option, the more demanding you are. Spotting that change of strategy on your own is the difference between a good answer and a hire.

## Odds, bets and implied probability

Another disguise of the same exercise: betting. The **fair odds** of an event with probability $p$ are $100/p$ in "for 1" convention (`coteEquitable`): winning at 25% deserves odds of 4 — collect 4, stake included, per 1 staked, zero expected value; a 6 on a die deserves odds of 6. The inverse reading is the most useful: **the implied probability of odds is 1/odds**. A bookmaker offering 7 for 1 on a 6 prices the event at $1/7 \approx 14.3\%$ when it happens 16.7% of the time: the bet has positive expected value ($\tfrac{1}{6} \times 7 = 1.166667$ per euro staked, i.e. +0.17). Tell the jury this is gymnastics you already know: extracting the probability from a price is *exactly* the implied PD of a credit spread in module 5 — the price contains the probability, you only have to invert it.

Spotting the edge is only half the answer; the other half is **sizing**. Even with a positive expected value, staking everything is a fault: a losing streak — certain if you repeat long enough — takes you out of the game before the law of large numbers pays off. "I bet small, and I repeat" is the sentence that sounds like a desk; the GoFurther puts a number on "small".

## Auctions: the winner's curse

The jury puts a jar of coins on the table: "bid." The trap has a name: the **winner's curse**. The value of the jar is common but unknown; the candidates' estimates are spread around it, some too high, some too low. Yet the winner of the auction is mechanically the one whose estimate is *the highest* — hence, almost surely, the one who overestimated the most. Winning is bad news: conditional on victory, you have overpaid. The defence: shade your bid clearly **below** your own estimate, all the more so the more participants there are. In overview, also know the **second-price auction** (Vickrey): the winner pays the *second* bidder's bid — and in that format, the dominant strategy is to state your true price, since your bid only decides *whether* you win, never *how much* you pay.

## Paying to know: the value of information

Last family: "how much would you pay to see the card before deciding?" The rule fits in one line: **information is worth the difference between the expected value with it and without it**. A numbered mini-game: one card, red pays you €10, black nothing; the right to play costs €5 — the fair price, since $E = 5$ (`esperanceJeu` on 50%/50% and 10/0). Without information, playing earns 0 in expectation. With the right to look at the card first: if it is red (50%), you play and win $10 - 5 = 5$; if it is black, you do not play. Expected value: $\tfrac{1}{2} \times 5 = 2.50$ euros. You can therefore pay up to €2.50 for that peek. Information only has value if it can *change your decision* — a clue that alters no choice is worth exactly zero, whatever its content.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The four errors that sink you">
The jury forgives a hesitation in calculation, not a professional fault. **One**: quoting without having computed $E$ — a price pulled out of a hat is worse than silence. **Two**: the enormous spread — quoting "I buy at 1, I sell at 6" on a d6 is refusing to quote while pretending to; nobody will ever trade, and the jury will say so. **Three**: not moving your price after being dealt on — staying at 3.3 / 3.7 while the jury lifts your ask on loop is ignoring the information the flow gives you (module 1's adverse selection). **Four**: staking everything on a 2% edge — positive expected value does not protect from ruin; the desk that bets everything has the life expectancy of one bad draw.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="the Kelly criterion: putting a number on 'I bet small'">
How much to stake when you have an edge? John Kelly (Bell Labs, 1956) showed that the fraction of capital that maximises long-run growth is the edge divided by the **net** gain: writing $b$ for the net gain per unit staked (the "for 1" odds minus the stake), $f^* = (bp - q)/b$. On a 2-for-1 bet apparently fair but that you actually win 52% of the time: edge $= 0.52 - 0.48 = 4\%$, so $f^* = 4\%$ of the capital — not 100%, not 20%: 4. Beyond $f^*$, the expected gain per play keeps rising but the *compound* growth of the capital falls, eventually turning negative: over-staking turns a winning game into near-certain ruin, because of the asymmetry of compound returns (losing 50% requires +100% to get back). In practice, desks stake even less — "half-Kelly" — because the edge is estimated, not known, and overestimating your edge at full Kelly is fatal. It is the computable version of module 12's entire risk management: the size of the position is a decision distinct from its direction, and often the more important of the two.
</GoFurther>

You now know how to hold the game table: compute an expected value silently, show two defensible prices, requote when the flow speaks, value a re-roll option, invert odds, distrust won auctions and pay information its fair price. In other words: you know how to count under pressure — the part of the job that can be tested with a die. There remains the part that cannot be computed: talking about *yourself*. Why markets, why this desk, why you — the pitch, the background, and the art of turning it into a story that holds for three minutes without wobbling. That is chapter 6.
