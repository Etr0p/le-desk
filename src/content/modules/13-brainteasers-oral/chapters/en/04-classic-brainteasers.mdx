Let us start with the truth that changes everything: the jury has already heard the answer. A hundred times. It knows the fly, the doors, the eggs, and above all it knows the look of a candidate reciting a solution learnt the night before — the delivery too fluid, the absence of hesitation where everyone hesitates, the panic as soon as one number in the statement changes. What it marks is not the answer: it is the **approach**. Verbalising your reasoning, exploring a small case, looking for a symmetry, working backwards from the goal, accepting a hint without taking offence — that is the real evaluation grid, because it is exactly what is expected of a junior facing a problem nobody has yet solved on the desk. A candidate who rediscovers the solution out loud, even slowly, even with an error corrected along the way, scores more points than a candidate who recites it. This chapter therefore unrolls the classic bestiary — but each riddle is presented through the method that unlocks it, not through its answer.

<Callout type="important" titre="The toolbox: six moves, in order">
1. **Reformulate** the statement in your own words — half the traps sit in an implicit assumption that reformulation brings to light. 2. **Explore a small case**: $n = 1$, 2, 3 — almost every structure reveals itself before $n = 5$. 3. **Look for the symmetry or the invariant**: the quantity that does not change while everything moves is often the key. 4. **Think backwards** from the goal: "what would I need just before the end?". 5. **Bound**: what does the dumbest strategy give? the best imaginable? — the answer lives between the two. 6. **Verbalise EVERYTHING**, dead ends included: a two-minute silence is unreadable for the jury; a dead end explored out loud is evaluation data in your favour.
</Callout>

## The fly between the two trains: look for the simple path first

*The statement.* Two trains 60 km apart race towards each other at 30 km/h each. A fly leaves the first at 60 km/h, flies to the second, turns around, returns to the first, and so on until the collision. What distance does it cover?

*The trap laid*: summing the infinite series of round trips — doable, but long and perilous. *The method*: change viewpoint and ask the simple question first — **how long** does the fly fly? The trains close at 60 km/h and have 60 km to cover: collision in 1 h. The fly therefore flies for 1 h at 60 km/h: **60 km**. Done. The canonical anecdote has the riddle put to John von Neumann, who answered instantly, and to the "ah, you know the trick" replied: "what trick? I summed the series". *What the jury watches*: is your first reflex to charge into the heavy calculation, or to spend thirty seconds looking for the reformulation that makes it unnecessary? On a desk, that difference is measured in hours.

## The 100 doors: the small case, then the invariant

*The statement.* 100 closed doors. On pass $k$ (for $k$ from 1 to 100), you toggle the state of every door that is a multiple of $k$. After 100 passes, how many doors are open?

*The method*: small case first — follow door 12: toggled on passes 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12, that is six times, hence closed. Door 9: passes 1, 3, 9 — three toggles, open. The invariant appears: door $n$ is toggled **once per divisor of $n$**, and divisors come **in pairs** $(d, n/d)$… except when $d = n/d$, that is, when $n$ is a **perfect square**. Even number of toggles ⇒ closed; odd ⇒ open. Left open: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100 — **10 doors**. *What the jury watches*: the discipline of the small case (did you follow ONE door before reasoning about a hundred?) and the clean statement of the parity argument.

## The two eggs and the 100 floors: bound, then balance

*The statement.* A 100-storey building, two identical eggs. There is a critical floor from which a dropped egg breaks. How many drops, in the worst case, to find it with certainty?

*The method*: bound first. With a single egg: floor by floor, 100 tries in the worst case. With naive bisection (drop from the 50th): if the egg breaks, one egg remains and 49 floors to test one by one — 50 in the worst case, unbalanced. The **minimax** idea: equalise the worst case across all branches. If the first drop is from floor $k$ and it breaks, $k - 1$ tries remain; if it holds, the second drop must be $k - 1$ floors higher (one try has been used up), then $k - 2$, and so on. You therefore need $k + (k-1) + (k-2) + \cdots + 1 \geq 100$, i.e. $\frac{k(k+1)}{2} \geq 100$: $k = 14$ ($105 \geq 100$). First drop from the 14th floor, then the 27th, 39th, 50th…: **14 tries in the worst case**. *What the jury watches*: the path — the bounds, the imbalance of bisection, the correction by decrement — is worth far more than the number 14; it is a risk-budget allocation argument, the heart of the job.

## The 9 balls and the scale: think in ternary

*The statement.* Nine identical balls, exactly one of them heavier. A two-pan balance scale. How many weighings to find it with certainty?

*The method*: count the information. The binary reflex — cut in half — is sub-optimal, because a weighing has not two outcomes but **three**: tips left, tips right, **balances**. Each weighing can therefore discriminate between three groups: weigh 3 balls against 3, and the third outcome (balance) points to the 3 balls left on the table. Two weighings distinguish $3^2 = 9$ cases: **2 weighings** suffice — 3 against 3, then 1 against 1 within the designated group. *What the jury watches*: did you see balance as an outcome in its own right? Widening the space of an instrument's possible answers is the same skill as reading in a market price the information it carries implicitly.

## The bridge and torch puzzle: sacrifice locally to win globally

*The statement.* Four people cross a bridge at night in 1, 2, 5 and 10 minutes respectively; a single torch, two people at most at a time, you walk at the pace of the slower. Minimal time to get everyone across?

*The natural reflex*: the fastest (1 min) escorts everyone — $10 + 1 + 5 + 1 + 2 = 19$ minutes. *The method*: look for where the reflex wastes — it pays for 10 and 5 separately. The idea: send **the two slow ones across together** so you pay for the slowest only once. 1 and 2 cross (2 min), 1 comes back (1), **5 and 10 cross** (10), 2 comes back (2), 1 and 2 cross (2): **17 minutes**. *What the jury watches*: do you accept worsening a local move (sending 2 back rather than 1) to improve the total? It is the anti-greedy — and the structure of most desk optimisations, where the cheapest execution trade by trade is not the cheapest for the portfolio.

## The bulb and the three switches: widen the space of observables

*The statement.* Three switches downstairs, one bulb in a closed room upstairs. You may manipulate the switches as you wish, then go up **once only**. Which switch controls the bulb?

*The method*: count, once again — a binary observation (on/off) cannot separate three hypotheses. You therefore need a third observable value: **heat**. Turn switch 1 on for ten minutes, turn it off, turn switch 2 on, go up: lit ⇒ 2; off and **warm** ⇒ 1; off and cold ⇒ 3. *What the jury watches*: the moment you realise the blockage lies in too narrow a definition of "observing" — a bulb is not just a light source. The best answers verbalise the counting ("two states are not enough for three switches, I need a third signal") before finding the heat.

## The prisoners and the hats: encode the common information

*The statement.* 100 prisoners in single file, each seeing all the hats (black or white) in front of him, none behind. Starting from the back, each announces a colour: if correct, he is freed. The prisoners may agree on a strategy beforehand. How many can be saved with certainty?

*The method*: think backwards and look for what the first one — who can know nothing about his own hat — can offer the others. His word is lost for him, but it is a **channel**: one bit of common information. The convention: he announces "black" if the number of black hats he sees is **odd**, "white" otherwise. The next one counts the black hats in front of him, compares the parity to the announcement, deduces his own hat; each subsequent announcement updates the running parity. **99 saved with certainty**, the first at 50%. *What the jury watches*: the mental flip — stop trying to save the first and turn him into a transmitter; it is information theory in one sentence, and the riddle whose variants (multiple colours: parity modulo $k$) test whether you understood or memorised.

| Riddle | The unlocking move | The answer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The fly and the trains | Reformulate: flight time, not trajectory | 60 km |
| The 100 doors | Small case, then divisor parity | 10 doors (the perfect squares) |
| The two eggs | Minimax: equalise the worst cases | 14 tries (14 + 13 + ⋯ ≥ 100) |
| The 9 balls | Think ternary: balance is an outcome | 2 weighings (3 groups of 3) |
| The bridge and torch | Send the slow ones across together | 17 minutes |
| The 3 switches | Widen the observables: heat | 1 trip up suffices |
| The prisoners | Encode parity in the first announcement | 99 saved for sure |

<Callout type="piege" titre="The four reflexes that sink an interview">
The brainteaser is rarely failed on the maths; it is failed on behaviour. **Going silent for two minutes**: the jury only evaluates what it hears — a mute reasoning does not exist; think out loud even in the dead end. **Charging into the calculation without reformulating**: it is the fly, infinite-series version — technically right, strategically losing. **Refusing the hint**: when the jury holds out a pole ("what if you thought about what else the scale can tell you?"), grabbing it with enthusiasm earns a point, clinging stubbornly to your own track out of pride costs three — on a desk, we want people who integrate new information. **Collapsing after an error**: everyone gets these riddles wrong; the candidate who says "no, wait, this breaks at the case $n = 2$, let me start over" has just shown exactly what is being sought — the recovery is worth more than the first attempt.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="k eggs, n floors: generalising the two eggs">
The version with $k$ eggs and $e$ tries is solved by the recurrence that made the two-egg solution: write $f(e, k)$ for the maximal number of floors covered. The first drop partitions the problem — if it breaks, $f(e-1, k-1)$ floors remain below; if it holds, $f(e-1, k)$ above — whence $f(e, k) = f(e-1, k-1) + f(e-1, k) + 1$. With two eggs, it closes on $f(e, 2) = \frac{e(e+1)}{2}$, our $105$ for $e = 14$; in general, $f(e, k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} C(e, i)$ — and chapter 3's combinations reappear where nobody expected them. Two illuminating limits: at $k = 1$, $f(e, 1) = e$ (floor by floor); at large $k$, $f(e, k) \to 2^e - 1$ — pure bisection, because an unlimited egg budget makes risk free. The whole family of riddles is therefore an interpolation between linear caution and binary boldness, indexed by the breakage budget: put that way, it is a risk-management problem, and the jury that pushes this far wants to hear you say it.
</GoFurther>

The bestiary is unrolled, the method is in place: reformulate, small case, invariant, backwards, bounds, and say everything out loud. But these riddles kept one comfort: they have an exact answer, and the jury expects you to find it. The next step removes that comfort too: "quote me a market on the number of windows in Paris", "I roll a die, make me a price". No more right answer — a bid, an ask, an expectation and a margin: when the jury moves you from riddle-solving to the **game**, it is no longer testing the logician, it is testing the trader. That is chapter 5.
