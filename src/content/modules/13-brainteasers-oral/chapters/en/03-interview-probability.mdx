Chapters 1 and 2 gave you mental math and the order of magnitude; this one gives you the main weapon. If probability dominates trading-floor interviews, it is not by academic tradition: it is because the job *is* a repeated bet. A market maker who quotes a thousand times a day does not win on one trade, he wins in expectation — and the jury wants to verify one single thing: that you think in expectation, not in anecdote. The full theory (axioms, random variables, distributions) is in module 2, chapter 3 "Probability: the brainteaser weapon"; here we assemble the oral's toolbox — four reflexes, a handful of numerical anchors, and the traps where intuition will systematically crash.

## Reflex no. 1: go through the complement

The most asked question in interview history: "what is the probability of getting at least one 6 in four rolls of a die?". Additive intuition answers $4 \times 1/6 = 66.7\%$ — and it is **wrong**. Successes do not add up, they compound: at that rate, six rolls would give a 100% chance, which is absurd. The right reflex: the words "at least one" must trigger the switch to the **complement** — the probability that there is *no* 6 at all.

$$
P(\text{at least one 6}) = 1 - \left(\frac{5}{6}\right)^4 = 51.774691\%
$$

<Callout type="definition" titre="The complement: the 'nothing happens' event">
For $n$ independent trials each with success probability $p$, the probability of **at least one** success is $1 - (1-p)^n$: you compute the probability that *all* the trials fail — a simple product, thanks to independence — and subtract it from 1. Two anchors to know by heart: at least one 6 in 4 rolls = $1 - (5/6)^4 = 51.8\%$; at least one heads in 3 tosses = $1 - (1/2)^3 = 87.5\%$. The additive trap $n \times p$ has exactly the same structure as module 5's cumulative default: survival probabilities multiply, default probabilities never add.
</Callout>

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The chevalier de Méré's wager — the birth of a discipline">
This question is not a schoolbook exercise: it is the birth certificate of probability theory. Around 1654, Antoine Gombaud, chevalier de Méré, a professional gambler, won regularly by betting that a 6 would come up within four rolls — the bet is favourable by a whisker, 51.8% against 48.2%. He then extended the bet to "at least one double 6 in 24 rolls of two dice", believed it equivalent by a rule of three… and lost: $1 - (35/36)^{24} = 49.1\%$, unfavourable by a whisker. Intrigued, he submitted the puzzle to Blaise Pascal, who began with Pierre de Fermat the correspondence of the summer of 1654 in which the two men invented the calculus of probabilities. Telling this anecdote in thirty seconds in an oral does two things: it shows you know *why* the rule of three fails, and it reminds the jury that even an experienced gambler gets trapped by additive intuition.
</Callout>

## The birthday paradox: count pairs, not people

Second classic: "how many people do you need in a room for two of them to have a better-than-even chance of sharing a birthday?". Intuition answers 183 — half of 365. The answer is **23**, and the exact probability is 50.729723%. At 50 people, you are already at 97.0%; at 70, at 99.9% — near-certainty well before a hundred.

Why does intuition miss by a factor of eight? Because it counts *people*, whereas collisions happen between *pairs*. With 23 people, there are $C(23, 2) = 253$ possible pairs — 253 distinct opportunities for a coincidence, each with probability $1/365$. The number of pairs grows like $n^2$, not like $n$: it is this quadratic growth that the brain, wired linear, does not see. The clean calculation goes — once again — through the complement: the probability that all the birthdays are distinct is $\frac{365}{365} \times \frac{364}{365} \times \cdots \times \frac{343}{365}$, a product of factors eroding one by one until it drops below 50%.

The lesson to carry to the desk: joint events are always more likely than they feel. Two "independent" defaults in a hundred-name portfolio, two outages on the same day, two traders making the same error — as soon as you count pairs, the rare becomes commonplace.

## Bayes: the absolute classic

Here is THE question, the one every desk asks in one form or another: "a disease affects 1% of the population; a test detects it in 99% of cases, with 5% false positives; your test is positive — what is the probability that you are ill?". Intuition answers 99%. The right answer is **16.666667%** — one sixth. The candidate who pulls out Bayes' formula is correct; the candidate who unrolls the **method of 10,000** scores, because it can be recited in three lines of mental arithmetic:

- Out of 10,000 people: 1% are ill, i.e. **100 ill** — the test detects 99 of them.
- The **9,900 healthy** suffer 5% false positives: **495** tests wrongly positive.
- Among the $99 + 495 = 594$ positives, only 99 are true: $99/594 = 1/6 \approx 16.7\%$.

False positives **drown** the true ones when prevalence is low: 495 against 99, five to one. The intuition error has a name — *base rate neglect*: you read "99% reliable test" and confuse $P(\text{test}^+ \mid \text{ill})$ with $P(\text{ill} \mid \text{test}^+)$. They are two different conditional probabilities, and the whole of Bayes' theorem lies in the art of moving from one to the other by reintroducing the prevalence. The oral sentence that sums it all up: *the strength of a signal depends on the rarity of what it seeks*.

## Combinations: counting without enumerating

When order does not matter, you count with $C(n, k)$ — the number of ways to choose $k$ objects among $n$. The universal anchor: $C(52, 5) = 2{,}598{,}960$ poker hands. In an oral, the distinguishing reflex is to **simplify before multiplying**: $\frac{52 \times 51 \times 50 \times 49 \times 48}{5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2}$ is tamed by dividing first — $50/5 = 10$, $48/4 = 12$, $51/3 = 17$, $52/2 = 26$ — to finish with $26 \times 17 \times 10 \times 12 \times 49 = 2{,}598{,}960$, without ever handling an eight-digit number.

Two mini-classics to warm up. *Two aces in a row*: you draw two cards without replacement — $\frac{4}{52} \times \frac{3}{51} = \frac{1}{13} \times \frac{1}{17} = \frac{1}{221} \approx 0.45\%$; the point being marked is the conditioning, the second factor *given* that the first ace is out. *Poker hands* in overview: a specific pair, a flush, four of a kind — all are computed by counting the favourable cases with combinations and dividing by 2,598,960; nobody will ask you for the full table, but knowing how to set up the quotient shows you know how to count.

## Streaks, and the information that changes everything

One last pair of tools. First, **streaks**: the probability of $n$ *consecutive* successes is $p^n$ — five heads in a row = $(1/2)^5 = 3.125\%$. It is the exact mirror of "at least one": here you multiply the successes, there you multiplied the failures; confusing the two directions is a classic end-of-interview fault, when fatigue sets in.

Then, the great classic of information: **Monty Hall**. Three doors, one car, two goats; you pick a door; the host — who *knows* where the car is — opens one of the other two, always on a goat, and offers you the switch. Should you switch? **Yes: switching raises your chances from 1/3 to 2/3.** The why, without formalism: your initial door had a 1/3 chance, and nothing the host does can change that fact — he will open a losing door no matter what. The remaining 2/3, initially spread over two doors, therefore concentrate entirely on the door he did *not* open: his move, constrained by his knowledge, carried information to you. If an ignorant friend had opened a door at random and found a goat by luck, switching would be pointless — 50/50. The whole difference lies in *who knows what*: it is Bayes in disguise, and it is exactly what the jury wants to hear you say.

<Callout type="piege" titre="'The coin is due': the gambler's fallacy">
A fair coin gives ten heads in a row. The next toss? **50%** — the coin has no memory, the tosses are independent, and believing a tails is "due" to rebalance the streak is the *gambler's fallacy*, the sophism that has filled casinos since forever (the law of large numbers dilutes deviations in the long run, it never compensates them toss by toss). But the oral has a second storey: if the jury insists — "really, after ten heads?" — the fine answer is Bayesian: *provided the coin is fair*, 50%; yet ten heads in a row happens only 0.1% of the time, so a Bayesian mind starts revising its confidence in the coin itself, and would rather bet heads. The two symmetric errors: assuming a dependence that does not exist (the coin is due), and defending an assumed independence against the evidence (the coin may be loaded).
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="Monty Hall run through Bayes">
The "with formalism" version, for juries that dig. Write $V_i$ for the event "the car is behind door $i$"; you pick door 1, the host opens door 3. The key is the likelihood of his move: if the car is behind door 1, he opened 2 or 3 at random — $P(\text{opens 3} \mid V_1) = 1/2$; if it is behind door 2, he was *forced* to open door 3 — $P(\text{opens 3} \mid V_2) = 1$. Bayes then gives $P(V_2 \mid \text{opens 3}) = \frac{1 \times 1/3}{1 \times 1/3 + 1/2 \times 1/3} = 2/3$. The constrained move is twice as likely when the car is behind the other door: there is the "added information", quantified. Same mechanics as the medical test: a signal is only worth the comparison of its likelihoods under the two hypotheses — and an ignorant host, whose move would have the same likelihood everywhere, would add rigorously nothing.
</GoFurther>

You now have the four reflexes: the complement for "at least one", pairs for coincidences, the 10,000 for Bayes, simplification for combinations — and two guardrails, the independence that forbids the "due" coin and the information that tips Monty Hall. But all these problems shared one luxury: they can be *computed*. The next chapter removes that net — pure logic riddles, where there is no longer a formula to apply, only a method to unroll out loud while the jury watches you search.
