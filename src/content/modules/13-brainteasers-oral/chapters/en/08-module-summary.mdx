This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the essentials chapter by chapter, the formula sheet, the traps and the reflexes — then, since this module is the last, the whole course rolled back up on one page. The guiding thread fits in one sentence: this module adds not a gram of material — the previous twelve supplied it — it adds the only two things still missing for the oral: **speed** and **composure**. The jury never tests the result, it tests the **method made audible**: decompose, bound, state the error, go through the complement, recite the 10,000, quote around the expectation, say "I don't know" properly — and all of it out loud, because a silent line of reasoning does not exist for a jury.

## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **Mental arithmetic: the desk's toolbox (ch. 1).** Nobody hires a calculator: the jury grades four moves — **decompose**, **round cleanly**, **bound**, **state the error** (the rarest and the most rewarding). The **rule of 72**: doubling in $72/t$ years — 8% ⇒ **9 years** (exact 9.006468, error −0.07%), 6% ⇒ 12, 2% ⇒ 36 against 35.0: at low rates the rule OVERESTIMATES, and saying so is part of the answer. Quick percentages: commutativity (8% of 25 = 25% of 8 = **2**), the asymmetry of variations (+10% then −10% = **−1%**), 100 bps = 1%, dividing by 0.8 = multiplying by 1.25. Multiplication: bound first (17 × 24 lives between 200 and 500), decompose next (17 × 25 − 17 = **408**). And the meta-reflex that caps it all: **announce, bound, refine** — the decimal only if the jury asks for it.
2. **Fermi estimates (ch. 2).** Fermi estimated Trinity to within a factor of 2 with confetti: a Fermi question requires NO knowledge and makes the whole reasoning visible. The five-step protocol: restate and fix the unit, decompose (population × rate × frequency), bracket with SAFE bounds, take the **geometric mean** $\sqrt{b \times h}$ — between 1,000 and 1,000,000, **31,622 ≈ 30,000**, never the arithmetic mean (500,500, off by a factor of 15: it crushes the lower bound) — and announce the order of magnitude with its uncertainty factor. The canonical walkthroughs: ~**30** piano tuners in Paris; FX rebuilt from scratch — \$120bn of real flows per day × a multiplier of ~63 ≈ **\$7.5tn** (m6's anchor as a guardrail); ~3.6 million ping-pong balls in the A320 (do not forget the ~60% packing rate). The counter-intuitive hierarchy: the **structure** is worth more than the result.
3. **Interview probabilities (ch. 3).** Four reflexes. "**At least one**" ⇒ complement rule: $1 - (5/6)^4 = $ **51.8%**, not 4 × 1/6 = 66.7% — de Méré's wager, the birth certificate of probability theory (1654). The **birthdays**: **23** people are enough (50.7%), not 183 — you count the PAIRS, $C(23,2) = 253$, growth in $n^2$. **Bayes** by the method of 10,000: prevalence 1%, sensitivity 99%, false positives 5% ⇒ 99 true against 495 false positives ⇒ 99/594 = **16.7%**, not 99% — base rate neglect; the oral-ready sentence: *the strength of a signal depends on the rarity of what it seeks*. **Combinations**: $C(52,5) = 2{,}598{,}960$, simplify before multiplying. Two guardrails: the streak $p^n$ (five heads = 3.125%) and its mirror; the coin is never "due" (gambler's fallacy) — but after ten heads, a Bayesian doubts the coin. And **Monty Hall**: switch, 1/3 → **2/3** — the whole difference lies in *who knows what*.
4. **The classic brainteasers (ch. 4).** The jury knows the answer: it grades the **verbalised approach** — six moves: restate, small case, symmetry or invariant, work backwards, bound, say EVERYTHING out loud. The bestiary by its unlocking move: the fly — restate as flight time, **60 km**; the 100 doors — parity of divisors, **10** left open (the perfect squares); the two eggs — minimax, $k(k+1)/2 \geq 100$ ⇒ **14** tries; the 9 balls — the scale has THREE outcomes, $3^2 = 9$ ⇒ **2** weighings; the bridge — the two slow ones together, **17 minutes**; the switches — widen the observables: heat; the 100 prisoners — encode the parity, **99** saved for certain. An error corrected out loud is a point WON; a two-minute silence is disqualifying.
5. **Market games (ch. 5).** "Make me a market on this die": the desk simulation. The expectation FIRST — $E = \sum p_i g_i$, an $f$-faced die $(f+1)/2$: **3.5** for a d6, not 3 — then a symmetric spread of defensible width (**3.3/3.7**), and make it LIVE: lifted twice at the ask, you requote higher — m1's adverse selection. The re-roll die: keep 4-5-6, $E = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 5 + \tfrac{1}{2} \times 3.5 = $ **4.25** — the re-roll option is worth 0.75 (m8); with two re-rolls, you keep only 5-6: 4.67, the threshold RISES. Odds: fair odds = 100/p, implied probability = **1/odds** — m5's implied PD in disguise. Auctions: winning a common-value pot almost surely means you overestimated (**winner's curse**); in a second-price auction (Vickrey), bid your true value. Information is worth the difference of expectations: **€2.50** to see the card. And sizing: **Kelly** $f^* = (bp-q)/b$ = 4% on a 4% edge — never bet everything.
6. **Defending your background (ch. 6).** Half of the oral does not test finance, and it is prepared like a pricing. The **pitch**: four blocks (where I come from, the trigger, what I built, why here), three lengths (30 s / 90 s / 3 min), and THE rule: one **verifiable fact per sentence**, never an adjective — "I backtested a momentum strategy over ten years" writes the jury's first five questions for you. "Why finance?": a precise moment THEN proof by action — never money, prestige or adrenaline (describing yourself as an operational risk). "Why this desk?": place yourself on m1's map of chairs — flow, structuring, sales, research — with an argued preference and genuine curiosity. The failure: the fact, your share, what you changed — never the fake failure ("I am too much of a perfectionist"). The **conviction**: thesis, mechanism, **invalidation condition** — without the third storey, it is an opinion. Current affairs: **three topics in depth** (rates, credit/equities, structural) in three layers — numbers, mechanism, both sides.
7. **The big day (ch. 7).** The universal structure: **restate** (5 seconds), **announce the plan**, **unroll**, **conclude in one sentence — and stop talking**: every word after the right answer is attack surface offered. The blank: **go up one level** — the forgotten constant can be reasoned out ("ln 2 ≈ 0.69, so ~70/rate"); the structured scratch work — write down ALL the data before computing. "I don't know" done properly: the admission THEN the reasoning — bound, decompose; a detected invention retroactively contaminates everything correct you have said. The mistake: **self-correct in one sentence, no apology, and keep going** — m11's catastrophes are born of UNacknowledged errors. The mechanics: slow delivery at the start, write big, narrate the board, sweep the jury with your eyes. Bridges between modules are graded transversally. And the final days: **timed simulation** out loud — runners, MCQs, jury questions, flashcards — then sleep.

## The traps that cost points

1. **The additive intuition n × p** (ch. 3): 4 × 1/6 = 66.7% is wrong — by that logic, six rolls would be a certainty. Successes compound: 1 − (5/6)⁴ = 51.8% — the lesson of m5's cumulative default.
2. **Counting people instead of pairs** (ch. 3): 183 instead of 23 — collisions live between pairs, and C(n, 2) grows in n², which the linear brain does not see.
3. **Base rate neglect** (ch. 3): answering 99% on the medical test — confusing P(test⁺ | sick) with P(sick | test⁺) by forgetting the prevalence; the right answer is 1/6, because 495 false positives drown 99 true ones.
4. **Confusing the two directions** (ch. 3): "at least one" multiplies the FAILURES, the consecutive streak multiplies the SUCCESSES — the classic end-of-interview mistake, when fatigue sets in.
5. **"The coin is due"** (ch. 3): tosses are independent, the law of large numbers dilutes without compensating — and the finer storey: after ten heads (0.1%), a Bayesian doubts the coin itself.
6. **The arithmetic mean across orders of magnitude** (ch. 2): 500,500 between 1,000 and 1,000,000 crushes the lower bound — the multiplicative middle is √(b × h) ≈ 30,000, and the arithmetic mean is off by a factor of 15.
7. **False precision** (ch. 2, 7): announcing "34,217" when the method only justifies "about 30,000" — every decimal beyond the order of magnitude admits you have not understood the exercise; in front of a jury, invented precision retroactively contaminates everything else.
8. **The "middle of the faces"** (ch. 5): reading the expectation of a d6 as 3 — it is (6 + 1)/2 = 3.5: the faces run from 1 to f, not from 0 to f.
9. **Quoting without having computed, or refusing to quote** (ch. 5): a price pulled out of a hat is worse than silence; and a 1/6-wide spread on a d6 is a refusal to quote in disguise — nobody will ever trade on it.
10. **Not moving your price after being traded** (ch. 5): sitting at 3.3/3.7 while the jury lifts the ask again and again is ignoring the information in the flow — m1's adverse selection.
11. **Betting everything on an edge** (ch. 5): positive expectation does not protect against ruin — Kelly says 4% on a 4% edge, and desks bet half-Kelly because the edge is estimated, not known.
12. **Reciting instead of searching** (ch. 4): the jury knows the answer and spots delivery that is too fluent — then changes one number in the statement. Rediscovering out loud beats reciting, and a two-minute silence is unreadable.
13. **The adjective without a fact, the fake failure, the invention** (ch. 6, 7): "passionate" carries no information; "I work too hard" is a refusal to answer; and false precision in front of professionals is the one fault that is never forgiven.

## The twelve reflexes for the oral

1. Facing any estimate: **announce, bound, refine** — the number, then its error or its range of validity, then the decimal only if asked.
2. Doubling in one division: **72/t** — and always with its limit: near exact around 8%, overestimates at low rates.
3. Percentages the easy way round: **8% of 25 = 25% of 8 = 2**; 100 bps = 1% without pausing for breath; dividing by 0.8 = multiplying by 1.25.
4. "At least one" ⇒ **complement rule**; a coincidence ⇒ **count the pairs**; a positive test ⇒ **the method of 10,000**.
5. Facing any signal: *its strength depends on the rarity of what it seeks* — the reliable indicator watching for a rare event mostly produces false alarms.
6. Fermi in five steps: unit, decomposition, **safe** bounds, **geometric** mean, announcement with an uncertainty factor — and the course's anchor as a guardrail at landing.
7. Brainteaser: **restate, small case, invariant, work backwards, bound — and say everything out loud**, dead ends included; seize the hint with enthusiasm.
8. "Make me a market on this die": the expectation **before opening your mouth**, a defensible symmetric spread, and **requote** when the flow speaks.
9. Every quoted odds inverts: **implied probability = 1/odds** — the same move as the implied PD of a spread; then size it: "I bet small, and I repeat".
10. The pitch: **one verifiable fact per sentence** — and the conviction in three storeys: thesis, mechanism, **invalidation condition**, with today's level.
11. In front of the jury: **restate, announce the plan, unroll, conclude in one sentence — and stop talking**; a blank is handled by going up one level, an "I don't know" is followed straight away by reasoning.
12. A mistake is corrected **in one sentence, without apology, and you keep going** — it is the "and you keep going" that is graded: a desk lives on the fast detection of its own errors (m11).

## The course on one page

Thirteen modules, each reduced to one idea and one number — the page to reread the day before the oral.

1. **m1 — Panorama: the spread.** Every market price is double — a bid, an ask — and the market maker lives off the spread times the turnover, against two risks: inventory and adverse selection. The number: a spread of **10 bps** (99.95/100.05) — the price of immediacy.
2. **m2 — Quantitative methods: compounding.** Variations multiply, they never add — returns, survivals, probabilities. The number: **+10% then −10% = −1%** — and after −50%, you need +100%.
3. **m3 — Equities: discounted cash flows.** A company is worth the sum of its future cash flows discounted — everything else (multiples, comparables) is a shortcut for that sentence. The number: at 8%, a cash flow in **9 years is worth half** — the rule of 72 read backwards.
4. **m4 — Rates: duration.** A bond's price and rates move in opposite directions, and duration puts a number on the slope. The number: duration 7, **+100 bps ⇒ −7%**.
5. **m5 — Credit: EL = PD × LGD.** The spread pays for the expected loss — probability of default times loss given default — plus the risk and liquidity premia. The number: PD 2%, R 40% ⇒ **120 bps** "deserved".
6. **m6 — FX: covered parity.** The forward rate is not a forecast, it is a rate differential locked in by arbitrage. The number: **\$7.5tn** per day (BIS 2022) — the biggest market in the world.
7. **m7 — Futures: the margin call.** The future settles every evening, and the initial margin manufactures the leverage — the margin call lands at the worst moment. The number: a few % of margin for 100% of the notional — a leverage of **10 to 20**.
8. **m8 — Options: the asymmetry of the payoff.** The buyer risks their premium for an unbounded gain; the seller pockets small and regular against a rare, massive loss. The number: the right to re-roll a die is worth **0.75** — an option always has positive value.
9. **m9 — Structured products: the options budget.** Capital protection is a zero-coupon carrying the guarantee plus whatever is left for the options — the level of rates manufactures the generosity of the formula. The number: at 3% over 10 years, the zero-coupon costs **74**, leaving a budget of 26.
10. **m10 — Central banks: the rate behind every pricer.** The policy rate is the r of every model in the course — discounting, CAPM, parity, options budget. The number: the **2%** target — prices doubling every 35 years, eight decisions a year.
11. **m11 — Crises: the leverage that kills.** Catastrophes are not born of errors but of unacknowledged errors — amplified by leverage and by liquidity shutting down. The number: Lehman, leverage of **31** — i.e. ~3.2% of cushion.
12. **m12 — Asset management: the only free lunch.** Return averages out, risk does not — diversification is the markets' only gift, and it is withdrawn in crises. The number: 60/40, vols 20/10, ρ 0.3 ⇒ **13.74%** against 16% as a weighted average.
13. **m13 — Brainteasers and the oral: method under pressure.** Announce, bound, refine; the complement, the pairs, the 10,000; the expectation before the quote; the verifiable fact; correct yourself in one sentence and keep going. The number: **16.7%** — Bayes's one-sixth, the answer that separates a trained candidate from an intuition that says 99%.

## The final word

Here you are at the end of the journey: the mechanisms, the orders of magnitude, the textbook cases, the scars — and, with this module, the method for delivering them under pressure. What remains can no longer be learned by reading: this application is your training room — the **runners** for calculation under time pressure, the **flashcards** in spaced repetition for the automatisms, the **jury questions** for rehearsing out loud, and the **mock exam** to endure the change of scale that tires you out. Twenty minutes of simulation are worth an hour of passive rereading, because they produce errors — and an error in simulation is the only one that costs nothing. When the day comes, you will need only one thing: to explain a mechanism, with a number, under pressure. You know how to do it — all that remains is to practise saying it. Good luck with the oral.
