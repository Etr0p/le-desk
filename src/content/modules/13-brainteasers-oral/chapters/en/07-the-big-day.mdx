Let us set the frame, because it is the one this whole course has been preparing for since the first chapter: a competition-style oral. A jury of professionals — traders, structurers, salespeople — facing you, questions in rapid fire that change scale without warning: a brainteaser (chapter 4), then a course question drawn from any of the twelve modules, then a numbered case to unroll at the board, then "a market view?" (chapter 6). The substance is in place: the mechanisms, the figures, the calculation reflexes, the pitch. This last chapter deals with what remains — the *method of delivery*: how the same knowledge earns twice as many points depending on how it is delivered. Nothing here is coaching: every piece of advice is an observable behaviour, which you can rehearse in simulation the way you rehearse a pricing.

## The universal answer structure

<Callout type="important" titre="The four beats of every answer">
Whatever the question — technical, brainteaser, motivation — the same structure: **1) reformulate** (5 seconds: "so you are asking me for the effect of a rate rise on the price of this swap, from the fixed payer's point of view?") — this checks understanding, corrects a misunderstanding before it costs three minutes, and buys legitimate thinking time; **2) announce the plan** ("two things: the mechanism, then a figure") — the jury knows where you are going and can follow you; **3) unroll** while looking at the jury, in the announced order; **4) conclude in one sentence** — the answer must fit in a final line — and **stop talking**. The silence after a good answer is an ally: it is the jury that must fill it, not you.
</Callout>

The fourth beat is the one candidates miss most. The temptation, once the right answer has been given, is to keep going — adding a nuance, a second illustration, an "and by the way…": every extra word is attack surface offered for free, and the jury ends up probing the poorly mastered nuance rather than the correct answer. Deliver, conclude, stop talking: handing the floor back to the jury is a sign of control, not of emptiness. Same discipline on the reformulation: five seconds, not thirty — reformulating every question word for word becomes a tic the jury spots by the third occurrence.

## The blank: the one-level-up technique

The blank will come — on the constant of a formula, the date of a crisis, the value of a factor. The counter has a name: **go up one level**. If the detail escapes you, the mechanism is still there: "I no longer remember the exact constant of the approximation rule, but the reasoning is this — doubling depends on the logarithm of 2, so the order of magnitude is 70 divided by the rate, and at 8% we are talking about roughly nine years". You have just turned a memory lapse into a demonstration of understanding: the jury marks the structure of the reasoning far more than the constant — a candidate who recites 72 without knowing where it comes from is worth less than a candidate who recovers 70 by reasoning it out.

On numbered cases, the blank is prevented by the **structured rough draft**: lay out *all* the data before computing anything — the notional, the rate, the maturity, the convention, what is being sought. Thirty seconds of inventory that feel slow and save two minutes: half of the collapses at the board come from a calculation launched before the data were stabilised. And if the void is total: ten seconds of *owned* silence — announced, even: "give me ten seconds to set up the reasoning" — always beat filler. Filler ("well, er, it's interesting because, so…") signals panic; announced silence signals control.

## "I don't know", done properly

There is a hierarchy of answers when facing a question whose answer you do not know, and you must know it before entering the room. The worst, by far, is **invention**: a jury of professionals detects false precision instantly — it is their daily material — and does not forgive it, because it answers the only question that really interests them: "can we trust what this candidate says?". A detected invention retroactively contaminates everything correct you have said. The right form comes in two beats: "**I do not know this specific point; here is how I would reason about it**" — then bound and decompose, exactly the reflexes of chapters 1 and 2: bracket the order of magnitude, cut the problem into pieces some of which are known, propose a way to verify. An "I don't know" followed by thirty seconds of reasoning scores *more* points than an average answer recited: it shows the jury exactly what they will see the day when, on the desk, you are asked a question nobody prepared you for — that is to say, every day.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The same memory lapse, two outcomes">
Question: "how long to double a capital at 6%?" Candidate A has forgotten the rule: "er… I'd say… fifteen years? maybe twelve?" — two numbers tossed out with no method, the jury notes the improvisation. Candidate B has forgotten the same rule: "I can't recall the exact constant, but doubling depends on ln 2, about 0.69; with annual compounding, the order of magnitude is 70 divided by the rate, so around twelve years at 6%". Same memory lapse, but B showed the structure beneath the formula — and he recovered the right figure. The difference between A and B is not memory: it is chapter 1 of this module, mobilised under pressure.
</Callout>

## Recovering from an error

You will make errors in front of the jury — an inverted sign, a forgotten factor, a mix-up between two curves. What is marked is not the error: it is what comes next. **Correcting yourself out loud** — "no, I am wrong: the sign is inverted, the fixed payer *gains* if rates rise, since he pays a fixed rate that has become cheap" — is one of the most positive signals a candidate can send, because it is literally the skill a desk lives on: the rapid detection of one's own errors, before they grow. Module 11 showed it in negative: financial catastrophes are not born of errors, they are born of errors *not acknowledged* — held on to, averaged down, hidden in a drawer. Conversely, the two behaviours that cost: holding on to the error hoping the jury did not see (they saw), and collapsing — apologising at length, losing the thread, letting the error contaminate the following questions. The correction is done in one sentence, without apology or commentary, and you carry on: it is that "and you carry on" that is marked.

## The physical mechanics

- **The pace**: slow at the start, deliberately — the first thirty seconds set the tempo of the entire oral, and a rushed start feeds on itself. You will speed up naturally; nobody slows down naturally.
- **The board**: write **big** (the jury member at the back must be able to read), and **announce what you write as you write it** ("I am setting down the notional, 10 million, maturity 5 years…") — a silent board cuts the thread, a narrated board turns the calculation into a demonstration. Lay out the data before computing, always.
- **The gaze**: sweep the whole jury during the answer, not only the one who asked the question — the others are marking too, and a jury member ignored for ten minutes rarely becomes an ally at deliberation.
- **The hands**: a pen held calmly settles 90% of the problem; the rest is not crossing your arms (closure) and not playing with the cap (the clicking is heard more than you think).
- **The water**: take some if it is offered — the sip is a legitimate five-second pause, usable exactly at the moment you need to think.

## What the jury marks

| Earns points | Costs points |
| --- | --- |
| The structure announced then held (reformulate, plan, one-line conclusion) | Unmastered jargon — every term used must withstand three questions of depth |
| Anchored figures: an order of magnitude cited at the right moment, with its source | False precision — inventing a decimal the jury knows |
| Bridges between modules ("it is the same mechanism as the structured product's zero-coupon") | Arrogance — correcting the jury curtly, brushing aside an objection |
| Calibrated honesty: the "I don't know" followed by a reasoning | Collapsing after an error — the prolonged apology that contaminates what follows |
| Self-correction out loud, in one sentence, without apology | Filler — talking so as not to stay silent |

One line of this table deserves insistence: the **bridges between modules**. A jury questions module by module, but it marks transversally — the candidate who links the credit question to the monetary cycle, or the structured product's coupon to the premium of the sold put, has just proved he owns a map and not merely regions. You have twelve modules' worth of bridges at your disposal; every answer that crosses one stands out.

## The final days

The final preparation is not reading: it is **timed simulation**. This application was built for that, and the course closes on its own tool: the exercise runners for calculation under time constraint, the MCQs to sweep the twelve modules in breadth, the jury questions to rehearse out loud — *actually* out loud: the difference between an answer thought and an answer spoken is exactly the difference that shows on the day. The **jury-reflex** flashcards in spaced repetition fix the automatisms that must come out without thinking: the orders of magnitude, the one-sentence definitions, the classic traps. Concretely, the programme for the last week:

- **short, timed sessions** rather than long re-readings — twenty minutes of runner are worth an hour of passive re-reading, because they produce errors, and the error in simulation is the only one that costs nothing;
- **the three news topics** from chapter 6 updated with the latest figures — the only part of the preparation that goes stale;
- **the pitch rehearsed one last time** in its three lengths, standing, stopwatch in hand;
- **sleep**. On the eve, the marginal revision is worth nothing against an hour of sleep: you are sitting an oral of reasoning under pressure, and reasoning under pressure is the first victim of fatigue.

<GoFurther suffixe="the protocol of a full mock oral">
A useful simulation reproduces the conditions, not just the questions. The set-up: 20 to 30 minutes, timed, standing if the oral will be standing, with a board or an A3 sheet as a flip chart, and if possible a third party asking the questions — failing that, the application's mock-exam mode, saying each answer out loud before validating it. Deliberately mix the registers within a single session: a brainteaser, two course questions from distant modules, a numbered case, a motivation question — it is the change of scale that tires, and it can only be trained by enduring it. After each session, ten minutes of written debrief in three columns: what was answered correctly but delivered badly (structure, pace, missing conclusion), what was invented instead of admitted (the capital fault — hunt it down without complacency), the bridges between modules that were missed. The third column is the gold mine: every bridge noted in debrief will resurface naturally in the real situation.
</GoFurther>

All of it converges on a single capability, the one this chapter has just equipped: **explaining a mechanism, with a figure, under pressure**. That is exactly what a desk expects from a junior — not omniscience, but a reasoning that holds, orders of magnitude that anchor, and the honesty to say what you do not know and then to reason it out anyway. You have the mechanisms, you have the figures; the pressure, for its part, is tamed in simulation. Good luck — the rest is training.
