Twelve modules in, you have accumulated material: yield curves, Greeks, spreads, VaR, central bank mandates. This final module does not add an ounce of it. It deals with the step that remains — knowing how to *say* it: under pressure, without a calculator, in front of a jury watching you search. Because the trading-floor interview has a peculiarity no other oral exam has: you will be asked to compute out loud, live, things you have never computed before. This module therefore adds the only two things still missing: speed, and composure. And the first training room is the one most wrongly feared — mental math.

## What the jury really tests

Let us defuse the fantasy first: nobody hires a trader for arithmetic virtuosity — machines compute better, faster, and do not ask for a bonus. When a jury throws you "17 times 24" or "how long for your portfolio to double at 6%?", it is not testing the result, it is testing the **method**: do you decompose cleanly, do you round in a controlled way, do you *bracket* the result before hunting for it, do you know how to say by how much you are off. A candidate who announces "about 400, between 200 and 500 for sure" then refines towards 408 systematically beats the one who stays silent for ten seconds and drops 408 without a net — because the first shows exactly what a desk does: produce a usable number *now*, with an error bar, rather than a perfect number too late.

<Callout type="important" titre="The four moves the jury watches">
Every mental-math interview exercise comes down to four moves, in this order: **decompose** (reduce the calculation to known building blocks), **round cleanly** (replace a hostile number with a friendly neighbour, keeping track of the direction of the gap), **bracket** (bound the result before computing it — the insurance against absurdities), **state the error** (say by how much the approximation is off, and in which direction). The fourth move is the rarest and the most rewarding: it is what turns "I can compute" into "I can be trusted with a book".
</Callout>

## The rule of 72

The most profitable mental-math tool of the whole interview fits in one division: capital invested at an annual compound rate of $t\%$ doubles in about $72/t$ years. The three anchors to know by heart: at 8%, $72/8 = 9$ years — the exact value is $\ln 2 / \ln(1.08) = 9.006468$ years, an error of −0.07%, undetectable in an oral; at 6%, $72/6 = 12$ years; at 2%, $72/2 = 36$ years against an exact 35.0 — at low rates, the rule **overestimates** by about a year, and saying so is part of the answer. You have in fact already met that last anchor: in module 10, the 2% promise meant prices that double "roughly every 35 years".

Why 72? Under continuous compounding, the exact constant would be $100 \cdot \ln 2 \approx 69.3$. But 69.3 is hellish to divide in your head, whereas 72 divides by 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9 and 12 — and the gap between 72 and 69.3 happens to offset the annual discretisation around 8%, exactly where the rule was calibrated. It is no accident that the rule's sweet spot coincides with the order of magnitude of long-run equity returns: the question "how long for your portfolio to double?" is settled in one division. ## Fast percentages

Four reflexes cover most interview percentages:

- **Commutativity**: $x\%$ of $y$ = $y\%$ of $x$, always ($x \cdot y / 100$ both ways). "8% of 25" freezes plenty of candidates; "25% of 8" — a quarter of 8 — freezes none: **2**. Likewise, 4% of 75 = 75% of 4 = 3. Systematically flip the calculation into the easy direction.
- **The asymmetry of variations**: +10% then −10% does not bring you back to the start but to $1.1 \times 0.9 = 0.99$, i.e. −1% — successive variations *multiply*, they do not add. It is the gentle version of a lesson module 11 paid for dearly: after −50%, you need +100% to get back; after the Nasdaq's −78%, +354.5% and fifteen years.
- **Basis points**: 100 bp = 1%, so 50 bp = 0.5% and 25 bp = 0.25% — the mother tongue of modules 4 and 5. The jury that says "the spread widens by 50 bp" expects you to convert without pausing for breath.
- **The disguised division**: dividing by 0.8 is multiplying by 1.25 (since $1/0.8 = 5/4$); dividing by 0.5, multiplying by 2; dividing by 0.25, multiplying by 4. Recovering a notional before a 20% haircut — $100/0.8 = 125$ — becomes a friendly multiplication instead of a hostile division.

## Multiplication by decomposition

Faced with "17 × 24", the desk move happens in two steps. **Bracket first**: the result sits between $10 \times 20 = 200$ and $20 \times 25 = 500$ — three seconds, and no absurdity is possible any more. **Then decompose**, looking for the friendly neighbour: $17 \times 24 = 17 \times 25 - 17 = 425 - 17 = 408$. The 25 is the friend here because multiplying by 25 is multiplying by 100 and dividing by 4. Another path, same result: $17 \times 4 \times 6 = 68 \times 6 = 408$ — there is never a single right path, only bricks (×25, ×4, ×11, doublings) that you assemble.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Squares near 50 and near 100">
Two remarkable identities work wonders in an oral. Near 50: $(50 \pm a)^2 = 2{,}500 \pm 100a + a^2$ — so $47^2$ reads "25 − 3 = 22 hundreds, plus $3^2$": **2,209**; and $53^2$ = "25 + 3 = 28 hundreds plus 9": **2,809**. Near 100: $(100 - a)^2 = 100 \cdot (100 - 2a) + a^2$ — so $96^2$ = "92 hundreds plus 16": **9,216**. Three seconds each, and the effect on a jury is out of all proportion to the learning effort: it is pure return.
</Callout>

## The course's anchors as ammunition

Naked mental arithmetic impresses; mental arithmetic *anchored in real reference points* gets hired. The candidate who, ordered to estimate a loss, answers "with €100M at 20% volatility, my 1-day 95% VaR is about €2.1M — beyond that, we are in the tail" is not just computing: he is showing that he keeps market orders of magnitude in working memory. You have met them all; here they are, assembled into a magazine:

| Daily FX market volume | ~\$7,500bn per day (BIS 2022) | module 6 |
| Euro IG corporate spread, calm times | 80-150 bp (high yield 300-500) | module 5 |
| Senior unsecured recovery rate | R ≈ 40% (a convention, not a law) | module 5 |
| Equity market risk premium | 4-6% a year over the long run | module 12 |
| 1-day 95% VaR, €100M at 20% vol | ≈ €2.1M | module 12 |
| After −50%, to get back to the start | you need +100% | module 11 |
| Doubling at 8% / at 2% | 9 years / 36 years (rule of 72) | this chapter |

These anchors are doubly useful: as direct answers ("what is the spread on an IG corporate?"), and as **guardrails** — if your implied PD calculation comes out at 45% on an investment grade issuer, an anchor in memory tells you immediately that a factor got lost along the way. The error caught *by you* before the jury earns points; the same error caught by the jury costs double.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The full chain: module 5's implied PD">
The jury loves combining the reflexes into a single question: "a bond trades at 300 bp of spread, recovery 40% — what default probability is the market pricing?". Run it in your head: 300 bp = 3% (conversion); the implied PD is the spread divided by the loss given default, $\text{LGD} = 100 - R = 60\%$ (course anchor); dividing by 0.6 is multiplying by 5/3 (disguised division): $3 \times 5/3 = 5\%$. Three reflexes from this chapter, one anchor from module 5, ten seconds — exactly the assembly this module trains.
</Callout>

## The meta-reflex: announce, bracket, refine

Let us end with the move that crowns all the others, the one the explorer above made you rehearse: faced with any request for an estimate, **announce** the approximate figure first, **then** its error or its domain of validity, **then** refine if — and only if — the jury asks. "About 9 years: rule of 72, near-exact at this level of rates; 9.006 if you want the decimal." Three beats, ten seconds, and the jury has heard everything it was looking for: a mastered tool, lucidity about its limits, precision available on demand but never imposed. The reverse order — hunting for the decimal first — is the top-of-the-class mistake: it costs time, silence, and the only thing it proves is that you do not know what matters.

A word on training, finally: these reflexes only settle in out loud. Interview mental math is an exercise in *speech* as much as in arithmetic — training in silence is preparing for a different exam than the one you will sit. Get into the habit of verbalising the bracket, the decomposition and the error on every everyday calculation: after two weeks, the three-beat becomes an automatism, and automatism is precisely what survives stress.

One frontier remains that this chapter has not crossed: all our calculations dealt with *given* numbers. Yet the most disorienting questions of the oral deal with numbers nobody knows — how many piano tuners in Paris, how many ping-pong balls in an aircraft, what volume the FX market trades. There, exactness no longer exists; only the order of magnitude remains, plus a method invented by a physicist who estimated the power of an atomic bomb with three scraps of paper. That is chapter 2.
