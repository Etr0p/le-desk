The first two chapters gave you the theory: Markowitz's frontier says how to build a portfolio, the CAPM says how much each unit of systematic risk must pay. This chapter turns the telescope around: the portfolio exists, the year is over, and the client asks THE question of the trade — "my manager made +12%, is that good?". The honest answer: impossible to say. A return figure on its own means nothing, exactly as a price on its own meant nothing in module 3 without a multiple to compare it to. You need a yardstick — and the buy-side has built three, nested like Russian dolls: cash (what does risk *as such* pay?), the market adjusted for risk (what does *talent* pay?), and the mandate (what does the *freedom* granted to the manager pay?). This chapter unrolls them in order, then dismantles the traps that make each of them lie.

## First yardstick: cash — the Sharpe ratio

<Callout type="definition" titre="Sharpe ratio">
The **Sharpe ratio** divides the excess return over the risk-free rate by the portfolio's total volatility:
$$
\text{Sharpe} = \frac{r - r_f}{\sigma}
$$
It is a **unitless** number: how much each point of volatility endured has paid above cash. A portfolio that returns 8% when cash pays 3%, with 10% volatility, shows a Sharpe of $(8-3)/10 = 0.5$: each point of risk paid half a point of excess return.
</Callout>

The Sharpe answers the most primitive question: was it worth leaving cash at all? It looks at neither the market nor the mandate — only the raw return/risk pair. Hence its working benchmarks, to know by heart for the interview: a passive equity portfolio over a long period lives around **0.3 to 0.5**; above **1** is excellent; above **2**, it is no longer impressive, it is *suspicious*.

<Callout type="piege" titre="A spectacular Sharpe is a question, not an answer">
LTCM showed a Sharpe above 4 before 1998 (module 11, chapter 3) — then lost \$4.3 billion in a few weeks. The mechanism is always the same: some strategies **sell insurance** — option selling (module 8), credit carry, convergence arbitrage. They collect a small regular premium (smooth return, tiny measured volatility, superb Sharpe) and carry a tail risk that simply has *not yet materialised*. Historical volatility cannot see a risk that has not happened within the window. Desk reflex in front of a Sharpe > 2: ask *where the return comes from* before admiring the ratio — does the distribution have a tail the standard deviation does not capture?
</Callout>

## Second yardstick: the market, risk-adjusted — Jensen's alpha

The Sharpe compares to cash, but the client can replicate the *market* for three basis points. The manager's true competitor is therefore not cash: it is chapter 2's CAPM, which says what the portfolio *should have* returned given its beta. What exceeds that requirement — and only that — is called **alpha**:

$$
\alpha = r - \left[ r_f + \beta \, (r_m - r_f) \right]
$$

Back to our client: his manager made +12%, with a beta of 1.2, in a year when the market returned 10% and cash 3%. The CAPM required $3 + 1.2 \times (10 - 3) = 11.4$%. The alpha is $12 - 11.4 = +0.6$%: the manager beat *his* risk — narrowly.

<Callout type="piege" titre="+12% against +10% is NOT 2% of talent">
The naive reading — "he made 12, the market made 10, so 2 points of talent" — forgets that the beta of 1.2 is an **amplification that was bought, not manufactured**. In a rising market, a portfolio riskier than the market *must* do better than it: that is compensation for risk, not selection. Of the 2 points of apparent outperformance, 1.4 points are due to the beta; the talent is 0.6 points. Symmetrically, a fund with a beta of 1 that exactly matches the market has **no alpha** — it delivered precisely what the CAPM required, and an ETF would have done it for next to nothing. Alpha is what remains once the beta has been paid for: the most expensive and rarest commodity on the buy-side.
</Callout>

## Third yardstick: the mandate — tracking error and information ratio

The institutional client does not merely say "beat the market": they sign a **mandate** against a precise benchmark, with a quantified leash. That leash is called tracking error.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Tracking error and information ratio">
The **tracking error** (TE) is the volatility of the return gap between the portfolio and its benchmark — the standard deviation of $(r_p - r_b)$. Crucial point: unlike market volatility, it is not endured, it is **chosen** — it is the budget of freedom the mandate grants the manager. An index fund lives below 0.5% of TE; a classic active manager between 2 and 6%. The **information ratio** judges the use of that budget:
$$
IR = \frac{\text{outperformance}}{\text{tracking error}}
$$
A manager who beats their index by 2% per year with 4% of TE shows an IR of $2/4 = 0.5$ — and an IR above 0.5 *sustained over time* is already very good.
</Callout>

The information ratio is the active manager's Sharpe: same construction — an excess return divided by the risk taken to obtain it — but measured against the mandate rather than against cash. The three yardsticks nest into each other:

| Yardstick | The question asked | The measure | Benchmark |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cash | Did total risk pay? | Sharpe $(r - r_f)/\sigma$ | 0.3-0.5 passive; > 1 excellent; > 2 suspicious |
| The market, beta-adjusted | Is there talent beyond the risk? | Jensen's alpha | > 0 after fees, already rare |
| The mandate | Was the freedom granted made profitable? | IR = outperformance / TE | > 0.5 sustained, very good |

## The traps of measurement

<Callout type="piege" titre="Four ways to make a track record lie">
**The chosen window.** Every manager has *one* flattering period; starting the measurement at the 2009 trough or stopping it before 2022 changes everything. Demand performance over imposed windows, not chosen ones.

**Survivorship bias.** Funds that die — and they die of having performed badly — drop out of the databases; the average of the survivors overstates the category's performance by **1 to 2% per year**. The killer question: "and the funds your firm has closed?"

**Leverage.** Borrowing at the risk-free rate to double the position doubles the excess return *and* the volatility: the displayed return swells, the Sharpe does not move a hair. A spectacular return with an ordinary Sharpe is leverage, not talent.

**Fees.** Performance is judged *net* of fees — that is what the client receives — but simulations and backtests often circulate gross. Always ask: before or after fees? Chapter 4 shows that over thirty years, the difference is not a detail: it is bigger than the initial stake.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="where the outperformance comes from: the Brinson decomposition">
Knowing *by how much* the manager beat their benchmark calls for the next question: *how*? The Brinson decomposition (1986) splits the outperformance into two distinct moves. The **allocation** effect: did the manager overweight the right asset classes or sectors (more technology, less energy than the index)? The **selection** effect: within each pocket, did they pick securities that beat their sector? A manager can be an excellent allocator and a poor stock picker, or the reverse — and the distinction is anything but academic: it says what the client is paying for. If all the outperformance comes from sector allocation, a set of cheap sector ETFs would replicate it; if it comes from selection, the talent is harder to substitute. The attribution reports produced by institutional reporting teams are Brinson tables — and knowing how to read them is an interview skill for any buy-side position.
</GoFurther>

You now know how to measure cleanly: adjust for risk, compare to the right yardstick, outwit the window, the survivor, the leverage and the fees. This is where the trade becomes uncomfortable — because once the measurement is made honest, the *average* performance of active managers tells a story the industry would rather keep quiet. A Nobel laureate formulated it in 1991 in two paragraphs of pure arithmetic, without a single data point: that is chapter 4.
