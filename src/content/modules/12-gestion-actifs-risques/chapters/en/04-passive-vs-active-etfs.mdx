Chapter 3 taught you to measure cleanly: adjust for beta, outwit the chosen window and survivorship bias, demand figures net of fees. Now apply that honest measurement to the industry as a whole, and a disturbing question emerges: on average, do active managers beat their index? In 1991, William Sharpe — the same man as the ratio and the CAPM — answered in two paragraphs, without a single data point, with a piece of reasoning nobody has refuted since. This chapter unrolls that arithmetic, verifies it in the data, quantifies what fees really cost over a lifetime of saving, dismantles the machine that industrialised the answer — the ETF — then restores the balance: where active management keeps its meaning, and what risks the triumph of passive manufactures in its turn.

## Sharpe's arithmetic: an accounting identity

<Callout type="important" titre="Sharpe's argument (1991)">
The market is the sum of all its holders. The **asset-weighted** average of all investors — passive and active combined — is therefore, by construction, the return of the market itself. Passive investors replicate the index: they make the market, before fees. Only one possibility remains: **before fees, the average active manager makes exactly the market** — for every buyer who overweights a winning stock, another active player underweights it. And since active management costs more than replication (analysts, portfolio turnover, commissions), **after fees, the average active manager makes less than the market**. This is not an opinion, nor a fragile empirical result: it is an accounting identity. Active management is, in aggregate, a zero-sum game before fees — negative after.
</Callout>

Note the precision of the statement: it speaks of the *average* manager, in *aggregate*. It forbids nobody from beating the market — it only guarantees that the winners win exactly what the losers lose, and that the fee bill applies to both. Looking for a good manager remains possible; it is looking for someone on the right side of a zero-sum game, while paying to take part.

## The data: SPIVA confirms

The arithmetic predicts, the data verify. The SPIVA reports (S&P Indices Versus Active), published twice a year since 2002, compare each active fund to its benchmark index, correcting for chapter 3's survivorship bias. The verdict is remarkably constant: over a horizon of **10 to 15 years, about 85 to 90% of active equity funds underperform their index after fees** — in the United States as in Europe, in large caps as in mid caps. Over one year, the results fluctuate; as the horizon lengthens, the proportion of losers rises, inexorably — fees compound, luck does not. And persistence is almost nil: the funds in the top quartile of one period are no more often first in the following period than chance would have it.

## The arithmetic of fees: the knockout demonstration

Why so few survivors over the long term? Because the manager's adversary is not the index: it is the **compounding of their own fees**. Take 100 invested for 30 years at a 7% gross annual return, and vary the fee line alone:

$$
V = 100 \times \left(1 + \frac{r - f}{100}\right)^{30}
$$

| Vehicle | Annual fees | Value after 30 years | Gap to gross |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| The market, without fees (theoretical) | 0% | 761.225504 | — |
| Index ETF | 0.2% | 719.676929 | −41.548575 |
| Classic active fund | 2% | 432.194238 | −329.031266 |

Read the last line twice: the 2% annual fees cost **329.03** — more than three times the initial stake — without any single year, taken in isolation, seeming expensive. That is the heart of the mechanism: 2% in one year is invisible; 2% *compounded* over thirty years confiscates 43% of the final value ($329/761$). Compounding, the force that module 4 put at the investor's service, works with the same power for whoever collects the fees. And to deserve those 2%, the active manager must beat the market by 1.8 points per year, every year, for thirty years — when Sharpe's arithmetic guarantees that the average of their peers will make zero before fees.

## The ETF: the machine that industrialised the arithmetic

The **ETF** (exchange-traded fund) is an index fund quoted continuously like a stock. Its growth says it all: about \$13 trillion of assets worldwide, for fees of 0.05 to 0.4% per year. But its real feat is technical: how can a fund quoted all day long stick to its index within a few basis points?

<Callout type="definition" titre="Creation-redemption: the arbitrage that holds the price">
The ETF does not trade only on the exchange: **authorized participants** (banks, market makers) can at any moment create or destroy units directly with the issuer, through an **in-kind** exchange: delivering the index's basket of securities against newly created units, or returning units against the basket. If the ETF's price exceeds the value of its basket, the arbitrage is immediate — buy the basket, deliver it, sell the created units — and pulls the price back to the net asset value; symmetrically in the case of a discount. The ETF's price is therefore held by a permanent arbitrage, not by a promise. The in-kind exchange has a second merit: the issuer does not sell securities to meet outflows, which reduces transaction costs and tax frictions.
</Callout>

The quality of the replication also owes to what it copies: an index weighted by free-float capitalisation (module 3, chapter 4) is a portfolio that rebalances *itself* — when a stock rises, its weight in the index and in the portfolio rise together, without a single transaction. Replicating a cap-weighted index is structurally cheap; that is what makes the 0.05% possible.

## Honesty in both directions

The case for the prosecution is damning, but the interview expects better than an indictment: it expects the limits of the argument, in both directions.

**Where active keeps its meaning.** Sharpe's arithmetic applies to the aggregate of a given market — it bites less where the market is inefficient and the index badly constructed: small caps thinly covered by analysts, distressed credit (module 5), where recovery analysis is everything and indexing impracticable. It also says nothing about mandates that do not seek to beat an index: managing the risk of an insurer's liability, matching bond flows under regulatory constraints — there, the "benchmark" is a commitment, not an index. Finally, bond indexing has a design flaw: weighting by capitalisation means lending the most to the most indebted.

**The risks of passive.** The success of passive manufactures its own fragilities. The first: in a cap-weighted index, every indexed euro buys securities in proportion to their size — the weight of the mega-caps is self-reinforcing, and the concentration of the American indices on a handful of technology names (module 3) makes "passive diversification" an ever-narrower bet. The second is more subtle:

<Callout type="exemple" titre="March 2020: when the ETF quotes and the underlying goes silent">
At the peak of the COVID panic, high yield bond ETFs quoted continuously at **discounts of several percent below their net asset value**. Scandal? Look closer: the underlying bonds had almost stopped trading, and the "net asset value" was computed on the last known prices — dead prices. The ETF, meanwhile, was aggregating in real time what actual sellers and buyers would accept. The ETF's price was not lagging the NAV: it was **ahead** of it — and bond prices converged towards it, not the other way round. The lesson: **price discovery had migrated** from the underlying market to the ETF. That is reassuring (the mechanism held, without a suspension) and worrying at the same time: an ETF's liquidity can never be better than its underlying's in a stress — it can only reveal it faster.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="in between: smart beta">
Between pure replication and discretionary active management, a third way has developed: **smart beta** (or factor indexing). The idea: academic research has documented persistent premia beyond market beta — *value* (cheap stocks), *momentum* (recent winners), *low volatility*, *quality*, *size*. Rather than paying 2% to a manager whose alpha is often just hidden exposure to these factors, you build a systematic index that captures the factor for 0.2 to 0.5% of fees. It is the logical continuation of the commoditisation movement: yesterday market beta went from expensive product to free product; today factor betas are following the same path; what remains sellable at a premium shrinks to what no index can capture. The usual caveats: factor premia can disappear for years at a time (value suffered a whole decade), and their public discovery erodes part of the seam — a factor everyone invests in is an expensive factor.
</GoFurther>

Active or passive, alpha or beta, 2% or 0.05%: this whole module has so far reasoned in terms of return. But no institutional investor signs a mandate on the mere promise of a return — their first question lies elsewhere, and it is the same as the regulator's after 2008: *how much can we lose, and how fast?* Giving that question a number, a method and limits is the risk profession. It begins in chapter 5.
