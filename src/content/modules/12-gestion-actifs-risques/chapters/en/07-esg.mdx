Chapter 6 closed the prudential toolbox: capital against losses, liquidity against runs — safeguards born of 2008, quantified, audited, uncontested in principle. This last thematic chapter tackles a much younger and much more disputed safeguard. Basel III asked: *can the world hurt your balance sheet?* ESG adds a second question, symmetric and explosive: *does your balance sheet hurt the world?* Three letters that have become unavoidable — in prospectuses, in investment committees, and in job interviews where "what do you think of ESG?" is used to sort the candidates who recite slogans from those who have understood what is being talked about. This chapter puts you in the second category.

## Three letters, two materialities

<Callout type="definition" titre="ESG and double materiality">
**ESG**: Environmental (climate, biodiversity, pollution), Social (working conditions, supply chain, customer data), Governance (board independence, remuneration, minority shareholder rights). The common idea: the portfolio has effects on the world, and the world has effects on the portfolio. The distinction that structures the WHOLE debate: **single materiality** looks only at the second direction — climate threatens my assets, that is a **financial risk**, and nobody disputes that a manager must measure it. **Double materiality** adds the first — my assets damage the climate, that is an **impact** — and that is where the debate begins: is minimising it the manager's job, or the legislator's?
</Callout>

Hammer this distinction home, because it defuses almost every trap question. That a coal utility is exposed to a future carbon tax is ordinary risk management — credit (module 5) with one more factor. That a fund refuses to finance it *to accelerate its closure* is an impact choice — legitimate or not, but of a different nature. Confusing the two sells a lot of products and loses a lot of interviews. A word of history to situate the object: the acronym dates from a 2004 UN report (*Who Cares Wins*), the institutional take-off from the 2006 Principles for Responsible Investment — and in twenty years, ESG has gone from activist niche to a mandatory box in every European institutional request for proposals. It is that massification, more than the idea itself, that created the measurement and marketing problems this chapter details.

## The landscape: precise vocabulary for very different practices

"Sustainable investing" covers strategies that have almost nothing in common — funds labelled sustainable weigh about \$2,500 to 3,000bn depending on the definitions, and that is precisely the problem: everything depends on the definitions.

| Strategy | Principle | The question to ask |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **ESG integration** | ESG factors enter the financial analysis, with no exclusion constraint | What does it actually change in the portfolio? Often: almost nothing |
| **Exclusions** | Banning sectors (tobacco, coal, controversial weapons) | What thresholds? 5% or 50% of revenue is not the same fund |
| **Best-in-class** | Keeping the best-rated of EACH sector — including the best oil major | Rated by whom? (next section) |
| **Shareholder engagement** | Staying in the capital and weighing in: votes, dialogue, resolutions | Voice rather than exit — but with what measured results? |
| **Impact investing** | Targeting a measurable impact ALONGSIDE the return | How is the impact measured, and what would it have been without you? |

The "voice vs exit" divide deserves a line in an interview: selling a polluter's stock does not remove a gram of CO₂ — someone else buys it, with fewer scruples; staying and voting can bend the strategy. Both camps have arguments, and knowing how to lay them out is worth more than choosing one with fervour.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Engine No. 1 vs ExxonMobil (2021): the proof of concept for voice">
A tiny activist hedge fund — about 0.02% of Exxon's capital — puts forward three board candidates in 2021, on a thesis mixing the two materialities: Exxon's all-oil strategy destroys *shareholder* value in a world in transition. The giants of passive management (BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street), who cannot "exit" since they replicate the index (chapter 4), vote in favour: the three seats are won. Double lesson: engagement can bite when the indexers get involved — they only ever have voice, never exit; and the argument that won was framed as **financial risk**, not as morality. That is exactly the grammar of this chapter.
</Callout>

## ESG ratings: the thermometer that gives four temperatures

Module 5 (chapter 2) showed you credit ratings: Moody's and S&P measure the same thing — the probability of default — and their ratings are correlated at about 0.99. The ESG ratings of the major agencies (MSCI, Sustainalytics, ISS…) show a correlation of about **0.4 to 0.6** with one another. That is not an execution detail, it is a disagreement about the question being asked: what perimeter (climate alone? all three letters?), what indicators (reported or estimated emissions?), what weightings (is governance worth 20% or 40%?). Two serious agencies, two defensible methodologies, two contradictory ratings for the same company — Tesla has been simultaneously very well and very badly rated depending on the agency.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The ESG rating does not (usually) measure what the public thinks">
Most ESG ratings measure the **risk TO the company** — the exposure of its value to ESG factors — and not the **impact OF the company** on the world. A well-run oil company, which documents its climate risks and governs its transition cleanly, can be rated better than a small, badly governed solar player. That is not an anomaly: it is single materiality, applied honestly. But the saver who buys an "ESG" fund believes they are buying impact. That misunderstanding — nurtured by marketing — is the heart of the regulatory problem and of greenwashing.
</Callout>

The problem is aggravated upstream by the data itself. Greenhouse gas emissions are reported in three perimeters — **scope 1** (direct emissions), **scope 2** (purchased energy), **scope 3** (the whole value chain, upstream and downstream) — and scope 3, often 80 to 90% of the total for a bank or an oil major, rests largely on estimates and models, when it is published at all. An ESG rating therefore aggregates declarative, heterogeneous data, partly estimated by the agency itself: the raters' divergence begins in the spreadsheets, before the weighting choices even come in. That is precisely the hole that European regulatory reporting (CSRD, below) wants to fill — audited data rather than brochures. If the overall rating is fragile, a more modest strategy consists of not rating the company but earmarking the money: that is the promise — and the limit — of the most liquid ESG instrument in the market.

## Green bonds: the money earmarked, the risk unchanged

The **green bond** is a bond whose issuance proceeds are earmarked for environmental projects — that is the **use of proceeds** principle: the money goes to the wind farm, the rail network, the thermal renovation, and the issuer reports on it. But — a decisive technical point, straight back to module 5 — the holder has **no recourse to the project**: the green bond is *pari passu* with the issuer's ordinary debt, same seniority, same credit risk. If the issuer defaults, the green bond defaults with the rest; if the wind farm fails but the issuer is doing fine, the coupon keeps coming. Pricing consequence: **the spread is the issuer's, not the project's** — pricing a green bond is pricing the signature (module 5, chapter 4); the green does not enter the formula.

Almost not, at least: green bonds often trade a little richer, and therefore yield a little less — the **greenium**, on the order of a few basis points, small and unstable across studies and periods. It is demand (dedicated funds, constrained mandates) that compresses the yield, not a lower risk. Orders of magnitude to remember: about \$500bn of green issuance per year in recent years, i.e., in outstanding terms, about 2 to 3% of the global bond market — visible, no longer anecdotal at all, but very far from having "greened finance". The ecosystem has equipped itself with private and then public safeguards: the ICMA's Green Bond Principles (a voluntary market standard), the *second party opinion* from a third party that validates the framework, and recently the **European Green Bond** label (EuGB), applicable at the end of 2024, which requires the projects to be aligned with the taxonomy — the strictest label, and for that reason the least used for now.

A variant to know: **sustainability-linked bonds** (SLB), where the money is NOT earmarked — it funds the company in general — but where the coupon is indexed to the issuer's ESG targets: typically, a 25 bp step-up if the emissions target at a given date is missed. Elegant on paper, because the commitment covers the whole company and not a showcase project; in practice, the trap is the **soft target**: objectives already almost reached at issuance, a tiny step-up placed on the bond's last two years of life, exit clauses. Desk reflex: read the clause before admiring the concept — an SLB's financial materiality can be computed in seconds (25 bp × years remaining after the test date), and it is often derisory.

## Europe regulates the definition, not the allocation

Facing a market where "green" meant nothing precise, the European Union chose a singular strategy: not to impose an allocation, but to write the dictionary. The **European taxonomy** is an official list of "sustainable" activities, with **quantified technical criteria** (emission thresholds per kWh produced, per tonne of cement…) and a **do no significant harm** principle: an activity that is green on one axis must not significantly harm the other environmental objectives. The dictionary has its disputed pages — the inclusion of gas and nuclear in 2022, under conditions, showed that the boundary of "green" is as political as it is technical. Second storey: **SFDR**, the regulation that classifies *funds* according to their declared ambitions:

| SFDR classification | What the fund declares | Desk reading |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Article 6** | No sustainability objective | The "normal" fund — the vast majority of assets |
| **Article 8** | Promotes environmental or social characteristics | Catch-all category ("light green"): from cosmetic exclusion to serious funds |
| **Article 9** | Sustainable investment objective | The "dark green" — that is where the bar is high, and where the wave of reclassifications struck |

Third storey: **CSRD**, the directive that requires large companies to produce extra-financial reporting in **double materiality**, standardised and audited — the data plumbing that the taxonomy and SFDR need in order not to run on empty. Its rollout, judged costly, was moreover lightened and refocused on the largest companies from 2025: European regulatory ambition has its own political cycle. Keep the overall philosophy, which makes a good summary answer: Europe does not tell you what to buy; it regulates the **definition**, not the allocation — it forbids you from calling "green" what is not green by its standards.

## Greenwashing: the DWS affair

An official dictionary only has value if someone punishes those who misuse it. **Greenwashing** — selling as sustainable what is not, or not as much — was long a sin without a judge: too vague to prosecute, too profitable to abandon. One affair changed the game, and it is worth knowing in detail because it set the precedent in every asset management firm in Europe.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="DWS, 2021-2023: the price of 'ESG integrated'">
DWS, Deutsche Bank's asset management subsidiary, displayed hundreds of billions of "ESG integrated" assets. In 2021, its former head of sustainability, turned whistleblower, states that these figures are largely inflated — the "integration" changed almost nothing in the decisions. There follow a **raid** on the Frankfurt offices in May 2022, the **CEO's resignation** the next day, a **\$19M fine imposed by the SEC** in 2023 for misleading statements about ESG processes, then a fine from the German prosecutor. The market lesson: nobody accused DWS of managing money badly — it was punished for the gap between the **marketing promise** and the **actual process**. Greenwashing has become a legal risk and a career risk, no longer merely a venial commercial sin.
</Callout>

The shockwave was general: at the end of 2022, when the regulator clarified its requirements, hundreds of **article 9 funds were downgraded to article 8** by their own management companies — about 40% of the category's assets — each preferring to deflate its promise rather than risk DWS's fate. That collective ebb is perhaps the best proof that the regulation has bitten: when managers voluntarily give up a commercially precious label, it is because the label has finally become costly to usurp. It remains to know how to read for yourself. Three desk reflexes in front of an ESG report: compare the **promises to the actual flows** — what the portfolio contains line by line, not what the brochure says; look for the **quantified thresholds** behind the adjectives ("excludes coal": from what percentage of revenue?); distrust any indicator that nobody outside can recompute. Greenwashing does not survive the question "show me the calculation" — which is, you will have noticed, the question this course has been teaching you to ask since module 1.

## And performance? Honestly

The awkward question: does ESG cost return? The honest answer, that of the meta-studies: **neither systematic sacrifice, nor systematic outperformance**. The results depend on the period, on the definition used and above all on **sector biases**: a typical "green" fund is structurally long technology and underweight energy (module 3's sector compositions) — it outperforms when tech rises (2020), underperforms when oil flares up (2022), and the ESG label has almost nothing to do with it. Two nuances to hold the discussion in an interview. In theory, a constraint cannot improve the optimum: excluding securities shrinks the investment universe, so chapter 1's efficient frontier can only recede — a little. And the long history documents the opposite of a punishment for the "sinners": tobacco stocks are among the best stock market performances of the 20th century, precisely because their ostracism compressed their price and inflated the return of those willing to hold them. The serious argument for ESG therefore plays out not in return but in **risk**: stranded assets (fossil reserves that can never be burned if the carbon constraint tightens), regulatory transition risk, physical risk, litigation risk — all factors a manager must price even if they could not care less about the planet. It is single materiality that commands consensus; it is on that ground that a solid interview answer is built.

<Callout type="important" titre="'What do you think of ESG?' — the answer that holds">
The question tests your capacity for nuance, not your convictions. Neither total cynicism ("it is all marketing") — you would insult half the bank's clients and an entire regulatory corpus; nor naive faith ("finance will save the planet") — you would show you have read no numbers. The answer that holds, in four steps: distinguish **risk and impact** (single vs double materiality); acknowledge the **measurement** problem (0.4 to 0.6 correlation between raters, versus 0.99 in credit); cite a **precise fact** (the greenium of a few basis points, or DWS); conclude that climate risk is ordinary risk management, that impact is a legitimate but distinct question, and that European regulation is in the process of forcing the clarification. Three minutes, zero slogans.
</Callout>

## ESG seen from the desk

Concretely, who touches ESG in a markets bank? The **DCM green bonds** desk, first: accompanying an issuer means structuring the use of proceeds framework, obtaining the second party opinion, documenting the reporting — an origination job in its own right, in continuous growth. **Structuring** next (module 9): ESG indices as underlyings for structured products, SLBs, derivatives on carbon allowances. **Risk** finally: the climate stress tests imposed by supervisors go into the same toolbox as chapter 5's stress tests — a scenario, not a probability. And the junior you will be: it is perhaps the only interview question where they test neither your arithmetic nor your market knowledge, but your judgement.

<GoFurther suffixe="the Atlantic in between: the American backlash">
Since 2022, ESG has become a political marker in the United States: several states — Texas and Florida in the lead — have passed laws excluding from public mandates the managers deemed "hostile to fossil fuels", while other states demand the opposite; large global managers have left the climate coalitions (Climate Action 100+, Net Zero Asset Managers) under pressure, while keeping their sustainable ranges in Europe. The result is an assumed commercial schizophrenia — the same manager touts its climate commitment in Paris and its energy pragmatism in Houston — and a new phenomenon, *greenhushing*: continuing ESG practices without claiming them any more, so as to be attackable nowhere. For the desk, the consequence is very concrete: regulation is diverging durably on the two sides of the Atlantic (double materiality mandatory in Europe, single materiality at best in the United States), and an "article 9" product perfectly sellable in Amsterdam can be commercially toxic in Dallas. Regulatory geography has become a structuring parameter in its own right.
</GoFurther>

<GoFurther suffixe="the cost of capital as an impact channel">
If double materiality has a financial mechanism, it is this one: when enough investors exclude a sector, demand for its securities falls, their price falls, so its **cost of capital** rises — and marginal projects no longer get financed. The channel exists in theory; the question is its magnitude. The empirical work that has measured it concludes the effect is **modest**: with the current share of genuinely exclusionary assets, the cost-of-capital gap inflicted on the excluded is counted in basis points, not percentage points — too little to close a mine. Two possible readings, and both play well in an interview: "exclusion is therefore symbolic, shareholder engagement or regulation are more effective"; or "the channel is real but sub-critical — it would become powerful if exclusions became majority and coordinated". In both cases, you will have replaced an opinion with a mechanism and an order of magnitude: exactly what is expected of you.
</GoFurther>
