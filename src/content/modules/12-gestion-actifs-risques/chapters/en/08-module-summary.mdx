This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the essentials chapter by chapter, the formula sheet, the traps and the reflexes. To reread the day before an oral — and to complete with the jury mode of the practice section. The guiding thread fits in one sentence: this module crosses the bridge to the **buy-side**, then changes chairs. On the manager's side, the job is not to *predict* but to **assemble** — choosing weights, not winners: return averages out, risk does not ($\sigma_p$ comes out BELOW the weighted average as soon as $\rho < 1$), and the market only pays for the risk you cannot eliminate ($\beta$, CAPM). On the desk head's side, the question changes: *how much can we lose?* — a quantile for ordinary days (the **VaR**), a scenario for the days that matter (the **stress test**), capital and liquidity to survive (**Basel III**), and a guardrail of a new kind where the measure itself is disputed (**ESG**).

## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **The portfolio: the only free lunch (ch. 1).** Asset management (~\$100tn, BlackRock > \$10tn) invests other people's money — the manager is a fiduciary. Return averages out: the 60/40 (equities 8%, bonds 3%) returns **6%**. Risk does not: $\sigma_p = \sqrt{w_1^2\sigma_1^2 + w_2^2\sigma_2^2 + 2w_1w_2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2}$ — THE formula, to unroll with your eyes closed. The anchor: 60/40, vols 20/10, ρ 0.3 ⇒ **13.74%** against 16% as a weighted average — more than two points of risk gone without sacrificing a cent of return. The three faces of ρ: at **1**, the weighted average (two perfectly correlated assets are ONE asset in disguise); at **−1**, the hedge (8%, cancellable by choice of weights); at **0**, divide by $\sqrt{n}$. What melts away: **specific** risk; what remains: **systematic** risk. And the queen's flaw: in crises, correlations rise towards 1 — diversification is a fair-weather tool, not a life jacket.
2. **The frontier and the CAPM (ch. 2).** Markowitz (1952): for each target return, ONE minimum-variance portfolio — the **efficient frontier**, which bulges to the left as soon as ρ < 1; any portfolio below it is dominated. Add the risk-free asset: the tangent line (**CML**) dominates the frontier, and the **separation theorem** falls out — everyone holds the SAME risky portfolio (the market, cap-weighted) and only doses cash or leverage. Hence the **CAPM**: only systematic risk is paid, measured by $\beta = \rho \times \sigma_a/\sigma_m$ — 0.8 × 25/15 = **1.33**. The required return follows the **SML**: $r = r_f + \beta(r_m - r_f)$ — 3 + 1.2 × 5 = **9%**, market risk premium **4-6%/year** over the long run. CML: total volatility, efficient portfolios only; SML: beta, ALL assets. Honest limits: value, momentum, low-vol anomalies — the CAPM is **a language more than a law**, but it provides the yardstick.
3. **Measuring performance (ch. 3).** "+12%, is that good?" — impossible to say without a yardstick, and there are three, nested. Cash: **Sharpe** $= (r - r_f)/\sigma$ — (8 − 3)/10 = **0.5**; benchmarks 0.3-0.5 passive, > 1 excellent, > 2 **suspicious** (LTCM > 4 before 1998: insurance sellers have a superb Sharpe until the day when). The market adjusted for beta: **Jensen's alpha** — +12% at beta 1.2, market 10%, cash 3%: the CAPM required 11.4%, the alpha is worth **+0.6%**, not 2 — beta is amplification bought, not manufactured. The mandate: the **tracking error** is CHOSEN (0.5% index, 2-6% active — the freedom budget) and the **information ratio** = outperformance/TE judges it — 2/4 = 0.5, very good if sustained over time. The four falsifiers: the chosen window, **survivorship bias** (+1 to 2%/year), leverage (inflates the return, not the Sharpe), gross instead of net.
4. **Passive versus active (ch. 4).** **Sharpe's arithmetic** (1991) — an accounting identity, not an opinion: the weighted average of all investors IS the market; before fees, the average active manager makes the market; after fees, less. SPIVA confirms: **85-90%** of active equity funds underperform over 10-15 years, near-zero persistence. The knockout argument: 100 invested for 30 years at 7% gross makes **761.23**; at 2% fees, **432.19** — the fees cost **329.03**, more than three times the stake, 43% of the final value, without a single year seeming expensive; the ETF at 0.2% leaves 719.68. The **ETF** (~\$13tn) holds its price through the **creation-redemption** arbitrage of authorised participants (in-kind exchange of the basket against shares). March 2020: HY ETFs traded below a NAV computed on dead prices — **price discovery had migrated** to the ETF. Honesty in both directions: active keeps its place (small caps, distressed, liability mandates); passive manufactures concentration and false liquidity; in between, **smart beta**.
5. **VaR and stress tests (ch. 5).** RiskMetrics, JPMorgan, 1994: the 4:15 pm number. **VaR**: a loss threshold, a confidence level, a horizon — NEVER a maximum loss: it says where the tail begins, not what is inside it. Parametric: $V \times z \times \sigma \times \sqrt{h/252}$, z = **1.65** (95%) and **2.33** (99%) — 100M at 20% vol: **2.08M** at one day; 99%/10d: 9.28M (the old Basel combination). Breaches are EXPECTED: (1 − c) × 252 ≈ **12.6 days/year** at 95% — backtesting counts them (traffic lights: green ≤ 4, amber 5-9, red ≥ 10 at 99%). The **square root of time** (2 × √10 = 6.32) assumes independence — which breaks in a crisis: a floor in calm weather, an optimistic ceiling in a storm. Three methods, three kinds of honesty: parametric (trusts its distribution), historical (trusts its sample), Monte Carlo (trusts its generator). The killer question — "and on that day?" — calls for the **expected shortfall** (average of losses beyond; FRTB: ES 97.5%) and the **stress test**: no probability, a scenario — $V \times \text{shock} \times \beta$ = 100 × (−0.20) × 1.2 = **−24M**. The most fruitful: the reverse one — "what kills us?". LTCM had an impeccable VaR.
6. **The four risks and Basel III (ch. 6).** Four drawers: **market** (1987), **credit** (Lehman, all of m5), **liquidity** (Northern Rock, LDI, SVB — the risk that turns the others into catastrophe), **operational** (Kerviel: €4.9bn, declared VaR tiny — the only risk NEVER rewarded). Basel III, written in the rubble of 2008: **RWA** = exposure × risk weight (~0% AAA sovereign, 20-50% banks, 75% retail — m5's ratings wired into the world's capital); **CET1** = core equity/RWA — 12/100 = 12%; requirements stacked 4.5% + conservation buffer 2.5% + G-SIB buffers; banks live at **12-15%** (Lehman: leverage of 31, i.e. ~3.2%). The crude guardrail: **leverage ratio** ≥ 3%, unweighted — the anti-RWA. Liquidity: **LCR** = HQLA/30-day outflows ≥ 100% — survive a month of run without the central bank; **NSFR** at one year. SVB: solvent the day before, dead the day after \$42bn of withdrawals — solvency does not protect against a run. And the humans: **three lines of defence** — the front owns the risk, risk management (independent, reporting to the CRO) limits it, audit checks.
7. **ESG (ch. 7).** Basel asked: can the world hurt your balance sheet? ESG adds: does your balance sheet hurt the world? THE distinction: **single materiality** (the climate threatens my assets — a financial risk, uncontested) versus **double materiality** (my assets damage the climate — an impact, and the debate begins). The measurement problem: ESG scores correlated at **0.4-0.6** across agencies, against 0.99 in credit — and they often measure the risk TO the company, not its impact. The **green bond**: earmarked money (use of proceeds) but NO recourse to the project — pari passu, the spread is that of the issuer's signature (m5), give or take the **greenium** (a few bps, driven by demand). Europe regulates the **definition**, not the allocation: **taxonomy** (quantified criteria, do no significant harm), **SFDR** (articles 6/8/9 — late 2022, ~40% of article 9 assets downgraded), CSRD. **Greenwashing** has become a legal risk: DWS — raid, CEO resignation, \$19m SEC fine, for the gap between promise and process. Performance: neither systematic sacrifice nor systematic outperformance — the serious argument is about **risk** (stranded assets, transition, litigation), not morality.

## The traps that cost points

1. **Believing that the number of lines diversifies** (ch. 1): a thousand assets correlated at 1 behave like a single one — it is correlation that diversifies, and it rises towards 1 in crises, precisely when you need it.
2. **Confusing beta and volatility** (ch. 2): an asset at 40% vol uncorrelated with the market has a beta of ~0 — its risk is diversifiable, hence free. You are not paid for the risk you take, but for the risk you cannot eliminate.
3. **Confusing CML and SML** (ch. 2): the CML carries TOTAL volatility and efficient portfolios only; the SML carries BETA and all assets — a volatile, uncorrelated biotech sits far below the CML, exactly on the SML.
4. **"+12 against +10, so 2 points of skill"** (ch. 3): at beta 1.2, the CAPM required 11.4% — the alpha is worth 0.6, not 2. Beta is amplification bought; a beta-1 fund that makes the market has ZERO alpha.
5. **Admiring a Sharpe > 2** (ch. 3): insurance sellers (options, carry, convergence) have a smooth return and a tail risk invisible in the window — LTCM displayed more than 4. Ask where the return comes from before admiring the ratio.
6. **The uncorrected track record** (ch. 3): the chosen window, survivorship bias (+1 to 2%/year), leverage (inflates the return, not the Sharpe), gross instead of net — four falsifiers to hunt down systematically.
7. **"2% fees × 30 years ≈ 60% of the stake"** (ch. 4): no — compounding carries the cost to 329 per 100 invested, 43% of the final value: every euro taken stops producing for all the remaining years.
8. **Reading VaR as a maximum loss** (ch. 5): it says where the tail begins, not what is inside it — on the 20th day, the loss can be 2.2M or 40M, the number is silent by construction.
9. **"The VaR was breached, the model is wrong"** (ch. 5): the breach is EXPECTED — 1 day in 20 at 95%. It is the frequency that judges (backtesting); and a VaR never breached overestimates and wastes capital.
10. **Dividing by 252 instead of taking the square root** (ch. 5): it is variances that add up, not standard deviations — and the square root of time itself becomes an optimistic ceiling in a crisis, when losses autocorrelate.
11. **Confusing solvency and liquidity** (ch. 6): SVB was solvent the day before and dead the day after — two distinct risks, two distinct ratios (CET1 versus LCR). And 12/200 = 6% is not a CET1: it is a leverage ratio — weight first.
12. **"The green bond carries the project's risk"** (ch. 7): no recourse to the project — pari passu with ordinary debt: the spread is that of the signature, green does not enter the formula, give or take the greenium.
13. **"The ESG score measures impact"** (ch. 7): it most often measures the risk TO the company — a well-run oil major can beat a badly governed solar firm. And the raters are correlated at 0.4-0.6 with each other, against 0.99 in credit.

## The twelve reflexes for the oral

1. **Return averages out, risk does not**: know how to unroll THE formula, three terms — and name the cross term as the only one where correlation lives.
2. Facing any diversification, ask m11's question: **what happens to ρ in a crisis?** — a fair-weather tool, not a life jacket.
3. Beta in one line: $\rho \times \sigma_a/\sigma_m$ — then hammer it home: the market only pays for **systematic** risk.
4. Before judging any performance, compute the **CAPM yardstick**: 3 + 1.2 × 5 = 9% — delivering 11% is skill, delivering 8% while calling yourself positive is destruction in make-up.
5. NEVER comment on a return without asking for **the risk and the benchmark**: three nested yardsticks — cash (Sharpe), beta-adjusted market (alpha), mandate (IR).
6. Facing a spectacular Sharpe: **"where does the return come from?"** — does the distribution have a tail the standard deviation cannot see?
7. Sharpe's arithmetic is cited as what it is: **an accounting identity** — before fees, the average active manager makes the market; after fees, less. Then SPIVA: 85-90% over 10-15 years.
8. The knockout fee argument in one line: **761 against 432** — 2% a year compounded over thirty years confiscates more than three times the stake.
9. VaR is recited in three words — **threshold, confidence, horizon** — followed straight away by the killer question: "and on that day, how much do we lose?" (expected shortfall).
10. The stress test is defended by its frugality: **the virtue is in the question**, not in the refinement — and the most fruitful one is reversed: "what kills us?".
11. Basel III in one sentence: **more capital, of better quality, and liquidity on top** — with its crude guardrails (leverage ≥ 3%, the anti-RWA) and its SVB lesson: solvency does not protect against a run.
12. "What do you think of ESG?": distinguish **risk and impact**, cite the 0.4-0.6 correlation and the greenium, conclude that climate risk is ordinary risk management — three minutes, zero slogans.

## Bridges to the rest of the course

- **Module 2 — quantitative methods.** The loop closes: volatility, correlation and the quantiles of the normal distribution, introduced as tools, find their terminal use here — correlation carries Markowitz and beta, the z quantile becomes the VaR, and m2's fat tails explain why the parametric method lies.
- **Module 3 — equities and indices.** M3's cap-weighted index is exactly what the ETF replicates — and what makes replication cheap: a portfolio that rebalances itself. Its concentration has become the Achilles heel of "passive diversification".
- **Modules 7 and 8 — futures and options.** Hedging at the beta is m7's contract-count calculation (144M of index-equivalent for 120M at beta 1.2); and ch. 3's suspicious Sharpe is the profile of m8's option seller — regular premium against a rare, massive loss.
- **Module 5 — credit.** Credit risk meets the RWAs here: m5's ratings are wired into Basel's risk weights, cliff effects included — and the green bond prices like any signature from m5: PD × LGD, pari passu, green does not enter the formula.
- **Module 10 — central banks.** The risk-free rate that anchors the CML, the CAPM and the Sharpe is the one m10 manufactures eight times a year — and supervisors' stress tests, climate ones included, have become a channel of prudential policy.
- **Module 11 — the crises.** The living counter-examples of each chapter: LTCM for the Sharpe and the VaR (impeccable measures, a deadly tail), 1987 and 2008 for the stress scenarios, Lehman for the leverage of 31 that CET1 corrects, SVB for the run the LCR never saw coming.
