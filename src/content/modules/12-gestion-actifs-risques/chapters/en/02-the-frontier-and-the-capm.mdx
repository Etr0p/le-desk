Chapter 1 left you facing an infinity of choices: every set of weights is a portfolio, every portfolio a point in the risk/return plane. In 1952, a 25-year-old Chicago doctoral student, Harry Markowitz, asks the question that will structure the entire buy-side — and earn him the Nobel Prize in 1990: among all those points, which ones deserve to be held? His answer fits in one sentence: for each targeted level of return, there exists **one** minimum-variance portfolio — and the set of those optimal portfolios draws a curve, the **efficient frontier**. Every portfolio below the frontier is dominated: another one offers the same return for less risk. The manager's job begins with refusing to be dominated.

## Markowitz: the geometry of the free lunch

Take chapter 1's two assets again — A, equities with an 8% expected return; B, bonds at 3% — and slide the weight of A from 0 to 100%. The return moves linearly from 3 to 8%; the volatility, meanwhile, follows chapter 1's formula, and the resulting path **bulges to the left** as soon as the correlation is below 1. Play with the three sliders below and rediscover the three faces of correlation: the straight line at $\rho = 1$, the belly hollowing deeper as $\rho$ falls, the tip touching the vertical axis at $\rho = -1$.

<FrontierExplorer />

Two readings to take away. First, the **minimum-variance point**: there exists a mix less volatile than *either* of the two assets — holding 100% bonds "to be cautious" is dominated by a portfolio that contains some equities. Second, only the **upper branch** of the curve is efficient: below the minimum-variance point, every portfolio has a twin above it, same risk, better return. With $n$ assets the principle is identical — the algebra turns to matrices, but the frontier remains a frontier.

## The risk-free asset changes everything: the CML

Markowitz works with risky assets. Now add the asset that module 10 manufactures before your eyes eight times a year: the deposit at the risk-free rate $r_f$, with zero volatility. Combining cash with a risky portfolio P traces, in the risk/return plane, a **straight line** between $r_f$ and P — the correlation with cash being zero and its volatility too, nothing survives under the root except P's own term. Among all those lines, one dominates: the one that starts at $r_f$ and comes to **tangency** with the frontier. That is the **capital market line** (CML):

$$
r = r_f + \frac{r_m - r_f}{\sigma_m}\,\sigma
$$

Every point of the CML dominates every point of the frontier (except the tangency point itself): same risk, higher return. The consequence is brutal and has a name, the **separation theorem**: every rational investor holds *the same* risky portfolio — the tangency portfolio — and tunes their risk appetite only by dosing the cash (or the leverage) they put around it. The cautious one: 30% tangency portfolio, 70% cash. The aggressive one: 150% tangency portfolio, funded by borrowing. But the risky *content* is identical. And if everyone holds the same risky portfolio, it can only be one thing: the whole market, each asset weighted by its capitalisation. The tangency portfolio **is** the market portfolio.

## The CAPM: the price of the only risk that remains

From there flows the central model of modern finance, the **CAPM** (capital asset pricing model, Sharpe 1964). If everyone holds the market, a security's specific risk is already diversified away inside every portfolio: nobody needs to be paid to carry it. The market therefore only rewards an asset's contribution to **systematic** risk — measured by its **beta**:

$$
\beta = \rho \times \frac{\sigma_{\text{asset}}}{\sigma_{\text{market}}}
$$

Read the formula carefully: beta blends the **correlation** with the market AND the **relative volatility**. An asset with 25% volatility correlated at 0.8 with a market at 15% has a beta of $0.8 \times 25/15 = 1.333333$: it amplifies the market's moves by a third. But a very volatile *uncorrelated* asset — a biotech awaiting a clinical trial, ρ close to 0 — has a beta close to 0: its risk, however enormous, is diversifiable, hence **free** in the market's eyes. The required return of an asset then depends only on its beta, along the **security market line** (SML):

$$
r = r_f + \beta \times (r_m - r_f)
$$

With a risk-free rate of 3%, a market premium of 5% and a beta of 1.2: $3 + 1.2 \times 5 = 9\%$. The **equity market risk premium** $(r_m - r_f)$ is the key empirical parameter: about **4 to 6% per year** over long periods — the price, remarkably stable at the scale of a century, of one unit of systematic risk.

<Callout type="important" titre="CML and SML: two lines not to confuse">
A classic interview distinction. The **CML** carries **total** volatility on its x-axis and contains only the efficient portfolios — the blends of cash and market portfolio. The **SML** carries **beta** on its x-axis and contains *all* assets, efficient or not: at equilibrium, every security, however badly diversified taken in isolation, sits on the SML — because its price only rewards its systematic contribution. A volatile, uncorrelated biotech is far below the CML (too much total risk for its return), but exactly on the SML (beta ~0, required return ~$r_f$).
</Callout>

<Callout type="piege" titre="Beta is not volatility">
A classic interview question: "should an asset with 40% volatility yield more than an asset at 15%?". The CAPM answer: it depends *only* on their betas. If the 40% is uncorrelated with the market, that risk diversifies away and earns no premium — beta ~0, required return ~$r_f$. Volatility measures **total** risk; beta keeps only the **systematic** share, the only one the holder of a diversified portfolio actually suffers. Confusing the two is missing the CAPM's one idea: you are not paid for the risk you take, you are paid for the risk you cannot eliminate.
</Callout>

## What beta tells the desk

The CAPM is an equilibrium model, but its vocabulary is the daily toolkit of the trade. A **portfolio's beta** is the weighted average of its lines' betas: a book with a beta of 1.2 "is long 1.2 times the market". Managers classify themselves in this language: the "**high beta**" funds that amplify the market in bullish phases, the "**defensive**" or "low-beta" strategies that cushion the falls — utilities and consumer staples versus semiconductors and small caps. And every fund factsheet you will read decomposes performance into two words inherited from the model: **beta**, the market exposure, which can be replicated for next to nothing; and **alpha**, what remains — chapter 3 will take care of it.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Sizing a hedge with beta">
This is exactly module 7's calculation (the number of hedging contracts). An equity portfolio of 120M with a beta of 1.2 is not exposed to the market "for 120M" but for $120 \times 1.2 = 144$M index-equivalent: it is on that amount, not on the book's value, that the futures sale is sized (144M divided by the notional of one contract). Selling too few leaves market risk on; selling at the beta neutralises it — and all that remains in the book is the manager's specific bet, alpha in its pure state. "Market neutral" funds live permanently in that state.
</Callout>

## The limits, honestly

The CAPM rests on openly unrealistic assumptions: homogeneous expectations, unlimited lending and borrowing at the risk-free rate, no fees or taxes, a "market portfolio" that should contain every asset in the world — real estate and human capital included — and that no index truly captures. More troubling: empirically, the model **predicts the cross-section of returns poorly**. Since the 1980s, persistent "anomalies" have contradicted it — **value** stocks (cheap ones) beat what their beta promises, **momentum** does too, and low-volatility stocks return *more* than their beta requires, the exact opposite of the SML. The measured beta/return relationship is flatter than the theory.

<GoFurther suffixe="multi-factor models">
The academic response to the anomalies was not to abandon the CAPM's logic but to generalise it: if a single factor (the market) does not explain returns, several are needed. The three-factor model of **Fama and French** (1993) adds to the market a size factor (SMB: small caps earn more) and a value factor (HML: cheap stocks beat growth stocks); Carhart grafts momentum onto it in 1997; Fama and French themselves move to five factors in 2015 (profitability, investment). Every asset now has a beta *per factor*, and alpha is what survives all of them. The industry turned this into a product — "smart beta" and factor ETFs sell exposure to these premia for a few basis points — and into an endless debate: are these premia compensation for real risks, a behavioural inefficiency destined to erode, or a data-mining artefact? Keep the practical consequence: the more factors you add, the higher the bar for alpha — what passed for talent under the CAPM is often just a value or momentum beta that does not say its name.
</GoFurther>

Hence the model's rightful place: the CAPM is **a language more than a law**. It predicts imperfectly, but it structures everything — fund factsheets speak beta and alpha, mandates set exposure budgets, management commentary explains "risk-adjusted" performance. And above all, it provides what the trade cruelly lacked: a **yardstick**. If the normal return of a portfolio with a beta of 1.2 is 9%, then a manager who delivers 11% has created something — and a manager who delivers 8% while calling it "positive" has destroyed value while dressing it up. Measuring that something — alpha, the Sharpe ratio, the information ratio — is chapter 3.
