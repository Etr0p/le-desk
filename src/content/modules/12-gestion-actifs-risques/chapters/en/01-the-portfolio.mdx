For eleven modules you have worked on the same side of the river: the side that manufactures and quotes. Pricing a bond, quoting a swap, structuring a capital-guaranteed note, selling volatility — the sell-side, the bank. This module crosses the bridge. On the other side stand the clients of all your desks: the people who manage other people's money — the buy-side, whose map of players module 1 drew. And their trade rests on an idea that is disconcerting at first: it does not consist in *predicting* — nobody reliably knows whether equities will rise next year — but in **assembling**. Choosing weights, not winners. This chapter shows why assembly creates value all by itself: finance's only free lunch.

## Managing other people's money

<Callout type="definition" titre="Asset management">
**Asset management** consists of investing the savings of third parties — individuals, companies, institutions — for a fee, within a contractual framework that sets the investment universe and the authorised risk. Two main formats: the **fund** (a collective vehicle — in Europe, mutual funds carrying the **UCITS** label, pooled and regulated, available to the general public) and the **mandate** (bespoke management for a single institutional client). The manager is a **fiduciary**: the money does not belong to them, and that obvious fact structures everything — reporting, constraints, benchmarks.
</Callout>

The orders of magnitude give the measure of the industry: global assets under management come to about **\$100 trillion** — the order of magnitude of annual world GDP. The largest manager, BlackRock, alone exceeds **\$10 trillion** of assets, more than the GDP of every country but two. Behind those totals, clients with very different horizons:

| Vehicle | Typical client | Horizon and dominant constraint |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Mutual fund / UCITS** | General public | Daily liquidity, diversification imposed by regulation |
| **Mandate** | One institution | Bespoke: benchmark, risk budget and universe negotiated in the contract |
| **Pension fund** | Employees and retirees | Very long term — the liability is pensions promised thirty years out |
| **Insurer** | Policyholders | Regulated liability (Solvency II), dominant bond allocation |
| **Hedge fund** | Sophisticated investors | Lightly constrained: leverage, short selling, performance fee |

All of them, from the most regulated to the freest, ask the same question — and it is the question of this module: for a targeted return, how do you carry as little risk as possible?

## Return averages out

Start with the easy half. A portfolio's expected return is the **weighted average** of its components' expected returns — nothing more:

$$
r_p = w_1 \, r_1 + w_2 \, r_2
$$

The canonical example of the trade, the **60/40** portfolio: 60% equities with an 8% expected return, 40% bonds at 3%. Result: $0.6 \times 8 + 0.4 \times 3 = 6\%$. Linear, no surprise, no magic: diversifying costs no expected return. Remember that sentence, because all of Markowitz lies in its contrast with the next one.

## Risk, however, does not average out

The portfolio's volatility is **not** the weighted average of the volatilities. The formula — THE formula of the module, to be unrolled with your eyes closed:

$$
\sigma_p = \sqrt{w_1^2 \sigma_1^2 + w_2^2 \sigma_2^2 + 2\, w_1 w_2\, \rho\, \sigma_1 \sigma_2}
$$

Three terms under the root, to be read one by one. The first, $w_1^2 \sigma_1^2$, is asset 1's contribution on its own; the second, asset 2's; the third, $2\, w_1 w_2\, \rho\, \sigma_1 \sigma_2$, is the **cross term** — and it carries the whole story, because it is the only one where the **correlation** $\rho$ between the two assets appears (defined in module 2). If $\rho < 1$, that term is smaller than a simple average would demand, and the root comes out *below* the weighted average.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The 60/40, term by term">
Equities: 60% of the portfolio, 20% volatility. Bonds: 40%, 10% volatility. Correlation: 0.3. Under the root: $0.36 \times 400 = 144$, plus $0.16 \times 100 = 16$, plus $2 \times 0.6 \times 0.4 \times 0.3 \times 20 \times 10 = 28.8$. Total 188.8, root: $\sigma_p = 13.740451\%$. The weighted average would have given $0.6 \times 20 + 0.4 \times 10 = 16\%$. **More than two points of volatility have vanished** — without sacrificing a cent of expected return, still 6%. That gap between 16 and 13.74 is finance's only free lunch: risk reduction obtained by pure assembly.
</Callout>

## The three faces of correlation

Vary $\rho$ in the formula and you obtain the three limiting cases a jury loves to have recited:

- **$\rho = 1$: no lunch left.** The cross term becomes exactly what is needed for the root to close back into $(w_1\sigma_1 + w_2\sigma_2)$: the volatility equals **16%**, the weighted average, not a point less. Two perfectly correlated assets are *a single asset in disguise* — stacking them diversifies nothing. Interview corollary: it is not the **number of lines** that diversifies, it is the correlation between them.
- **$\rho = -1$: the perfect hedge.** The same 60/40 falls to $|0.6 \times 20 - 0.4 \times 10| = 8\%$. Better: there exists a choice of weights (one third in equities, two thirds in bonds, so that $w_1\sigma_1 = w_2\sigma_2$) that cancels **all** the volatility. You already know this object: it is a **hedge** — selling a future against a position (module 7) amounts to adding an asset correlated at −1.
- **$\rho = 0$: divide by $\sqrt{n}$.** Two identical assets at 20% volatility, independent, held 50/50: the root returns $\sqrt{200} = 14.142136\%$ — the volatility divided by $\sqrt{2}$. Generalise: $n$ independent assets divide the volatility by $\sqrt{n}$. It is the law of large numbers put to work for the portfolio.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The queen has a flaw: she betrays you in crises">
Correlation is the queen of this module — but keep in mind what module 11 showed you: in crises, correlations **rise towards 1**. When everyone sells everything at once, equities, credit, property and commodities all start falling together, and the cross term that protected you swells at the worst possible moment. The free lunch disappears precisely when you need it most. It is the same lesson as module 5's structured AAA: protection built on correlations *estimated in calm times* is protection that evaporates in a storm. Diversification is a cruising tool, not a life jacket.
</Callout>

## What melts away and what remains

Push the logic further: add lines, then more lines. With each added asset, the share of risk specific to each security — a factory that burns down, a CEO who resigns, a profit warning — dilutes into the average: that is **specific** (or idiosyncratic) risk, and the law of large numbers melts it away. But the volatility curve does not fall towards zero: it hits a floor. That floor is **systematic risk** — the risk of the market itself, the one that hits every line at once (a recession, a rate shock from module 10, a crisis from module 11). No assembly eliminates it: when the whole market falls, holding 500 stocks rather than 5 does not save you.

<GoFurther suffixe="how many lines does it take to diversify?">
The classic empirical studies (Evans and Archer 1968, then Statman 1987) show that most of an equity portfolio's specific risk disappears quickly: some twenty well-chosen stocks capture the greater part of the benefit, and beyond 30 to 50 lines the marginal gain becomes tiny — the portfolio's volatility converges towards the market's. Two modern nuances: on one hand, average correlations between stocks have risen (indexation, basket trading), which raises the systematic floor; on the other, diversifying *across asset classes* (equities, rates, commodities, currencies) remains more powerful than multiplying lines within a single class, because cross-class correlations are lower — that is the whole rationale of the 60/40, and of its "risk parity" heirs.
</GoFurther>

This distinction sets up the next chapter's twist: if anyone can melt specific risk away for free, by diversifying it, why would the market pay you to carry it? The CAPM's answer: it does not pay you. **Only the risk that remains — the systematic — is rewarded.**

One question of geometry remains. You now know how to compute the return and the risk of *one* assembly. But the weight combinations are infinite: each one is a point in the risk/return plane. What does the set of those points draw? A curve appears, with an edge — a **frontier** beyond which no portfolio exists, and on which live all the ones worth holding. That is chapter 2.
