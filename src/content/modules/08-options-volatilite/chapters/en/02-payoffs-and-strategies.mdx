Chapter 1 set the kink; this one teaches you to draw with it. In an options market, every position — from the simplest to the most baroque — can be read off a diagram at expiry, and every strategy is nothing but a sum of four elementary building blocks. But before stacking, a distinction juries love to see confused: what the contract pays is not what you make.

## From payoff to P&L: the premium shifts everything

The **payoff** is what the contract pays at expiry — chapter 1's $\max$ functions, always positive or zero for the holder of the right. The **P&L** subtracts what that right cost:

$$
\text{P\&L} = \text{direction} \times (\text{payoff} - \text{premium})
$$

with direction $+1$ for the buyer, $-1$ for the seller: the seller's P&L is the exact mirror of the buyer's — the option, like any derivative, remains a zero-sum game (module 7). Graphically, the premium **shifts the hockey stick downwards** for the buyer, who starts from $-\text{premium}$, and reflects it upwards for the seller.

Put numbers on the call struck at 100, bought for 4. Stock at 110: payoff 10, P&L $+6$. Stock at 95: payoff zero, P&L $-4$ — the premium, the maximum loss. Stock at 103: payoff 3... and P&L $-1$. In the money, exercised, and yet losing.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Finishing in the money does not mean making money">
Between 100 and 104, the call gets exercised — recovering 3 beats nothing — but does not pay back its premium of 4. The profit threshold is not the strike: it is the **breakeven**, strike **plus** premium for a call ($100 + 4 = 104$), strike **minus** premium for a put. In an interview, "I exercise therefore I profit" is a classic mistake; the clean answer distinguishes three zones: below 100, the whole premium is lost; between 100 and 104, you exercise to *reduce* the loss; only beyond 104 does the profit begin.
</Callout>

## The four basic positions

The rest of the chapter is a combination of these four lines:

| Position | Premium | Maximum loss | Maximum gain | Breakeven | You are betting on |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Long call** | paid | the premium | unlimited | $K$ + premium | a decisive rally |
| **Short call** (naked) | collected | unlimited | the premium | $K$ + premium | stagnation or decline |
| **Long put** | paid | the premium | $K$ − premium (the stock cannot go below zero) | $K$ − premium | a decisive fall |
| **Short put** | collected | $K$ − premium | the premium | $K$ − premium | stagnation or a rise |

Two readings of this table are worth the effort. Vertically: buyers pay for bounded losses and open gains, sellers collect for bounded gains and open losses — chapter 1's asymmetry, laid out in columns. Horizontally: each seller line is the mirror of the corresponding buyer line, breakeven included — at the breakeven, nobody wins or loses, on either side of the contract.

## Dressing up a portfolio: covered call, protective put, collar

The most widely traded strategies are not naked bets: they dress up an existing stock position.

The **covered call**: you hold the stock, you sell an OTM call on it. The premium lands — an income, collected no matter what. In exchange, if the stock climbs past the strike, you deliver your shares: the upside beyond it is no longer yours. The resulting profile is that of... a short put — chapter 3's parity will prove this is no coincidence. Use: seeking yield on a stock you expect to stay quiet, knowing that the "income" is paid for with the abandoned upside tail — chapter 1's insurance business, equities edition.

The **protective put**: stock plus a long put. It is portfolio insurance in the literal sense: a guaranteed floor at the strike (minus the premium), upside kept (minus the premium). The choice of strike is a choice of deductible — a more OTM put costs less and leaves a larger loss before protecting. The real cost is the time value, which melts as expiry approaches: staying permanently insured, rolling the puts, durably eats into performance.

The **collar**: a protective put financed by a covered call — you buy the put, you sell an OTM call, and the premium collected pays for all or part of the premium paid. Result: protected below the put's strike, capped at the call's strike, for a net cost close to zero. It is the classic outfit of the executive who must keep his shares but cannot afford to carry all of their risk.

## Cutting the bill: vertical spreads

Bullish, but not to the moon? The **bull call spread** buys one call and sells another, higher one: the 100 call bought for 4, the 110 call sold for 1.50 — net debit 2.50 instead of 4. The short leg finances the long leg; in exchange, any gain beyond 110 is given up:

$$
\text{max loss} = 2.50
\qquad
\text{max gain} = (110 - 100) - 2.50 = 7.50
\qquad
\text{breakeven} = 102.50
$$

The breakeven drops (102.50 versus 104 for the outright call), the stake shrinks, the gain gets capped: that is the signature of a **moderately bullish** view. The **bear put spread** is its bearish twin — a put bought at a high strike, a put sold at a lower one. Remember the grammar rather than the bestiary of names: *every leg sold reduces the cost of the position and gives up a piece of the profile*.

## Betting on movement, not on direction: straddle and strangle

An earnings announcement is coming: you are convinced the stock will move hard — without knowing which way. The **straddle** buys call and put at the same strike: strike 100, call at 6 plus put at 4, total cost 10. At expiry, one of the two legs pays the distance to the strike, whatever the direction:

$$
\text{breakevens} = K \pm \text{total cost} = 100 \pm 10 \;\Longrightarrow\; 90 \text{ and } 110
$$

The stock must break out of the 90–110 band — move by more than 10% — for the bet to pay; at exactly 100, both legs die together and the loss is total: 10. Buying a straddle is **buying movement**; selling one is selling calm — an insurer on both sides at once, gain capped at the cost collected, loss open in both directions. The **strangle** spreads the strikes apart (a 90 put plus a 110 call, for instance): cheaper, but the required move is bigger. And the practitioner's trap: if the announcement is expected by everyone, the premiums have already priced it in — the move has to beat *the one the market has priced*, not zero. That "priced move" has a name, implied volatility: chapter 6.

## Which strategy for which view

| Strategy | Construction | View | Max loss | Max gain |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Long call | call | strong rise | premium | unlimited |
| Long put | put | strong fall | premium | $K$ − premium |
| Covered call | stock + short call | calm or gentle rise | the stock's decline, softened by the premium | capped at the strike, plus the premium |
| Protective put | stock + long put | rise, but insured | down to the strike, plus the premium | unlimited, minus the premium |
| Collar | stock + long put + short call | protection without paying | down to the put's strike | capped at the call's strike |
| Bull call spread | long call $K_1$ + short call $K_2$ | moderate rise | net debit | $(K_2 - K_1)$ − debit |
| Long straddle | call + put, same strike | big move, direction unknown | total cost | unlimited |
| Short straddle | call + put sold, same strike | dead calm | unlimited | total cost |

All these constructions rest on premiums — 4 for the call, 6 for the put. But where do these numbers come from, and are they even free of each other? Before any model, an arbitrage argument welds the call, the put, the stock and cash into a rigid square: put-call parity. That is chapter 3 — and it is the most elegant argument in the module.
