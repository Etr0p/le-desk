Change seats: you are now the seller. Ten contracts of the canonical call (S = 100, K = 100, r = 5%, σ = 20%, one year), 100 shares per contract — one thousand options sold, premium collected: 10,451. Chapter 1 was clear about what you have just done: selling insurance, capped gain, open-ended loss. Waiting for expiry and praying is not risk management. The trading floor manages this risk with a dashboard — the **greeks**, the sensitivities of the price to each of its ingredients — and a driving discipline, **delta-hedging**. This chapter is the one that comes closest to the real job of an options desk: this is where the difference between selling an option and carrying it plays out.

## Delta: the slope that doubles as a recipe

The **delta** measures the premium's sensitivity to the underlying: how much the option moves when the stock moves by 1. For a Black-Scholes call, the result is of rare elegance: $\Delta = N(d_1)$ — a weight that already appeared in the pricing formula. On the canonical call: **0.6368**. Three readings of the same number:

- **a slope**: the stock goes from 100 to 101, the call gains about 0.64. It is the tangent of the price curve — valid for small moves, and the rest of the chapter will say why;
- **a hedge ratio**: holding 0.6368 shares per call sold neutralizes the position. It is the $\Delta$ of chapter 4's tree — the spread of values over the spread of prices — taken to continuous time;
- **roughly a probability of exercise**: "delta 64" reads on the floor as "about two chances in three of finishing in the money". A convenient but **approximate** reading: the risk-neutral probability of exercise is $N(d_2) = 0.5596$, and $N(d_1)$ systematically overstates it — it also weights by the stock's value in the exercise states (chapter 4).

A put's delta is negative — the right to sell appreciates when the market falls: **−0.3632** on the canonical case. And parity keeps the books: differentiate $C - P = S - K\,e^{-rT}$ with respect to $S$ and you are left with $\Delta_{call} - \Delta_{put} = 1$, exactly — $0.6368 + 0.3632 = 1$. The bounds can be guessed: far out of the money, the call reacts to nothing ($\Delta \to 0$); deep in the money, it **is** the stock ($\Delta \to 1$); at the money, above 0.5 — 0.6368 here: the strike is compared to the compounded spot, not the spot, and the $\sigma^2/2$ term in $d_1$ pushes the same way. ## Delta-hedging: the seller's discipline

Your 10 contracts sold amount to 1,000 options, i.e. a position delta of $-1\,000 \times 0.6368 = -636.8$ "share equivalents". Neutralizing it is mechanical: **buy 637 shares** (standard rounding — tenths of a share do not trade). The book is **delta-neutral**: on a small move, what the sold calls cost, the shares give back — the market's direction no longer concerns you.

If the story ended there, selling options would be an annuity. It does not end there: **the delta moves with the market**, and the hedge must follow. The stock rises to 105: the call's delta moves to 0.7237, you now need 724 shares — buy 87 more, at 105. The stock falls back to 100: the delta returns to 0.6368 — sell 87 shares back, at 100. The market has made a perfect round trip, your book is back where it started… minus $87 \times 5 = 435$. No mistake was made: the delta-hedged seller **buys after the rise and sells after the fall**, by construction. He runs after the market, and every lap has a price.

## Gamma: convexity, and its price

What cost you 435 has a name. The delta itself has a slope — the **gamma**, the second derivative of the price: **0.018762** on the canonical case; read it as "the stock moves by 1, the delta moves by about 0.019". Gamma is identical for the call and the put with the same characteristics — their deltas differ by a constant, parity again — and it is **positive for whoever holds the option**: the buyer's position curves in his favor, his delta grows when the market goes his way and melts when it turns. The seller carries the exact mirror image: **negative gamma**, the wrong-footed readjustment, the losing round trip of the previous paragraph.

The cost can be quantified: a market move of $\Delta S$ costs the negative-gamma book about $\tfrac{1}{2}\,\Gamma\,(\Delta S)^2$, i.e. $\tfrac{1}{2} \times 0.018762 \times 25 \approx 0.23$ per option for a move of 5 — about 470 on the 1,000-option round trip, the order of magnitude of the 435 observed (the gap comes from the gamma moving along the way). And the parameter the floor watches: gamma is **highest at the money and explodes near expiry** — at the money, it goes from 0.019 at one year, to 0.063 at five weeks, to 0.20 with a few days left. The delta of an expiring ATM option jumps from 0 to 1 at the slightest quiver of the spot: this is the hour when hedgers sweat — and the peak of ATM optionality already spotted in chapter 1.

## Theta: the rent on gamma

If option buyers pocketed the round trips that sellers pay, with nothing in return, nobody would ever sell an option. The counterpart exists: **theta**, the sensitivity to passing time. For the holder of an at-the-money option, it is negative: each day without a move eats away at chapter 1's time value, faster and faster as expiry approaches. Gamma and theta are the two sides of the same rental contract: **whoever holds the convexity pays a daily rent; whoever suffers it is paid to wait**. The P&L of a delta-hedged book, over a small interval, fits on one line:

$$
\text{P\&L} \;\approx\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\,\Gamma\,(\Delta S)^2}_{\text{the market moves}} \;+\; \underbrace{\Theta\,\Delta t}_{\text{time passes}}
$$

The first term is in $(\Delta S)^2$: the **square** erases direction — only the amplitude counts, up or down. On agitated days, it pays the gamma buyer; on calm days, it pays nothing and the theta rent falls due anyway.

<Callout type="important" titre="The gamma-theta break-even">
What daily move puts the book exactly at break-even? At the Black-Scholes price, the answer is remarkable: the "one standard deviation" move promised by the pricing volatility, i.e. $\sigma/\sqrt{252}$ per day — for $\sigma = 20\%$, about 1.26% (20/15.87), i.e. 1.26 on a spot at 100. If the underlying durably **realizes** more than that, the delta-hedged gamma buyer wins; if it realizes less, the seller pockets the difference. Read that again: direction has vanished from the problem. Buying an option and delta-hedging it is not a bet on a rise — it is **buying volatility**, here at an entry point of 20%. That sentence is the door to chapter 6.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="the Taylor expansion behind the P&L">
Expand the option's value $V(S, t)$ for a small move $\Delta S$ and a small time step $\Delta t$:

$$
\Delta V \;\approx\; \Delta \cdot \Delta S \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Gamma\,(\Delta S)^2 \;+\; \Theta\,\Delta t \;+\; \mathcal{V} \cdot \Delta\sigma
$$

(the first $\Delta$ is the option's delta, $\Delta S$ the change in the spot — the notation collision is a tradition of the trade; $\mathcal{V}$ is the vega). The delta-hedged book holds the option and $-\Delta$ shares: the first-order term cancels, and with volatility unchanged there remains exactly $\tfrac{1}{2}\,\Gamma\,(\Delta S)^2 + \Theta\,\Delta t$. The Black-Scholes partial differential equation then says this: at the model price, for a book financed at the risk-free rate, theta exactly offsets the half-gamma if the market realizes the pricing volatility $\sigma$ — that is where the "one standard deviation" break-even comes from. Over the whole life of the position, the cumulative P&L can be written as a sum of terms in $\tfrac{1}{2}\,\Gamma S^2\,(\sigma_{\text{realized}}^2 - \sigma_{\text{implied}}^2)\,\Delta t$: the final result has the sign of the gap between realized volatility and the volatility paid — the exact bridge to chapter 6.
</GoFurther>

## Vega: the greek that should never have existed — and rho, for the record

One sensitivity remains, and it carries a whiff of paradox: the **vega**, the price's reaction to volatility — quoted **per vol point**, the desks' convention. On the canonical call: **0.3752**. Full-scale check: σ moves from 20% to 21%, the call goes from 10.4506 to 10.8263 — it gains 0.38. Vega is identical for the call and the put (parity, always: $S - K\,e^{-rT}$ contains no $\sigma$), **highest at the money**, and — unlike gamma — **increasing with maturity**: 0.20 at three months, 0.38 at one year, 0.50 at two years. Hence the desk's map: short-dated options are gamma instruments, long-dated ones vega instruments.

The paradox makes an excellent oral remark: Black-Scholes assumes $\sigma$ is **constant**, and the floor spends its days measuring the sensitivity of prices to variations in a parameter that is not supposed to vary. The vega is the numerical admission that the model knows itself to be wrong — chapter 6 shows how the market institutionalized that admission. There remains **rho**, for the record: the sensitivity to the risk-free rate, positive for the call (the discounted strike weighs less when rates rise), negative for the put — second-order on this module's short horizons, watched on long-dated options, mentioned in the oral in one sentence.

You now hold the full dashboard: delta for direction, gamma for curvature, theta for the rent, vega for volatility. But this whole chapter took σ = 20% as a given fallen from the sky — when it is the only ingredient of the formula that nobody observes. Where does that number come from? From the market itself, which quotes options in vol — and does not quote the same one for every strike. Implied volatility, realized volatility, the smile: chapter 6.
