Black-Scholes, in chapter 4, demands five ingredients. Four can be read off a screen: the spot, the strike, the rate, the expiry. The fifth — volatility — can be read nowhere: it is a bet on the **future** agitation of the underlying. This chapter turns the problem around, and this inversion is one of the most fertile in modern finance: since the option's price, for its part, is observable, one can **extract** from it the volatility the market has lodged inside. This "implied" volatility has become the language of the trade — and its deformations, the smile, tell what the market truly fears.

## Realized volatility: measuring what has already happened

First definition, inherited in a straight line from module 2: **realized volatility** (also called historical) is the standard deviation of observed returns, annualized. Variances add up over time, so the standard deviation grows like the square root of time:

$$
\sigma_{\text{ann}} = \sigma_{\text{day}} \times \sqrt{252}
$$

An index that moves 1.2% per day shows $1.2 \times \sqrt{252} = 1.2 \times 15.8745 = 19.05\%$ of annualized volatility. The desk's mental shortcut — √252 ≈ 16 — gives the order of magnitude instantly: 1% per day ≈ 16% per year.

Its strength: it is objective — observed prices, a formula, a number, no opinion. Its weakness, disqualifying for pricing: it looks **backward**. The option you trade today will pay on the agitation of the next 252 days, not the last 252. Plugging historical volatility into Black-Scholes is driving while staring into the rearview mirror.

## Implied volatility: inverting Black-Scholes

Take chapter 4's canonical call again: S = 100, K = 100, r = 5%, one year. The market quotes it at **10.4506**. The question, turned around: what volatility must be fed into Black-Scholes to land back on that price? Chapter 5's vega guarantees that the answer is **unique** — the price increases strictly with σ: more agitation always promises more to the option — and that it exists for any premium within the arbitrage bounds. So it is enough to bracket by bisection: at 15% vol, the model gives 8.59 — too low; at 25%, it gives 12.34 — too high; cut the interval in half, again and again, and the machine converges to **20%**. That is the **implied volatility**: the unique σ that reconciles the model and the observed price.

$$
\sigma_{\text{imp}} \text{ such that } C_{BS}(S, K, r, \sigma_{\text{imp}}, T) = \text{quoted premium}
$$

The practical consequence goes beyond the technique: **options desks do not quote in euros, they quote in vol**. "I pay 20 for the 100 strike" means: I buy it at the premium Black-Scholes displays for σ = 20% — i.e. 10.45. And the vega translates instantly: if the quote moves from 20 to 21, the premium rises by about 0.38 (10.45 → 10.83). Comparing two options of different strikes and maturities by their premiums in euros makes no sense; comparing their implied vols does.

<Callout type="important" titre="An option's price is read in vol, as a bond is read in yield">
You have already met this gymnastics in module 4: nobody judges a bond by its price in euros — you convert to yield, the scale that makes everything comparable. Options have the same common scale: implied volatility. "Is this call expensive?" is never answered in euros; it is answered "it quotes 20 vol while the underlying only realizes 14". Mastering this translation reflex is speaking the desk's language — and it is exactly what a jury tests by asking whether an option is "expensive".
</Callout>

## Implied versus realized: the price of insurance

Now put the two measures side by side, day after day, on the major equity indices: implied volatility sits **on average above** the volatility the underlying subsequently realizes. The gap has a name — the **volatility risk premium** — and a crystal-clear interpretation: an option is insurance, and insurance costs more than its actuarial cost. The protection buyer agrees to overpay a little, on average, to be covered on the day everything breaks; the seller demands that margin to carry a tail risk — rare, but devastating. A home insurance premium exceeds the expected claims; implied vol exceeds the expectation of realized vol. Same logic, same consequences: selling that insurance pays regularly… until the claim. Chapter 7 is entirely devoted to that "until".

## The VIX: fear quoted around the clock

This implied volatility has a worldwide thermometer: the **VIX**, computed by the CBOE — intuitively, the roughly 30-day implied volatility of options on the S&P 500, aggregated over a whole basket of strikes and expressed as an annualized percentage. Its nickname says it all: "the fear index". It rises when markets fall — everyone rushes into puts, their price in vol soars — and sags in quiet markets. The reference levels to know by heart: around **12**, a sleepy market; around **20**, the long-term average; above **40**, open crisis; above **80**, the historic peaks of autumn 2008 and March 2020. The VIX itself is traded — futures, options, index products — which makes it both an indicator and an asset: remember that dual status, it will sit at the heart of one of chapter 7's accidents.

## The smile: what 1987 etched into prices

If Black-Scholes described the world, all options on the same underlying and the same expiry would show the **same** implied vol, whatever the strike — a single σ for a single lognormal law. That was roughly true before October 1987: the sheet of implied vols was flat. Then came Black Monday — October 19, 1987, the Dow Jones loses **22.6% in one session**, a move the normal law rates as more or less impossible — and the sheet never flattened again. Since then, on equity markets, **out-of-the-money puts durably cost more in vol** than at-the-money options: plotted strike by strike, implied vol draws a slope — the **skew** — or an asymmetric grin — the **smile**.

Two readings complement each other. **Crash-o-phobia**: the market has learned that crashes exist, that the true distribution of returns has a fat left tail the lognormal ignores — and it charges for that tail in the puts, the anti-crash insurance. The **leverage effect**: when a stock falls, the company's debts do not fall with it — the shareholder is mechanically more leveraged, hence the stock more volatile on the way down than on the way up; vol rises as the price falls, and the low strikes inherit it.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The smile does not refute Black-Scholes — it demotes it">
Telling the jury "the smile proves Black-Scholes is wrong, therefore unusable" is a misreading. The smile proves the world is not lognormal with a single σ — each strike has **its own** implied vol, which is logically incompatible with the model. But the market has known that since 1987 and keeps quoting **through** BS: the model has been demoted from a description of the world to a **quoting language** — a price ↔ vol converter, universal and invertible. The formulation that sets you apart: "Black-Scholes is wrong as a model and indispensable as a convention; the smile is precisely where the market corrects the model."
</Callout>

## The volatility surface: the desk's real object

Add the maturity dimension and you obtain the object an options trader actually contemplates: the **volatility surface** — one implied vol for each (strike, maturity) pair. The cut at a fixed strike is called the **term structure**: in calm times it is upward-sloping — long-dated uncertainty costs more than short-dated — and it **inverts** in a crisis, when the day's panic propels short vols above long ones. The desk does not manage a price: it manages this entire sheet, rippling continuously — the level that breathes, the skew that tightens ahead of political deadlines, the term slope that flips at the first shock.

One last conceptual step: if volatility can be measured, quoted and traded, then it is no longer a parameter — it is an **asset class**. Buying a straddle — call plus put at the same strike — is, once direction is neutralized, buying volatility; selling it is selling volatility. Dedicated contracts — *variance swaps*, VIX futures — even allow the implied-realized gap to be traded in its pure state, without options.

<GoFurther suffixe="buying pure volatility, from the straddle to the variance swap">
The delta-hedged straddle is the practitioner's vol position: buy the at-the-money call and put, neutralize the delta continuously, and the final P&L no longer depends on direction but on the gap between the vol **realized** over the life of the position and the **implied** vol paid at entry — the gamma collects the agitation, the theta pays the rent, and the balance has the sign of (realized − implied). Yet the exposure is not perfectly pure: it deforms as the spot drifts away from the strike. Hence the **variance swap**: a contract that exchanges, at expiry, the observed realized variance against a level fixed at signature — an exposure exactly linear in variance, with no delta-hedging. Theory shows it is replicated by a basket of options of **all** strikes; that recipe is precisely the one used to compute the VIX — which is why its official definition aggregates a whole basket of out-of-the-money puts and calls rather than a single at-the-money option.
</GoFurther>

You now know how to read an option price the way the desk reads it: in vol — implied against realized, strike by strike, maturity by maturity. It remains to see what the players **do** with these instruments — hedge, sell insurance, speculate — and what happens when volatility wakes up against them: the last chapter is the chapter of accidents.
