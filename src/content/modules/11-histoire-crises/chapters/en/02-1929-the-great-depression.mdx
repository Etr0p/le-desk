Every crisis in this module descends from this one: 1929 is the laboratory where the West learned — at the cost of a lost decade — almost everything chapter 1 stated as theorems. A legitimate boom turned euphoric, leverage 10 handed out to the general public, a central bank looking the other way, bank runs with no safety net, a deflation strangling debtors: every phase of the textbook is played out life-size, and every institution that frames your job today — module 1's SEC, the FDIC, module 10's activist Fed — is a scar from this story. Let us tell it in order: the party, the credit, the crash, then the real catastrophe — which is not the crash.

## The Roaring Twenties: a displacement turned "new era"

The American boom of the 1920s begins with a perfectly real **displacement**, in Minsky's sense: the electrification of factories and homes, the mass-produced automobile, and radio — the first instantaneous mass medium. RCA, the star radio stock, is **multiplied by ten in four years**: it is the growth stock of its day, a distant promise discounted with fervor — you recognize the long-duration asset of module 10, chapter 7, seventy years before the Nasdaq. On this authentic foundation the theory of euphoria is built, the *this time is different* of the era: the "**new era**", in which the science of management, the brand-new Fed and electric productivity had supposedly abolished the business cycle. On October 16, 1929, Irving Fisher — the greatest living American economist, the very man whose real-rate/nominal-rate relation you have been using since module 10 — declares that stocks have reached "**a permanently high plateau**". Eight days later comes Black Thursday. Keep the lesson in humility: euphoria is not a disease of the ignorant — it recruited the best quantitative mind of its generation.

## Call loans: leverage 10 for everyone

The boom's fuel is a product of fearsome simplicity: the **call loan** (or *broker loan*). You want \$1,000 of stock? Put down 100, your broker borrows the remaining 900 overnight, securities as collateral. 10% margin, hence **leverage 10** — and you know chapter 1's formula of ruin: at leverage 10, the distance to death is **−10%**. As long as the market rises, the machine is magical: +10% on stocks makes +100% on your stake. Millions of Americans discover it, and broker loans outstanding exceed **\$8.5bn in 1929** — a colossal sum for the time, fed by a detail that says it all: the call money rate at times exceeds **15%**, so that *industrial companies* find it more profitable to lend to speculators than to invest in their own factories. When financing speculation pays better than the real economy, Minsky's finance has reached its terminal stage. And the mechanism is reflexive in both directions: on the way up, credit feeds the prices that justify more credit; on the way down, every 10% decline wipes out a layer of speculators whose positions are liquidated automatically — chapter 1's forced selling, pre-wired into millions of accounts.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The arithmetic of the margin desk">
A speculator buys \$1,000 of stock with a \$100 stake: the broker lends 900. The market falls 5%: the portfolio is worth \$950, the debt is still 900 — equity has dropped to 50, i.e. **−50% for a −5% market move** (leverage 10 × −5%). The broker calls: restore the margin *today*, or be liquidated. At −10%, there is nothing left to restore. Now measure what Black Monday (−12.8%) and Black Tuesday (−11.7%) mean: two days *each* of which exceeded the fatal shock for the entire margin speculation of the country. Liquidation was not a decision — it was pre-wired.
</Callout>

## October 1929: the anatomy of the crash

The chronology, to know with its numbers. On **September 3, 1929**, the Dow Jones touches its peak: **381.17**. The erosion begins, with no apparent drama. On **Thursday, October 24** — Black Thursday — the wave of selling swamps the system: **12.9 million shares** traded, an all-time record, such that the ticker tape runs up to **four hours late** — millions of holders spend the whole day not knowing the real price of what they own, and in the dark, people sell. At noon, the great bankers gathered at Morgan send Richard Whitney, vice-president of the NYSE, to buy US Steel ostentatiously above the market: the theatrical gesture — copied from J.P. Morgan in 1907 — stabilizes the session. But 1929 is not 1907: on **Monday, October 28**, the Dow loses **−12.8%**; on **Tuesday, October 29** — Black Tuesday — another **−11.7%** on **16.4 million shares**. Two consecutive sessions down more than ten percent: margin calls mow down the leverage-10 accounts by the hundreds of thousands, and each liquidation feeds the next — chapter 1's spiral, with no circuit breaker.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The rebound trap: the worst was not the crash">
Here is the fact almost everyone ignores, and that makes the good interview answers: after the November 1929 trough, the market **rebounds by about +48% into April 1930**. Six months of gains — the press celebrates the all-clear, the dip-buyers congratulate themselves. Then the real descent begins, slow, grinding, almost without spectacular days: the Dow touches **41.22 on July 8, 1932**. From the peak of 381.17, the drawdown is **−89.19%** (precisely −89.185928% — the reference value of drawdownPct in your calculations): a dollar invested at the top is worth eleven cents. And chapter 1's asymmetry delivers the sentence: after −89%, you need **+809%** to get back. The trap to remember: the October crash destroyed "only" half the value; it was the depression of 1930-1932, with its false rebound in the middle, that did the rest. A 48% rebound inside a market that will end at −89%: beware of rebounds — they are the rule of great bear markets, not the exception.
</Callout>

## From crash to depression: the three transmission belts

A stock market crash, even of this size, is not mechanically a depression — 1987 will prove it. To turn one into the other took three transmission belts.

**First belt: wealth and uncertainty.** Households whose wealth has been amputated cut their consumption; above all, uncertainty freezes decisions — why buy an automobile on credit when you don't know whether you will have a job? Business investment collapses along with expected demand.

**Second belt: bank runs — money destroyed.** There is at that time **no deposit insurance**: your bank closes, your savings vanish. The run is therefore perfectly **rational** — first to the counter, first served — and self-fulfilling, exactly chapter 1's daily referendum. From 1930 to 1933, in successive waves, **about 9,000 American banks close**. Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz, in their monumental *Monetary History of the United States* (1963), draw from this the indictment that renamed the period "the **Great Contraction**": every bank failure destroys deposits, that is, money — and **the American money supply falls by a third** between 1929 and 1933, while the Fed, born in 1913 precisely for this, **stands aside**. The lender of last resort existed; it did not lend.

**Third belt: debt deflation.** Prices fall by roughly **10% a year** in the early 1930s — and you know since module 10, chapter 4, why deflation is the poison central bankers fear more than inflation.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Irving Fisher's debt-deflation (1933)">
Ruined by the crash he had not seen coming, Fisher drew from it the theory that bears his name. Debts are fixed in **nominal** dollars; when prices fall 10% a year, the dollar you must repay is worth 10% *more* every year in goods, in hours of work, in harvests: **the real debt grows heavier even as it is being repaid**. Debtors sell assets and squeeze their spending to deleverage — but those sales and that contraction push prices *down further*, which makes everyone else's real debt heavier still. Hence Fisher's formula: the more the debtors pay, the more they owe. It is chapter 1's deleveraging spiral, transposed from a fund's balance sheet to the entire economy — and it is why module 10 taught you that a central bank would rather target 2% inflation than flirt with zero.
</Callout>

## The policy mistakes: the manual of what not to do

The depression was not fate: it was worsened by three mistakes that became the founding counter-examples of the profession. **The Fed, passive then perverse**: not content with letting the banks die, it **raises rates in 1931** — in the middle of the contraction! — to defend the dollar's gold parity after sterling left the gold standard: the rules of the gold game came before the economy, exactly the external anchor whose abandonment module 10, chapter 1, recounted. **Protectionism**: the **Smoot-Hawley** tariff of June 1930 raises duties on thousands of products; trading partners retaliate, and world trade collapses **by about two-thirds** between 1929 and 1933 — international contagion organized by statute. **Fiscal austerity**: the orthodoxy of the balanced budget dictates spending cuts and tax hikes into a collapsing demand. The human toll of the whole: **US GDP amputated by about 30%**, **unemployment at about 25% in 1933** — one worker in four. It is that landscape, and not the stock market crash alone, that the word "1929" designates in collective memory.

## 1933-1934: the institutions born from the rubble

Everything 1907 had taught in miniature, 1933 engraves at scale — remember chapter 1's pattern: the crisis reveals the institutional hole, the institution is born from the hole. In **April 1933**, Roosevelt suspends the dollar's gold convertibility: monetary policy stops being hostage to the metal, and prices stop falling almost immediately — a real-time demonstration that the deflation was a choice of monetary regime. The **Banking Act of 1933** — known as **Glass-Steagall** — separates commercial banking (deposits, lending) from investment banking (securities, speculation), so that household savings no longer fund market positions; and it creates the **FDIC**, federal deposit insurance. Measure what the FDIC changes in chapter 1's logic: if the state guarantees your deposit, running to the counter no longer serves any purpose — **the run, from rational, becomes irrational**, and therefore stops being triggered. It is the purest example of an institution that removes a panic equilibrium *without spending a dollar in normal times*. Finally, the **Securities Acts of 1933 and 1934** impose disclosure on issuance and create the **SEC** — the regulator you met in module 1: mandatory information, prosecution of manipulation, margin regulation. The end of the world in which one bought, on 10% cover, securities whose issuer published nothing.

<GoFurther suffixe="Glass-Steagall, dead and haunting">
The wall of 1933 held for two generations, cracked through successive exemptions from the 1980s onward, then was formally repealed in **1999** by the **Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act** — in the name of modernization and the economies of scale of universal banking. Nine years later, 2008: the debate is instantly reborn — did merging the businesses put deposits in the service of market leverage? The honest answer is nuanced (Lehman and Bear Stearns were *pure* investment banks that Glass-Steagall would not have saved; the savings and loans of the 1980s died without speculating), but the Volcker rule of 2010, which bans proprietary trading at deposit-taking banks, is a Glass-Steagall in miniature. Remember for the interview: the institutional scars of 1929 are not monuments — they reopen, they close again, and every crisis replays the debate.
</GoFurther>

## Twenty-five years to come back

The Dow Jones does not see its 381 points of September 1929 again until **November 1954**: a quarter of a century. Check the consistency with your chapter 1 tools: recovering from −89% at 7% annual growth would take $\ln(1/0.11)/\ln(1.07) \approx$ **32.6 years** — anneesDeRecuperation(89, 7). Reality was slightly kinder than the price alone, and the gap has an explanation: first **deflation** — with 1932 prices roughly 25% below those of 1929, the *real* trough was less deep than the nominal −89%; then **dividends** — yields in the 1930s-1940s were high (often above 5%), and in real *total return*, an investor who had held on (and reinvested) recovered his purchasing power years before 1954. The nuance plays well in an interview: the "25 years" figure measures the holder's suffering in nominal price — the only number he saw — not quite that of his real wealth. But who held on? The leverage-10 speculator was dead in October 1929; the saver often sold in the revulsion; and Kindleberger told you: after a bubble, an entire generation turns away — Americans will not return to the stock market in force until the 1950s.

## What 1929 taught you — the interview crib sheet

- **Leverage turns a correction into an erasure.** Without leverage-10 call loans, October 1929 would have been a bear market; with them, every 10% decline destroyed a layer of holders and forced the next layer's sales. Leverage is the first question to ask of any bubble — you will ask it again in every chapter.
- **Without a lender of last resort or deposit insurance, panic is rational.** The 9,000 dead banks and the third of the money supply that evaporated are not crowd madness: they are perfectly logical run equilibria, which the FDIC abolished with a stroke of the pen. Bagehot (chapter 1) is not a moral option, it is a mechanical necessity.
- **Deflation is the poison.** Fisher's debt-deflation explains why module 10 targets 2% and not 0, why the BoJ fought for thirty years, and why "prices are falling" is never good news for an indebted economy.
- **Your institutions are scars.** FDIC, SEC, Glass-Steagall, a Fed that floods the market at the first tremor: none of this is theoretical — every rule in module 1 and every reflex in module 10 has a birth certificate dated 1933. When the Fed of 2008 or 2020 opens the floodgates within days, it is reciting the lesson of Friedman and Schwartz — Ben Bernanke promised it publicly to the two authors in 2002: "thanks to you, we won't do it again."

After 1933, a banking system padlocked by Glass-Steagall and exchange rates fixed by Bretton Woods (module 10) give the West half a century without a major financial crisis — the longest lull in modern history, and, as Minsky taught you, a lull is never free: it manufactures forgetting. As the memory of 1929 fades, deregulation reopens the game, and a novelty settles into the trading rooms: the computer, which promises to sell automatically, without emotion, at the right moment. On Monday, October 19, 1987, the machines will all sell together — **−22.6% in one session**, the worst day in Wall Street history, worse than 1929. That is chapter 3.
