Fall 1998: LTCM is in its death throes, the Fed cuts rates three times to cushion the shock — and the Nasdaq, briefly shaken, goes vertical again. After the models of 1987 and 1998, here is an old-fashioned bubble, a cousin of 1929: a genuine story of technological disruption, a crowd that believes in it, valuations that break free of all arithmetic. But it will teach you two things 1929 could not: what happens to those who are right too early, and why a bubble can destroy \$5 trillion of market capitalization *without* triggering a systemic crisis. Chapter 1's grammar is all here — except one ingredient, and that absence is the key to the chapter.

## 1995: Netscape and the new economy

On August 9, 1995, Netscape — a web browser, sixteen months old, no profits — goes public: the offering price doubles in its first session. The signal is heard by an entire generation of entrepreneurs and bankers: the market *pays* for the Internet, now, without waiting for profits. The narrative that takes hold has every trait of chapter 1's "displacement": the Internet will change everything (commerce, the press, communication — and that was true), therefore the old valuation rules no longer apply (and that was false). A substitute accounting is invented: absent earnings, one counts "**eyeballs**" — the pairs of eyes on the site — clicks, unique visitors. The official doctrine: "growth first, profits later" — conquer territory at a loss, profitability will follow. For some, it truly would. For most, never.

<Callout type="important" titre="'Irrational exuberance': lucid four years too early">
On December 5, 1996, Alan Greenspan wonders aloud: haven't markets been seized by "irrational exuberance"? A perfect diagnosis — and perfectly unusable: the Nasdaq will still **more than triple** after the phrase, the peak not arriving until March 2000, four years later. The lesson holds for the central banker as for the trader: **being lucid does not protect you; being lucid too early ruins you**. Shorting a bubble (module 3) exposes you to unlimited losses through the entire euphoria phase, with margin calls at every new high: being right about the destination is worthless if you are dead before arrival — Keynes's line from chapter 3, this time applied to the pessimists.
</Callout>

## 1999: the year doubt costs a career

Terminal euphoria can be measured: in 1999, **486 IPOs** in the United States, of which about **117 double on the first day** — Netscape's doubling, exceptional in 1995, has become the norm. Capital pours onto business models that posterity has turned into case studies:

- **Pets.com**: selling bags of kibble online, at a loss, with shipping costs exceeding the margin — and a blockbuster-sized advertising budget;
- **Webvan**: home grocery delivery, pharaonic warehouses built before the first customers;
- **boo.com**: pan-European online fashion, \$135m burned in eighteen months.

And the cruelest mechanism of every bubble kicks in: it **eliminates its skeptics before it dies**. Julian Robertson, a legend of value investing, watches investors desert his Tiger fund for refusing tech — he closes in **March 2000**, the exact month of the peak. Warren Buffett, mocked in late 1999 by the financial press as a man of the past, holds firm — but how many managers could afford to? The market's sanction falls *before* the truth of prices: when the bubble finally bursts, those who were right are no longer around to profit.

<GoFurther suffixe="what 117 first-day doubles say about the primary market">
Reread the number through module 1's lenses: a stock that doubles on day one is a company that raised half of what it could have — money "left on the table" for the benefit of the clients allocated at the IPO. That an issuer accepts this once is an anomaly; that it happens 117 times in a year is a symptom: the first-day *pop* had become the deal's marketing pitch, the media signal that set up the next raise. When the primary market stops financing projects and starts manufacturing listing fireworks, the capital-allocation function — the very reason a stock exchange exists — has stopped working. It is a more reliable end-of-cycle marker than any index level.
</GoFurther>

The symbolic summit is a merger: in January 2000, AOL — the internet access provider — announces the acquisition of Time Warner — studios, networks, magazines — for about **\$165bn**, paid essentially in AOL shares. The clicks buy the factories: the market values AOL's audience above a century of real assets. The deal will become the era's symbol of value destruction.

## The peak: Gordon-Shapiro versus the eyeballs

The Nasdaq touches **5,048.62** on March 10, 2000, with an index P/E above **100** — against 15 to 20 on historical average. Module 3 gave you the tool to say what that number means:

$$
P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r - g}
$$

A valuation is not a popularity vote: it is a **promise of discounted cash flows**. Paying 100 times earnings means promising either decades of flawless growth, or a durably crushed discount rate — and requiring that *nothing* derail along the way. Turn the DCF around: at peak prices, dozens of competing companies in the same sector all had to win their bet *simultaneously* — even though each one's promise assumed taking the others' market. The arithmetic was impossible collectively, not individually: that is what made the bubble so hard to fight — each story, taken alone, was plausible.

## The fall: 31 months of erosion, not one day of crash

Unlike 1929 or 1987, there is no *single* black Monday to memorize: the Nasdaq dies slowly, in waves, with 20% rebounds that ruin the dip-buyers. From the peak of March 10, 2000 to the trough of October 9, 2002 — **1,114.11** — 31 months of erosion:

$$
\frac{1\,114.11}{5\,048.62} - 1 = -77.9\%
$$

Chapter 1's asymmetry delivers the sentence: after −78%, you need about **+354.5%** to get back to the starting point. The Nasdaq will not see 5,000 points again until **April 2015** — fifteen years. A generation of American savers learned the duration of distant promises inside their retirement plan.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The slow crash is more dangerous than the fast crash">
A −22.6% in one session (1987) is terrifying but legible: the price has fallen, you know where you stand. A 31-month erosion is a perpetual trap: every rebound looks like the turn, every plateau like a floor — and "buying the dip", the winning reflex of the whole 1990s, becomes the ruin machine. An investor who buys after −40% still suffers, from his entry point, a further loss of more than 60% to the final trough. Interview moral: in a deflating bubble, yesterday's price is not information about value — it is a memory. Only discounted cash flows (module 3) provide a floor, and when the P/E starts at 100, that floor is very low.
</Callout>

## Low tide: Enron, WorldCom and the certification of accounts

"It's when the tide goes out that you find out who's been swimming naked" — Buffett's line became the canonical summary of 2001-2002. The decline does not only expose the bad business models: it exposes the frauds that the rise had financed without looking. **Enron**, America's seventh-largest market capitalization, collapses in December 2001: years of debts and losses hidden in off-balance-sheet structures, with the blessing of its auditor Arthur Andersen — which will not survive the scandal. **WorldCom** follows in July 2002: \$3.8bn of expenses dressed up as investment, the largest US bankruptcy of the era. The legislator's response, to know for the regulation bridge (module 1): the **Sarbanes-Oxley** Act of 2002, whose emblematic measure requires executives to **personally certify** the accounts — under threat of criminal penalties. Trust cannot be decreed; accountability can.

<Callout type="important" titre="The bubble was right about the technology — and wrong about the prices">
The nuance that makes the good papers. The dot-com narrative was **true**: the Internet did transform commerce, the press, finance — the optimists of 1999 won on the facts. They lost everything on prices and on selection: **Amazon**, a real business model, saw its stock go from about \$107 to about \$6 — nearly **−95%** — before becoming one of the largest companies in the world; Pets.com vanished in nine months. Two distinct errors, then: overpaying for a genuine revolution is still an investment error (Amazon's −95% required an 18x just to get back to even), and in a stampede, the market finances the future giants and the future corpses **at the same price**. Being right about the technology does not excuse you from being right about the valuation.
</Callout>

## Why \$5 trillion evaporated did not kill the system

Here is the missing ingredient announced in the introduction. The burst destroys on the order of **\$5 trillion** of market capitalization — and yet: no run, no chain of bank failures, a short and shallow US recession in 2001. Compare with 2008, where a *smaller* loss on housing nearly swept away the global financial system. The difference is not the size of the shock, it is **who carried the risk and with whose money**: dot-com losses sat in equity portfolios — pension funds, households, venture capital — held **without bank leverage**. Equity that evaporates means impoverished savers; leveraged assets that collapse mean insolvent bank balance sheets and a payment system in danger. A sectoral bubble only becomes systemic if it is **financed by debt and housed in the banks**: hold on to that theorem, it is the reading grid for the next chapter. And the final irony: to cushion the burst, the Fed cuts rates to **1% by 2003** — cheap money hunting for new yield will find it in housing. The next bubble will be on credit.

<GoFurther suffixe="the four most expensive words in history">
"This time it's different": John Templeton made them the four most expensive words in the history of investing. Every bubble in this module believed itself an exception to the laws of valuation, and each time with a *locally* serious argument: investment trusts were modernizing stock ownership in 1929, the Internet really was changing the world in 1999, securitization really was dispersing risk in 2006. The trap is not that the argument is false — it is that it can be true and **change nothing about the arithmetic**: whatever the technology, an asset is worth its discounted future cash flows, and a required gain of +354.5% after −78% remains a required gain of +354.5%. In an interview, the phrasing that lands: technological revolutions are real; suspensions of arithmetic, never.
</GoFurther>

## The lessons — the interview list

- **A valuation is a promise of discounted cash flows, not a popularity vote.** When the metric of the moment can no longer be converted into cash flows (eyeballs, clicks, unique visitors), it is the metric that has a problem, not the DCF.
- **The market can eliminate those who are right.** Robertson closes at the peak, Greenspan is lucid four years too early: seeing the bubble is not enough, you must survive its duration — the pessimist's counterpart to chapter 3's LTCM lesson.
- **"This time it's different"**: the four most expensive words in history (Templeton) — all the more dangerous because the underlying technological narrative can be perfectly true.
- **The banking channel makes the difference** between a costly bubble and a systemic crisis: the dot-com burned \$5 trillion without taking down a single major bank.

The next chapter tells the exact opposite: a housing loss more modest than the dot-com's \$5 trillion, but financed on credit and housed at the heart of leveraged bank balance sheets — and this time, the whole system wobbles. Subprime: chapter 5.
