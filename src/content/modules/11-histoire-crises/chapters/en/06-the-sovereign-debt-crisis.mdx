2008 ends with a transfer: to keep the Great Depression from starting over, governments guaranteed the banks, recapitalized the balance sheets, stimulated the economies — and absorbed into public debt what the private sector could no longer carry. The crisis did not disappear, it changed carrier. It remained to be seen whether the new carrier was indestructible. The answer arrives from Athens in October 2009, and it occupies Europe for three years: no — a state can shake too, and in an unfinished monetary union, its shaking drags down its banks, which drag it down in return. That loop has a name every candidate must be able to draw on the whiteboard: the *doom loop*.

## October 2009: the number that changes everything

Greece's new government, barely elected, opens the books and revises the 2009 public deficit: not ~6% of GDP as announced, but **12.7%** — the final figure will be 15.4%. The size of the revision matters less than what it reveals: Greek statistics had been false for years. Now, sovereign debt is lent on the faith of public numbers; from the day the numbers lie, nothing can be valued anymore — you recognize BNP Paribas's August 9, 2007, transposed to states: it is not the loss that triggers the panic, it is the discovery that one no longer knows how to *measure*. Statistical trust is a credibility asset on a par with module 10's 2% promise; Greece had just pulverized it.

## The thermometer: the spread against the Bund

A euro area sovereign's fever reads on a single screen: the gap between its 10-year yield and Germany's, the **spread against the Bund**, quoted in basis points — module 4's bond vocabulary, applied to states. The orders of magnitude to know: the French OAT lives its ordinary life between 30 and 60bp. Greece crosses **1,000bp** in the spring of 2010; Italy passes **500bp** in the fall of 2011 — and for a country at 120% debt-to-GDP, 500bp more on refinancing is not a nuance, it is a regime change. At the paroxysm, in March 2012, the Greek 10-year exceeds **35%**: against a Bund at 1.5%, the spread is $(35 - 1.5) \times 100 = 3{,}350$ bp. Remember the reading: at those levels, the yield no longer compensates a risk — it *is* the anticipated default, discounted into the price.

## The arithmetic of unsustainability

Why can a rising rate kill a state? Because the interest burden is the product of two numbers: the debt (as % of GDP) and the average rate paid. At 180% debt-to-GDP — the Greece of 2011 — each point of rate costs 1.8 points of GDP:

$$
\text{burden} = \frac{180 \times 5}{100} = 9\%\ \text{of GDP}
$$

At a 5% average rate, the Greek state would devote 9% of its GDP to debt service alone, before the first euro of schools or hospitals — and the market was demanding seven times that. The loop is then engaged: rates rise, so the burden rises, so solvency deteriorates, so rates rise. One more self-fulfilling prophecy — you have already seen them on banks (chapter 1) and on haircuts (chapter 5). But the euro area adds a story of its own.

<Callout type="definition" titre="The doom loop (the bank-sovereign death spiral)">
The **doom loop** is the mortal embrace between a state and its banks, tied by two links. First link: national banks hold massive amounts of **their own** state's debt (home bias) — if the sovereign debt trades at a discount, their equity melts. Second link: the state is the ultimate guarantor of **its** banks — if they wobble, it is its debt that swells. Each leg drags the other, in both directions. **Ireland** enters through the banking leg: rescuing its banks (~€64bn, about 40% of GDP) ruins a hitherto exemplary state. **Greece** enters through the sovereign leg: the state's default ruins banks stuffed with its debt. Two entry doors, a single room — and you do not get out by saving only one of the two.
</Callout>

## Rescue plans and contagion: 2010-2012

May 2010: first Greek program, **€110bn** lent by the European Union and the IMF, under the watch of a trio quickly nicknamed the **troika** (European Commission, ECB, IMF), in exchange for drastic fiscal adjustment. In its wake the **EFSF** is born, the euro area's temporary rescue fund, which the **ESM** will make permanent in 2012. The ECB, for its part, crosses a Rubicon: the **SMP**, purchases of sovereign debt in the secondary market — limited, sterilized, and so controversial in Germany that they cost the ECB two resounding resignations, Axel Weber and then Jürgen Stark.

Nothing works: contagion follows the list of weak links, each through its own door into the doom loop.

| Country | Date | Program | Entry door |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Greece | May 2010 | €110bn (EU-IMF) | Sovereign: statistics and deficit |
| Ireland | Nov. 2010 | €85bn | Banking: the bank rescue ruins the state |
| Portugal | Apr. 2011 | €78bn | Sovereign: weak growth, chronic deficits |
| Spain | July 2012 | Up to €100bn (banks) | Banking: the domestic housing bubble |

Summer 2011, the market goes after **Italy** and **Spain** — and the game changes in nature, because these two are too big for the EFSF: you can rescue Greece, not the euro area's third-largest economy. December 2011-February 2012, the ECB tries the indirect weapon: two **3-year LTROs**, about €1 trillion lent to the banks at a reduced rate.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The LTRO, a painkiller that worsens the disease">
What do the Spanish and Italian banks do with this 3-year liquidity? They buy… their own state's debt, which yields 5 to 7% when the ECB loan costs about 1%. Immediate effect: sovereign yields ease, everyone breathes. Structural effect: home bias **increases** — the southern banks are even more stuffed with their sovereign's debt than before. The LTRO relieved the symptom while tightening the doom loop itself: that is the trap to state in an interview, because it shows you distinguish liquidity (provided) from solvency (unchanged) — chapter 1's grammar, yet again.
</Callout>

## March 2012: the largest sovereign default in history

The official euphemism is called **PSI** (*Private Sector Involvement*): a "voluntary" bond exchange, under the explicit threat that there would be no plan B. On about €200bn of Greek debt held by the private sector, creditors accept a nominal haircut of **53.5%** — and, accounting for the new coupons and new maturities, a loss in present value of about **75%**. It is the largest sovereign default in history, and it did not happen in an emerging market of the '80s: it happened in a developed country, a member of the euro area. Greek sovereign CDS are triggered — the mechanics will wait for module 5; simply note that the insurance worked. The taboo has fallen: the debt of a euro member state can go unpaid.

## The real price: a Great Depression at the scale of one country

While the spreads flash on the screens, the adjustment is paid in the Greek economy: GDP falls by about **25%** between 2008 and 2013 — a Great Depression in the literal sense, the American order of magnitude of 1929-1933 —, unemployment reaches ~27%, and passes 50% among the young. Austerity demanded in the middle of a recession cost more than planned: the IMF will acknowledge in 2013 that it underestimated the **fiscal multipliers** — cutting 1 of public spending in an economy already in free fall removes distinctly more than 1 of activity, which worsens the very debt-to-GDP ratio the program claimed to repair. A rescue plan can be arithmetically coherent and economically self-defeating.

## Summer 2012: the sentence that put out the fire

July 2012: Spanish yields exceed 7.5%, and the spreads are no longer pricing only default risk — they are pricing **redenomination** risk, the return of the peseta and the lira. On July 26, in London, Mario Draghi delivers the "*whatever it takes*"; in September, the promise becomes a program, the **OMT**: potentially **unlimited** purchases of a state's debt under conditionality. You already know this story — module 10 (chapter 5) told it from the central bank's point of view. What matters here is the crisis's point of view: the OMT was **never used**, not one euro spent, and the spreads melted by several hundred basis points.

<Callout type="important" titre="The credible backstop: the central lesson of 2012">
The OMT is the "whatever it takes" institutionalized, and it illustrates the most counter-intuitive lesson of the module: against a self-fulfilling panic, **the best money is the money you never spend**. The SMP, limited and ambiguous, was tested and overrun; the OMT, unlimited and credible, never had to be used — nobody sells against a buyer without limits. It is module 10's expectations channel (chapters 1 and 3) applied to a sovereign crisis: credibility is an asset that substitutes for ammunition. Interview corollary: judge a backstop by its *announced* size and its credibility, not by its usage.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="De Grauwe: borrowing in a currency you do not issue">
Why did the United Kingdom, with comparable debt and deficits, never suffer these spreads? The economist Paul De Grauwe gave the answer that structures every modern reading of the crisis: a state that borrows **in a currency it does not issue** is in the position of an emerging market indebted in dollars — it can go bankrupt through mere **illiquidity**, if investors refuse to roll its debt on a given date, even if it is solvent in the long run. It is chapter 1's grammar — liquidity versus solvency — at the scale of a state: Greece, Spain, Italy borrowed in a currency (the euro) whose tap belonged to Frankfurt, without a sovereign lender of last resort; London borrows in a currency its own central bank issues, and a self-fulfilling run is impossible there. The crisis thus revealed that the euro was an incomplete construction: a monetary union without a fiscal union, and without a banking union — the latter only starts in 2014, with single supervision of the major banks entrusted to the ECB, precisely to loosen the doom loop. The OMT did not fix the design flaw; it supplied the missing lender of last resort, under conditions.
</GoFurther>

## The lessons for the interview

- **The doom loop**: banks and sovereign hold each other up — saving one without treating the other tightens the loop (the LTRO is its involuntary demonstration).
- **Liquidity versus solvency applies to states too**: without a lender of last resort in its own currency, a solvent sovereign can die of a refusal to refinance.
- **The credible backstop extinguishes panic without spending ammunition**: SMP tested and overrun, OMT never used and decisive.
- **Austerity in a recession has high multipliers**: it can worsen the very ratio it claims to repair (the IMF's mea culpa, 2013).
- **Statistics are a credibility asset**: the Greek revision of October 2009 cost more than years of deficits — a false number destroys the valuability of the entire signature.

2008 unfolded over eighteen months, the euro crisis over three years: *slow* crises, in which you could watch the dominoes fall one by one. The following decade invents something else — the **flash** crisis, at the scale of days, sometimes hours: a virus that shuts down the world economy in March 2020, a British budget that brings pension funds to their knees in 72 hours, a Californian bank drained of a quarter of its deposits in one day of mobile apps. That is chapter 7.
