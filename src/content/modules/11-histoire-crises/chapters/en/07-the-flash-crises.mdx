It took the Dow of 1929 34 months to hit its trough, the S&P 500 of 2008 seventeen months. The crises of the 2020s compress the same journey into **days** — sometimes hours. Three reasons for the acceleration: leverage is now systemic (chapter 1's grammar applies to entire sectors, no longer to isolated speculators); information — and the sell order — travels at smartphone speed; and module 10's firefighter, schooled by 1929 and 2008, intervenes in hours, no longer in years. Three case studies — COVID, gilts, SVB — then the module's closing exhibit: the museum of crashes.

## COVID, March 2020 — the fastest crash in history

On February 19, 2020, the S&P 500 closes at a record: 3,386.15. The virus is already circulating, but the market is pricing a distant flu. Then Italy locks down, and the repricing becomes free fall: on March 23, the index touches 2,237.40 — the module's drawdown, $2\,237.40/3\,386.15 - 1$, gives **−33.9% in 23 sessions**: faster than 1929 and than 1987 to reach −30%. The level-1 circuit breakers (−7%, module 1 bridge), born of Black Monday (chapter 3: this is exactly their reason for being), trip **four times** — on March 9, 12, 16 and 18. On March 16, the VIX closes at **82.69**, a record close above the 2008 peaks (module 8): the price of insurance when everyone wants it at the same time.

<Callout type="definition" titre="The dash for cash">
At the worst of the panic, a phenomenon found in no diversification textbook: **even Treasuries and gold fall**. It is not their fundamentals plunging — it is that everyone is selling whatever is *liquid* to raise cash: bond funds facing redemptions, levered cash-futures basis trades unwinding under margin calls (module 7 bridge), foreigners repatriating dollars. Remember the signature: when the hedge falls **together with** the risky asset, you are no longer in a valuation crisis but in a **liquidity crisis** — chapter 1's sovereign distinction.
</Callout>

The physical plumbing is not spared either: on April 20, 2020, the WTI front month closes at **−\$37.63** a barrel — a *negative* price for oil, because holders of expiring futures have nowhere to store the barrels (module 6: storage, contango). A lesson in one number: in a real crisis, material constraints — full tanks, collateral demanded, cash nowhere to be found — dominate the models.

Facing the spiral, the Fed rolls out in three weeks what had taken eighteen months in 2008: **March 3**, −50bp in an emergency move; **March 15**, a Sunday, −100bp to 0-0.25% plus \$700bn of QE announced; **March 23**, **unlimited** QE and corporate credit facilities — investment grade first, extended to fallen angels — unheard of for the American central bank. The QE mechanics are those of module 10, chapter 5: we will not re-explain them; what matters here is the date.

<Callout type="important" titre="The floor: the Fed's balance sheet">
The market trough — 2,237.40 — is reached **on March 23, the very day** of the unlimited QE announcement. Not the week after, not at the economy's low point (the worst of unemployment is still ahead): the day the unlimited buyer walks into the room. The lesson holds for the whole module: in a liquidity crisis, the floor is not a valuation, it is a **balance sheet** — that of the player who cannot run out of cash.
</Callout>

The S&P 500 regains its peak in **August 2020: five months**, against 25 years for the Dow of 1929. But the medal has a reverse: every successful intervention teaches the market that the Fed will come — the "Fed put", direct heir of Greenspan's 1987 statement (chapter 3), emerges from March 2020 more credible than ever. The ultra-fast firefighter shortens the crises *and* feeds the moral hazard that prepares the next ones.

## Gilts and LDI, September 2022 — the spiral under laboratory conditions

Module 10 (chapter 7) told this episode from the Bank of England's point of view — the conflict between tightening and financial stability. Here, let us reread it with chapter 1's grammar, because it is the purest forced-selling spiral ever observed, leg by leg. **The shock**: on September 23, 2022, Liz Truss's mini-budget — about £45bn of unfunded tax cuts — sends the 30-year gilt up **+130bp in three sessions**. **The margin**: the LDI funds, which hedge pension fund liabilities with *levered* gilts (swaps, repo), receive massive margin calls (module 7, chapter 2 bridge). **The forced sale**: to find the collateral, they sell their most liquid asset — gilts. **The re-marking**: those sales push yields up, which triggers new calls. The loop is closed; it turns on its own.

The irony is total, and it is what makes the episode so precious: rising yields **improve** a pension fund's economic solvency — they deflate the present value of its liabilities, pure duration mechanics (modules 4 and 10). Institutions *enriched* by the shock nearly died of being unable to post Tuesday's margin. Solvent, illiquid, almost dead: if you retain only one example of chapter 1's distinction, make it this one.

On September 28, the BoE — in mid-tightening, on the eve of *selling* gilts under QT — announces purchases that are **explicitly temporary**: thirteen business days, about £19bn used. Firefighter and arsonist at once, the conflict of objectives owned and bounded in time so as not to look like budget financing. And it is enough: the spiral dies out in hours — that is the message of chapter 1's LeverageSpiralSim: the buyer of last resort does not need to buy everything, it only needs to **crush the discount** feeding the loop. Truss resigns after 44 days.

## SVB and Credit Suisse, March 2023 — the run at smartphone speed

### The bank that died in silence

Silicon Valley Bank: deposits more than tripled between 2019 and 2021 — from about \$60bn to about \$190bn — from a concentrated tech clientele, of which **more than 90% above the \$250k insurance cap**, hence uninsured. Those deposits are parked in **long** Treasuries and MBS (duration close to 5.7), bought at the top of the bond market. Then 2022: the Fed hikes +425bp (module 10, chapter 7). The module's arithmetic fits on one line:

$$
\frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -D \times \Delta y = -5.7 \times 2\ \text{points} \approx -11.4\%
$$

Apply −11.4% to about \$140bn of securities: **on the order of −\$16bn of unrealized losses** — roughly the entirety of the equity. The bank was economically dead by the end of 2022; nobody saw it, because the accounting did not say so.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Held-to-maturity: the loss the accounting cannot see">
Securities classified as **held-to-maturity** stay at purchase cost: as long as you do not sell, the unrealized loss does not exist in the accounts. The trap is double. For the bank: the latent loss is very real — it only takes being *forced to sell* for it to materialize, and that is precisely what a run forces you to do. For the analyst: a balance sheet can look presentable while the economic net worth is destroyed. Interest rate risk **is not** credit risk — not a single issuer defaulted in SVB's portfolio, and it cost the equity all the same.
</Callout>

### Forty-eight hours

On March 8, 2023, forced to raise cash, SVB sells \$21bn of securities — realized loss: \$1.8bn — and announces a capital raise. That is the signal: the latent loss becomes public. On March 9, coordinated by Twitter and the venture capital group chats, depositors request **\$42bn of withdrawals in a single day** — about a quarter of the deposits. Run that number through chapter 1's coverage ratio (tauxCouvertureDepots): no bank in the world covers a quarter of its deposits with same-day liquid assets. The run of 1907 took weeks of queues; that of 2023 takes hours, without a single queue. On the morning of March 10, the FDIC closes the bank — the second-largest US bank failure, behind WaMu in 2008. And the uninsured deposits, which have nothing to gain by staying, ran **first**: deposit insurance only anchors what it covers.

On Sunday, March 12, the backstop: a guarantee of **all** SVB deposits, beyond \$250k, under the "systemic risk exception" — and the Fed's **BTFP**, which lends *at par* against collateral trading below par. Reread Bagehot (chapter 1): lend freely against good collateral, yes — but valued generously, eyes closed to the unrealized loss: a bent Bagehot, tailor-made for a duration crisis. Contagion runs anyway: Signature closes on March 12, First Republic agonizes until May 1 before being seized and taken over by JPMorgan.

### Credit Suisse: 167 years, one weekend

Across the Atlantic, the panic finds the weakest bank: Credit Suisse, 167 years of history, worn down by years of scandals — including Archegos, about \$5.5bn of losses on a levered family office (modules 7 and 8 bridges). The run there is the wealthy clients' version: **CHF 110bn of outflows in Q4 2022 alone**, discreet, electronic, with no images of teller windows. On March 19, 2023, the Swiss authorities orchestrate in one weekend the takeover by UBS for CHF 3bn. A detail that became a global affair: the **AT1** bonds — CHF 17bn — are written down *to zero*, while shareholders receive 3 billion.

<Callout type="piege" titre="AT1: read the prospectus, not the theoretical ranking">
"Creditors before shareholders": module 4's hierarchy seems violated — AT1 holders lose everything, shareholders get something. But the prospectuses of the Swiss AT1s explicitly provided for total write-down at the regulator's discretion, independently of the fate of the shares. The interview lesson: the effective subordination ranking is **contractual**, not theoretical — read the documentation. AT1s are Basel III instruments, designed to absorb losses *before* the taxpayer: they worked as written, not as perceived (module 12, to come).
</Callout>

March 2023 in three lessons: **duration kills banks too** — interest rate risk is not credit risk; a modern run travels at **social media speed** — 48 hours where 1907 took weeks; and **uninsured deposits run first** — client concentration is a risk datum on a par with the balance sheet.

Every crisis in the module left an institutional scar — that is how the system learns, one catastrophe at a time:

| Crisis | Dominant mechanism | Institutional scar |
|---|---|---|
| 1907 | Bank panic without a lender of last resort | Creation of the Fed (1913) |
| 1929 | Leverage 10:1, chain runs, passive central bank | FDIC, SEC, Glass-Steagall |
| 1987 | Mechanical selling by portfolio insurance | Circuit breakers; the volatility smile (module 8) |
| LTCM 1998 | Extreme repo leverage on convergence trades | Monitoring of fund leverage |
| 2000 | Valuations without earnings | Sarbanes-Oxley |
| 2008 | Run on repo, opaque securitization, contagion | Dodd-Frank, Basel III, EMIR |
| 2010-2012 | Bank-sovereign doom loop | ESM, banking union, OMT |
| 2020-2023 | Speed: dash for cash, LDI spiral, mobile run | Permanent backstops — and open questions |

<GoFurther suffixe="moral hazard: the bill for successful rescues">
Follow the thread: Greenspan's 1987 statement ("the Fed stands ready to provide liquidity") becomes the "Fed put" of the 1990s-2000s, which becomes the unlimited QE of March 23, 2020, which becomes the guarantee of *all* deposits on March 12, 2023. Each rescue was, taken alone, the right decision — and each successful rescue teaches the market to carry more leverage, since the extreme loss is insured by the firefighter. Add the questions 2020-2023 leaves open — central bank balance sheets multiplied by about 9 since 2007, public debts at their peak, a backstop now *expected* — and you hold the unresolved question of the decade: has the system become safer, or merely insured by an ever more loaded insurer?
</GoFurther>
