This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the essentials chapter by chapter, the formula sheet, the applied grammar crisis by crisis, the traps and the reflexes. To reread the day before an oral — and to complete with the jury mode of the practice section. The guiding thread fits in one sentence: under very different sets — call loans, futures, CDOs, gilts —, every crisis conjugates the same verbs: a **bubble** (Minsky), **leverage** that amplifies it ($\Delta E\,\% = \lambda \times \Delta \text{assets}\,\%$), a **liquidity/solvency** confusion that triggers it, a **fire-sale spiral** that propagates it, **contagion** that generalises it, a **firefighter** who contains it (or not), and an **institutional scar** that buries it — until stability manufactures instability again. What changes from one crisis to the next is never the grammar, only the vocabulary.

## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **The grammar of crises (ch. 1).** The Minsky-Kindleberger cycle in five phases: displacement (a real innovation), credit-fuelled boom, euphoria (*this time is different*), insiders taking profits, panic — then revulsion, which lasts a generation. The financial instability hypothesis in four words: **stability breeds instability** — finance slides from hedge to speculative to Ponzi with no madness required. Leverage is the universal accelerator: $\Delta E\,\% = \lambda \times \Delta \text{assets}\,\%$, distance to death $-100/\lambda$ — leverage 10 (1929): dead at −10%; leverage 27 (LTCM): −3.7%; leverage 1: never through prices. Illiquid is not insolvent, but both die — hence Bagehot: lend freely, at a penalty rate, against good collateral (1907: J.P. Morgan does it freehand; 1913: the Fed institutionalises it). The deleveraging spiral: $S = (A - \lambda E)/(1 - \lambda d)$, and the **death zone** $\lambda d \ge 1$ where selling no longer deleverages. And the asymmetry of losses: after $-p\ \%$, you need $100/(100-p) - 1$ — **−50% requires +100%**, −89% requires +809%.
2. **1929 (ch. 2).** A real displacement (electricity, the automobile, radio — RCA ×10 in four years), a euphoria theory (the "new era", Fisher's "permanently high plateau" eight days before Black Thursday), and leverage 10 for everyone through **call loans** (\$8.5bn outstanding, call money above 15%). The crash: peak at **381.17** (3 Sept.), Black Thursday 24 October (12.9m shares, ticker four hours late), **−12.8%** on Monday 28th, **−11.7%** on Tuesday 29th — each day exceeded leverage 10's fatal shock. Then the trap: a **+48% false rebound** to April 1930, before the trough at **41.22** (8 July 1932): **−89.2%**, 25 years to come back (Nov. 1954). The real catastrophe is not the crash: ~9,000 banks dead with no FDIC, money supply −1/3 (Friedman-Schwartz: the "Great Contraction", the Fed passive then perverse — it RAISES rates in 1931 for gold), deflation of about 10% a year and Fisher's **debt-deflation** (the more debtors repay, the more they owe), Smoot-Hawley (world trade −2/3). The toll: GDP −30%, unemployment 25%. The 1933 scars: FDIC, Glass-Steagall, the SEC, leaving the gold standard.
3. **1987 and LTCM (ch. 3).** Two crises of **models**. 1987: portfolio insurance (a synthetic put through delta-hedging, \$60-90bn) sells mechanically into the fall — the **fallacy of composition** manufactures the insured scenario: **−22.6%** on 19 October (peak-to-trough: **−36.1%**). The next morning, one Greenspan sentence — Bagehot in one line — and no second act: peak regained within two years. Scars: circuit breakers, and the **volatility smile** (m8) — born on 19 October 1987. LTCM: \$125bn of assets on 4.7 of capital (**leverage 27**, dead at −3.7%), near-zero repo haircuts (2% ⇒ max leverage 50), convergence trades. The Russian default (17 August 1998) triggers the flight to quality: all spreads widen together — a hundred positions, one single bet (that calm continues), correlations towards 1. **−44%** in August, leverage beyond 250 by late September. The 23 September rescue: a **private** consortium of fourteen banks, \$3.625bn, **zero public money** — the Fed lent its meeting room.
4. **The dot-com (ch. 4).** Netscape (August 1995) opens the *eyeballs* era; Greenspan diagnoses "irrational exuberance" in December 1996 — and the Nasdaq **triples again**: being lucid too early ruins you (Robertson closes Tiger in March 2000, the month of the peak; short sellers die of margin calls). 1999: 486 IPOs, 117 first-day doublings; January 2000: AOL buys Time Warner (\$165bn). The peak: **5,048.62** on 10 March 2000, index P/E above 100 — Gordon (m3) says what that price promised: the collectively impossible. The fall: **−77.9%** over **31 months** of erosion, down to 1,114.11 — not one day of crash, but 20% rebounds that ruin dip buyers; +354.5% required, fifteen years to come back (April 2015). Low tide reveals Enron and WorldCom → Sarbanes-Oxley. And THE theorem: **\$5tn** destroyed with no systemic crisis, because held in equities **without bank leverage** — a bubble only becomes systemic when financed by debt and lodged in banks. The irony: the Fed at 1% in 2003 prepares the next one.
5. **2008 (ch. 5).** The enigma: ~**\$500bn** of subprime losses nearly kill a \$60tn system — ten times less than the dot-com. The chain: cheap money + global savings hunting AAA; the assumption that kills ("house prices have never fallen nationally"); **originate-to-distribute** (no one is exposed, no one checks); Russian-doll securitisation (MBS → CDO → CDO², subordination remanufactures AAA while multiplying correlation sensitivity — m9's worst-of, a thousand times bigger); agencies paid by the issuer; AIG sells ~\$500bn of CDS with no reserves. The gunpowder: **shadow banking** — SIVs, 30-day ABCP, overnight repo. Gorton's run: the haircut goes from 2% to 25%, funding melts from 98 to 75, the spiral engages — **a run on haircuts**, without a single queue. Lehman at leverage ~31: dead at −3.2%. The timeline: BNP 9 August 2007, Northern Rock, Bear Stearns (March 2008), **Lehman 15 September 2008** (\$613bn), AIG rescued on the 16th (\$85bn for 79.9% — interconnection, not size), the Reserve Primary breaks the buck, TARP rejected (S&P −8.8%) then passed (\$700bn), QE1. The trough: 676.53 on 9 March 2009, **−56.8%**. Scars: Dodd-Frank, Basel III, EMIR, stress tests.
6. **Sovereign debt (ch. 6).** October 2009: Greece revises its deficit from ~6% to 12.7% (final: 15.4%) — the statistics were lying, nothing can be valued anymore (the sovereigns' 9 August 2007). The thermometer: the spread against the Bund — OAT 30-60 bps, Greece above 1,000 bps (2010), Italy above 500 bps (2011), and March 2012: Greek 10-year above 35%, i.e. **3,350 bps** — the rate IS the anticipated default. The arithmetic: burden = debt/GDP × rate — at 180% and 5%, **9% of GDP** before the first euro of spending; the loop rates → burden → solvency → rates self-fulfils. The **doom loop**: banks stuffed with their own sovereign, the sovereign guaranteeing its banks — Ireland enters through the banking leg (~€64bn, 40% of GDP), Greece through the sovereign one; the LTRO (~€1tn) relieves the rates while TIGHTENING the loop. **PSI**, March 2012: a 53.5% haircut, ~75% in present value — the biggest sovereign default in history. The price: Greek GDP −25%, unemployment 27% (the IMF confesses its multipliers in 2013). The exit: "whatever it takes" (26 July 2012) then the **OMT** — unlimited, conditional, **never used**: spreads melt without one euro. De Grauwe: a State borrowing in a currency it does not issue is an emerging market in foreign currency — it can die of illiquidity; the banking union (2014) starts cutting the loop.
7. **The flash crises (ch. 7).** COVID: **−33.9% in 23 sessions** (3,386.15 → 2,237.40), VIX at 82.69, four circuit breakers, WTI at **−\$37.63** — and the **dash for cash**: even Treasuries and gold fall, the signature of a liquidity crisis. The Fed replays 2008 in three weeks: unlimited QE on 23 March — **the trough is that very day**: the floor is not a valuation, it is a balance sheet. Peak regained in five months — and the Fed put comes out stronger. Gilts/LDI (September 2022): the Truss mini-budget, +130 bps in three sessions on the 30-year, margin calls on the LDI funds, gilt sales, rates pushed higher — the spiral in laboratory conditions, on funds the rate rise was actually **enriching** (solvent, illiquid, nearly dead); the BoE breaks the loop in thirteen business days and ~£19bn, in the middle of QT. SVB (March 2023): deposits ×3 invested in long securities (duration 5.7), +400 bps ⇒ $-5.7 \times 2 \approx -11.4\%$ on ~\$140bn ≈ **−\$16bn** ≈ the equity, masked by **held-to-maturity** accounting; the forced sale of 8 March (\$1.8bn loss booked), **\$42bn of withdrawals on the 9th** — over 90% of deposits uninsured, and they run first; the Sunday backstop: full guarantee + **BTFP** (loans at par). Credit Suisse: 167 years, one weekend — and the **AT1s (CHF 17bn) wiped out before the shareholders**: the effective rank is contractual.

## The module's formula sheet

| Formula | When to use it |
| --- | --- |
| $\lambda = \dfrac{\text{assets}}{\text{equity}}$ | The first question in any crisis: who carries leverage, how much — 10 (1929), 27 (LTCM), ~31 (Lehman), 1 (cash, which never dies through prices) |
| $\Delta E\,\% = \lambda \times \Delta \text{assets}\,\%$ | The misfortune formula: at leverage 25, −4% on assets makes −100% of equity — and +2% makes +50%: the asymmetry that manufactures the boom's "geniuses" |
| $\Delta \text{assets}_{\text{fatal}} = -\dfrac{100}{\lambda}$ | The distance to death: −10% at leverage 10 (each black day of 1929 exceeded it), −3.7% for LTCM, −3.2% for Lehman |
| $\left(\dfrac{\text{trough}}{\text{peak}} - 1\right) \times 100$ | The drawdown, the crash's identity card: −89.2% (1929-32), −36.1% (1987), −77.9% (2000-02), −56.8% (2007-09), −33.9% (2020) |
| $\dfrac{100}{100 - p} - 1$ | The required gain, convex: −50% requires +100%, −89% requires +809%, −22.6% "only" +29.2% — always compute it before talking about a rebound |
| $n = \dfrac{\ln(1/(1-p))}{\ln(1+g)}$ | The recovery years: −50% at 7%/year = 10.2 years; −89% = 32.6 years — the order of magnitude of the Dow's 25 years; the firefighter makes the difference (2020: 5 months) |
| $\text{funding} = V(1 - h)$ | Repo: 100 of securities at a 2% haircut raises 98; at 25%, 75 — the missing 23, to be found the same day, are the 2008 run (Gorton) |
| $\lambda_{\max} = \dfrac{100}{h}$ | The leverage a haircut allows: 2% ⇒ 50 — the leverage invisible to regulatory ratios, and LTCM's funding condition |
| $\dfrac{\text{cash need}}{1 - d}$ | The forced sale: raising 23 under a 5% discount forces selling 24.2 — the spiral's engine (1987, 2008, 2022) |
| $S = \dfrac{A - \lambda E}{1 - \lambda d}$ | The sale size to deleverage: at target leverage 25 and a 2% discount, you must sell DOUBLE the apparent excess — and if $\lambda d \ge 1$, death zone: selling no longer deleverages, only rescue or bankruptcy remain |
| $\dfrac{\text{liquid assets}}{\text{deposits}} \times 100$ | Deposit coverage: no bank covers a quarter of its deposits in one day — SVB, \$42bn on 9 March 2023 |
| $\text{burden} = \dfrac{\text{debt/GDP} \times \text{rate}}{100}$ | The sovereign arithmetic: 180% × 5% = 9% of GDP — the same debt at 1.5% costs 2.7%: a debt is only sustainable AT A GIVEN RATE (hence the OMT) |
| $(\text{rate} - \text{rate}_{\text{ref}}) \times 100$ | The spread in bps against the Bund: OAT 30-60, Italy 2011 above 500, Greece 2012: (35 − 1.5) × 100 = 3,350 bps — the rate that IS the anticipated default |

## The traps that cost points

1. **Confusing −22.6% and −36.1%** (ch. 3): Black Monday is the worst *session* (−22.6%); the 1987 peak-to-trough drawdown is −36.1% — nearly two thirds in one day, not the whole of it.
2. **"+50% repairs −50%"** (ch. 1): the required gain is $100/(100-p) - 1$, convex — after −50%, +100%; after −89%, +809%; naive symmetry leaves you at −25% of the account.
3. **"The Fed bailed out LTCM"** (ch. 3): a **private** consortium of fourteen banks, \$3.625bn — the Fed lent its meeting room and its authority, not a dollar.
4. **"The OMT bought massively"** (ch. 6): the OMT was **never used** — not one euro spent, hundreds of bps of relief: a credible backstop is judged by its announced size, not by its use.
5. **"Creditors always rank ahead of shareholders"** (ch. 7): Credit Suisse's AT1s (CHF 17bn) were wiped out while shareholders received 3bn — the effective rank is **contractual**: read the prospectus.
6. **"The balance sheet is sound, so the bank is fine"** (ch. 7): held-to-maturity keeps securities at cost — SVB was economically dead by late 2022, ~\$16bn of unrealised losses ≈ the equity, invisible in the accounts.
7. **The false rebound** (ch. 2): +48% between November 1929 and April 1930, before the bulk of the −89% — violent rebounds are the rule of great bear markets, not an end signal.
8. **"The bigger the loss, the worse the crisis"** (ch. 4, 5): \$5tn of dot-com losses with no systemic crisis, ~\$500bn of subprime losses and a near-collapse of the world system — everything depends on where the losses sit and who funds them (leverage, short-term funding, banks).
9. **"The 1929 crash caused the depression"** (ch. 2): the Great Contraction did — 9,000 banks, money supply −1/3, a passive Fed; 1987, nearly twice as bad in one day, produced nothing.
10. **"Selling always deleverages"** (ch. 1): if $\lambda d \ge 1$, each euro sold destroys more equity than it reduces leverage — the death zone, where only rescue or bankruptcy remain.
11. **"Rate risk is a form of credit risk"** (ch. 7): not one default in SVB's portfolio — only Treasuries and agency MBS — and the equity destroyed anyway: duration kills without a default.
12. **"Rising rates ruined the British pension funds"** (ch. 7): the rise improved their solvency (discounted liabilities deflate) — it is **Tuesday's margin** that nearly killed them: solvent, illiquid, nearly dead.

## The twelve reflexes for the oral

1. Facing any bubble, the first question: **who carries leverage, and how much?** Without debt, disappointed savers (dot-com); on credit inside the banks, bankruptcies (1929, 2008).
2. Minsky in four words: **stability breeds instability** — the calm years are the mechanism, not the proof of safety.
3. Three numbers linked in your head: leverage $\lambda$, fatal shock $-100/\lambda$, and the gain required after the loss — always convert a drawdown into a required gain before talking about a rebound.
4. Illiquid is not insolvent — **but both die** if no one lends: the forced sale at a discount turns the illiquid into the insolvent. Hence Bagehot: freely, penalty rate, good collateral.
5. Never judge a crash by its market violence, always by its **transmission to credit**: 1987 (−22.6%, zero recession) versus 1929 (−12.8% and a lost decade).
6. The spiral is broken through the **discount**, not by buying the whole market: one sentence (1987), one meeting room (1998), thirteen business days (2022).
7. Systemic risk is measured in **links, not billions**: Lehman (one counterparty among others) falls, AIG (\$500bn of CDS sold to every bank) is rescued the next day.
8. The modern run has no counter: repo haircuts in 2008, a mobile app in 2023 — \$42bn in one day, and **uninsured deposits run first**.
9. A structured AAA is not a corporate AAA: subordination protects against isolated defaults, never against a common shock — correlation rises towards 1 exactly when you count on it (2008 CDOs, LTCM 1998, m9's worst-of).
10. For a sovereign: a debt is only sustainable **at a given rate** (burden = debt/GDP × rate), and a State borrowing in a currency it does not issue can die of illiquidity (De Grauwe) — the credible backstop extinguishes without firing (OMT).
11. Being lucid too early ruins you: Greenspan 1996 (the Nasdaq then triples), Robertson closes at the peak — "the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent".
12. Every successful rescue teaches the market to carry more leverage: from the Greenspan statement to the Fed put, to unlimited QE, to the guarantee of all deposits — **moral hazard** is the firefighter's deferred bill.

## Bridges to the rest of the course

- **Module 4 — rates and duration.** Duration is not just a pricer's sensitivity: it is SVB's murder weapon — $\Delta P/P \approx -D \times \Delta y$, i.e. $-5.7 \times 2 \approx -11.4\%$ on ~\$140bn: the equity. Rate risk is measured by duration, materialises through liquidity.
- **Module 7 — futures and margin calls.** Margin is the mechanical trigger of 1987 (S&P futures sold by portfolio insurance), of 1929 (automatic liquidation of call loans) and of 2022 (LDI funds killed by Tuesday's collateral): the margin call turns the unrealised loss into a forced sale.
- **Module 8 — options and volatility.** The smile is no surface nicety: it was **born on 19 October 1987** — ever since, every option surface permanently prices the possibility of the crash; and portfolio insurance was m8's delta-hedging played by \$90bn at the same time.
- **Module 9 — structured products and correlation.** The 2008 CDO is m9's worst-of, three orders of magnitude bigger: subordination manufactures AAA as long as correlation stays low — and correlation jumps towards 1 in the exact scenario where you were counting on it.
- **Module 10 — the firefighter.** Every crisis exit in this module is m10 applied: Bagehot (1907, 1987, 2008), QE (2008, 2020), the OMT (2012), the BTFP (2023) — and the 2022 gilts conflict, where financial stability and tightening contradicted each other on a Tuesday morning. Credibility is an asset spent in crises.

Each one-line answer is an entry point, not a complete answer: train yourself to develop them over two minutes in jury mode, and to put numbers on them with the module's exercises. You now know that a crisis is measured neither by the size of the loss nor by the violence of the session, but by the leverage carrying it, the funding fleeing it and the firefighter containing it.
