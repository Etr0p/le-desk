The *Rates & Bonds* module seated you on the creditor's side: contractual cash flows, a maturity, a promise. Change chairs. The shareholder lends nothing: the shareholder owns. A share is a fraction of a company's capital, and everything that drives its value — and its risk — flows from that simple status of owner. This chapter lays out the vocabulary, the rights attached to the security, how to read a quote line and the market's orders of magnitude; the chapters that follow will deal with the big question: how much it is worth.

## What you actually own

<Callout type="definition" titre="Share">
A share is a security of ownership representing a fraction of a company's share capital. It confers three essential rights: a right to the profits, a voting right at the general meeting and a right to the net assets in the event of liquidation. Unlike a bond, it promises neither income nor repayment: it has no maturity.
</Callout>

The first right is the most misunderstood. The shareholder is entitled **to the profits**, not just to the dividend. Each year, the general meeting decides how earnings are allocated: part is distributed (the dividend), the rest is put into reserves. But retained earnings are not lost to the shareholder: they stay inside the company, increase its equity, finance its growth — and therefore the value of each share. A company that pays no dividend at all can perfectly well enrich its shareholders: it reinvests on their behalf. The ratio of dividend to earnings has a name, the **payout ratio**: a 45% payout means that 45 cents of every euro of earnings is distributed, the rest being reinvested.

The second right is political: **one share, one vote** at the general meeting — in principle; we will see the exceptions. Shareholders elect the board of directors, approve the accounts, vote on the structuring resolutions (mergers, capital increases). Holding 50% of the votes plus one means controlling the company.

The third right is exercised only at the company's death: the **liquidation surplus**, that is, whatever remains of the assets once the creditors have been repaid and the capital contributions returned. You have already met this queue in module 4: senior secured debt, senior debt, subordinated debt, hybrids… and the shareholders **last**. Bottom of the hierarchy: in a bankruptcy, the shareholder most often recovers nothing. That is the price of ownership — the shareholder absorbs all the uncertainty, in both directions.

## No promise, no maturity: the price of risk

Compare the two securities methodically — it is a classic interview question:

| | Bond | Share |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nature | Debt security | Ownership security |
| Income | Contractual coupon, known in advance | Dividend decided each year, never guaranteed |
| Horizon | Maturity fixed in the contract | No maturity: the life of the company |
| In bankruptcy | Creditor, paid according to rank | Paid last — most often nothing |
| Upside | Capped (coupons + principal) | Unlimited on the upside, loss capped at the stake |

The first two lines say it all: the bondholder holds a promise, the shareholder an expectation. For a rational investor to agree to swap certain cash flows for flows that are uncertain, perpetual and last in line, they demand a **higher expected return**. That extra required return is the **equity risk premium**: over long periods, equities in the major developed markets have returned on the order of 4 to 6 points more per year than risk-free investments — a historical order of magnitude, variable across periods and measurement methods, to be quoted with caution. Four to six points a year, compounded over decades, produce considerable differences in wealth: it is the pay of the one who agrees to be paid last. Module 12 will give this intuition its theoretical form (the CAPM); chapter 2 will use it right away as a discount rate.

## The gallery of varieties

The "one share, one vote, one dividend" share is the general case, but reality is more inventive.

- **The ordinary share**: the standard security described so far — voting right, ordinary dividend, last rank.
- **The preference share** (*preferred stock*): a priority dividend — often higher or cumulative — generally in exchange for giving up the voting right. Halfway between share and bond: yield traded for power.
- **The double voting right**: in France, the Florange Act (2014 — to gloss: a law that makes the double vote *automatic* for any share held in registered form for at least two years, unless the bylaws opt out) rewards long-term shareholding. Consequence: in many French listed companies, a historic shareholder carries far more weight in votes than its slice of the capital.
- **A/B/C share classes**: the American version of the same decoupling. At Alphabet (Google) or Meta, the founders hold a class of shares with multiple voting rights (10 votes per share at Meta) while the public holds the ordinary class — or even, at Alphabet, a class C with no voting rights at all. A minority of the capital there controls a majority of the votes.
- **ADRs and GDRs** (*American / Global Depositary Receipts*): certificates issued by a depositary bank, representing foreign shares it keeps in custody, and listed in New York or London. They let you buy TotalEnergies in dollars on the NYSE without touching the Paris-listed share. Three lines are enough: it is a wrapper, not a new instrument.

## Reading a quote line

Here is the screen line of a fictional but realistic stock, Valmer SA, an industrial equipment maker listed in Paris. Know how to dissect every field — it is a frequent interview exercise.

> Valmer SA — €120.00 · −0.85% · vol. 180,000 · mkt cap €6.0bn · EPS €8.00 · P/E 15.0 · yld 3.0%

| Field | Value | How to read it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Price | €120.00 | The price of one share at the last trade |
| Change | −0.85% | Versus the previous day's close |
| Volume | 180,000 shares | I.e. ≈ €21.6m traded today at the current price |
| Shares outstanding | 50,000,000 | All the shares issued, free-floating or not |
| Market capitalization | €6.0bn | Price × number of shares: €120 × 50m |
| Free float | 65% | The share of the capital actually in circulation: €3.9bn |
| EPS | €8.00 | Earnings per share: net income / number of shares |
| P/E | 15.0 | Price / EPS: you pay 15 times annual earnings |
| Dividend | €3.60 | I.e. a payout of 45% of EPS |
| Yield | 3.0% | Dividend / price: 3.60 / 120 |

Two reading traps. First, **market capitalization** is computed on the *totality* of the shares issued, including the locked-up blocks (founding family, the State, treasury shares); but only the **free float** (the share of the capital actually tradable) changes hands day to day. Valmer "weighs" €6bn, but there are only €3.9bn of shares available in the market — and it is in fact on the free float that the major indices weight their constituents, as you will see in the chapter devoted to indices. Second, the P/E and the yield can be deduced from each other: yield = payout / P/E (check: 45% / 15 = 3%). A quote line is a coherent system, not a list of independent numbers.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Market cap ≠ enterprise value: the debt is missing">
Market capitalization measures the value of the *equity*, not of the enterprise. Buying 100% of Valmer's shares costs €6bn — but the buyer also takes over its debt. If Valmer carries €2bn of net debt, the deal economically "costs" €8bn: that is the **enterprise value** (EV = market cap + net debt). Comparing market caps across unequally indebted companies makes no sense — the entire EV/EBITDA toolkit of chapter 3 starts from there. Interview reflex: at the words "market cap", always ask yourself where the debt went.
</Callout>

## The big numbers of the equity market

Finally, let us put module 1's orders of magnitude back in place. Global market capitalization is on the order of **\$130tn** — a *stock*, never to be compared with a daily volume. The United States alone accounts for about half of it, and concentration at the top is unprecedented: in early 2026, several companies — essentially the American technology giants — each exceed **\$3tn** of market capitalization, more than France's GDP. By comparison, Europe's largest capitalizations count in the hundreds of billions, and the whole of the CAC 40 weighs on the order of €2,500bn. Remember the hierarchy more than the exact figures, which move every month: United States ≈ half the world, a handful of mega-caps at the top, Europe far behind in market weight.

<GoFurther suffixe="preference shares and dual-class structures: the governance debate">
The principle "one share = one vote" is the golden rule of shareholder democracy: whoever bears the economic risk should hold the corresponding power. Dual-class structures (A/B classes, multiple votes) break it deliberately: they protect the founders' long view from the short-termist pressure of markets — Silicon Valley's argument — but they create near-irremovable executives, holding control with a fraction of the capital and of the risk. The large institutional investors and the proxy advisory firms are mostly hostile to them; some index providers for a time restricted the entry of new dual-class structures. The Florange Act belongs to the same debate: a reward for loyalty to some, a premium to incumbent control for others. There is no canonical answer — in an interview, know how to lay out both camps.
</GoFurther>

You now know what a share is and what you are buying when you pay €120 for one. The real question remains: why €120 rather than 80 or 200? Chapter 2 answers it with the tool you already master — discounting cash flows — applied to flows that promise nothing.
