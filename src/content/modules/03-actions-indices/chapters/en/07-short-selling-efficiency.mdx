This whole module has reasoned in a single direction: buy what is undervalued. But what do you do with an *over*valued stock you do not own? Answer: sell it anyway. Short selling is the most counter-intuitive, the most hated and — this is the thesis of this chapter — one of the most useful operations in the equity market. Understanding it will lead you naturally to the big question left hanging since Module 1, Chapter 1: to what extent do prices really reflect information? That is the efficiency debate, and it ends with a lesson in humility that cuts both ways.

## The mechanics: borrow, sell, buy back, return

Selling a security you do not own first requires getting hold of it. That is the role of **securities lending** — the repo of equities, to reuse the plumbing of Module 4, Chapter 7: the short seller borrows the shares from a long-term investor (an index fund, an insurer — their inventories lie idle, might as well monetise them), against collateral and a borrowing fee. He sells the shares into the market, waits for the hoped-for fall, buys them back, and returns them to the lender. Four steps: borrow, sell, buy back, return.

The borrowing cost runs for the whole life of the position, on the Actual/360 money market convention — the same basis as repo. Let us run through the reference example: short sale of 1,000 shares at €100, bought back 90 days later at €90, borrowing rate of 2% per annum. The gross gain first:

$$
\text{P\&L}_{\text{gross}} = (100 - 90) \times 1{,}000 = \text{€}10{,}000
$$

Then the borrowing fees, computed on the notional sold (€100 × 1,000 shares = €100,000):

$$
\text{fees} = 100{,}000 \times 2\,\% \times \frac{90}{360} = \text{€}500
\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad
\text{P\&L}_{\text{net}} = 10{,}000 - 500 = \text{€}9{,}500
$$

Note that the fees are due no matter what: had the stock climbed back to €110, the gross loss of €10,000 would have become €10,500 net. The 2% rate of the example is that of a large, liquid capitalisation; for a stock everyone wants to sell — *hard to borrow* — the fee soars to tens of percent annualised. You recognise the phenomenon: it is the exact equivalent of a bond trading "special" in the repo market — scarcity in the lending market has a price, and that price is a signal.

## An asymmetric risk profile

The buyer of a share risks their stake, nothing more: from €100 to zero, the loss stops at 100%. The short seller lives in the inverse world: their gain is capped — at best, the stock goes to zero and they pocket the sale price — but their loss is **unlimited**, since no ceiling bounds a share's rise.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Capped gain, unlimited loss">
Short at €100: at best +€100 per share (the issuer goes bankrupt), but if the stock rises to €300, the loss is €200 — double the maximum gain. And unlike the buyer, the short seller does not always choose their horizon: the lender can *recall* the shares at any moment, forcing a buy-back at the worst time; and margin calls on a losing position force you to post cash every day or to cut. This asymmetry is what makes squeezes possible — and it is what the panel expects in any question on shorting.
</Callout>

The asymmetry turns explosive when too many sellers pile into the same stock. January 2021, GameStop, the market side of the story this time: the *short interest* — the outstanding stock of short positions — exceeded the free float, in the order of 140% according to the data of the time. More than 100% of a float sold short is nothing impossible: a share borrowed and sold can be re-borrowed from its new holder and sold again — the chain counts double. When buyers coordinated on social media push the price up, the spiral kicks in: the rise inflicts losses on the sellers; margin calls force some of them to buy back; those buy-backs push the price higher, which worsens the others' losses — the *short squeeze*, literally the wringing-out of the sellers. The stock goes from around \$20 to nearly \$500 intraday; the most exposed fund, Melvin Capital, loses several billion and will shut down the following year. As for the halt on purchases at several brokers at the peak of the fever — the part of the story everyone tells wrong — the plumbing that blocked the buying is dissected in Module 1, Chapter 7: clearing house margin calls, not a conspiracy.

## What short sellers are for

Three services, to be unrolled calmly before a panel tempted by caricature. **Liquidity** first: market makers can only quote on the offer side without holding every stock because shorting lets them deliver — without it, no more continuous quoting. **Price discovery** next: without short selling, only the optimists vote — those who judge a stock overvalued and do not own it have no way to weigh on the price, which leans structurally upward. Shorting brings the pessimists into price formation.

**Fraud hunting**, finally — the most spectacular service. Activist short sellers investigate, publish, and bet their capital on their conclusions: they are the only market players financially incentivised to discover what is wrong. The textbook case is Wirecard, the German payments champion: from 2015, short sellers and the journalists of the *Financial Times* documented accounting inconsistencies. The reaction of the German regulator, BaFin, in 2019: temporarily ban short selling in the stock and file a complaint… against the journalists. June 2020: Wirecard admits that €1.9bn of cash does not exist, and collapses within days. The shorts were right, and the market that had believed them would have lost less than the one that silenced them.

The regulatory debate remains. In panics — financial ones in 2008, a broad swathe of European markets in March 2020 — regulators temporarily ban short selling, on the grounds that it feeds the downward spiral. Academic studies of these episodes converge instead on an embarrassing finding: degraded liquidity, wider spreads, with no clear price-support effect. The effectiveness of these bans remains debated — the prudent phrasing that suits the oral.

## Market efficiency: settling Module 1's promise

Module 1 posited that prices aggregate information. The efficient market hypothesis — formalised by Eugene Fama in the 1960s — sharpens the claim into three nested forms. The **weak** form: prices incorporate all the information contained in past prices — charts predict nothing exploitable. The **semi-strong** form: prices incorporate all *public* information — accounts, announcements, the press; analysing documents anyone can read provides no systematic edge, because the information is in the price moments after publication. The **strong** form: prices incorporate even private information — that one is rejected by just about everyone, starting with the courts: if insider trading were not profitable, there would be no need to ban it.

The supporting evidence is massive, and the most telling piece fits in one number: the chronic difficulty of beating the indices. According to the SPIVA scorecards, which have compared active funds with their benchmark index for twenty years, in the order of 80 to 90% of equity managers do worse than their index over ten years, depending on categories and periods — and past outperformance hardly persists. If public information left banknotes on the floor, thousands of over-equipped professionals would pick them up; that they fail to, on average and after fees, is exactly what the semi-strong form predicts.

And yet, documented **anomalies** exist. *Momentum*: what has risen over six to twelve months continues, on average, to outperform — directly contrary to the weak form. *Value*: stocks trading at a discount to their fundamentals have historically outperformed — rational risk premium or inefficiency, the debate has been running for forty years. **Small caps**: a documented historical outperformance, grown markedly more fragile since its publication — to the point of illustrating another law of the genre: a published anomaly erodes.

Hence the pragmatic synthesis that will serve you at the oral: markets are **efficient most of the time, not all of the time**. Efficient enough that beating the index is exceptionally difficult; not enough to stop the bubbles of Module 1 — tulips, 1929, the internet — from recurring century after century (their full anatomy awaits you in Module 11). Both facts are true at the same time, and the credible candidate holds both.

## What this implies — for you, and for the oral

First implication: the rise of **passive management**. If alpha is rare, uncertain and expensive, replicating the index at minimal cost becomes the default choice — the reasoning that has carried index funds and ETFs to the heart of modern asset management, and that Module 12 will detail.

Second implication, subtler: why do inefficiencies, once spotted, not vanish instantly? Because arbitrage has physical **limits**: it takes capital, borrowable shares, and the capacity to hold the position while the anomaly worsens before it corrects — the arbitrageur can be stopped out by margin calls before being proven right. The line that sums it all up, attributed to Keynes (the attribution is uncertain, the wisdom is not): "the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent". The box below gives its most chemically pure illustration in market history.

<GoFurther suffixe="the limits of arbitrage: 3Com/Palm, March 2000">
March 2000, the peak of the internet bubble. 3Com floats 5% of its subsidiary Palm (the fashionable personal digital assistants) and announces that the remainder — about 1.5 Palm shares per 3Com share — will be distributed to its shareholders within the year. The arithmetic is limpid: one 3Com share should be worth at least 1.5 times the Palm price, plus the value of the rest of 3Com. Yet by the close of the first day, Palm trades so high that the 95% held by 3Com is worth more than the whole of 3Com: the market assigns the rest of the company — profitable businesses and cash included — a value of about −\$60 per share, according to the study by Lamont and Thaler that has remained a classic. A negative value for a company full of cash: the most flagrant inefficiency ever quoted. The arbitrage was blindingly obvious — buy 3Com, short Palm — and yet impracticable: the few floating Palm shares were nearly impossible to borrow, at prohibitive cost, and the gap took months to close. The lesson closes this chapter: efficiency does not descend from heaven, it is manufactured by arbitrageurs who need the securities lending plumbing to do their work — when the plumbing is missing, the absurd can stay quoted for months.
</GoFurther>

The tour of the equity market is complete: the value of a share, its multiples, its indices, its life as a listed company, its birth on the stock market — and, to finish, the mechanism that disciplines all prices, and its limits. You now hold both ends of the rope: enough respect for the market not to believe in miracle recipes, enough lucidity to know that it derails — and why.
