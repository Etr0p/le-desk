Chapter 5 followed the life of the listed share; this one tells the story of its birth. The initial public offering (IPO) is the moment a private company sells its shares to the public for the first time — the archetype of the primary market from Module 1, and one of investment banking's most profitable operations. Behind the folklore — the bell, the founders posing for photos, the first-day "pop" — lies precise machinery, riddled with crossed incentives you must know how to take apart: who sets the price, who pockets the discount, and why an apparent gift almost never is one.

## Why go public — and why hesitate

Four motives push a company toward a listing. **Raising capital**: an IPO allows new shares to be issued and growth to be funded on a scale private capital does not always reach. **Providing an exit**: founders, employee shareholders and venture capital funds hold illiquid wealth; a listing makes it sellable — often the real motivation behind the deal. **Acquiring a deal currency**: a listed, liquid share serves to pay for targets without spending cash. **Gaining visibility and credibility**: with customers, with banks, and with the talent paid in shares that can now be valued.

On the other side, the bill is steep. The **direct fees** first: the banking syndicate's commission represents in the order of 3 to 7% of the amount raised — the top of the range remaining remarkably stable in the United States for mid-sized deals — not counting lawyers, auditors and communication. **Transparency** next: a prospectus, published and audited accounts, continuous disclosure to the market under the regulator's eye — every competitor now reads your margins. **Short-termism**, finally: the dictatorship of the quarterly number and of a share price scrutinised around the clock. These costs explain a structural trend: companies stay private longer — the number of listed companies in the United States has roughly halved since the 1990s peak, private equity now offering funding once reserved for the stock market.

## The process: from the banks to the final price

An IPO unfolds in a well-rehearsed sequence of steps, over several months.

| Step | Who | What is at stake |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Mandate | The issuer picks its banks | The lead managers (*bookrunners*) of the syndicate — the sell-side of Module 1, Chapter 2 |
| Due diligence and prospectus | Banks, lawyers, auditors | The offering document approved by the AMF (Module 1, Chapter 6) — the legal basis of the offer |
| Roadshow | Management and syndicate | One to two weeks of presentations to institutional investors, city after city |
| Bookbuilding | The syndicate | Building the order book over an indicative price range |
| Pricing | Issuer and syndicate | The final price, set in light of demand — often the evening before listing |
| Allocation and listing | The syndicate | Who gets served, and how much; then the first traded price |

The heart of the machinery is **bookbuilding**: the banks collect investors' buying intentions — quantity, price sensitivity — and build a demand curve. You already know this mechanism: it is exactly the bond syndication of Module 4, Chapter 2, applied to another asset. The difference is uncertainty: a new bond is priced off an existing reference curve; an IPO values a company that has never had a market price — hence the indicative range, tightened then adjusted depending on whether the book overflows or struggles to fill.

## IPO underpricing: the first-day "pop"

The most famous stylised fact in the finance literature: on average, the first traded price clearly exceeds the offer price. Over long periods, this first-day "pop" runs in the order of 10 to 20% depending on markets and eras — with spectacular excesses, such as the 1999-2000 internet bubble, when it temporarily exceeded 50% on average in the United States.

First panel question: who pays for this gift? The issuer and the selling shareholders. Every euro of first-day rise is a euro they could have pocketed by selling higher — the Anglo-Saxons speak of *money left on the table*. For a €1bn IPO that "pops" 15%, that is €150m handed to the investors allocated at the offering.

Second question, finer: why has this underpricing persisted for decades, across all markets? Three explanations stack up. **Information asymmetry**: the best-informed investors only subscribe to the good deals; for the others to stay in the game despite that disadvantage, an average discount must compensate them. **Insurance against failure**: an IPO that breaks its offer price on the very first day durably dents confidence — better to start a little low than to botch the entrance. **The banks' incentives**, finally: underpricing reduces their placement risk and pleases their recurring investor clients, who will remember the winning allocation — whereas the issuer, for its part, only does an IPO once. The debate over the respective weight of these three drivers is not settled; what is certain is that they all pull in the same direction.

<Callout type="piege" titre="An underpriced IPO is not a gift to you">
If the average pop exists, why not subscribe to every IPO? Because you do not receive the average: allocations are rationed on the good deals — too much demand — and generous on the bad ones. If you are served in full, beware: it is probably because the informed investors did not want it. That is the *winner's curse*, already met in the auctions of Module 4, Chapter 2: winning the auction is bad news when the others knew something you did not. Corrected for this allocation bias, the "subscribe to everything" strategy loses most of its appeal.
</Callout>

## Lock-up and stabilisation: the guardrails of the aftermath

Two mechanisms frame the first months of trading. The **lock-up** first: executives and historical shareholders commit not to sell for a period of 90 to 180 days. The aim is to prevent the market, with the IPO barely digested, from seeing insiders' shares come flooding in. The lock-up expiry is a date the whole market has in its calendar: a massive potential supply becomes free at a stroke, and the price is frequently under pressure as the deadline approaches.

**Stabilisation** next, and its cleverest mechanism: the **overallotment option**, known as the *greenshoe* (after the Green Shoe Manufacturing Company, the first to use it in 1963). The principle in four lines: the syndicate places 115% of the planned shares, ending up short 15%; if the price weakens, it buys those 15% back in the market — supporting the price with its purchases — and closes its short at a profit; if the price rises, it exercises the option granted to it by the issuer and buys the missing 15% at the IPO price. In both cases the bank is covered — the box at the end of the chapter walks through the numbers. It is one of the rare cases of organised, perfectly legal price support, because it is framed by regulation and announced in the prospectus.

## The alternative routes

The classic IPO is no longer the only way in. The **direct listing** admits the existing shares to trading without a new issue, without a placement syndicate or bookbuilding: no funds raised, but no IPO underpricing and no placement fees — the route chosen by Spotify in 2018, then Slack in 2019, well-known companies that did not need fresh money.

The **SPAC** (*special purpose acquisition company*) reverses the order of operations: an empty shell raises funds on the stock market, then looks for a private company to absorb — which ends up listed through the merger, without going through the classic IPO process. The formula enjoyed a spectacular boom in 2020-2021 — more than 600 vehicles raised in the United States in 2021 alone — followed by a collapse in volumes and, on average, disappointing returns for subscribers, which has brought the instrument back to a niche role.

<GoFurther suffixe="the greenshoe, walked through in numbers">
An IPO of 10 million shares at €20. The syndicate allocates 11.5 million — a 15% overallotment — and finds itself short 1.5 million shares, holding an option from the issuer to buy 1.5 million shares at €20, exercisable for 30 days.

**Scenario 1 — the stock weakens, €19 in the first week.** The syndicate buys back 1.5 million shares in the market: that demand cushions the fall — the intended stabilising effect. Its short is closed: sold at €20, bought back around €19, the syndicate makes in the order of €1.5m along the way. The option expires unexercised; only 10 million shares remain outstanding.

**Scenario 2 — the stock rises, €24.** Buying back in the market would cost €24 against a sale at €20: out of the question. The syndicate exercises the greenshoe: the issuer creates 1.5 million additional shares at €20, which cover the short exactly. The issuer raises 15% more than planned, 11.5 million shares are outstanding, and the bank has lost nothing.

The genius of the structure: the bank holds a short that protects it on the downside and an option that covers it on the upside — it cannot lose, and that is deliberate: it is paid that way to stabilise the market risk-free, at the issuer's (measured) expense. At the oral, being able to walk through these two scenarios instantly sets you apart.
</GoFurther>

The share is born, its price has a life of its own. It remains to meet the most hated and most misunderstood character in the equity market — the one who wins when everyone loses, whom regulators ban in a panic and whom fraudsters fear more than auditors: the short seller. That is the subject of the last substantive chapter of this module, which will seize the occasion to settle an old promise — market efficiency.
