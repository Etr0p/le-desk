Chapters 2 and 3 taught you to value a share. But the most-heard sentence on the markets never mentions one share: "the CAC 40 lost 1.2%". The stock market index is the most quoted financial object in the world — and one of the least well understood. Behind the number on the evening news lie very concrete construction choices, which determine what the index measures, what the saver who replicates it actually holds, and reading mistakes that are expensive in interviews.

## The three lives of an index

An index lives three lives at once:

- **Thermometer.** Summarizing in a single number the prices of dozens or hundreds of companies: its historical function, the one of the TV news and of the historians of bubbles and crashes.
- **Yardstick** (*benchmark*). All portfolio management is judged against a reference index: a European equities manager who gains 6% when the index gains 9 has destroyed relative value. Beating the index — or settling for replicating it at low cost — is the front line of the entire asset management industry, the great debate awaiting you in module 12.
- **Underlying for products.** An index cannot be bought in itself: it is a calculation formula, not a security. But an entire industry has been built to make it investable — the ETFs that replicate it (module 12), the futures that let you gain exposure or hedge in a single trade (module 7), the options written on it (module 8). Trillions of euros mechanically follow these formulas: an index's calculation rule has become a force that moves markets.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Stock market index">
An index is a basket of securities defined by public rules — eligibility universe, selection criteria, weighting rule, review calendar — and summarized by a level: the value of the basket divided by a **divisor** that ensures the continuity of the number through time. Everything is in the rules: for an identical basket, changing the weighting rule changes the index — and the performance.
</Callout>

## Three ways to weight the same basket

The central construction question: what weight should each company get? Three answers coexist.

**Weight by prices.** Each stock's weight is its price divided by the sum of the prices. It is the oldest method — that of Charles Dow who, in 1896, added up by hand the prices of twelve companies and divided by twelve to publish his "industrial average". The Dow Jones still lives that way: a methodological fossil maintained by the force of media habit, where a company weighs according to the face level of its share price — which says strictly nothing about its size.

**Weight by float-adjusted capitalization.** Each stock's weight is its float-adjusted market cap — price × number of shares × free float — over the basket total. It is the method of the CAC 40, the S&P 500 and almost all modern indices: the weight reflects the market size actually tradable.

**Equal weighting.** Each stock weighs 1/n, by decision, and the basket is rebalanced periodically. Simple, but costly in transactions — and very different in behavior, as we will see further down.

**The free float**: why exclude part of the capital? Because the stable blocks — State holdings, founding families, treasury shares, cross-shareholdings — do not trade. Including them would produce weights unrelated to what index flows can actually buy: an ETF that must replicate the index buys free float, not shares locked up in a family pact. In our toy basket, IndusGroupe has a €60bn capitalization but only 55% floats: €33bn counts for the index. The emblematic real-world case is Hermès, whose founding family controls about two thirds of the capital: its weight in the CAC 40 is far below what its total capitalization would suggest.

**The divisor**, finally, turns a capitalization into an index level:

$$
\text{Index} = \frac{\sum_i \; p_i \times n_i \times f_i}{D}
$$

where $p_i$, $n_i$ and $f_i$ are each constituent's price, number of shares and free float. At creation, $D$ sets the base — the CAC 40 is worth 1,000 points on 31 December 1987, by convention. Thereafter, the divisor absorbs everything that is not a price move: a stock entering or leaving, a capital increase, a delisting. The rule is always the same:

$$
D' = D \times \frac{\text{float-adjusted cap after the event}}{\text{float-adjusted cap before the event}}
$$

so that the level does not jump at the moment of the change. This is the **continuity principle**: the index must move only when prices move, never because its composition changes. A split, for its part, requires no adjustment in a cap-weighted index — the price is halved, the share count doubled, the capitalization unchanged — but it devastates a price-weighted index: the box at the end of the chapter walks through the full mechanics.

## The major indices to know

| Index | Universe | Weighting | Particulars |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Dow Jones | 30 large US stocks | Price | The fossil of 1896; weights unrelated to company size |
| S&P 500 | ≈ 500 large US capitalizations | Float-adjusted cap | The global benchmark; selection by committee, about 80% of US market capitalization |
| Nasdaq-100 | The 100 largest non-financial Nasdaq stocks | Capped float-adjusted cap | Very concentrated in technology |
| CAC 40 | 40 stocks on Euronext Paris | Float-adjusted cap, weight capped at 15% | **Price** index: dividends not reinvested (see CAC 40 GR); base 1,000 at end-1987 |
| DAX 40 | 40 large German stocks | Float-adjusted cap | **Total return** index by default: dividends reinvested — the notable exception |
| Euro Stoxx 50 | 50 large euro-area capitalizations | Float-adjusted cap | Europe's most traded derivatives underlying |
| MSCI World | ≈ 1,400 stocks, 23 developed markets | Float-adjusted cap | Targets ≈ 85% of each developed market's float-adjusted cap; "World" without the emerging markets, United States around 70% of the weight |
| MSCI Emerging Markets | ≈ 24 emerging markets | Float-adjusted cap | China, India, Taiwan, Korea in the lead; combined with the World, gives the MSCI ACWI |

Two readings of this table are worth the effort. First the MSCI World: its name promises the world, it covers only the developed markets — and the United States weighs on the order of 70% of it; a "globally diversified" saver is first and foremost exposed to Wall Street. Then the DAX: almost alone among the major indices, it reinvests dividends by default. That detail sets up the next trap.

## Price index, total return index

The CAC 40 the press quotes is a **price index**: when a company detaches its dividend, its price mechanically drops by the detached amount — the next chapter details this — and the index takes the fall without ever crediting the dividend. Its **total return** version, the CAC 40 GR (*gross return*, gross dividends reinvested), reinvests every dividend into the index — it also exists in an NR version (*net return*, dividends net of withholding tax), the contractual benchmark of most ETFs.

The long-run gap is spectacular. The two indices start from the same base — 1,000 points at end-1987 — and, in early 2026, the CAC 40 price index hovers around 8,000 points while the CAC 40 GR is above 25,000: more than three times higher. The order of magnitude can be grasped in your head: an average dividend yield of about 3% a year, compounded over nearly forty years, multiplies the stake by more than three all by itself. Remember the careful phrasing: over long periods, reinvested dividends account, depending on markets and eras, for on the order of half of the total return on equities — the half the evening-news index never shows.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Comparing a price index with a total return index">
"For thirty years, the DAX has crushed the CAC 40": the argument comes up constantly, and it is structurally biased — the DAX reinvests dividends, the CAC does not. With about 3% of annual yield reinvested on one side and nothing on the other, the gap compounds into a massive mechanical advantage that says nothing about the companies themselves. The honest comparison is price against price, or total return against total return (CAC 40 GR against DAX). Same vigilance everywhere else: a backtest, a marketing brochure or a press chart that compares indices without specifying price or TR (*total return*) should immediately arouse suspicion — it is the most profitable beginner's mistake to catch in an interview.
</Callout>

## Concentration, or what "diversified" means

One last construction lesson: cap-weighting concentrates. In early 2026, the ten largest capitalizations in the S&P 500 weigh on the order of 35 to 40% of the index — against about 20% in the mid-2010s — a concentration driven by the technology mega-caps and the artificial intelligence theme. Direct consequence for the saver: an S&P 500 tracker "holds 500 companies", but more than a third of its portfolio sits in ten lines heavily exposed to the same factor.

This is the loop back to module 2: diversification reduces specific risk provided the positions are numerous **and** weakly correlated — the number of lines is not enough. Yet cap-weighting mechanically overweights what has already risen: the more a stock performs, the bigger its weight grows, the more the index depends on it. Index designers know this and correct at the margin — a 15% cap per stock in the CAC 40, periodic capping of the Nasdaq-100, equal-weighted versions of the S&P 500 — but the core remains: **no weighting is neutral**. Cap-weighting means following the momentum of the weights; equal weighting means rebalancing by selling the winners; price weighting means measuring nothing at all. Choosing an index is already choosing a strategy.

<GoFurther suffixe="the divisor through a split, full example">
A price-weighted index, three stocks: A trades at €100, B at €50, C at €30. Initial divisor $D = 3$ — the level is $(100 + 50 + 30)/3 = 60$ points. A announces a 2:1 split: its price becomes $100/2 = \text{€}50$, the share count doubles, no shareholder gains or loses a cent.

Without adjustment, the sum of the prices would drop from 180 to 130 and the index to $130/3 = 43.33$ points: a displayed fall of nearly 28% without the slightest economic event. Continuity is therefore imposed by adjusting the divisor:

$$
D' = D \times \frac{\text{sum after}}{\text{sum before}} = 3 \times \frac{130}{180} = 2.1\overline{6}
$$

Check: $130 / 2.1667 = 60$ points — the level does not move. But look at the weights: A weighed $100/180 = 55.6\%$ before the split; it weighs only $50/130 = 38.5\%$ after. An economic non-event has redistributed the index's weights — that is the structural vice of price weighting, and the reason why the Dow's divisor, patched operation after operation for more than a century, is today far below 1. In a cap-weighted index, none of this: A's capitalization is unchanged (price halved, share count doubled), the weights do not move, no adjustment is required. The divisor there is touched only for entries and exits and capital operations — same continuity formula, applied to the float-adjusted cap.
</GoFurther>
