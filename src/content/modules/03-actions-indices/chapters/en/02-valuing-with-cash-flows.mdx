How much is a share worth? You already know the answer. Module 4 hammered it home for bonds: *an asset is worth the sum of its discounted future cash flows*. Module 2 gave you the tool for never-ending flows: the perpetuity. This chapter contains no new idea тАФ it simply changes the cash flows. In place of contractual coupons and a principal repaid on a fixed date: expected dividends, uncertain, with no maturity. The whole difficulty of equity valuation lies in that shift, and it is a difficulty of assumptions, not of mathematics.

## One principle: cash flows, discounted

For the shareholder, the cash flows are the dividends тАФ and, on a sale, the exit price, which is itself nothing but the value of the subsequent dividends in the buyer's eyes. Step by step, everything comes back to dividends:

$$
V_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{E[D_t]}{(1+r)^t}
$$

Three differences from module 4's bond pricing, all in the symbols. The $E[\cdot]$: dividends are expectations, not promises. The infinity: no maturity, the sum never stops тАФ module 2's perpetuity is back. And the rate $r$: it is the return required by shareholders, risk-free *plus* risk premium (chapter 1), hence markedly higher than a bond yield. The models that follow тАФ the DDM (*dividend discount model*) then the DCF (*discounted cash flows*) тАФ are merely ways of making that infinite sum computable.

## The Gordon growth model: the perpetuity that grows

The simplest assumption that makes the sum computable: a dividend growing at a constant rate $g$, forever. The sum then closes тАФ it is the growing perpetuity, derived at the end of the chapter with the same geometric series as module 2's:

$$
V_0 = \frac{D_1}{r - g}, \qquad r > g
$$

with $D_1$ the dividend expected *one year from now* тАФ not the last one paid. The condition $r > g$ is not decorative: if growth caught up with the discount rate, the sum would diverge and the value would be infinite.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The canonical example: тВм5, 8%, 3%">
A company will pay a тВм5 dividend next year ($D_1 = 5$), expected perpetual growth of 3%, required return of 8%:

$$
V_0 = \frac{5}{0.08 - 0.03} = \frac{5}{0.05} = \text{тВм}100
$$

How to read it: you pay 20 times the dividend тАФ the "dividend multiple" $1/(r-g)$. And if $g = 0$, Gordon collapses back to exactly module 2's simple perpetuity: $5/0.08 = \text{тВм}62.50$. The 3% perpetual growth is "worth" тВм37.50 all by itself.
</Callout>

That leaves the question of where the two parameters come from. The rate $r$ is the return required by shareholders: risk-free rate plus risk premium, which module 12 will teach you to calibrate security by security with the CAPM. Growth $g$, for its part, is not a wish: a company can only grow durably at the pace of what it reinvests. Hence the **sustainable growth** formula:

$$
g = ROE \times b
$$

where $ROE$ (*return on equity*) measures what each reinvested euro earns, and $b$ is the **retention ratio** тАФ the share of earnings put into reserves, i.e. $1 - \text{payout}$. The logic loops back to chapter 1: undistributed earnings swell equity, which produces earnings at the rate $ROE$, so earnings тАФ and the dividend тАФ grow at $ROE \times b$. Example: ROE of 12%, payout of 60%, hence $b = 40\%$ and $g = 12\% \times 0.40 = 4.8\%$. A $g$ above what the ROE-payout pair can sustain should put you on alert.

## The two-stage DDM: THE interview exercise format

For a company whose growth is strong but temporary, you slice time: an explicit phase in which each dividend is projected, then a terminal phase where Gordon takes over at a calmer pace. It is the most frequent interview exercise format тАФ always run it in the same order.

Typical problem: last dividend paid $D_0 = \text{тВм}2$, growth of 10% for 3 years, then 2% forever, required return 8%.

**Step 1 тАФ project the explicit dividends**: $D_1 = 2.20$; $D_2 = 2.42$; $D_3 = 2.662$.

**Step 2 тАФ terminal value with Gordon at year 3**, on the *first dividend of the stable phase* $D_4 = D_3 \times 1.02 = 2.71524$:

$$
TV_3 = \frac{D_4}{r - g_2} = \frac{2.71524}{0.08 - 0.02} = \text{тВм}45.254
$$

**Step 3 тАФ discount everything back to today**:

| Cash flow | Amount | Factor $1/1.08^t$ | Present value |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $D_1$ (year 1) | тВм2.20 | 0.9259 | тВм2.0370 |
| $D_2$ (year 2) | тВм2.42 | 0.8573 | тВм2.0748 |
| $D_3$ (year 3) | тВм2.662 | 0.7938 | тВм2.1132 |
| $TV_3$ (year 3) | тВм45.254 | 0.7938 | тВм35.9241 |
| **Total** | | | **тВм42.15** |

The three explicit dividends contribute тВм6.22; the terminal value, тВм35.92 тАФ that is, **85% of the total value**. Remember that imbalance, it returns throughout what follows. And note the two classic mistakes that cost you the exercise: forgetting to discount the terminal value (it is expressed in year-3 euros and must be divided by $1.08^3$), and building the TV on $D_3$ instead of $D_4$ тАФ Gordon always applies to the cash flow *following* the valuation date.

## The DCF: the same machine, firm side

Many companies pay little or no dividend. You then discount the cash flows the company *generates*, whether it distributes them or not: the **FCF** (*free cash flows*, the cash available after investments). The recipe is identical to the two-stage DDM: an explicit horizon of 3 to 10 years where each flow is projected, a terminal value beyond it, the whole lot discounted. The rate changes name: it is the **WACC** (*weighted average cost of capital*) тАФ the average of the cost of equity and the cost of debt, weighted by their weights on the balance sheet, since the FCF accrues to both providers of capital at once. Its detailed construction will wait for module 12.

Let us run it: FCF of 100, 110 and 121 over three years, a WACC of 10%, a terminal value of 1,500 at year 3.

| Cash flow | Amount | Factor $1/1.10^t$ | Present value |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| FCF year 1 | 100 | 0.9091 | 90.91 |
| FCF year 2 | 110 | 0.8264 | 90.91 |
| FCF year 3 | 121 | 0.7513 | 90.91 |
| TV (year 3) | 1,500 | 0.7513 | 1,126.97 |
| **Total** | | | **1,399.70** |

A wink in passing: cash flows growing at 10% discounted at 10% all have the same present value тАФ growth exactly offsets discounting. The result, 1,399.70, is an **enterprise value**: to get to the value of the shares, subtract net debt тАФ chapter 1's trap looping back. And look at the weight of the blocks: the terminal value accounts for 1,126.97 out of 1,399.70, i.e. **80% of the total**.

<Callout type="important" titre="The DCF is an assumption machine">
In a real DCF, the terminal value commonly weighs **60 to 80%** of total value. Yet it boils down to two unverifiable parameters тАФ a perpetual growth rate and a discount rate тАФ to which the value reacts explosively, as in Gordon. A DCF is therefore never "the" value: it is a transformation of assumptions into a price. Its output is worth what its inputs are worth, and the professional presentation is always a sensitivity matrix crossing WACC and terminal growth, never a single number.
</Callout>

## Strengths, limits тАФ and when to switch tools

To the credit of the DCF and the DDMs: they force you to make *all* the assumptions explicit тАФ growth, margins, investment, risk тАФ and to make them debatable one by one. Their real virtue is less producing a number than **disciplining the reasoning**: a disagreement about the valuation becomes a localized disagreement about one precise assumption.

On the debit side: the explosive sensitivity to the parameters, the false comfort of decimal precision, and the practical inapplicability to companies with no predictable positive cash flows тАФ cash-burning start-ups, cyclicals at the bottom of the cycle. In those cases, and whenever a quick answer anchored in market prices is needed, you switch to valuation **by multiples** тАФ P/E, EV/EBITDA тАФ the subject of chapter 3. The two approaches are not opposed: the DCF says what the asset *should* be worth under your assumptions, multiples what the market pays *today* for its peers тАФ a professional produces both.

<GoFurther suffixe="the Gordon derivation">
Same geometric series as module 2's annuity, different common ratio. With a dividend growing at $g$, the year-$t$ cash flow is $D_1(1+g)^{t-1}$, so:

$$
V_0 = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{D_1 (1+g)^{t-1}}{(1+r)^t} = \frac{D_1}{1+r} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k \quad \text{with} \quad q = \frac{1+g}{1+r}
$$

If $r > g$, then $q < 1$ and the series converges to $1/(1-q)$. Now:

$$
1 - q = \frac{(1+r)-(1+g)}{1+r} = \frac{r-g}{1+r}
$$

Putting it back together: $V_0 = \frac{D_1}{1+r} \times \frac{1+r}{r-g} = \frac{D_1}{r-g}$. Everything is there: the condition $r > g$ is the series' convergence condition, and $g = 0$ gives back the simple perpetuity $D_1/r$ тАФ Gordon is nothing but the infinite tail of the geometric series, growing version.
</GoFurther>

You now know how to turn cash-flow assumptions into value тАФ and how to measure the fragility of the exercise. Chapter 3 takes the opposite road: start from the prices the market pays for comparable companies, and deduce a value without projecting a single cash flow.
