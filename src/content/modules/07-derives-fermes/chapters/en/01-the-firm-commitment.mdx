A derivative is a contract whose value *derives* from an underlying asset — a stock, an index, an interest rate, a barrel of oil. You have already handled some without calling them that: the FX forward of module 6, chapter 2, with which the exporter locked in his six-month rate, and the commodity futures of chapters 5 and 6 — WTI and its 1,000 barrels, the roll, contango. This module finally takes them as its subject: three chapters on the machinery (the commitment, the margins, the price), then rates — FRAs and swaps, the biggest market in the world. Before the machinery, the logic: what exactly is a *firm* commitment, and why do contracts that create no net wealth move notionals in the hundreds of trillions?

## An obligation, not a right

<Callout type="definition" titre="Firm commitment (forward commitment)">
A forward commitment is a contract that **binds both parties**: one undertakes to buy, the other to sell an underlying (or to settle its value) at a future date, at a price fixed today. Neither can walk away, whatever the market does in the meantime. That is the whole difference with the **option**, which gives its holder a *right* without obligation — a right paid for with a premium, and which will wait until module 8. The firm commitment, for its part, costs nothing to sign: its initial value is zero, precisely because the forward price is calibrated to make it so.
</Callout>

The vocabulary of positions is that of the whole course: being **long** (the forward buyer) means gaining if the underlying rises; being **short** (the forward seller) means gaining if it falls. And the payoff profile is utterly simple — **linear and symmetric**. At maturity, if $F_0$ is the agreed price and $S_T$ the underlying's price:

$$
\text{Long's gain} = (S_T - F_0) \times \text{size}
\qquad
\text{Short's gain} = -(S_T - F_0) \times \text{size}
$$

Linear: every point of upside earns as much as the one before, every point of downside costs as much — no floor, no cap, no curvature. Symmetric: the long's gain is, sign aside, exactly the short's loss. No money is created, none is destroyed: every firm derivative is a **zero-sum game**.

<Callout type="important" titre="Zero-sum — so where does the usefulness come from?">
One side's P&L is exactly the other's loss: aggregated over both parties, a derivative produces nothing. What is it for, then? For what any insurance produces: a **transfer of risk**. The module 6 exporter was not trying to make money on his forward — he wanted to stop carrying the currency risk, and someone else, better equipped or better paid for it, took it on instead. Risk never disappears: it changes hands, and the derivatives market is where that move is negotiated. This is the expected answer in an interview when someone throws the "zero-sum casino" line at you.
</Callout>

## What it is for: the three faces, and two more services

Take out module 1's map of players: the three profiles map onto it line for line.

- **The hedger** has an exposure he is stuck with — dollar revenues, a jet-fuel tank to fill, an equity portfolio — and uses the derivative to neutralize it. He agrees to give up the possible gain in order to eliminate the possible loss.
- **The speculator** takes the position the hedger is shedding. He has no prior exposure: he buys risk because he has a view — and his presence is what guarantees that a hedger finds a counterparty at any hour.
- **The arbitrageur** bets on nothing: he watches for gaps between the derivative and its underlying and closes them — you saw him at work on covered parity in module 6, you will see him again in chapter 3 with *cash and carry*. He is the one who welds the derivative's price to the spot price.

To this trio add two collective services. **Price discovery** first: futures quote continuously, in a public order book, assets the cash market displays only lazily — the world price of oil "is" the Brent and WTI futures, and the ten-year cost of money in Europe is read off the Bund. Often, the derivative is not the reflection of the price: it is its source. **Access** second: in a single contract, exposure to an entire index, on the way up or the way down — selling a futures short requires no stock borrowing — for a reduced initial outlay. That reduced outlay has a name: leverage. It deserves its own section, because it is what kills.

## Forward or futures: two outfits for the same commitment

The firm commitment comes in two institutional guises, and the distinction is exactly the module 1, chapter 1 split between over-the-counter and organized markets.

| | **Forward** | **Futures** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Trading | *OTC*, bilateral, with a bank | organized exchange, central order book |
| Contract | bespoke: amount, date, quality all free | standardized: size, expiries, tick imposed |
| Counterparty | the other party — their default is your problem | the clearing house (CCP) steps in between |
| Margins | not systematic, collateral negotiated | mandatory, recalculated every day |
| Liquidity | exit by renegotiating with your counterparty | position reversible at any moment in the book |
| Settlement | one single settlement at maturity, delivery frequent | daily *mark-to-market*, delivery rare |

Economically, it is the same animal: a price fixed today for an exchange tomorrow. Institutionally, everything differs: the forward hugs the client's exact need — the module 6 exporter wanted precisely \$10m on March 15, no standard contract delivers that — while the futures sacrifices the bespoke to gain liquidity and safety. Chapter 2 will strip down the futures machinery; remember here that the two columns answer the same question with two opposite trade-offs.

## Leverage: the double-edged sword

You do not pay for a futures: you guarantee it. To carry a contract with €50,000 of notional, you only post a **margin** — say 10%, or €5,000. You therefore control ten times your stake, and every move in the underlying is magnified by as much on your deposit:

$$
\text{change in stake} = \frac{\text{change in underlying}}{\text{margin}} = \frac{+2\%}{10\%} = +20\%
$$

A 2% rise in the index earns €1,000 on €5,000 committed: +20% in one session, where the cash shareholder makes 2%. The appeal is obvious. Now run the same arithmetic the other way, on a crash day at −12%:

$$
\frac{-12\%}{10\%} = -120\%
$$

Minus one hundred and twenty percent of the stake: the €5,000 are wiped out, and €1,000 is still missing — which you owe. The loss has gone straight through your deposit and kept running.

<Callout type="piege" titre="You can lose more than your stake">
Buy a share and your maximum loss is 100%: the share can fall to zero, not below. A firm commitment knows no such floor: you are committed on the **entire notional**, not on your deposit. At 10% margin, a 10% adverse move is enough to erase the stake — and every point beyond it is paid in fresh money. That is the difference between *investing* a sum and *committing* your signature: leverage does not create return, it stretches both tails of module 2's distribution. Anyone who presents leverage to you as a gain amplifier without mentioning ruin beyond the margin is selling you half a product.
</Callout>

## The big picture: an ocean of notionals — mostly rates

What is the scale of all this? The BIS statistics are dizzying: over-the-counter derivatives represent **several hundred trillion dollars of notional** — several times world GDP — on top of which come the organized markets (CME, Eurex, ICE). And the hierarchy always surprises: it is neither equities nor FX that dominate, but **interest-rate derivatives** — on the order of three quarters of the total — with swaps in the lead: every bank, every insurer, every treasurer runs a balance sheet exposed to rates, and module 4 showed you why. Then comes FX, and, far behind, equities, credit and commodities.

<Callout type="important" titre="Notional measures scale, not potential loss">
"Hundreds of trillions of derivatives: a bomb the size of ten world GDPs" — the classic misreading. The **notional** is the base on which the flows are computed, not an amount at stake: on a €100m interest rate swap, the €100m never changes hands, only the interest differentials derived from it. The market value of these contracts — what would actually be lost if everything were unwound — is a small fraction of the notional, on the order of a few percent. Notional states the scale of the activity; risk is measured elsewhere: in net exposure, in margins, in market value. Same reflex as in module 6 on FX volumes: never compare a scary number to a GDP without checking what the figure measures.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="Dojima and Chicago: older than listed stocks">
The forward commitment is probably the oldest financial instrument in the world: Mesopotamian tablets already record grain deliveries at an agreed price — millennia before the first listed share. The first organized futures *market* was born in Japan: at **Dojima**, near Osaka, samurai paid in rice and merchants were trading standardized rice tickets from the early eighteenth century, with expiries, clearing and settlement of differences — an almost complete futures market, recognized by the shogunate in 1730. The West followed a century later: in **Chicago**, the crossroads of American grain, the Chicago Board of Trade (1848) standardized the qualities, quantities and dates of "to arrive" contracts to end the chaos of bilateral haggling between farmers and millers — silos full in the autumn, prices collapsing, then scarcity in the spring. The modern futures contract — standardization plus a clearing house — was born in rice and wheat, for harvest problems. Finance merely borrowed the tool.
</GoFurther>

You have the logic: a bilateral, zero-sum commitment that moves risk around, in two outfits — the bespoke forward, the standardized futures. It remains to pop the hood on the second: how can strangers promise each other millions six months out without ever meeting or trusting one another? The answer takes two words — clearing and margins — and it is chapter 2.
