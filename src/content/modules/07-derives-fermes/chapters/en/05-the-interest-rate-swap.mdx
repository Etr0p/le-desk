The FRA met earlier in this module fixes a rate over **one** future period. Extend the idea: fix a rate not over one period but over ten consecutive periods, in a single contract. You have just invented the **interest rate swap** (IRS) — economically, a chain of FRAs laid end to end and, in practice, the most heavily traded rates instrument in the world. This chapter takes it apart completely: the mechanics, the price, the value, the market.

## An exchange of flows, never of principal

<Callout type="definition" titre="Interest rate swap (IRS)">
A contract by which two parties exchange, at regular dates and for an agreed term, interest flows computed on the same reference amount — the **notional**: one pays a **fixed** rate set at signature, the other a **floating** rate observed period after period. The notional is **never exchanged**: it serves only to compute the interest. No principal moves, neither at the start nor at the end.
</Callout>

The contract is described in two **legs**. The **fixed leg** pays, say once a year, the same coupon: notional × fixed rate. The **floating leg** pays a rate observed on a reference index — historically 3- or 6-month Euribor, increasingly the compounded overnight rates (€STR, SOFR) since the benchmark reform. Frequencies may differ (annual fixed, semi-annual floating: the euro standard); in practice, at each common date, only the **net** of the two amounts changes hands.

Then comes the vocabulary that trips up beginners: the **fixed-rate payer** (*payer*) pays the fixed and receives the floating; the **fixed-rate receiver** (*receiver*) does the opposite. And by desk convention, "**paying** the swap" means paying the fixed — as does "buying" the swap. Three years in the life of a swap where you pay 3.9738% against 12-month Euribor, on €100m:

| Date | You pay (fixed leg) | You receive (floating leg) | Net flow for you |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| End of year 1 | €3,973,800 | Euribor fixed at the start: 3% → €3,000,000 | −€973,800 |
| End of year 2 | €3,973,800 | Euribor observed at end of year 1 (unknown today) | ? |
| End of year 3 | €3,973,800 | Euribor observed at end of year 2 (unknown today) | ? |

The whole contract fits in this table: one certain flow against a series of uncertain flows, and a €100m notional that appears in no cell.

## What the world's most traded rates instrument is for

**The corporate fixing its cost.** A company indebted at a floating rate — Euribor + margin, the bank-credit convention seen in module 4 — suffers every rise in the index. It enters a **fixed-payer** swap: it pays the fixed, receives the Euribor that reimburses its loan's, and its total cost becomes fixed without renegotiating its debt.

**The insurer steering its duration.** Module 4 showed the insurer matching the duration of its assets to that of its liabilities. The swap is the dream tool for that asset-liability management: **receiving the fixed** lengthens the balance sheet's duration without buying a single bond or tying up any cash — a few contracts replace billions in securities purchases.

**The directional macro trader.** Betting on rising rates by selling bonds requires borrowing them; **paying the fixed** in a swap gives the same exposure, with no securities to source and no capital to immobilize. Hence the market's depth: according to the BIS, notional outstandings of OTC interest-rate derivatives run to **hundreds of trillions of dollars** — a multiple of world GDP. A notional is not a risk for all that: chapter 7 will come back to it.

## Pricing in two ideas

The whole pricing of a swap rests on two ideas, and the first makes the apparent difficulty vanish — those unknown floating flows you would supposedly have to forecast.

<Callout type="important" titre="Idea 1: the floating leg is worth par">
Fictitiously add the notional at the maturity of both legs: it changes nothing in the swap (the same amount is added on both sides), but each leg becomes a bond. Now "floating leg + notional" is exactly a **rolled money-market deposit**: a security which, at each fixing, pays the market rate for the period. Nobody would pay more or less than face value for such a security — it is worth 100, always, at every fixing date. The magical consequence: **pricing a swap requires no rate forecast whatsoever**. The floating leg's value is known (par); all that remains is to value the fixed leg — a plain bond.
</Callout>

**Idea 2: the par rate equalizes the two legs.** "Fixed leg + notional" is a bond with coupon $C$: its value is $C \times \sum df_i + 100 \times df_n$, where the $df_i$ are the discount factors off the zero curve — $df = 1/(1+z)^T$, i.e. $1/1.04^2 = 0.924556$ for 4% at 2 years. The **par swap rate** is the coupon that makes this bond worth par, hence the fair swap, of zero value at signature:

$$
C^* = \frac{1 - df_n}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} df_i}
$$

Read the formula: $1 - df_n$ is the **discount on the notional** — what a face amount that only comes back in $n$ years "loses" once discounted — and each point of coupon brings in $\sum df_i$. The par rate is **the coupon that exactly amortizes the discount on the notional**. Run it on the 3% / 3.5% / 4% zero curve:

$$
df_1 = \frac{1}{1.03} = 0.970874 \qquad df_2 = \frac{1}{1.035^2} = 0.933511 \qquad df_3 = \frac{1}{1.04^3} = 0.888996
$$

$$
C^* = \frac{1 - 0.888996}{0.970874 + 0.933511 + 0.888996} = \frac{0.111004}{2.793381} = 3.9738\%
$$

The par rate is a weighted average of the curve — it settles between the 1-year and the 3-year, pulled toward the long pillars. A limiting case worth knowing: on a curve **flat** at 4%, the par rate is exactly 4%. Now work the mechanics yourself:

## Mark-to-market: the swap lives, its value moves

Worth zero at signature, the swap takes on value — positive or negative — as soon as the curve moves. Valuation follows from the two ideas: value to the fixed payer = notional (the floating leg, at par) minus the value of the fixed leg (the bond). Example: a year ago, you signed as fixed payer at **5%** on €100m; three years remain, and the curve today is flat at **4%**. You pay 5% when the market only asks for 4%: you overpay 1% of the notional, i.e. €1m a year for three years, which only needs discounting:

$$
V = -1 \times (0.961538 + 0.924556 + 0.888996) = -2.775091 \text{ €m}
$$

The swap is worth **−€2.775091m** to you — that is what your bank would charge you to unwind it today.

<Callout type="piege" titre="“Rates are rising, I pay the fixed, so I lose?”">
No — and this misreading gets you eliminated in an interview. Your fixed rate is **contractual**: it never moves again. What moves is the market par rate, the price at which new swaps get signed. If rates **rise**, the fixed you pay becomes **cheap**: you pay 4% when your competitors must now pay 5 — your swap has positive value. And symmetrically through the leg you receive: you collect a floating rate that climbs against an unchanged fixed. Hammer the reflex home: **rates rise ⇒ the fixed payer wins** — exactly like a bond seller, and the opposite of module 4's bondholder.
</Callout>

This parallel is no mere image: the swap **has a duration**. Receiving the fixed is carrying a fixed-rate bond funded at a floating rate — long duration; paying the fixed is the reverse — negative duration. Desks summarize the sensitivity with the swap's DV01, the value gained or lost per basis point of curve move: module 4's entire toolkit applies as is.

## A market transformed by 2008

Before 2008, swaps formed a web of bilateral contracts between banks; the crisis revealed that nobody had a map of it. Since the G20 reforms — EMIR in Europe, Dodd-Frank in the United States — standardized swaps are **mandatorily cleared** through clearing houses: LCH (SwapClear), the global reference for interest rate swaps, and CME are the major players, with daily margins to match — the discipline of futures applied to the OTC world. Portfolios are regularly **compressed**: redundant contracts between members are terminated and replaced by a reduced set of swaps with the same net risk, which deflates gross notionals that had become astronomical. One last role: the swap curve, quoted continuously across all maturities, serves as the market's **reference curve** — and its gap to government yields, the *swap spread*, has become a closely watched indicator.

<GoFurther suffixe="the asset swap and the spread against the sovereign">
An investor can buy a fixed-rate bond — an OAT, a corporate issue — and **swap** it: pay its fixed coupon in a swap, receive the floating rate plus a margin. The package, the **asset swap**, turns the security into a floating-rate investment; the margin obtained, the *asset swap spread*, measures the security's richness **against the swap curve** — the same role as module 4's OAT-Bund spread, with the swap as the yardstick instead of Germany. Hence a closely watched thermometer: the gap between the swap rate and the government yield at the same maturity. A striking fact on the American side: since the 2008 crisis, long *swap spreads* there have been **negative** — the 30-year swap rate has settled durably below the Treasury yield, a configuration long deemed impossible. The dominant readings: the balance-sheet cost of the arbitrage for banks under post-crisis regulation, and the massive supply of government paper to be absorbed.
</GoFurther>

You now know how to price, value and position the king contract of the rates market. But "swap" is a family name more than an instrument: people also exchange currencies, inflation, the performance of a stock — the extended family of the next chapter, where the most spectacular structures also hide.
