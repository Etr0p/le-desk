The treasurer of a mid-sized company knows today that she will have to borrow €10m **in 6 months**, **for 6 months** — long enough to fund an inventory peak. The 6-month rate quotes 3%; but that is today's rate, not the one she will be charged in 6 months. If rates rise in the meantime, her interest bill swells through no decision of her own. Her solution: freeze the rate on that future loan now, with a **FRA** (*forward rate agreement*). This chapter applies chapter 3's cash and carry to the most immaterial underlying there is — an interest rate that does not yet exist — then moves on to its exchange-listed cousin, the short-term interest-rate future, the one whose price walks backwards.

## Freezing tomorrow's rate today

<Callout type="definition" titre="The 6×12 FRA">
A FRA is an over-the-counter contract by which two parties fix **today** the rate of a **future** loan or deposit. The notation counts in months from today: a **6×12** FRA (read "six against twelve") covers the period that **starts in 6 months and ends in 12** — i.e. a 6-month rate, 6 months from now. No principal moves: on the fixing date, the guaranteed rate is compared with the observed market rate and only the difference is settled. The **long** side of the FRA (*the fixed-rate payer*) receives that differential if rates have risen: it is the position of the borrower who hedges.
</Callout>

Chapter 3's question remains, transposed: at what rate should this freeze be done? And the answer has the same structure: not a forecast — a manufacture.

## The FRA rate comes out of the curve, not a crystal ball

Suppose the money market quotes 3% at 6 months and 3.5% at 1 year. To find the equilibrium forward rate, compare the **two investment paths** to the 1-year horizon — the exact reasoning of module 4, chapter 5:

- **Path A — in one block**: invest €1 at 3.5% for 1 year → $1 + 0.035 = 1.035$ euros.
- **Path B — in two steps**: invest for 6 months at 3% → $1 + 0.03 \times 0.5 = 1.015$ euros, then reinvest for 6 months at the forward rate $f$ fixed today.

Two riskless strategies, same stake, same horizon: same final wealth, otherwise arbitrage. Hence, in linear interest:

$$
1.015 \times \left(1 + f \times 0.5\right) = 1.035
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
f = \frac{1}{0.5}\left(\frac{1.035}{1.015} - 1\right) = 3.9409\%
$$

Intuitive reading: to deliver 3.5% over the full year when the first half earns only 3, the second half must earn about 3.94. The 6×12 FRA therefore quotes 3.9409% — the only rate the bank can guarantee the treasurer, because it is the only one it can **manufacture**: borrow for 1 year at 3.5%, place for 6 months at 3%, and the rate for the second period falls out by itself. The forward is to the yield curve what cash and carry is to the index.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Two conventions for one forward: 3.9409 here, something else in module 4">
In module 4, chapter 5, the forward rate came out of a **compound** formula: $(1+z_2)^2 = (1+z_1)(1+f)$ — the bond world, which counts in years and compounds. Here, everything is **linear**: the money-market world, under one year, where interest does not compound. The two conventions coexist on trading floors, and they do **not** give the same number. The test that proves it: on a money-market curve **flat at 4%**, the linear 6×12 forward comes out at $\frac{1}{0.5}\left(\frac{1.04}{1.02} - 1\right) = 3.9216\%$ — not 4%. Why? Because rolling two 6-month deposits at 4% linear returns $1.02 \times 1.02 = 1.0404$, i.e. **more** than the one-block 1-year deposit ($1.04$): the roll compounds the first half's interest, the one-block linear deposit does not. The forward must therefore set **below** 4% to equalize the two paths. In compound terms, flat curve ⇒ forward = exactly 4%. In an interview, being able to say "linear in the money market, compound in bonds, and here is why the numbers differ" is worth a great deal.
</Callout>

## Settlement: a difference, and a discounting

A change of scene for the settlement: a treasurer is long a 6×12 FRA on €10m struck at 3% a few months ago — the curve has risen since. Fixing day: the observed 6-month rate comes out at 4%. She has won — rates went up. How much does she receive? First, the interest differential over the covered period:

$$
10\,000\,000 \times \frac{4 - 3}{100} \times 0.5 = 50\,000
$$

But those €50,000 compensate an extra interest cost she will only pay at the **end** of her loan, in 6 months — whereas the FRA settles **now**, at the fixing, at the very start of the interest period. Paying €50,000 today for a €50,000 harm six months away would be overpaying: the differential is therefore **discounted** over the period, at the observed rate:

$$
\frac{50\,000}{1 + 0.04 \times 0.5} = \frac{50\,000}{1.02} = 49\,019.61
$$

The hedge closes exactly: those €49,019.61, reinvested for 6 months at 4%, give back €50,000 — precisely the extra interest on the loan contracted at 4% instead of 3. All in, the treasurer does borrow at 3%, as promised 6 months earlier.

And let us hammer the direction home, because it is the chapter's most frequent interview trap: **the long FRA wins when rates rise**. The misleading intuition — "long = I make money when it goes up... but when what goes up?" — is repaired by going back to the use case: the future borrower is **hurt** by rising rates, so his hedge must **pay off** when rates rise; he hedges by going long. The future lender — an insurer waiting for a premium to reinvest — fears the fall: he hedges by going **short** the FRA, and receives the differential if rates fall. Keep the anchor: *long FRA = fixed-rate payer = friend of rising rates*.

## Short-term interest-rate futures: the price that walks backwards

The FRA is an over-the-counter contract. Its standardized, exchange-listed version is the **short-term interest-rate future** (*"STIR"*): **3-month SOFR** in Chicago, **3-month Euribor** on ICE — among the most heavily traded contracts in the world. To fit them into the futures mould (a price that rises and falls, chapter 2's daily margins), the market adopted a display convention:

$$
\text{price} = 100 - \text{rate}
$$

A 3-month rate expected at 3% quotes 97.00; at 3.5%, the contract quotes 96.50. The consequence takes some getting used to:

<Callout type="piege" titre="The price FALLS when rates RISE">
On a Euribor futures, "being a buyer" means betting on **falling** rates, and the borrower hedging against a rise must **sell** contracts — the exact mirror of the FRA, where the same borrower was long. The price = 100 − rate convention was chosen precisely to recreate module 4's bond reflex: prices fall when rates rise, the seller wins when rates rise. Check yourself on the value of a move: on the 3-month Euribor (€1m notional, quarter-year period), one basis point is worth $1\,000\,000 \times 0.0001 \times 0.25 = \text{€}25$ — same order of magnitude on 3-month SOFR, \$25 per basis point — and the minimum price increment, the 0.005 **tick**, has a tick value of €12.50. A treasurer short 100 Euribor contracts makes €2,500 per basis point of rate rise: her hedge.
</Callout>

These contracts quote in quarterly expiries over several years: the **strip** of successive prices traces out, in negative, the path of short rates the market is pricing — and therefore, almost directly, the expected path of policy rates. It is the screen every trading floor watches on central-bank evenings, and the one module 10 will dissect. The media derive the famous "hike probabilities" from it via the **Fed funds futures** — a useful reading, to be handled with care: it assumes the price reflects nothing but expectations, when a risk premium can lodge there too; the "80% probability" figure is a computational convention, not a poll of central bankers.

## From the FRA to the swap: the last step

One last move before leaving the chapter. Mentally chain FRAs end to end — 0×6, 6×12, 12×18, 18×24… — on one and the same notional: each period, you pay a rate fixed in advance and receive the market rate. Exchanging fixed for floating, period after period, for years: that stack of FRAs has a name, the **interest rate swap** — the most important instrument in modern finance, and the subject of the next chapter.

<GoFurther suffixe="the death of Libor">
For forty years, the reference rate of every FRA, future and swap in the world was **Libor**: not a rate observed on transactions, but the average of rates **declared** each morning by a panel of banks. In 2012, regulators established that several banks had submitted biased declarations — to favour their derivatives positions, or to look healthier during the crisis — and handed down billions in fines: one of the great scandals of the decade, and an accelerator of the regulatory crackdown seen in module 1, chapter 6. The structural lesson: you do not index hundreds of trillions to a declarative number. Hence the transition, essentially completed between 2021 and June 2023, to rates computed from **actual overnight transactions**: SOFR for the dollar, €STR for the euro (Euribor, reformed, survives in the euro area). A subtle but deep change: 3-month Libor was **forward-looking** — known at the start of the period, like a FRA fixing — whereas SOFR and €STR compound along the way and are only known at the **end** (*backward-looking*): the borrower discovers his interest bill on arrival, and the classic FRA has largely given way to SOFR/€STR futures and to swaps referencing those rates. Conventions change; the forward arithmetic has not moved an inch.
</GoFurther>

Index forwards, FRAs on rates: you now know how to price and handle firm commitments one period at a time. Chapter 5 assembles the bricks: the interest rate swap, a machine for exchanging fixed against floating over ten or thirty years — and, incidentally, the biggest market on the planet.
