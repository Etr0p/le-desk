## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **The forward commitment (ch. 1).** A forward commitment binds both parties — a linear, symmetric payoff, zero value at signature. Zero-sum in euros, not in utility: the market transfers risk from the hedger to the speculator, the arbitrageur welds prices together, and price discovery and leveraged access come on top. The outstandings: several hundred trillion of notional, three quarters of it in rates — and notional measures scale, never the possible loss.
2. **Futures, margins and the clearing house (ch. 2).** Futures = forward + standardisation + clearing house + margins. Novation makes the CCP the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer — hence the harmless anonymity and multilateral netting. Marking to market settles the P&L every evening; maintenance margin triggers the call, initial margin is the target of the payment; and the variation margins sum exactly to the P&L — the cash flow along the way, however, can kill.
3. **Cash-and-carry pricing (ch. 3).** The forward forecasts nothing: it is manufactured — borrow, buy spot, carry. $F = S \times (1 + (r - q)\,T)$, and the great unification: $q$ is the dividends for an index, the foreign rate for a currency, the net convenience yield for a commodity. The basis $S - F$ converges to zero at expiry and carries no directional signal.
4. **FRAs and rate futures (ch. 4).** The 6×12 FRA locks a 6-month rate starting in 6 months, at the forward rate manufactured from the curve (3% and 3.5% give 3.9409% in linear terms). The settlement is discounted at fixing (€49,019.61 on €10m), and the long — the fixed-rate payer — wins when rates rise. Rate futures quote price = 100 − rate: the borrower hedges by *selling*; a basis point is worth €25 on the 3-month Euribor.
5. **The interest rate swap (ch. 5).** Fixed against floating on a notional that is never exchanged — a chain of FRAs, the biggest market in the world. The floating leg is worth par at every fixing: no forecast needed, and the par rate $(1 - df_n)/\sum df$ comes out at 3.9738% on the 3/3.5/4 curve. Rates rise ⇒ the fixed-rate payer wins: his contractual fixed rate has become cheap.
6. **The swap family (ch. 6).** Same grammar, other legs: the cross-currency swap *also* exchanges the notionals (it genuinely converts debt from one currency to another); the inflation swap quotes the breakeven, the market measure of expectations; the total return swap gives exposure without ownership — Archegos's invisible leverage (March 2021, ~\$10bn of bank losses); the commodity swap settles everything in cash, never in barrels.
7. **Uses, risks and famous accidents (ch. 7).** Intent cannot be read off the contract: the same swap is a hedge here, a bet there. Hedging trades price risk for basis risk (1% on €25m = €250,000). Metallgesellschaft: a defensible hedge killed by cash flow; Barings, Amaranth: leverage, concentration, failed controls. Post-2008: mandatory central clearing, bilateral margins, reporting — the risk moved into CCPs, not removed.

## The formula sheet

| Formula | When to use it |
| --- | --- |
| $F = S \times \left(1 + \dfrac{r - q}{100}\,T\right)$ | The forward by cost of carry: funding $r$ added, income $q$ deducted — 5,000 × (1 + 2%) = 5,100, and the 100 points forecast nothing |
| $\text{P\&L} = (P_{\text{exit}} - P_{\text{entry}}) \times m \times N \times \text{sign}$ | The futures P&L (sign: +1 long, −1 short): (4,900 − 5,000) × 10 × 2 = −€2,000 — the short's gain, with the sign flipped |
| $\text{VM} = (P_{\text{today}} - P_{\text{yesterday}}) \times m \times N \times \text{sign}$ | Variation margin, the daily mark-to-market flow: (4,960 − 5,000) × 10 × 2 = −€800 debited the same evening |
| $\text{payment} = M_{\text{init}} - \text{balance}$ if balance $<$ maintenance | The margin call restores the account to INITIAL margin: 12,000 − 8,400 = €3,600 — not 600; maintenance triggers, initial is the target |
| $\sum \text{VM} = (P_{\text{final}} - P_{\text{entry}}) \times m \times N \times \text{sign}$ | The telescoping: −800 − 1,600 − 1,200 + 1,600 = −€2,000 — same P&L as a forward, only the timing differs |
| $\Delta_{\text{stake}} = \dfrac{\Delta_{\text{underlying}}}{\text{margin}}$ | Leverage: at 10% margin, +2% makes +20% — and −12% makes −120%: you can lose more than your stake |
| $f = \dfrac{1}{t_2 - t_1}\left(\dfrac{1 + r_2\,t_2}{1 + r_1\,t_1} - 1\right)$ | The linear implied forward rate: 3% at 6 months, 3.5% at 1 year ⇒ 3.9409% — flat curve ⇒ forward slightly BELOW the flat rate |
| $\text{settlement} = \dfrac{N \times \Delta r \times \delta}{1 + r_{\text{observed}}\,\delta}$ | The FRA settles at fixing, differential discounted: €10m × 1% × 0.5/1.02 = €49,019.61 received by the long if rates rise |
| $df = \dfrac{1}{(1 + z/100)^T}$ | The discount factor (compounded, beyond one year): 1/1.04² = 0.924556 — the building block of all swap valuation |
| $C^* = \dfrac{1 - df_n}{\sum df_i}$ | The par swap rate: 0.111004/2.793381 = 3.9738% on the 3/3.5/4 curve — flat curve at $r$ ⇒ par rate = $r$ |
| $V_{\text{fixed}} = N \times \dfrac{C}{100} \times \sum df_i + N \times df_n$ | The fixed leg is a bond: 5% fixed on a flat 4% curve, 3 years, €100m ⇒ €102.7751m |
| $V_{\text{payer}} = N - V_{\text{fixed}}$ | The floating leg is worth par: paying 5% when the market asks 4% is worth 100 − 102.7751 = −€2.7751m |
| $N = \dfrac{\text{exposure}}{F \times m}$ | The number of hedge contracts: €25m/(5,000 × €10) = 500, sold — rounded to a whole contract, beta assumed equal to 1 |
| $\text{price} = 100 - \text{rate}$ | Short-term rate futures: 3% ⇒ 97.00 — the price falls when rates rise, the borrower SELLS |
| $\text{bp value} = N \times 10^{-4} \times \delta$ | The value of a basis point: €1m × 0.0001 × 0.25 = €25 on the 3-month Euribor — the 0.005 tick is worth 12.50 |
| $\text{floating debt} + \text{payer swap} = \text{fixed debt}$ | Synthetic fixed-rate debt: the Euribor received refunds the Euribor paid, leaving the fixed rate plus the margin — without renegotiating the loan |

## The classic traps

1. **"At worst, I lose my stake"** (ch. 1): no — the commitment is on the full notional, not the deposit. At 10% margin, a −12% move in the underlying makes −120% of the stake: the deposit is wiped out and money is still owed. And derivatives' notional (hundreds of trillions) is not a possible loss: market value is only a few percent of it.
2. **The margin call paid up to the threshold** (ch. 2): paying €600 to climb back over maintenance is the most expensive interview mistake — the US futures convention requires restoring the *initial* margin: 12,000 − 8,400 = €3,600. And the trigger is binary: strictly below maintenance, whatever the size of the day's loss.
3. **"The futures quotes above spot: the market expects a rise"** (ch. 3): the 100-point gap is the rent of money minus the dividends, by cash and carry — no signal. The exact twin of "the FX forward predicts the euro" (module 6) and "the forward rate predicts the central bank" (module 4): three outfits, one single trap.
4. **The FRA's direction — and the forgotten discounting** (ch. 4): the long FRA (fixed-rate payer) wins when rates *rise* — it is the borrower's hedge. And the settlement is not €50,000 but €49,019.61: paid at fixing, at the start of the period, it is discounted at the observed rate.
5. **"Rates are rising, I pay fixed, so I lose"** (ch. 5): a disqualifying misconception — the fixed rate you pay is contractual, it is the market par rate that rises: your payer swap gains value. Twin trap: announcing −€3m instead of −€2.775m — a sum of future flows is not a value today.
6. **The notional "never exchanged"... except when it is** (ch. 6): true for the interest rate swap, false for the cross-currency swap, where the exchange of principal at inception and maturity is the heart of the contract — and the source of far higher counterparty risk.
7. **"Hedged, therefore riskless"** (ch. 7): a hedge trades price risk for basis risk — and an economically sound hedge can kill through cash flow: Metallgesellschaft paid its futures losses in cash every evening while its client gains stayed latent. The professional question: "can I fund the worst margin scenario all the way through?"

## What the jury asks most

| Standard question | The one-line reflex |
| --- | --- |
| "The index quotes 5,000, the 1-year futures 5,100: is the market expecting +2%?" | No: F = S × (1 + (r − q)·T) — 4% funding, 2% dividends, the 100 points are a cost of carry manufactured by cash and carry, not a poll |
| "Balance €8,400, maintenance €9,000, initial €12,000: how much do you pay?" | €3,600: maintenance triggers (strictly below), initial margin is the target — paying €600 to float on the threshold is the classic mistake |
| "Hundreds of trillions of derivatives: a bomb worth ten world GDPs?" | No: notional is a calculation base, never exchanged on most contracts — risk reads in market value (a few percent), net exposures, margins, DV01 |
| "The 6×12 FRA quotes 3.9409%: a forecast of the rate in 6 months?" | No: the only rate the bank can manufacture — borrow 1 year, reinvest 6 months — hence the only one it can guarantee; linear in money markets, compounded in bonds, and the numbers differ |
| "Rates rise by 100 bp: your payer swap?" | It gains: the contractual fixed rate has become cheap and the floating leg received rises — negative duration, like a bond seller; at the par rate, the value was zero at signature |
| "Selling 500 futures against €25m of equities: a perfect hedge?" | Only if beta = 1 and the maturity matches: otherwise basis risk (1% = €250,000) — and you must survive the margin calls along the way: same P&L as a forward, radically different cash flow |

Each one-line answer is an entry point, not a complete answer: train yourself to develop them over two minutes in jury mode, and to put numbers on them with the module's exercises. One last word for the road: this whole module lived on a straight line — linear, symmetric payoffs, where every euro won by one side is lost by the other, because both parties are *committed*. In module 8, one of the two buys the right to walk away: the payoff becomes ASYMMETRIC — a floor on one side, a premium on the other, and a whole new geometry to learn. Welcome to options.
