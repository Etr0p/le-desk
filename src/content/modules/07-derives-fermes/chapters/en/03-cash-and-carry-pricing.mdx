The index trades at 5,000 points. The one-year futures trades at 5,100. Why 100 points more — is the market "forecasting" a 2% rise? It is one of the juries' favourite questions, and the trap is in the verb: the futures forecasts nothing. Its price follows from spot and two observable rates, by the same no-arbitrage argument you have already met twice — covered interest parity in module 6, chapter 2, and the commodity curve in chapter 6 of the same module. This chapter installs the argument once and for all, in its purest form: **cash and carry** (buy spot and carry). By the end, you will know how to price a forward on anything — and, above all, how to explain why it is the only possible price.

## Manufacturing the future with the present

Put yourself in the shoes of the seller of an index forward: you have committed to delivering the index in one year, at a price $F$ fixed today. Must you guess where the market will be? No — it is enough to **manufacture the delivery today**. You borrow enough to buy the basket of stocks at spot $S = 5\,000$, at the funding rate $r = 4\%$; you carry it for a year; and meanwhile, the basket pays you its dividends, $q = 2\%$ of spot. Do the carry accounting:

- the **funding** costs you $5\,000 \times 4\% = 200$ points;
- the **dividends** collected along the way bring you $5\,000 \times 2\% = 100$ points.

Your all-in cost for having the index available in one year is therefore $5\,000 + 200 - 100 = 5\,100$ points. Sell the forward above that, you win for sure; below it, you lose for sure. The only price at which nobody makes free money:

$$
F = S \times \left(1 + \frac{r - q}{100} \times T\right) = 5\,000 \times \left(1 + \frac{4 - 2}{100} \times 1\right) = 5\,100
$$

in simple linear interest — the money-market convention from module 4, chapter 1, natural at these horizons. The term $(r - q) \times T$ is the **net cost of carry**: what holding the asset until delivery costs, net of what it pays.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Cash and carry">
Buy the underlying for **cash**, fund it, and **carry** it to maturity to honour a forward sale. The equilibrium forward price is the all-in cost of that manufacture: spot, plus funding, minus the income paid by the asset during the carry. When $r > q$, $F > S$: the forward trades at a **premium**. When $q > r$, $F < S$: at a **discount**. No expectation enters the computation — three of today's numbers suffice.
</Callout>

Now reread the opening question: the 100-point gap is not a forecast of a rise, it is the rent on money minus the rent on the asset. The same dismantling as for the FX forward: an arbitrage price is not a poll.

## The arbitrage that keeps order

The best way to believe the formula is to watch what happens when the market strays from it. Give ourselves a contract whose **multiplier** is €10 per index point — exactly the mechanics of the CAC 40 futures.

**The futures trades at 5,150, 50 points above the arbitrage price.** Too expensive? Then sell it, and manufacture the delivery yourself — the cash and carry:

- **Day one**: you borrow €50,000 at 4%, you buy the basket of stocks (5,000 points × €10), and you sell the futures at 5,150. Out of your own pocket: zero.
- **During the year**: the basket pays you €1,000 of dividends (100 points); your debt swells to €52,000 (5,200 points).
- **At maturity**: you deliver the index against €51,500 (5,150 points × €10).

Tally: $51\,500 + 1\,000 - 52\,000 = +\text{€}500$ per contract — the 50-point gap, times the multiplier. With no initial capital and no market risk whatsoever: every price and every rate was locked at the outset, no matter where the index ends up.

**The futures trades at 5,060, 40 points below.** Too cheap? The **reverse cash and carry**: you sell the basket short (through securities lending), you invest the €50,000 at 4% — they become €52,000 —, you pass the €1,000 of dividends back to the stock lender, and you buy the index back at maturity through the futures at 5,060, i.e. €50,600. Tally: $52\,000 - 1\,000 - 50\,600 = +\text{€}400$ per contract — the 40 points. In practice, this direction is somewhat more constrained (you must find the stock to borrow, and borrowing has a cost): the market price therefore lives inside a narrow **no-arbitrage band** around 5,100, not on a knife edge.

## One engine, every asset class

Here is the moment when three scattered chapters fold into a single idea.

<Callout type="important" titre="The grand unification: every forward = spot + cost of carry">
The formula $F = S \times (1 + (r - q)\,T)$ is **the same everywhere** — only the content of $q$ changes with the asset class:

| Underlying | What the carry pays ($q$) | Already seen in |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Equity index | the basket's dividends | this chapter |
| Foreign currency | the interest rate of the currency bought | module 6, ch. 2 (CIP) |
| Commodity | convenience yield **minus** storage costs | module 6, ch. 6 |
| Gold | almost nothing (cheap storage, no convenience) | near-permanent premium |

Covered interest parity **is** a cash and carry: buying the currency spot and investing it at its own rate is manufacturing the forward delivery — the foreign deposit plays exactly the role of the dividend. And the oil curve comes out of the same formula, with a $q$ that can turn negative when storage costs more than availability earns. In an interview, this is THE reflex that sets you apart: whatever the underlying, start with "a forward price is spot plus the net cost of carry" — then identify the asset class's $q$.
</Callout>

## The basis, and which way it slopes

The gap between cash and forward has a name: the **basis**, $S - F$ in module 6's convention. It has one mechanical property: at expiry, it equals **zero** — an expiring futures becomes spot, by construction. Any position that plays the basis is therefore a bet with a known finish line: only the path is uncertain.

The sign of the basis tells the story of the era. During the zero-rate years — in the euro area, roughly 2015-2021 — the dividend yield of the big European indices (around 3%) far exceeded the funding rate (near zero, sometimes negative): $q > r$, hence $F < S$ — index futures traded **below** spot, at a permanent discount. Generations of students read pessimism into it; it was arithmetic. With the 2022-2024 tightening, the configuration flipped: $r > q$, the forward moved back **above** spot, to a premium — and it flips back the moment dividends overtake rates again. Same index, same formula — only the rates move.

<Callout type="piege" titre="“The futures trades below spot, so the market is bearish”">
No. The level of the futures relative to spot contains **no directional information**: it reflects the funding-minus-dividends differential, full stop. A CAC futures at a 2% discount with rates at zero is exactly as neutral as a futures at a 2% premium with rates at 4%. If the discount meant "decline ahead", it would suffice to buy the futures and sell the basket to pocket the certain convergence of the basis — and that arbitrage exists, it is precisely what sets the price. The misreading is the exact twin of "the FX forward predicts the euro" (module 6) and "the forward rate predicts the central bank" (module 4): three outfits, one trap.
</Callout>

## Futures versus forward: the second-order subtlety

This whole chapter prices a *forward* — a commitment settled once, at maturity. The futures, though, settles its gains and losses **every evening** through margin calls (chapter 2), and those daily flows are invested or funded at the prevailing rate. If the underlying's price is positively correlated with rates, the long futures collects its gains when rates are high (they reinvest well) and funds its losses when they are low: a slight edge to the futures, whose price sets a little above the forward — and the reverse if the correlation is negative. Over the short horizons of an index futures, the gap is negligible; it only becomes significant on short-rate futures with distant expiries — the multi-year SOFR/Euribor strip, where it has a name, the convexity bias — and where desks genuinely price it.

<GoFurther suffixe="index arbitrage in practice">
Every morning before the stock market opens, desks publish the futures' **fair value**: yesterday's spot carried forward by the formula, expected dividends deducted. The futures, for its part, is already trading — it opens before the basket, and it has lived through the night. The gap between its price and fair value gives the likely direction of the cash open: that is what trading floors are commenting on at 8:59. From the open, **program trading** takes over: algorithms buy or sell the complete basket of index stocks against the futures as soon as the gap exceeds the friction costs. In the 1980s, this arbitrage was done in the pit, in minutes; today, at the speed of module 1, chapter 3's machines, the gap closes in a few seconds — and it hardly opens any more, precisely because everyone is watching it. There remain the days when the basis "breaks" anyway: suddenly uncertain dividends (the 2020 cuts), year-end balance-sheet costs, panicking markets. The index basis is then, like module 6's cross-currency basis, a small stress thermometer that desks read continuously.
</GoFurther>

You now know how to price a firm commitment on an asset that can be stored — a basket of stocks, a currency, a barrel. The next chapter applies the same argument to the most abstract underlying there is: an interest rate that does not yet exist. The contract is called a FRA, its price comes out of module 4's yield curve — and it holds two sign traps that juries love.
