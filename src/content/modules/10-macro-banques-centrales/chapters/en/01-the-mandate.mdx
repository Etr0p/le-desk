Since module 4, all your models have started with the same letter. The $r$ that discounted a bond's cash flows, the risk-free rate in Black-Scholes in module 8, the zero-coupon that carried the guarantee of structured products in module 9: every time, the rate fell from the sky — "given in the problem statement". This module opens the door of the building where that number is manufactured. Eight times a year in Washington, eight times a year in Frankfurt, some twenty people around a table decide the overnight price of money — and that decision propagates, link by link, all the way to the last line of every one of your pricers. Before looking at the lever (chapter 2), you need to understand the hand that holds it: who these institutions are, what they have been asked to do, and why their promise fits in a single number — 2%.

## The bank of banks

<Callout type="definition" titre="Central bank">
A **central bank** is the institution that holds the **monopoly on base money**: banknotes, and above all **reserves** — the accounts commercial banks hold with it and with which they settle among themselves. This monopoly gives it two powers no other player has: setting the **interest rate** at which this money is lent overnight (the policy rate, chapter 2), and creating it without limit in a crisis — that is the role of **lender of last resort**. Its modern mandate is organized around a third, more subtle power: anchoring **price stability**, that is, the future value of money itself.
</Callout>

The important word in the definition is *monopoly*. A commercial bank can fail because it can run out of base money; the central bank, which issues it, cannot — in its own currency, it is the one player that is never short. That is what makes the lender of last resort credible: when confidence evaporates and every bank wants cash at the same time, a single institution can supply it at will. The doctrine fits in one sentence written by Walter Bagehot in 1873 and recited in every crisis since: lend **freely**, at a **penalty** rate, against **good collateral** — save the system's liquidity without subsidizing insolvency. You will see it at work from 2008 to 2023, from Lehman to the US regional banks.

But the lender of last resort is the bad-days role. The everyday role, the one that fills the press releases and moves your screens, is managing the value of money over time: **inflation**. And there, every major central bank has been handed a written road map — its **mandate**.

## The mandates: one target, two philosophies

Almost all the major central banks today aim at the same number — 2% inflation per year — but they did not get there by the same route, and the hierarchy of their objectives differs. The two models to know by heart:

- **The Fed** has carried a **dual mandate** since 1977: maximum employment AND price stability, *on an equal footing*. The 2% target (measured on the PCE index, not the CPI) was only made official in 2012. Practical consequence: the Fed permanently arbitrates between its two legs — it can tolerate a little inflation if employment is suffering, and vice versa. That is why the US employment report (the NFP) is a market event on a par with the CPI.
- **The ECB** has a **hierarchical** mandate: price stability *first*; support for the economy second, "without prejudice" to the first objective. The target: HICP inflation (the euro area's harmonized index of consumer prices) of **2% over the medium term**, **symmetric** since the 2021 strategy review — an undershoot is as undesirable as an overshoot. Before 2021, the wording was "below, but close to, 2%": a deliberate asymmetry, inherited from the Bundesbank, that ten years of inflation running too *low* finally brought down.

| Central bank | The mandate in one line |
| --- | --- |
| **Fed** (United States) | Dual mandate: maximum employment and stable prices (2% on the PCE), on an equal footing |
| **ECB** (euro area) | Price stability first: HICP at 2%, symmetric, over the medium term |
| **BoE** (United Kingdom) | 2% on the CPI, target set by the Treasury — more than one point off, and the Governor owes the Chancellor an open letter of explanation |
| **BoJ** (Japan) | 2% since 2013 — after three decades spent fighting deflation rather than inflation |
| **SNB** (Switzerland) | Price stability defined as inflation between 0 and 2%, with a permanent eye on the franc |

<Callout type="important" titre="The mandate is a promise, not a forecast">
An interview answer to polish: the 2% target is not what the central bank *forecasts*, it is what it *promises* — an anchor point for everyone's expectations. If firms set their prices, unions their wages and markets their long rates assuming 2%, then inflation has a restoring force towards 2%: the promise is self-fulfilling. This is what is meant by **anchored** expectations, and it is a central bank's most precious asset — slow to build, quick to lose. Everything you will see in this module (Taylor rule, communication, credibility) reads as the defence of that asset.
</Callout>

## Why 2% — and not zero

A deceptively naive question, formidable in an interview: if the mission is price *stability*, why aim at 2% of increase per year rather than 0? Three reasons, in order of importance:

- **A safety margin against deflation.** Falling prices are not symmetric good news: when everything will be cheaper tomorrow, everyone postpones purchases, debts — fixed in yesterday's euros — grow heavier in real terms, and the economy sinks into a spiral that monetary policy struggles to stop, because rates can hardly be cut below zero. Aiming at 2% rather than 0 means stepping back from the cliff edge: starting from a higher nominal rate gives more room to cut when recession strikes.
- **A measurement bias.** Price indices overstate the inflation people actually experience: they capture quality improvements and consumer substitutions poorly. Inflation *measured* at 0% would probably be *actual* deflation.
- **Nominal rigidities.** Wages fall very reluctantly in nominal terms — nobody accepts a smaller paycheck. With 2% inflation, a struggling firm can cut *real* wages simply by freezing them: inflation greases the adjustments that psychology forbids.

<Callout type="piege" titre="“Price stability” does not mean stable prices">
At the target, prices are not stable: they double roughly every 35 years, since compounding is at work — module conventions: discrete annual compounding, the one used by price indices. A purchasing power of 100 is worth only $100/(1.02)^{10} = 82.03$ after ten years at 2% — and 61.39 after ten years at 5%, the 2022 regime. The 2% promise is therefore a *slow, mild and above all predictable* erosion: gentle enough to be ignored in everyday decisions, positive enough to keep deflation at bay. "Stability" here means: inflation that is no longer part of the conversation.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="why not zero — and why not 4">
Putting numbers on the three arguments has a history. The measurement bias was quantified by the Boskin Commission in 1996: the US CPI was then overstating inflation by about 1.1 points per year — at a measured 2%, you are already not far from "true" zero. The margin against deflation became the dominant argument after Japan: prices falling on and off for two decades from the late 1990s, rates at zero, and a central bank discovering that it is far harder to *create* inflation than to extinguish it — the BoJ only gave itself a 2% target in 2013 and only exited negative rates in 2024. Hence the reverse question, asked seriously by Olivier Blanchard in 2010: why not 4%, to double the room for rate cuts? The central bankers' answer: because the 2% target is worth its *accumulated credibility* — change it once, and you admit it can be changed again, de-anchoring precisely what you wanted to anchor. At 4%, inflation once again becomes a variable everyone thinks about, indexes to, hedges against. There is nothing magic about the number 2; the fact that it has never moved — that is the magic.
</GoFurther>

## Independence: the Burns-Volcker lesson

Why entrust money to unelected bankers rather than to the government? Because the government has a structural *interest* in surprise inflation: it boosts activity in the short run — before an election, for instance — and lightens the public debt. The problem is that everyone knows it: if the hand holding the rates is the one standing for election, nobody believes the 2% promise, expectations de-anchor, and inflation rises *without the government even reaping the benefit of a surprise*. The institutional solution: take the lever out of the hands of those who would be tempted, and hand it to an institution whose mandate is written down, whose officials serve long terms, and whose dismissal is next to impossible. Independence is not a technocrats' privilege: it is a commitment device — the institutional version of Ulysses tying himself to the mast.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Two Fed chairmen, two legacies">
**Arthur Burns, 1972.** Richard Nixon wants his re-election and lets his Fed chairman know it — the White House tapes attest to it. Burns keeps monetary policy accommodative while the economy overheats; Nixon is re-elected; American inflation, for its part, settles in, and will end up at 13.5% in 1980. The cost of one act of complacency: a decade.

**Paul Volcker, 1979.** Appointed to put out that fire, he does the opposite of everything an elected official would have chosen: he takes rates up to 20%, owns two recessions, unemployment at 10.8% in late 1982, car dealers mailing him the keys of unsold cars. Inflation falls back to 3.2% in 1983 — and the Fed earns the asset it still lives on: the certainty, in every head, that it will do "whatever is needed". An interview point: the real cost of a disinflation is measured by the **sacrifice ratio** — the points of output lost per point of inflation eliminated; the rest of the module will put numbers on it. The Burns-Volcker lesson: credibility is paid for in cash and pays itself back for forty years.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="when the Treasury holds the pen: fiscal dominance">
Independence has a quieter enemy than the presidential phone call: **fiscal dominance**. When public debt becomes so heavy that any rate hike threatens the state's solvency, the central bank loses its freedom without a single law changing — raising rates to fight inflation would amount to triggering a debt crisis, so it does not raise them, and inflation becomes the adjustment variable that erodes the debt in the taxpayer's place. The American textbook case: from 1942 to 1951, the Fed capped long rates at 2.5% to finance the war, and it took the 1951 "Accord" with the Treasury for it to regain control of its balance sheet. The contemporary textbook case: Turkey in 2021-2023, where rate cuts imposed by the government in the middle of a price surge sent inflation beyond 85% and the lira to the floor — a real-time demonstration of what a central bank is worth when nobody believes in its independence. Hence a market reflex to remember: any political attack on a central bank reads immediately in the currency and in long rates — investors re-price the 2% promise downwards, and demand a premium for future inflation risk.
</GoFurther>

## Where the regime comes from: from gold to the target

The 2% promise is historically very recent. For a century, the value of money was anchored not by an institution but by a metal: under the **gold standard**, every banknote is convertible into gold at a fixed parity — inflation is mechanically restrained, at the price of a non-existent monetary policy (impossible to cut rates in a recession if gold is leaving the country). **Bretton Woods**, in 1944, keeps the outline: currencies are pegged to the dollar, only the dollar remains convertible into gold, at \$35 an ounce. The system holds as long as the United States disciplines itself — and dies when it stops doing so: on August 15, 1971, Nixon closes the gold window, and for the first time in modern history, *all* the world's currencies become **fiat** — their value rests on nothing but confidence in the institution that issues them.

The decade that follows shows what happens when that confidence has not yet found its anchor: oil shocks, wage-price spirals, double-digit inflation on both sides of the Atlantic. The answer comes in two stages — Volcker proves in the 1980s that a determined central bank can break inflation, then the 1990s institutionalize the recipe: New Zealand invents **inflation targeting** in 1990 (a numerical, public target the bank is accountable for), followed by Canada, the United Kingdom, Sweden; the ECB is born in 1998 with price stability carved into the treaty; the Fed puts a number on its 2% in 2012. In fifty years, the *external* anchor — gold, then the dollar — has been replaced by an *internal* one: a promised number, and an institution credible enough to be believed. That is the regime you work in.

## Who decides, when, how

Concretely, the promise is administered by a committee, on a calendar every desk in the world knows by heart:

- **At the Fed: the FOMC** (Federal Open Market Committee). Twelve voters — the seven governors of the Board, the president of the New York Fed (permanent, because it executes the operations), and four of the eleven other regional Fed presidents, on annual rotation. **Eight scheduled meetings per year**, roughly every six weeks: statement at a fixed time, press conference by the Chair, and publication of the **minutes** three weeks later — each of these three moments can move markets. Votes are public and dissents are named: a "dissent" by a governor is a signal in itself.
- **At the ECB: the Governing Council.** The six Executive Board members plus the twenty governors of the national central banks; since 2015, the governors share fifteen voting rights on monthly rotation (the six Board members always vote). Eight monetary policy meetings per year, also every six weeks, with a statement, a press conference — and an **account** of the meeting published four weeks later. A cultural difference worth knowing: the ECB decides by consensus and publishes no named votes; the balance of power there is read between the lines, by distinguishing "hawks" (priority on inflation, tightening bias) from "doves" (priority on activity, easing bias).

Hold on to the symmetry: two committees, a six-week rhythm, and a graduated sequence of speech — the decision, the explanation, then the record. Chapter 2 will show that this speech *is* an instrument of monetary policy in its own right.

## The rate behind all your models

It remains to stitch this chapter back onto the rest of the course — the bridge that makes good exam papers. The overnight rate these committees set is the starting point of **all** the finance you have learned: it is the rate that, propagated along the curve (chapter 2), becomes the $r$ that discounts module 4's cash flows, the risk-free rate of Black-Scholes in module 8, the price of module 9's zero-coupon. The most telling example is one you already know: when the ECB took its deposit rate from −0.5% to 4% between 2022 and 2023, the structurers' 5-year zero-coupon went from almost 98 to below 87 — and the resurrected option budget brought capital-guaranteed products back into the shop windows (module 9, chapter 2). Nobody on a structuring desk "decided" that comeback: twenty-six people in Frankfurt made it possible without ever thinking about it.

An institution with a monopoly on money, a mandate that fits in one number, an independence that makes that number credible, and a committee that meets every six weeks: you have the hand. What remains is the lever — the policy rates themselves: how three rates in Frankfurt and a range in Washington become the money-market rate, how a simple rule written in 1993 prescribes their level, and why the central banker's words move curves more surely than his deeds. That is chapter 2.
