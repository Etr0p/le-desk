You know how to read a central bank: Fisher, Taylor, the statement, indicator surprises. This final chapter plugs the machine into the markets: **every asset class is a discounted future cash flow, and the rate sits in the denominator** — when it moves, everything reprices. Then, as in modules 8 and 9, the days when the mechanics run away: Volcker, the taper tantrum, the 2022 gilts, SVB. Four true stories, one same lesson.

## The reading grid: everything is a discounted flow

**Bonds first**, the only class where transmission is an exact formula — module 4's:

$$
\frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y
$$

Modified duration times the rate change, minus sign: the most mechanical channel in all of monetary policy. Put numbers on 2022: the US 10-year goes from about 1.5% to nearly 4%, +300 bp on some European curves. On a duration of 7, +300 bp gives **−21%**; on a duration of 8, **−24%**. That is how the "risk-free asset" signed the worst year of its modern history: the Bloomberg Global Aggregate, the global bond basket, lost **on the order of 17%** in 2022. Risk-free means *free of default risk* — not free of price risk.

**Equities** obey the same law, in a fuzzy version. Bridge to module 3: a stock is worth its discounted flows —

$$
P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r - g}
$$

— and the risk-free rate is the bedrock of r. The effect depends on **where the flows sit in time**: a growth stock promises the bulk of its cash flows ten years out — it is a **long-duration asset**. Check it on Gordon by moving r from 8% to 9%: with g = 6% (growth), the price loses **33%**; with g = 2% (value), **14%**. There is the growth/value rotation of 2022 — the Nasdaq loses a third while value indices contain the damage to a few percent. Rates reprice the distance of promises.

**Credit** stacks two storeys (module 5): risk-free rate **plus spread**. Monetary policy strikes twice — the rate rises with the central bank, the spread widens if the tightening stokes recession fears. Double punishment in 2022.

**Currencies** live off rate differentials (module 6): capital flows toward the currency that pays best. 2022 is the pure demonstration: the Fed hikes faster and higher than everyone else, and the dollar steamrolls everything — the DXY gains on the order of 8% over the year (peak near +19% in September), the euro drops below parity, the yen, whose central bank refuses to follow, collapses.

**Gold** is the limiting case that illuminates everything: zero cash flow, nothing to discount. Its opportunity cost is *entirely* the yield you forgo, and the right thermometer is the **real rate** (tauxReelFisher, chapter 1): holding gold when the nominal rate is at 1.5% under 7% inflation means forgoing a real rate of **−5.1%** — you forgo nothing, gold shines. Its enemy is **positive real rates**: in 2022, despite inflation at 8-9%, gold ends flat — the US 10-year real rate had just gone from about −1% to +1.5%. Inflation proposed, the real rate disposed.

**Real estate**, in one line: long duration bought on credit — the rate strikes the price (rental yields reprice against bonds) and the demand (the mortgage cost). **Crypto**, in one line: the self-proclaimed digital gold behaved in 2022 like the most extreme duration on the board — bitcoin on the order of −65%, correlated to the Nasdaq.

## Regimes: the same rate hike is not the same story

First distinction: **orderly hike or shock**. A gradual, telegraphed tightening — the Fed's 17 hikes of 25 bp in 2004-2006 — slips into prices without drama: the consensus has already discounted it. A **shock** — 1994, 2013, 2022 — is a hike the prices did not contain: that is the one that breaks things. What matters is never the level of rates, it is **the gap between the realized path and the path already priced**.

Second distinction: **where the news comes from**. An inflation surprise and a growth surprise both push rates up, but they do not tell the same story:

| Surprise (vs consensus) | Bonds | Equities | Credit (spreads) | Dollar | Gold |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Higher inflation** | Fall | Fall (discounting ↑, tougher central bank) | Widen | Rises | Ambiguous: inflation helps, real rates kill |
| **Lower inflation** | Rise | Rise | Tighten | Falls | Rather up (real rates ↓) |
| **Stronger growth** | Fall | Depends on the regime: earnings ↑… or "good news is bad news" | Tighten | Rises | Falls |
| **Weaker growth** | Rise (safe haven) | Depends on the regime: earnings ↓… or "bad news is good news" | Widen | Ambiguous | Rises |

The two "depends on the regime" cells are the heart of the chapter. In a regime of **contained inflation**, growth is the only question: a good employment number is good news. In a regime of **dominant inflation** — 2022 — every good number means "the central bank will have to squeeze harder": the good number sends the stock market *down*. Same data, opposite sign — it is the regime that gives the sign.

Structural consequence: **the stock-bond correlation changes sign with the inflation regime**. Through twenty years of quiet disinflation, it was negative: when equities dived (growth scare), bonds rose — the insurance the 60/40 portfolio lived on. When inflation becomes the problem again, the rate strikes *both* legs: the correlation turns positive, the insurance fails exactly when you need it. 2022: S&P 500 on the order of −19%, bonds −13 to −17%, the US 60/40 **around −17%, its worst year since 2008**. "The 60/40 is dead", ran the headlines — it was not dead, it was in an inflation regime. The portfolio that survives this switch is module 12's programme.

## Volcker, 1979-1982: credibility bought at full price

Summer 1979. US inflation tops 11%, the third wave in a decade; expectations are unanchored — everyone demands wage rises because everyone expects price rises. Paul Volcker, appointed in August, understands that the problem is no longer the level of rates: it is that **nobody believes the Fed anymore**. On October 6, 1979, a Saturday evening, he announces that the Fed will stop steering the rate and target the money supply instead — translation: rates will go wherever they have to go. And they go: fed funds touch **on the order of 20%** in 1980, then again in 1981; mortgage rates exceed 18%, farmers blockade the Fed's headquarters with their tractors. Two recessions follow back to back — brief in 1980, severe in 1981-82 — and unemployment peaks at 10.8%. But inflation bends: **from about 13% in 1980 to about 4% at the end of 1982** — chapter 4's sacrifice ratio, made flesh. The bond market takes time to believe it: the 10-year climbs to nearly 16% in September 1981, the premium on a disinflation it does not believe in — before beginning, credibility established, four decades of falling rates. The lesson founds the modern craft: **credibility is the central bank's asset** — ruinously expensive to buy back once lost; Volcker paid for it in cash, his successors spent the interest on it for forty years.

## 2013: the taper tantrum, or the price of a sentence

May 2013. The Fed is buying \$85 billion of securities per month; the economy is improving. On May 22, before Congress, Ben Bernanke cautiously floats the possibility of **reducing the pace of purchases** at the coming meetings. No rate hike, no security sales, no date — just buying *a little less quickly*. The market hears: *the exit begins*. The US 10-year goes from about 1.6% at the start of May to about 3% at the start of September: **on the order of +130 bp in four months** — on a duration of 8, roughly −10% in price, for one sentence. The tremor crosses the planet: the capital that had gone hunting for yield in the emerging markets flows back toward a dollar that pays again, and the current-account-deficit countries financed by those flows — the "Fragile Five": Brazil, India, Indonesia, Turkey, South Africa — watch their currencies and bonds plunge. The irony: when the actual reduction begins, in December 2013, the market absorbs it without flinching — it was in the prices; the first hike would not come until 2015. The tantrum was not caused by the policy conducted, but by **the communication**: an expectation brutally reset. When the central bank steers expectations, every word is an instrument, and a badly calibrated word is a 130 bp shock — the next exit, in 2021-2022, would be telegraphed months in advance.

## September 2022: gilts, LDI and the margin-call spiral

London, Friday September 23, 2022. Liz Truss's government, three weeks in office, presents a "mini-budget": about £45 billion of unfunded tax cuts, without independent costing, in a country where inflation exceeds 10%. Immediate verdict: the pound plunges toward its historic low against the dollar, gilts collapse — the 30-year goes from about 3.6% to more than 5%, **on the order of +130 bp in three sessions**. On a duration of 20, the formula gives **−26%**: a quarter of the value of a "risk-free" asset, in three days.

That is when the market move becomes a plumbing accident. British pension funds hedge their long-term liabilities through **LDI** (*liability-driven investment*) strategies: interest rate swaps and gilts on repo, with leverage, to replicate the duration of their liabilities. Economically, rising rates make them *richer* — their liabilities are worth less. But their hedges lose, and hedges, as you have known since module 7, come with **daily margin calls**. To find the collateral, the funds sell their most liquid asset: gilts. Those sales push rates up, which triggers new calls, which forces new sales — the **forced-selling spiral**, on the market reputed to be the safest in the kingdom. On September 28, the Bank of England — which was preparing, in perfect irony, to *sell* gilts under QT — makes a U-turn: emergency purchases **explicitly temporary, thirteen business days**, until October 14 — the firefighter fences in its intervention so as not to look like budget financing. It is enough: the spiral breaks. Truss resigns on October 20, after 44 days. Keep the hierarchy: **solvent** funds, nearly killed by *liquidity* — and the hedge turned, through its leverage and its margins, into the source of the risk. Module 12 will make it a cornerstone.

## March 2023: SVB, duration lodged on a bank's balance sheet

Silicon Valley Bank, the startups' bank. Between 2020 and 2021, venture capital money pours down and its deposits more than triple, toward \$190 billion. Startups do not borrow — they deposit: so SVB invests massively in **long** government bonds and MBS, bought at the top of the bond market at yields on the order of 1.5%, and classifies most of it, about \$90 billion, as **held-to-maturity** — an accounting category where securities stay at purchase cost. The accounting sees nothing; the economics does. 2022: the Fed hikes by more than 400 bp; on a duration close to 6, the unrealized losses reach **on the order of \$15 billion by the end of 2022 — roughly the bank's equity**. SVB is economically insolvent; all it takes is for someone to say it out loud.

On March 8, 2023, forced to raise cash, SVB sells \$21 billion of securities, booking a \$1.8 billion loss, and announces a capital raise. The signal crosses the venture capital group chats within hours, and the VCs advise their startups to get out: on March 9, **\$42 billion of withdrawals requested in a single day** — a quarter of the deposits, from a smartphone, without a single queue. On the morning of the 10th, the regulator closes the bank. **48 hours** — the nineteenth-century bank run at fiber-optic speed, aggravated by one detail: more than 90% of deposits exceeded the insurance cap, so everyone had an interest in running first. The diagnosis fits in two words from module 4: **duration mismatch** — a liability payable on demand (duration zero) funding an asset of duration 6, with no interest-rate hedge worthy of the name. The panics and contagions — Signature falls two days later, Credit Suisse is absorbed the following week — are module 11's programme; the asset-liability management that would have prevented it all, module 12's.

It would take until **March 2024** for the first Japanese hike in seventeen years. Monetary cycles are not synchronous: their lags make the great currency moves of module 6.

<Callout type="important" titre="The cross-cutting lesson: duration is everywhere, and margins force the sales">
Four episodes, one common grammar. **One**, duration does not live only in bonds: it is in growth stocks, pension funds, real estate, bank balance sheets — everywhere a distant promise meets a rate. **Two**, the accident does not erupt where the risk is visible, but where it is carried **with leverage and margin calls**: margin turns an unrealized loss into a forced sale, and one player's forced sale is another's shock. **Three**, the trigger is always a reset expectation — a sentence (2013), a budget (2022), a loss announcement (2023). In front of a jury: interest-rate risk is *measured* by duration, it *materializes* through liquidity.
</Callout>

## In the oral: the three questions that come up

**"Why did bonds fall in 2022?"** The formula, then the numbers: ΔP/P ≈ −D×Δy; hikes of 250 to 450 bp depending on the region on durations of 7 to 8 is mechanically −20% and more — the Global Aggregate gave back on the order of 17%. The finishing touch: "risk-free" qualifies the *default*, never the *price* — and because rates started from zero, no coupon cushioned the fall: maximum duration, zero buffer. Bonus: the stock-bond correlation turned positive, hence the 60/40's worst year since 2008.

**"A good employment number drops and the stock market falls: explain."** A regime question. When inflation dominates, the central-bank channel crushes the earnings channel: a robust labour market means higher rates for longer — "good news is bad news". Be precise (chapter 6): it is never the number that moves the market, it is **the gap to consensus**. And point out that the sign flips back as soon as inflation becomes secondary again: knowing which regime you are in is half the job.

**"What did the LDI episode teach the market?"** Three things. That the hedge itself can become the source of the risk when it carries leverage and margin calls: the funds were solvent, the rate rise *suited* them, and they nearly went under — liquidity can kill a solvent player. That forced selling is contagious: when an entire sector sells the same asset at the same moment, the price stops informing, it amplifies. And that a central bank may have to play firefighter *in the middle of its own tightening*: financial stability and monetary policy can contradict each other on a Tuesday morning.

The module closes where it began: a central bank sets only an overnight rate, but that rate is the denominator of all prices — bonds through duration, equities through discounting, credit on top, currencies through differentials, gold through the real rate. As long as the cycle is orderly, the grid suffices. On the days when everything breaks, another discipline takes over: understanding crises (module 11) and building portfolios that survive them (module 12).
