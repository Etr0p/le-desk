The first Friday of the month, 2:29 p.m. Paris time. On the desks, positions have been trimmed since the day before, market makers have widened their quotes, someone starts the countdown out loud. At 2:30 p.m. sharp, the Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes one number — US job creation — and within the same second, rate futures, the dollar and equity indices jump. Not in minutes: in **milliseconds**, the time it takes the algorithms to read the headline. This chapter explains the ritual: which numbers matter, how a "surprise" is read, why good economic news can send the stock market down, and how a few futures prices reveal what the market thinks about the Fed's next meeting.

## The ritual: consensus, release, reaction

Every indicator lives on an **economic calendar** known months in advance — date and time, to the minute. Before the release, bank economists submit their forecasts, whose median forms the **consensus**. And here is where the whole chapter is decided: at the moment of publication, **the consensus is already in the prices**. If the market expects 180,000 job creations, rates, the dollar and equities are *already* quoting a 180,000 world — that is module 1's efficiency at work. The release only moves prices through its **gap to consensus**: the surprise. A number in line with expectations, however spectacular in absolute terms, is a non-event; an unremarkable but unexpected number is a bomb. Hence the apparent paradox every intern discovers: "the economy created 200,000 jobs and rates did not move" (that was the consensus), "inflation fell to 3% and the market collapsed" (2.6% was expected).

## The stars of the calendar

**The NFP** (*non-farm payrolls*), first Friday of the month, 8:30 a.m. in New York: the past month's net non-farm job creation, flanked by the unemployment rate and hourly wages. The cruising speed of a healthy US economy, the one that absorbs demographic growth: about **150,000 to 200,000 job creations per month** — above that, the economy is accelerating; near zero or negative, recession is prowling. Reading trap: each release **revises the two previous months**, sometimes by more than 100,000 — the market reacts to the month's number *and* to the revisions, and a beautiful print paired with massive downward revisions can read as bad news.

**The CPI**, around the 13th of the month: the consumer price index. Two mandatory pairs of glasses. *Headline vs core*: the overall index against the index **excluding food and energy** — the core, stripped of the most volatile components, is what the central bank watches to judge the trend. *MoM vs YoY*: the one-month change against the twelve-month change. The YoY is legible but drags a year of history behind it; the MoM is the fresh information — provided you know how to **annualize** it, and this is a compounding trap, not a multiplication:

$$
(1 + 0.005)^{12} - 1 = 6.17\ \% \quad \neq \quad 12 \times 0.5 = 6\ \%
$$

A CPI at +0.5% on the month is running at 6.17% at an annual rate, not 6% — and the gap to the naive calculation grows with the number (+1% per month makes 12.68%, not 12%). Oral reflex: annualize in your head at 12×, then *flag* that compounding adds a few tenths.

**The PMIs** (*purchasing managers' indexes*, including the American ISM): diffusion surveys of purchasing managers — orders, production, employment, delivery times. The reading fits in one number: **50 = the border between expansion and contraction**. A PMI at 55 says activity is accelerating decisively, at 45 that it is contracting. Their value to the market is not precision but **lead time**: published as early as the end of the month they describe, they precede the "hard" data (production, GDP) by one to two months — they are the car's headlights.

**GDP**, quarterly, published in **three successive estimates** (*advance*, second, final) a month apart — only the first really moves prices, the later ones refine. Transatlantic trap: the United States publishes an **annualized** quarterly change (an American +2% corresponds to ~+0.5% on the quarter), Europe publishes the raw quarter — comparing the two without conversion is a classic mistake.

Complete the dashboard: on the US labour side, the **JOLTS** (open job vacancies — labour-market tightness) and the weekly Thursday ***jobless claims***, the freshest indicator of all; on the European side, the German **IFO** (business climate, the benchmark for industry) and the **ZEW** (investor sentiment) — Germany serving as the euro area's leading thermometer.

| Indicator | When | The one-number reading |
|---|---|---|
| NFP | 1st Friday, 8:30 a.m. NY | ~150-200k = cruising speed; watch the revisions |
| CPI | around the 13th | core MoM annualized vs the 2% target |
| PMI / ISM | end of month / start of month | 50 = expansion-contraction border |
| GDP | quarterly, 3 estimates | US annualized vs Europe raw quarter |
| Claims | every Thursday | employment at weekly frequency |
| IFO / ZEW | monthly | German morale, headlight of the euro area |

## Reading a surprise in sigmas

An NFP printing 40,000 above consensus — is that huge or anecdotal? Impossible to say without a yardstick: 40,000 is colossal for weekly *claims*, negligible for an NFP whose historical surprises spread over ±100,000. The yardstick is the historical standard deviation of surprises — module 2's $\sigma$, recycled once more — and the professional reading **standardizes**:

$$
\text{surprise} = \frac{\text{released} - \text{consensus}}{\sigma_{\text{surprises}}} = \frac{300{,}000 - 180{,}000}{60{,}000} = +2\sigma
$$

An NFP released at 300,000 against 180,000 expected, with a $\sigma$ of 60,000, is a **+2 sigma** surprise: rare (a few percent of releases), hence violent for prices. The same raw gap on a more erratic indicator would be worth only half a sigma — a shrug. This normalization also lets you *compare* indicators with one another: a CPI at +2σ and an NFP at +2σ are the "same size" of shock, whatever their units. That is exactly the `surpriseIndicateur` function in this module's engine: released minus consensus, divided by sigma — unitless.

<Callout type="piege" titre="It is the surprise that moves prices, never the level">
"Inflation is at 8.6%, bonds are going to collapse" is TV-news reasoning, not desk reasoning: if the consensus was 8.6%, the number is already in the prices and nothing will happen. The desk version: consensus 8.3%, released 8.6%, σ of surprises 0.15 points → **+2σ**, big inflationary surprise, rates rise — and through module 4's duration, a 10 bp rise inflicts about −0.7% on a duration-7 bond. Oral corollary: a catastrophic number that is *perfectly anticipated* can even push the market up, if short positions get bought back — "sell the rumor, buy the fact".
</Callout>

## "Good news is bad news": the reading regime

A surprise has a sign; its *translation* into asset prices depends on the regime. The question the desk asks is never "is this good for the economy?" but "**what does this change in the central bank's reaction function?**". In a normal regime — inflation tame, central bank neutral — a good NFP is good news for equities: more growth, more profits. But in 2022, inflation at 8-9% makes the Fed an adversary: every good employment number means *more rate hikes* — and module 8 taught you what higher rates do to valuations. The result, documented all year long: NFP above consensus → equities down, bonds down; and symmetrically, a *rise in unemployment* greeted with a rally. **Good news is bad news**: the sign of the reaction flips depending on whether the market fears the recession or the central bank. Same mechanics on the CPI, the absolute star of 2022 to the point of dethroning the NFP: on CPI days, the S&P 500 moved on average two to three times more than on an ordinary day. The hierarchy of indicators is not fixed — the one that reigns is **the one the central bank is watching**.

## Pricing the next meeting: the arithmetic of futures

The market does not just comment on the central bank: it **quotes** its future decisions, via short-rate futures — fed funds in Chicago, €STR in Europe, the firm derivatives of module 7. The convention: price = 100 − expected average rate over the month. A future for the month following a meeting quoting 95.85 says the market expects an average rate of **4.15%** over that month. Suppose the current effective rate is 4.00% and a possible 25 bp hike is in play: 4.15% is neither 4.00 (no hike) nor 4.25 (certain hike) — it is a **probability-weighted average**:

$$
\mathbb{P}(\text{hike}) = \frac{4.15 - 4.00}{4.25 - 4.00} = \frac{0.15}{0.25} = 60\ \%
$$

The market is pricing a 60% chance of a hike. It is this arithmetic — three subtractions — that manufactures the "market-implied" probabilities quoted in the press (the CME's FedWatch tool does nothing else). By chaining the futures month after month, you rebuild the whole expected path up to the **terminal rate** — the peak of the cycle: from 2.50%, four 50 bp hikes lead to 4.50% — that is the module's `tauxTerminalAnticipe` function, the arithmetic of the Fed's *dot plots* (and a negative step describes a cutting cycle: from 5.25%, six 25 bp cuts bring you back to 3.75%). Hence the cascade reading of a release: the CPI surprise moves the expected rate path, the path moves the whole curve, the curve moves the dollar and equities — **the indicator does not move prices directly, it moves the expected central bank, which moves everything else**.

## Release day on a desk

There remains the scene from the market maker's side — back to module 1's microstructure. A major release is a **scheduled information asymmetry**: for a few seconds, the fastest algorithms can read the number before quotes absorb it, and the market maker who stayed tightly quoted gets systematically picked off on the wrong side — adverse selection in its chemically pure state. The defence is ritual: **reduce positions** before the appointed hour (you do not carry risk against a known lottery), **widen the quotes** or pull them in the final minute, let the wave pass. Liquidity evaporates precisely when the orders pour in: the first seconds see a **whipsaw** — the price overreacts to the raw number, then reverses when the algorithms read the second line (the NFP's revisions, the CPI's core, the wages) and the first move gets unwound. The price displayed five seconds after the hour is not an opinion, it is order-book mechanics. Hence the rule on serious books: the P&L of an NFP day is judged at 3 p.m., never on the first second.

<GoFurther suffixe="nowcasting: GDPNow, or GDP in real time">
GDP has a crippling flaw for a desk: it describes a quarter that ended a month ago. Hence *nowcasting* — estimating the quarter **in progress**, continuously. The most followed is **GDPNow**, from the Atlanta Fed: a purely mechanical model that aggregates each release (retail sales, foreign trade, construction, ISM...) into an estimate of annualized quarterly GDP, updated six to seven times a month — with no human judgment whatsoever, which the Atlanta Fed proudly claims. Honest user's manual: early in the quarter, the estimate rests on a handful of numbers and can wander by several points (the April −2% that ends at +2% in June is a classic); it only becomes precise in the final weeks, when almost everything has been published. Desks therefore read it less for its *level* than for its *revisions*: a GDPNow that lurches down after a volley of releases says the quarter is deteriorating in real time — it is the PMI generalized, with hard data on top. Transposable reflex: the ECB and the New York Fed publish their own nowcasts, and desks build their own.
</GoFurther>

<Callout type="important" titre="The complete reading grid, in four questions">
Facing any release, the desk runs through: **1. What surprise?** — released minus consensus, divided by the historical sigma: ±0.5σ is noise, ±2σ is an event. **2. What regime?** — good economic news = good or bad market news, depending on what the central bank is fighting (2022: good NFP = bad news for equities). **3. What meeting?** — what does the surprise do to the futures-implied probabilities and to the expected terminal rate? **4. What transmission?** — the displaced rate path propagates through duration to bonds, through discounting to equities, through the rate differential to currencies. A candidate who runs these four questions on a numbered example — +2σ on the CPI, hike probability going from 60 to 90%, −0.7% on a duration of 7 — has understood what "markets watch the indicators" means.
</Callout>
