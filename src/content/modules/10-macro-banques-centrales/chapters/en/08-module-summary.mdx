This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the essentials chapter by chapter, the formula sheet, the channels, the rate shock by asset class, the episodes, the traps and the reflexes. To reread the day before an oral — and to complete with the jury mode of the practice section. The guiding thread fits in one sentence: the central bank manufactures the $r$ of all your previous modules — the one that discounted in module 4, priced in module 8, built the zero-coupon in module 9 — and it manufactures it meeting after meeting, with a single day-to-day lever, five channels and eighteen months of lags. The desk corollary: you never trade "the economy" — the consensus is already in the prices — you trade the **surprises** and the shift in **expectations**; what moves markets is not the number, it is the gap between the number and the world the prices were already quoting.

## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **The mandate (ch. 1).** The central bank holds the monopoly on base money: it sets the overnight price of money and lends as a last resort (Bagehot). Its promise — 2% — is not a forecast but an **anchor**: if everyone believes it, inflation gravitates back to it on its own. Fed: **dual mandate** (employment AND prices, hence the weight of the NFP); ECB: hierarchical (prices first, symmetric since 2021). Why 2 and not 0: a margin against deflation, measurement bias, nominal rigidities. Independence is a commitment device — Burns yields to Nixon and bequeaths 13.5% inflation; Volcker goes to 20% and bequeaths forty years of credibility. And even "at target", prices double in 35 years: 100 of purchasing power is worth 82.03 after ten years at 2% — and **61.39** after ten years at 5%.
2. **Policy rates (ch. 2).** The ECB sets a corridor of three rates, but in excess liquidity the market sticks to the floor: THE rate that matters is the **deposit rate** (the €STR lives a few bp below it); the Fed holds its target range through the IORB. The Taylor rule prescribes the level: $i = r^* + \pi + a\,(\pi - \pi^*) + b \cdot \text{gap}$ — with (2, 4, 2, −1) and $a = b = 0.5$: $2 + 4 + 1 - 0.5 = 6.5\%$; for Volcker 1980 (inflation 13, gap −2): **19.5%** — he went to 20. The real content is the **Taylor principle**: move by *more* than one for one, otherwise the real rate falls while prices are on fire. And the real rate is computed by exact Fisher: at (10%, 8%), $1.10/1.08 - 1 = 1.85\%$, not 2 — the approximation always overestimates. The cycle arithmetic: from 2.50%, four 50 bp hikes make a **terminal rate of 4.50%**; 2022-2023 for real: Fed **+525 bp** in sixteen months, ECB **+450 bp** in fourteen.
3. **Transmission (ch. 3).** From the policy rate to inflation, **five channels**: rates (the monthly payment on the €250,000 20-year mortgage goes from €1,150 to €1,515, +32%; at constant monthly payment, −24% of borrowing capacity), credit (banks tighten, spreads widen), exchange rate (euro below parity in 2022), asset prices (bonds through duration: **−7%** for +100 bp on a duration of 7; equities through discounting; wealth effect), expectations (the free channel — "whatever it takes"). Two structural properties: **long and variable lags** (12-18 months: you steer looking in the rear-view mirror, hence the "pause" debate) and an **asymmetry** (braking always works, stimulating does not: *pushing on a string*). The dashboard is read off the curve — the 10-year minus 2-year inversion has preceded every US recession since the 1960s, with an unusable lead time (6-24 months) and one recent false positive (2022-2024).
4. **Inflation (ch. 4).** Measuring it without getting trapped: core (excluding energy and food) drives the decisions, because rates do not make wheat grow; the year-on-year reading drags base effects along; and you **compound**, you do not multiply — +0.5% on the month runs at $(1.005)^{12} - 1 = 6.17\%$ annualised, not 6. Chaining never forgets (2, 3, 10% make 115.57, not 115) and **disinflation is not deflation**: the pace slows, the level stays. Three engines (demand, costs, money) and one accelerator: the wage-price spiral, which only takes hold if expectations de-anchor. The cost: the inflation tax — €100 in a non-interest-bearing account is worth **€61.39** after ten years at 5%, to the benefit of fixed-rate debtors, the State first among them. The bill for eradication: the **sacrifice ratio** — Volcker pays for ~10 points of disinflation with two recessions and 10.8% unemployment (ratio ~1.5); 2021-2023 (peaks of **9.1%** US, **10.6%** euro area) gets away almost without a recession, because part of the shock was supply-side and expectations held: the sacrifice had been **prepaid**, by forty years of credibility.
5. **QE, QT and the unconventional (ch. 5).** When Taylor prescribes −1.25% (2009) and the banknote at 0% bounds the rate, you change tools. QE swaps reserves for duration: the selling bank trades a 10-year security for a deposit, nothing lands with households — hence M0 multiplied by five **without** inflation (2020-2021, where fiscal transfers reached M2, is the counter-example). A double channel: signalling + duration scarcity (~50-100 bp for QE1, less afterwards). The orders of magnitude: Fed balance sheet ~\$9 trillion at the peak, **~35% of GDP**; Eurosystem **~65%**; BoJ over 120%. QT is done through capped non-reinvestment, never through sales: you are searching for an unknown reserve floor — September 2019, intraday repo near 10%, found it. The arsenal sorts into three families: quantities (QE/QT), prices (negative rates — with a *reversal rate* — and Japanese YCC), promises (forward guidance, OMT) — and the most powerful was the cheapest: three words from Draghi, zero euros spent.
6. **The indicators (ch. 6).** The ritual: a known calendar, a consensus already priced in, only the **surprise** moves prices — standardised in sigmas: NFP printed at 300,000 against 180,000 expected, sigma 60,000, surprise **+2σ**, rare and therefore violent. The stars: NFP (cruising range 150-200k, watch the revisions), CPI (core, MoM annualised: 6.17%), PMI (50 = the frontier), GDP (US annualised vs Europe quarter-on-quarter raw). The regime gives the sign: in 2022, good NFP = bad news for equities (**good news is bad news**) — the desk's question is never "is it good?" but "what does it change in the reaction function?". And the market quotes the next meeting: a future at 95.85 implies an average rate of 4.15%, i.e. $\dfrac{4.15 - 4.00}{4.25 - 4.00} = 60\%$ probability of a 25 bp hike — three subtractions, that is FedWatch. On the day itself, on the market maker's side: reduced positions, widened quotes, whipsaw — module 1's adverse selection in its purest form.
7. **Asset classes and accidents (ch. 7).** Everything is a discounted flow, the rate sits in the denominator. Bonds: the only exact transmission, $\Delta P / P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y$ — +300 bp on a duration of 8: **−24%**; the Global Aggregate returns **~−17%** in 2022, the worst year of the "risk-free asset" (free of default risk, not of price risk). Equities: Gordon — r from 8 to 9%, growth ($g = 6\%$) loses 33%, value ($g = 2\%$) 14%: the 2022 rotation. Credit: double whammy (rates + spreads). FX: differentials — the dollar crushes everything in 2022. Gold: zero cash flow, its thermometer is the **real** rate — flat in 2022 despite 9% inflation, because the real rate went from −1 to +1.5%. And the equity-bond correlation changes sign with the inflation regime: the US 60/40 ~−17%, worst year since 2008. Then four accidents, one grammar: duration is everywhere, and margin calls turn the unrealised loss into a forced sale.

## The formula sheet

| Formula | When to use it |
| --- | --- |
| $i = r^* + \pi + a\,(\pi - \pi^*) + b \cdot \text{gap}$ | The Taylor rule: (2, 4, 2, −1) gives 6.5%; Volcker 1980: 19.5% prescribed, 20 delivered; 2009: −1.25%, hence QE — a conversational benchmark, not an autopilot ($r^*$ unobservable, gap revised) |
| Taylor principle: move by $1 + a$ per point of inflation | The criterion that separates catching up from running behind: one for one leaves the real rate unchanged, less than one for one makes it FALL in the middle of a flare-up — the 1970s |
| $r = \dfrac{1 + i}{1 + \pi} - 1$ | Exact Fisher: at (10%, 8%), $1.10/1.08 - 1 = 1.85\%$ — restrictive or not is read off the real rate compared with $r^*$, never off the nominal |
| $r \approx i - \pi$ | The mental approximation: 2% on the same example — it neglects the cross term and always OVERESTIMATES the real rate, all the more so as levels are high (15 bp here, 6 bp at (5, 2)) |
| $\dfrac{100}{1.05^{10}} = 61.39$ | The inflation tax: erosion discounts purchasing power the way a rate discounts a cash flow — 38.6% of the banknote eaten away in ten years at 5%; 82.03 left at the 2% target |
| $(1 + m)^{12} - 1$ | Annualising a monthly figure: +0.5% on the month runs at 6.17%, not 6 — you compound, you never multiply; the gap to the naive answer grows with $m$ (1% makes 12.68%) |
| $100 \times 1.02 \times 1.03 \times 1.10 = 115.57$ | Chaining an index: never $100 + 2 + 3 + 10$ — and +10% then −10% make 99, not 100 |
| $\text{sacrifice ratio} = \dfrac{\text{cumulative gaps (in absolute value)}}{\text{disinflation}}$ | The bill for disinflation: ~15 points of GDP for 10 points of inflation under Volcker (ratio 1.5); nearly zero in 2023 — the ratio is the price FOR A GIVEN STOCK OF CREDIBILITY |
| $y_{10} = \text{average of expected short rates} + TP_{10}$ | The decomposition of the long rate (m4): the central bank only controls the overnight, but everything it hints at moves the whole curve — and QE attacks both terms |
| $M \times V = P \times Y$ | The quantity theory: an identity always true, a theory if $V$ is stable — the QEs inflate M0 without M2 ($V$ collapses), 2020-21 reaches spending and inflation follows |
| $\text{surprise} = \dfrac{\text{printed} - \text{consensus}}{\sigma_{\text{surprises}}}$ | The reading in sigmas: (300,000 − 180,000)/60,000 = +2σ — rare hence violent, and comparable across indicators with different units |
| $\mathbb{P}(\text{hike}) = \dfrac{4.15 - 4.00}{4.25 - 4.00} = 60\%$ | The implied probability from futures: price 95.85 ⇒ average rate 4.15%, a weighted average between 4.00 and 4.25 — three subtractions, that is FedWatch |
| $\text{terminal} = \text{current rate} + n \times \dfrac{\text{step in bp}}{100}$ | The expected peak of the cycle: from 2.50%, four 50 bp hikes make 4.50% — and the real scale of 2022-23: Fed +525 bp, ECB +450 bp |
| $\Delta P / P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y$ | The only exact channel of monetary policy: +100 bp on a duration of 7: −7%; +300 bp on a duration of 8: −24% — that is the Global Agg at −17% in 2022 |
| $P_0 = \dfrac{D_1}{r - g}$ | Gordon (m3) under the cycle: r from 8 to 9%, growth loses 33%, value 14% — rates reprice the distance of the promises |

## The five transmission channels

| Channel | The causal chain | Lag / strength |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Rates** | policy rate ⇒ more expensive loans ⇒ monthly payment +32%, borrowing capacity −24%, projects abandoned (DCF) ⇒ demand curbed | Slow (loans already signed, projects under way); touches everyone a little |
| **Credit** | bank balance sheets under pressure ⇒ tighter standards, credit volume reduced ⇒ spreads widened | An amplifier: hits the fragile hard (SMEs, modest households) — thermometer: the spreads (⇒ m5) |
| **Exchange rate** | higher rates ⇒ capital inflows ⇒ currency appreciates ⇒ imported disinflation, exports curbed | Fast on the currency, slow on prices; euro below parity in 2022 (⇒ m6) |
| **Asset prices** | more expensive discounting ⇒ bonds (−D×Δy), equities, property fall ⇒ negative wealth effect | Immediate on prices, slow on consumption; strong in the US (401k), weak in Europe |
| **Expectations** | a believed promise ⇒ prices and wages set accordingly ⇒ inflation returns on its own | Instant and free — but reserved for credible central banks; the most powerful of the five |

The whole takes **12 to 18 months** to bite fully (*long and variable lags*: you steer looking in the rear-view mirror) and works better at braking than at stimulating (*pushing on a string*).

## The rate shock by asset class (rate hike)

| Class | Direction | Mechanism |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Bonds** | Down — exact | $\Delta P / P \approx -D_{\text{mod}} \times \Delta y$: −7% for +100 bp (duration 7), −24% for +300 bp (duration 8); "risk-free" = free of default risk, not of price risk |
| **Equities** | Down — growth far more than value | Discounting of cash flows: the more distant the cash flows, the longer the duration — Gordon: −33% (g = 6%) versus −14% (g = 2%); Nasdaq −33% in 2022 |
| **FX** | The tightening currency rises | Rate differentials ⇒ capital flows (m6): the Fed faster than everyone in 2022 ⇒ king dollar, euro below parity, yen collapsed (BoJ motionless) |
| **Gold** | Down if the REAL rate rises | Zero cash flow: its opportunity cost is the real rate — flat in 2022 despite 9% inflation, the real rate going from −1 to +1.5%; inflation proposes, the real rate disposes |
| **Credit** | Double whammy | Risk-free rate + spread (m5): the rate rises with the central bank, the spread widens if the tightening raises fears of recession |

And the meta-rule of 2022: when inflation dominates, the equity-bond correlation turns **positive** — the rate hits both legs, the 60/40 loses ~17%, the insurance fails exactly when it is needed.

## The episodes to know

| Episode | Date | The mechanics | The lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Volcker** | 1979-1982 | Expectations de-anchored after ten years of broken promises; fed funds ~20%, two recessions, 10.8% unemployment; inflation from ~13% to ~4% | Credibility is the central bank's asset: paid for in cash once, it yields for forty years — the sacrifice ratio depends on the stock of credibility |
| **"Whatever it takes"** | July 2012 | Spanish and Italian spreads in a self-fulfilling loop (doubt ⇒ rates ⇒ insolvency ⇒ doubt); three sentences + unlimited OMT, never activated | Against a self-fulfilling prophecy, the weapon is the credible unlimited promise: hundreds of bp of relief for zero euros |
| **Taper tantrum** | May 2013 | Bernanke mentions a mere slowing of purchases; the 10-year goes from ~1.6 to ~3% (+130 bp in four months), the "Fragile Five" tumble | The market does not react to actions but to recalibrated expectations: one ill-calibrated word is a 130 bp shock — the next exit would be telegraphed |
| **Gilts / LDI** | September 2022 | Truss mini-budget ⇒ +130 bp in three sessions on the 30-year (duration 20: −26%); margin calls on the hedges ⇒ forced gilt sales ⇒ spiral; the BoE buys for thirteen business days, in the middle of QT | SOLVENT funds nearly killed by LIQUIDITY: the leveraged hedge becomes the source of the risk; financial stability and monetary policy can contradict each other on a Tuesday morning |
| **SVB** | March 2023 | Demand deposits funding ~\$90 billion of long bonds at 1.5% in held-to-maturity; +400 bp ⇒ ~\$15 billion of unrealised losses ≈ the equity capital; \$42 billion of withdrawals in one day, closure within 48 hours | **Duration mismatch**: duration also lives on bank balance sheets, and panic travels at smartphone speed — rate risk is measured by duration, materialises through liquidity |

## The classic traps

1. **"The 2% target is a forecast"** (ch. 1): it is a *promise* — an anchor for expectations that self-fulfils as long as it is believed. And "price stability" does not mean stable prices: at target, prices double in 35 years — the erosion is slow, small and above all predictable, and it is a choice (a margin against deflation).
2. **Confusing nominal and real** (ch. 2): a 4% policy rate under 7% inflation is an *accommodative* policy disguised as rigour — summer 2022, four hikes in, the ECB's real rate was still around −5%. And the $i - \pi$ approximation always overestimates: at (10, 8), the true real rate is 1.85%, not 2.
3. **Judging policy on today's inflation** (ch. 3): published inflation is the product of decisions taken eighteen months ago — tightening until you see it fall is guaranteeing one brake stroke too many. The right question: what will today's decisions produce by the end of next year?
4. **"Inflation is falling, so prices are falling"** (ch. 4): disinflation is not deflation — the derivative comes back to 2%, the level never comes back down. Twin arithmetic traps: annualising by multiplying by 12 (0.5% monthly = 6.17%, not 6) and reading a base-effect disinflation as a victory (the first anniversary of a shock drops out of the window, nothing more).
5. **"Money printing = inflation"** (ch. 5): QE creates reserves — the *banks'* money — not deposits of the public: M0 × 5 between 2008 and 2014, M2 unmoved, inflation below target. It is not the size of the balance sheet that makes inflation, it is the money that reaches spending (2020-21, fiscal transfers: M2 +26%, inflation).
6. **Reacting to the level rather than the surprise** (ch. 6): "inflation is at 8.6%, sell bonds" is evening-news talk — if the consensus was 8.6, nothing happens. The desk version: printed minus consensus, divided by sigma, then the regime (good news is bad news when the central bank is the adversary), then the implied probabilities.
7. **"Risk-free" read as "free of price risk"** (ch. 7): the risk-free asset lost ~17% in 2022 (Global Agg) — risk-free qualifies the default; the price, for its part, obeys duration, maximal precisely when rates start from zero (zero coupon, no cushion). And bonds only insure equities in a regime of controlled inflation.

## What the jury asks most

| Standard question | The one-line reflex |
| --- | --- |
| "Concretely, how does a rate hike bring inflation down?" | Unroll the five channels with one number each (monthly payment +32%, spreads, FX, −D×Δy, expectations) — then the two properties: 12-18 months of lags, braking/stimulating asymmetry |
| "Why 2% and not 0?" | The trio: a margin against deflation (the Japanese spiral, room to cut), measurement bias (~1.1 pt, Boskin), nominal rigidities (freezing a wage = cutting it in real terms) — and at 2%, inflation drops out of the conversation |
| "Is policy restrictive at a 4% policy rate?" | It is never read off the nominal: exact Fisher, compared with $r^*$ — 4% under 7% inflation is a negative real rate, hence accommodative; 2022 played it out for real |
| "QE quintupled the monetary base: why no hyperinflation?" | M0 is not M2: reserves only circulate between banks and the central bank — the multiplier collapsed; inflation comes when money reaches spending (2020-21) |
| "Good employment number, the stock market falls: explain" | Regime: when inflation dominates, the central-bank channel crushes the earnings channel — good NFP = higher rate path = lower valuations; and it is the surprise in sigmas that moves things, never the level |
| "Why did bonds crash in 2022?" | $\Delta P / P \approx -D \times \Delta y$: +250 to +450 bp on durations of 7-8, starting from zero coupons — −20% and more, Global Agg ~−17%; bonus: the 60/40 correlation turned positive |

## The twelve reflexes for the oral

1. The rate in all your models is manufactured: the $r$ of m4, m8 and m9 comes out of a committee that meets every six weeks — the generosity of structured products is a thermometer of long rates.
2. The target is a promise, the anchor is the asset: long to build (Volcker, paid for in recessions), quick to lose (Burns), profitable for forty years (2021-23, near-free disinflation).
3. Faced with "what is the ECB's rate?": the one that steers the market — the **deposit rate**, floor of the corridor, to which the €STR is glued in excess liquidity.
4. Taylor from memory: $r^* + \pi + 0.5\,(\pi - \pi^*) + 0.5 \cdot \text{gap}$ — and the principle: move by MORE than one for one, otherwise the real rate falls while prices rise.
5. Restrictive or accommodative is judged on the real rate (exact Fisher, the approximation overestimates) compared with $r^*$ — never on the nominal.
6. Transmission takes 12-18 months: you steer looking in the rear-view mirror — judging a policy on today's inflation is judging the braking on the speed of a year and a half ago.
7. You compound, you never multiply: $(1 + m)^{12} - 1$ to annualise, chaining for indices, and erosion works against the saver (61.39 after ten years at 5%).
8. QE swaps reserves for duration: two channels (signalling, scarcity), M0 is not M2 — and QT is not the film in reverse: you are searching for an unknown floor (September 2019).
9. The hierarchy of the unconventional is counter-intuitive: quantities < prices < **promises** — the most powerful tool of the 2008-2022 period cost three sentences (OMT, never activated).
10. A release is read in four questions: what surprise (in sigmas)? what regime (good news is bad news?)? which meeting (implied probabilities, terminal rate)? what transmission (duration, discounting, FX)?
11. Duration is everywhere — bonds, growth stocks, pension funds, bank balance sheets — and the accident is born where it is carried with leverage and margin calls: margin turns the unrealised loss into a forced sale.
12. *Don't fight the Fed*: you do not position against the announced cycle — but the adage promises neither a Fed put (2022: −25% with no rescue) nor a naive reading of decisions (an expected hike moves nothing).

## Bridges to the rest of the course

- **Module 1 — markets and microstructure.** CPI day is microstructure in its purest form: scheduled information asymmetry, market makers widening or pulling their quotes, adverse selection, the whipsaw of the first seconds — and the consensus already in the prices is module 1's efficiency at work.
- **Module 4 — rates and the curve.** Module 10 is m4 traced back to its source: the long rate is an average of expected short rates plus a **term premium** (which QE compresses), the curve inversion is a vote on future cuts, and duration — $\Delta P / P \approx -D \times \Delta y$ — is the exact bridge between a committee decision and a bond P&L.
- **Module 6 — foreign exchange.** The exchange-rate channel IS m6: rate differentials drive the flows — the carry trade lives off mismatched monetary cycles (the motionless BoJ made the yen in 2022), and 2022 showed monetary policy acting as exchange-rate policy without saying so.
- **Module 9 — structured products.** The zero-coupon that guaranteed the capital is manufactured here: the ECB going from −0.5 to 4% took the 5-year ZC from ~98 to ~87, resurrecting capital protection — nobody on the desk decided that comeback, twenty-six people in Frankfurt made it possible without a thought for it.

Each one-line answer is an entry point, not a complete answer: train yourself to develop them over two minutes in jury mode, and to put numbers on them with the module's exercises. One last word for the road: this module taught you to read the hand that manufactures the denominator of every price — the mandate, the lever, the channels, the target variable, the arsenal for days without ammunition, the calendar that sets the desks' rhythm, and the grid that translates all of it into bonds, equities, currencies and gold. You now know that the desk does not trade the economy but the surprises, and that credibility is an asset paid for in cash and spent in crises. And this module ended on its accidents: gilts sold in a spiral by solvent funds, a bank killed in 48 hours by its own duration — days when transmission no longer slows the economy but **breaks** something, when the credit channel becomes a credit crunch and module 5's spreads stop being a thermometer and become the fire. Those days have a history, recurring mechanisms — leverage, liquidity, panic, contagion — and lessons every generation relearns at its own expense.
