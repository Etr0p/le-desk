Autumn 2008. The Fed has slashed its policy rate from 5.25% to nearly zero in fifteen months, and it is not enough: the economy is collapsing faster than the rate can fall. Run the situation through the Taylor rule met earlier in this module — real neutral rate 2%, inflation down to 0.5%, output gap of −6%:

$$
i = 2 + 0.5 + 0.5\,(0.5 - 2) + 0.5\,(-6) = -1.25\ \%
$$

The rule prescribes a **negative** rate. But the policy rate cannot follow: this is the **zero lower bound** (ZLB). The reason is child's play — there exists an asset that always yields exactly 0%: the banknote. Push rates decisively below zero and every rational depositor empties his account to fill a safe; the short-rate weapon jams. This chapter tells the story of what central banks invented when their single tool hit the floor: creating money to buy bonds, promising the future, targeting an entire curve — and, once, saving a currency with a sentence.

## Mechanical QE: reserves in exchange for duration

*Quantitative easing* fits into one accounting operation. The central bank buys bonds — government bonds mostly, sometimes mortgage or corporate — and pays for them by **crediting the reserve account** the selling bank holds with it. It draws this money from nowhere: it writes it into existence. Two balance sheets move:

| | Assets | Liabilities |
|---|---|---|
| **Central bank** | + 100 government securities | + 100 reserves (bank A's account) |
| **Bank A** (seller) | − 100 government securities, + 100 reserves | unchanged |

The central bank grows on both sides; the commercial bank, for its part, merely **swaps one asset for another** — a 10-year security for a sight deposit at the central bank. Not a single euro lands in a household's account at this stage: reserves are the money *of banks*, circulating only between them and the central bank.

Why would this swap bring long rates down? Decompose a 10-year rate as module 4 taught:

$$
y_{10} = \underbrace{\text{average expected short rates over 10 years}}_{\text{expectations}} + \underbrace{TP_{10}}_{\text{term premium}}
$$

QE attacks **both terms** — that is its double channel. The **signalling channel**: a central bank stuffing its balance sheet with 10-year securities ties its own hands — raising its rates would inflict losses on its own portfolio; the purchase makes the promise of durably low rates credible, and the expectations term falls. The **duration-scarcity channel** (*portfolio rebalancing*): by pulling hundreds of billions of duration out of the market, the central bank drains the supply of interest-rate risk; the investors who wanted it — insurers, pension funds — fight over what is left, the **term premium** compresses, and the crowded-out migrate into credit and equities, whose premia compress in turn. That is the avowed goal: since the overnight rate can fall no further, go after **every other rate in the economy** — mortgage, corporate, equity — directly.

<GoFurther suffixe="“money printing”: why M0 exploded while M2 did not follow">
The reflex objection — "creating money = inflation" — confuses two monies that the aggregates keep apart. **M0**, the monetary base, is banknotes + reserves: the *central bank's* money. **M2** is the public's deposits + cash: the money *that circulates in the economy*, created by **bank credit**. QE inflates M0 mechanically; it only inflates M2 if banks lend more — and nothing forces them to: reserves are not "lent out" to households, they stay inside the interbank circuit. The American numbers speak for themselves: from 2008 to 2014, the monetary base is multiplied by almost five (from about \$850 billion to ~\$4 trillion), while M2 keeps growing at its pre-crisis pace (~6% per year) and core inflation languishes *below* the 2% target — the money multiplier collapsed, that is all. The 2020-2021 episode is the instructive counter-example: this time M2 jumps by ~26% in one year — not through QE alone, but because **fiscal** transfers (checks, guaranteed loans) deposit money directly into the public's accounts — and inflation follows in 2021-2022. The takeaway for the oral: it is not the size of the central bank's balance sheet that makes inflation, it is the money that reaches the real economy.
</GoFurther>

## The programmes: fifteen years of balance-sheet inflation

**The Fed** opens the ball in November 2008: QE1 (~\$1.75 trillion of mortgage-backed securities, agency debt and Treasuries), then QE2 in 2010 (\$600 billion of Treasuries), then QE3 in 2012 — the first *open-ended* programme: \$85 billion per month "until substantial improvement in the labor market". The balance sheet goes from about \$900 billion in mid-2008 (~6% of GDP) to ~\$4.5 trillion at the end of 2014 (~26% of GDP). March 2020 pulverizes everything: in a few weeks, the Fed buys more than in a full year of QE3, then \$120 billion per month — the balance sheet peaks near **\$9 trillion in the spring of 2022, roughly 35% of GDP**.

**The ECB**, after the emergency purchases of the sovereign debt crisis, launches the **APP** (*Asset Purchase Programme*) in March 2015, whose core is the **PSPP** — public-sector securities, €60 to 80 billion per month depending on the phase, more than €3 trillion cumulated. March 2020: the **PEPP** (*Pandemic Emergency Purchase Programme*), envelope raised to €1.85 trillion, endowed with unprecedented flexibility — purchases may deviate from the country allocation keys to crush spreads as they reopen. The Eurosystem's balance sheet peaks near €8.8 trillion in mid-2022: **around 65% of euro-area GDP**. The prize goes to the Bank of Japan, pioneer of the genre since 2001: more than **120% of GDP**, with equity ETFs on the asset side along the way. The orders of magnitude to keep for a jury: in fifteen years, the major balance sheets went from a low-water mark of 5-15% of GDP to a range of **30% to 60% and beyond**.

## What it did — and what it stands accused of

Measuring the effect of QE is a treacherous exercise: you never observe the world without QE. The dominant method, the **event study**, measures the jump in yields in the hours surrounding the announcements. Verdict for QE1: on the order of **50 to 100 bp of decline in the US 10-year** depending on the windows and the studies — mostly through the term premium, in line with the scarcity channel. The later programmes measure smaller: anticipated, hence already in the prices — you recognize the logic of the consensus, which returns in the next chapter. Handle these numbers with tongs in front of a jury: the academic ranges are wide, and saying "about 100 bp for QE1, less afterwards, with genuine uncertainty" beats false precision.

The criticisms, for their part, are solid. **Inequality**: QE works *by inflating asset prices* — bonds, equities, real estate — held first and foremost by the wealthiest households; the redistributive effect is the mechanism, not an accident. **Zombification**: crushed rates keep non-viable firms on life support — BIS studies document the rise of "zombie firms" — at the expense of capital reallocation. **Fiscal dominance**: when the central bank holds a quarter or a third of its government's debt, the border with monetary financing — forbidden by the European treaties — becomes a matter of faith; and the day inflation demands rate hikes, every extra point makes the public debt dearer, the very debt the central bank is supposed to ignore. These three criticisms share one trait: they do not bite at three months, but at ten years.

## QT: the film in reverse, much slower

*Quantitative tightening* runs the machine backwards: the balance sheet must slim down. Two methods. **Selling** the securities — almost never used (the Bank of England in 2022 is the exception): selling duration by the hundreds of billions means triggering exactly the rate shock you want to avoid. **Letting it run off** (*run-off*): not reinvesting the securities as they mature, with **monthly caps** to smooth the flow — the 2022 Fed caps at \$60 billion of Treasuries plus \$35 billion of mortgage-backed securities per month. Janet Yellen promised a process "as boring as watching paint dry".

Why so much caution, when QE had been done at forced march? Because of a fundamental asymmetry: in QE, the central bank *adds* reserves to a system that absorbs them without flinching; in QT, it *withdraws* them — without knowing where the floor is, the level of reserves below which the financial plumbing seizes up. Nobody knows that number in advance; you discover it by touching it.

<GoFurther suffixe="September 2019: the day QT found the floor">
The Fed's first QT, 2017-2019: reserves melt from about \$2.8 trillion to \$1.4 trillion. On September 17, 2019 — an ordinary Tuesday, corporate tax payment day and Treasury auction settlement day — the demand for cash jumps and the supply does not follow: the overnight **repo** rate, the collateralized-lending rate met in module 4, spikes intraday to **about 10%**, and SOFR prints 5.25% while the policy rate targets 2-2.25%. Nothing insolvent anywhere: a pure plumbing failure — reserves, which everyone believed "abundant", had become just scarce enough for banks to refuse to lend them on one evening of tension. The Fed reinjects tens of billions in emergency repo the very next day, then resumes buying Treasury bills. The moral for the oral: QT is not QE in reverse — QE is flown by the dashboard, QT is flown by the sound of the engine, and the "ample reserves" regime adopted since is designed precisely never to grope for the floor again.
</GoFurther>

## Negative rates: going below the floor, in small doses

The zero floor turned out to be softer than advertised — physically storing billions in banknotes costs money (vaults, insurance, transport), which opens a little room below zero. The ECB dares in June 2014 (deposit rate at −0.10%) and goes down to **−0.50%** from 2019 to 2022; Switzerland goes to **−0.75%**, Denmark, Sweden and Japan follow. The mechanism: the excess reserves banks hold now *cost* instead of yielding — a tax on holding cash, meant to push them to lend.

The problem sits on the banks' balance sheets: they dare not pass the negative rate on to household deposits (which would flee into banknotes), so their liabilities stay at 0% while their assets yield less and less — the **net interest margin compresses**, and with it the capacity to lend. Hence the concept of the *reversal rate*: below a certain threshold, cutting the rate becomes **contractionary** — the weapon turns against its wielder. The ECB rigged up a shock absorber in 2019, *tiering*: part of the reserves is exempted from the negative rate, which makes the tax bearable without cancelling its signal.

## Forward guidance: the tool that costs only its credibility

If long rates are made of expected short rates, then **steering expectations is steering the curve** — without buying a single security. That is *forward guidance*, in two flavours. **Calendar-based**: "rates will stay low at least until mid-2013" (Fed, 2011) — simple, legible, but rigid: if the economy takes off, the promise becomes a trap. **State-contingent**: "as long as unemployment exceeds 6.5% and expected inflation stays below 2.5%" (Fed, 2012) — the commitment adjusts itself to the data, at the price of a more demanding read. In both cases, the tool is worth only what the word is worth: a central bank that reneges on its guidance will never be able to use it again. And exiting it is a minefield — in May 2013, Bernanke merely floats the idea of *slowing* the purchases: the US 10-year jumps from about 1.6% to 3% in four months, the **taper tantrum** — the market did not react to an act, but to the death of an expectation.

## YCC: Japan buys a price, not a quantity

In September 2016, the Bank of Japan clicks the last notch: *yield curve control*. No more target quantity — a target **price**: the Japanese 10-year will stay "around 0%", in a band initially of ±0.1%. The theoretical elegance is real: a *perfectly credible* price target is self-fulfilling — nobody sells against a central bank with infinite ammunition, so it barely has to buy. The flip side reveals itself in 2022: when global inflation climbs back and everyone doubts the target, the BoJ must **buy massively to defend the band** — to the point of holding more than half of all Japanese government bonds, with a JGB market at times literally without a single trade. The exit proceeds by successive widenings of the band (±0.25%, then ±0.5% at the end of 2022, then a "reference threshold" at 1%), until abandonment in **March 2024** — accompanied by the first Japanese rate hike since 2007 and the end of negative rates. Eight years of YCC: the demonstration that an administered price is easy to hold as long as nobody tests it.

Mention finally, in the European arsenal, the **TLTROs**: long-term loans to banks whose rate *improves* if they lend to the real economy — during the pandemic, a bank meeting its credit targets borrowed from the ECB at **−1%**. Conditional subsidies to credit, in short: the unconventional does not travel only through securities markets.

## "Whatever it takes": three words, zero euros

Summer 2012. The euro area is coming apart at its spreads: the Spanish 10-year tops 7.5%, the Italian 6.5% — levels at which a debt stops being refinanceable — because the market is pricing a **redenomination** risk: the return to the peseta and the lira. On July 26, in London, Mario Draghi improvises three sentences, among them this one: "*Within our mandate, the ECB is ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the euro. And believe me, it will be enough.*" In September, the promise receives a vehicle: the **OMT** (*Outright Monetary Transactions*) — **unlimited** purchases of the debt of a state that accepts an adjustment programme.

The OMT has **never been activated**. Not one euro spent — and the Spanish and Italian spreads deflate by several hundred basis points in a few months. It is the purest illustration in the chapter: the market was pricing a self-fulfilling loop (doubt → higher rates → insolvency → doubt), and an *unlimited and credible* promise suffices to kill the loop — nobody sells against a buyer without limits anymore, so the buyer has nothing to buy. The word was worth billions precisely because none had to be spent.

<Callout type="important" titre="The arsenal in one grid: quantities, prices, promises">
All of the unconventional sorts into three families. **Quantities**: QE and QT — you buy or let melt a *stock*, and the price adjusts. **Prices**: negative rates and YCC — you fix a *rate*, and the quantity becomes endogenous (sometimes monstrous, ask the BoJ). **Promises**: forward guidance and the OMT — you commit the *future*, and if credibility holds, neither quantity nor price has to move. The efficiency ranking is counter-intuitive and makes good oral answers: the most powerful tool of the 2008-2022 period cost nothing (three words from Draghi), and the most balance-sheet-expensive one (the Fed's ~\$9 trillion) has effects estimated in tens of basis points. A central bank's reserve currency is not its balance sheet: it is its word.
</Callout>
