After equities and bonds, this module opens territory of a different nature: here, what trades is not a security but money itself. A currency pays neither coupon nor dividend — held in an account, it simply earns the interest rate of its monetary zone, and you will see in chapter 2 that this nuance contains the entire theory of forward FX. Before the theory, the terrain: who trades what, where, how a quote is read, and why it moves.

## The biggest market in the world — in volume

The figure was already laid down in module 1: about **\$7,500bn traded every day** in the foreign exchange market (BIS triennial survey, 2022), and the 2025 survey points to an even higher volume, on the order of \$9,600bn. The reflex learned in module 1 applies as is: this is a **volume** — a daily flow — not an outstanding stock. Comparing this throughput to the stock of global equity market capitalization would make no sense; in throughput, on the other hand, no market comes anywhere near FX.

Second singularity: there is no stock exchange for currencies. FX is an **OTC** market (*over the counter*): a worldwide network of banks quoting one another and quoting their clients, with no central order book and no single listing authority. Direct consequence: the market never closes during the week. It opens Monday morning in Wellington and Sydney, follows the sun through Tokyo, Singapore and Hong Kong, passes through London, and closes Friday evening in New York — **24 hours a day, 5 days a week**.

Decentralized does not mean evenly spread. Activity concentrates in a few centres: **London alone handles on the order of 38% of global volumes** — a time-zone inheritance (the London day overlaps Asia in the morning and America in the afternoon) as much as an ecosystem one — ahead of New York (about 19%), Singapore, Hong Kong and Tokyo. And one currency crushes all the others: the US dollar appears in **nearly 9 trades out of 10**, including between two counterparties with nothing American about them — it is the system's vehicle currency.

## Reading a quote: direction above all

Every exchange rate is written as a **pair**:

<Callout type="definition" titre="BASE/QUOTE pair">
A pair is written BASE/QUOTE, and the rate is **the price of one unit of the base currency, expressed in the quote currency**. EUR/USD = 1.10 reads: 1 euro (the base) is worth 1.10 dollars (the quote). Buying the pair means buying the base and selling the quote; selling it is the reverse.
</Callout>

This convention is the first thing to lock down, because everything else in the module — forwards, carry, parities — breaks if the direction of reading is allowed to drift.

<Callout type="piege" titre="THE FX trap: direction">
EUR/USD moves from 1.10 to 1.15: **the euro appreciates** (it buys more dollars) and the dollar depreciates — even though the "dollar number" went up. Symmetrically, USD/JPY going from 150 to 155 is a **stronger dollar** against the yen, since the dollar is the base currency there. "The dollar is up" is therefore an ambiguous sentence until you specify the pair and the dollar's place in it. The reflex that saves you in an interview: before any reading, restate the rate as "price of one unit of BASE in QUOTE" — a rise in the rate is always an appreciation of the base, never anything else.
</Callout>

Four pairs, the **majors**, account between them for nearly half of all volumes:

| Pair | Reading | Share of volumes (BIS 2022, order of magnitude) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| EUR/USD | price of €1 in \$ | ≈ 23% |
| USD/JPY | price of \$1 in ¥ | ≈ 13% |
| GBP/USD | price of £1 in \$ — "cable" | ≈ 10% |
| USD/CHF | price of \$1 in CHF | ≈ 4% |

Pairs without the dollar — EUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, EUR/CHF — are called **crosses**: historically, their rate was derived by going through the two dollar pairs, and desks still arbitrage them against that implied value. Who quotes whom is not a free choice: the market applies a conventional hierarchy — **EUR > GBP > AUD > NZD > USD > the rest** — with the higher-ranked currency taking the base position. That is why you will always see EUR/USD and never USD/EUR, always USD/JPY and never the reverse.

That leaves the unit for measuring moves: the **pip** (*percentage in point*). On pairs quoted to 4 decimal places, the pip is the **4th decimal** (0.0001): from 1.1000 to 1.1025, EUR/USD gained 25 pips. One important exception: yen pairs quote to 2 decimal places, and the pip there is the **2nd decimal** (0.01) — USD/JPY going from 155.02 to 155.32 has moved 30 pips, not 3. The "big" number in front (1.10; 155) is called the *big figure*: between professionals, often only the pips are announced, the big figure being assumed known.

## Bid/ask: a market of price makers

Module 1 laid down the mechanics: a market maker quotes a two-way price, and the spread is its pay. In FX, that machinery runs at its most extreme setting. A bank asked for EUR/USD answers, say, **1.0998 / 1.1002**: it buys your euros at 1.0998 (the *bid*) and sells you euros at 1.1002 (the *ask*). On the majors in liquid hours, the spread routinely comes down to **around one pip** — the tightest market in the world, in keeping with its volumes.

Tight does not mean free:

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The cost of a round trip: €363.64 per million">
You buy €1m against dollars at the ask 1.1002, then sell it back immediately at the bid 1.0998 — a 4-pip spread, mid-rate 1.1000. The friction cost is the relative spread applied to the amount:

$$
1\,000\,000 \times \frac{1.1002 - 1.0998}{1.1000} = 1\,000\,000 \times \frac{0.0004}{1.1000} = \text{€}363.64
$$

And not €400: the 4 pips are collected in *dollars* (\$0.0004 per euro traded, i.e. \$400), which must be converted back at the mid-rate to express them in euros — 400/1.10 = €363.64. That is 0.036% of the amount: trivial for a one-off transaction, considerable for anyone who turns their book over several times a day.
</Callout>

Who trades? The map of players from module 1, almost line for line. At the centre, a **handful of large market-making banks**, which trade with one another in the interdealer segment and quote everyone else. Around them: **corporates** (an exporter converts its receipts, hedges its future invoices — it opens chapter 2), **asset managers and hedge funds** (which convert in order to invest, or take currency positions in their own right), and **central banks** — managing reserves, and intervening, rarely but memorably, to weigh on their currency.

## Spot, T+2 and the plumbing

The "spot" market does not settle on the spot. A **spot** transaction settles on **value date T+2**: two business days between the trade and the actual exchange of the two amounts (T+1 on USD/CAD). This lag, a legacy of the era of manual confirmations across time zones, carries its own risk: paying your leg and never receiving the other — the settlement risk identified in module 1 on the post-trade side, which the CLS infrastructure neutralizes today by settling both legs simultaneously for a large share of the market.

Execution, for its part, has become almost entirely electronic. Two interdealer platforms long structured the reference prices — EBS and Reuters Matching — before the landscape fragmented into a mosaic: multi-contributor platforms, single-bank platforms, algorithmic execution. In the middle of this continuous flow, the market gave itself a fixed point: the **4pm London WM/Reuters fix**, a reference rate computed over a short trading window, used to value funds and indices the world over. Its fame has a flip side: in 2013, investigations revealed that traders at several large banks were sharing information about their client orders and coordinating their executions around that window; the fines ran into the billions and the calculation window was widened to make manipulation harder. A reminder: even the most liquid market in the world is not above microstructure — a fixing is a point of concentration, hence a target.

## Why FX moves

Three families of drivers are enough for this module.

- **Interest rate differentials.** Capital goes where the pay is: if rates rise faster on one side of the Atlantic, flows follow and the currency concerned tends to appreciate. It is the most powerful driver at the horizon of a few months — and chapter 2 will show you the most surprising part: this differential *mechanically* sets the currency's forward price, with no forecast whatsoever.
- **The balance of payments.** A country that exports more than it imports generates net purchases of its currency; a deficit country the reverse. It is a slow, underlying force, whose horizon is counted in years rather than weeks.
- **Risk appetite.** Currencies have personalities: when markets panic (*risk-off*), capital flows back into the **safe-haven currencies** — the yen and the Swiss franc first among them, the dollar often alongside — and high-yielding currencies sell off. The yen owes that status, among other things, to its role as a borrowing currency: in times of stress, positions funded in yen are unwound and the massive buying back of yen drives it up. Chapter 3 will give those positions a name: the carry trade.

<GoFurther suffixe="why cable quotes 'the wrong way round'">
Most currencies quote with the dollar as the base (USD/JPY, USD/CHF, USD/CAD) — the legacy of a post-war monetary system organized around the dollar. Two big exceptions, and two stories. Sterling first: until 1971 it was not decimal (1 pound = 20 shillings = 240 pence), and quoting "so many dollars for 1 pound" was infinitely more convenient than the reverse — the convention outlived decimalization. Its nickname "cable" comes from the 1866 transatlantic telegraph cable, which transmitted precisely this rate between London and New York. The euro next: at its birth in 1999, the convention was fixed from the outset — the euro is the base currency against *all* others, dollar included; yet the legacy currencies it replaced (the mark, the franc) quoted the other way round. From these successive inheritances came the modern hierarchy EUR > GBP > AUD > NZD > USD > the rest — the AUD and NZD, currencies of British tradition, having kept the pound's convention. No economic logic in any of it: pure history, fossilized in the quote screens.
</GoFurther>
