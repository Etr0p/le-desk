Chapter 5 ended on a promise: to put a number on what it costs to "stay invested" in commodities via futures. For that, you must first give up an illusion: there is no such thing as **the** price of oil. There are dozens of them — the July contract's, August's, December's, next year's — and their alignment draws a **futures curve**. The shape of that curve has two names every commodities professional says ten times a day, and its slope determines whether a long position gets richer or quietly erodes. Welcome to the chapter that separates the pros from the tourists.

## The futures curve and the cost of carry

Where does the price of a futures contract expiring at $T$ come from? From the same arbitrage reasoning as the FX forward of the previous chapters: the **cash and carry** (buy spot and carry). If I can buy the commodity at spot $S$, finance it, store it, and sell it forward at price $F$, then $F$ cannot durably stray from the all-in cost of the operation — otherwise, arbitrage. In simple linear terms:

$$
F(T) = S \times \left(1 + \frac{c}{100} \times T\right), \qquad c = \text{financing} + \text{storage} - \text{convenience yield}
$$

The first two terms speak for themselves: carrying the position ties up cash (financing) and tanks (storage). The third is the subtlety that makes the whole chapter: the **convenience yield** — literally the "dividend of availability". It is the value, for whoever holds the physical, of having it **on hand**: the refinery with crude in its tanks never grinds to a halt for lack of feedstock, the miller with wheat honours his contracts come what may. That service shows up on no bank statement — it is inferred from prices — but it reduces the net cost of carry exactly as a dividend reduces the cost of carry of a share.

Run the canonical example: spot at \$80, financing 4%, storage 2%, convenience yield 1%, 1-year horizon. The net carry is $4 + 2 - 1 = 5$%:

$$
F = 80 \times \left(1 + \frac{4 + 2 - 1}{100} \times 1\right) = 80 \times 1.05 = 84
$$

The 1-year futures trades at \$84: the curve rises. Now work the machinery yourself — it is the gesture that anchors it:

## Two regimes, two states of the physical world

**Contango**: the curve rises, $F > S$. It is the "normal" regime of a **well-supplied** market — inventories are comfortable, nobody pays a premium to have the material right now, and the forward simply quotes spot plus the cost of carrying it there. Gold lives in near-permanent contango: negligible storage, zero convenience (nobody urgently needs a bar), which leaves financing.

**Backwardation**: the curve falls, $F < S$. Rerun the formula with a tight market — financing 2%, storage 1%, but convenience yield 6%:

$$
F = 80 \times \left(1 + \frac{2 + 1 - 6}{100} \times 1\right) = 80 \times 0.97 = 77.60
$$

Spot is worth **more** than the forward, because having it **now** is expensive: low inventories, fear of shortage, the dividend of availability crushes the costs of carry. Backwardation is the signature of physical tightness — which is why desks read the short end of the crude curve as a real-time inventory indicator. One last landmark: the **basis** (spot − futures, negative in contango, positive in backwardation) converges mechanically to zero at expiry — the expiring futures becomes spot.

## The roll yield: where money changes hands in silence

A futures position is not eternal: the contract expires. To stay exposed, you must **roll** — sell the near contract, buy the next one. Innocuous in appearance; in reality this is where everything plays out. In contango, you sell 80 and buy back 84: at every roll, you pay the gap. The **roll yield** annualizes this friction:

$$
\text{roll yield} = \left(\frac{F_{\text{near}}}{F_{\text{far}}} - 1\right) \times \frac{1}{T} = \left(\frac{80}{84} - 1\right) \times \frac{1}{1} = -4.76\ \% \text{ per year}
$$

Read that number carefully: a long ETF in this market loses about 4.8% a year **without spot having moved a cent**. The loss comes from no fall in oil — it comes from the slope of the curve, which the fund climbs against the grain at every expiry, like a moving walkway taken the wrong way. In backwardation, the walkway reverses: you sell the near at 80, buy back the far at 77.60, and the roll **pays** $(80/77.60 - 1) = +3.09$% per year with spot unchanged. That is how producers and specialized funds make a living on markets that "don't go up".

<Callout type="piege" titre="&quot;The barrel did +20%, my ETF +6%: was I robbed?&quot;">
No: you paid the roll. This is THE costliest misunderstanding in retail commodities — a tracker's performance is not spot's, but spot **plus** roll yield, and in contango the roll yield is negative, year after year. The textbook case remains the American ETF USO in April 2020: massively invested in the nearest WTI contract just as the market was sinking into a super-contango — and as the May expiry was heading into negative territory —, the fund had to roll its positions in emergency, in disastrous conditions, then changed its methodology and carried out a reverse share split (1-for-8). The retail investors who had "bought oil cheap" watched the barrel rebound… and their ETF stay far behind. In an interview, knowing how to unroll this chain — contango → negative roll → structural erosion — classes you immediately.
</Callout>

## Who earns what: the risk-transfer reading

If the roll loses money for some, who wins it? The answer lies in the oldest theory on the subject: Keynes's **normal backwardation**. The producer — oilman, farmer — is structurally long his future production and wants to sleep at night: he **sells forward** to fix his selling price today. That permanent selling pressure on the maturities pulls futures **below** the future spot level the market expects. The speculator who takes the other side — the forward buyer — therefore collects, on average, the gap: a **risk premium**, payment for the insurance service he renders the producer. The futures market, in this reading, is above all a **risk transfer** mechanism: the hedger pays an implicit premium to be rid of his uncertainty, the speculator collects it for absorbing it.

A nuance for the panel, handled with care: *normal backwardation* and *observed backwardation* are not synonyms. The first compares the futures to the **expected** future spot ($F < \mathbb{E}[S_T]$ — unobservable); the second compares the futures to the **current** spot ($F < S$ — readable on the screen). A market can be in quoted contango while still offering a risk premium to the futures buyer. Confusing the two is the classic error — distinguishing them, the reflex that reassures a panel.

## Reading a curve in practice

Three reading gestures to finish. **The short end** (the first maturities) tells the immediate physical state: inventories, logistics, the tension of the moment — it is the part that inverts violently in crises. **The long end** tells something else: expectations of marginal production cost and long-run supply-demand equilibrium — it moves slowly. **Seasonality**, finally, bends the curve into recurring humps: natural gas quotes its winters dearer than its summers (heating), gasoline its summers (*driving season*), grains their lean periods before harvest — the explorer's "Winter gas" preset gives you the silhouette. Professionals actually trade these deformations without touching the price level: **calendar spreads** — buy one maturity, sell another — bet purely on the change in the curve's shape; it is one of the most active trades in the energy markets.

<GoFurther suffixe="decomposing the total return of a futures position">
The performance of an investor in commodity futures — and that of the BCOM or GSCI indices in their *total return* versions — decomposes into three additive bricks:

$$
\text{total return} \approx \underbrace{\text{spot return}}_{\text{change in spot}} + \underbrace{\text{roll yield}}_{\text{slope of the curve}} + \underbrace{\text{collateral yield}}_{\text{interest on cash}}
$$

The third term often surprises: a futures position ties up only the margin, the rest of the capital sleeps in Treasury bills — the *collateral yield* is their pay. Worked example on our market from the text: spot +5% over the year, roll yield −4.76% (contango), collateral invested at 3% ⇒ total return ≈ +3.2%, almost none of which comes from oil itself. The *excess return* versions of the indices exclude the collateral; the *total return* versions include it — yet another comparison never to make across different families (module 3's reflex on price versus total return indices). Historically, over the 2000-2010 years, some energy indices posted investor performance far below the spot trajectory: the roll, negative for years on end, had eaten the difference — the life-size demonstration that the slope of the curve is a performance component in its own right, not a technical detail.
</GoFurther>

Contango, backwardation, roll yield: you now hold the complete reading grid for a futures curve — the shape, its physical cause, and its cost for whoever takes the exposure. The module's last chapter changes universe one final time: an asset class born in 2009, with no physical to store and no cash flows to discount, that markets took fifteen years to tame.
