The previous chapter locked down the forward: it follows from spot and the rate differential by pure arbitrage — not an opinion, a trading-floor theorem. This chapter asks the forbidden question: and spot itself, what determines it? Two grand "economic parities" claim to answer — purchasing power parity for the very long run, uncovered interest parity for the medium run. The first walks slowly but eventually gets there; the second fails so consistently that its failure feeds one of the most famous and most dangerous strategies in the FX market: the carry trade.

## PPP: the law of one price applied to currencies

<Callout type="definition" titre="Purchasing power parity (PPP)">
**Purchasing power parity** starts from the **law of one price**: the same tradable good should, shipping costs aside, cost the same everywhere once converted — otherwise, you buy it where it is cheap and sell it where it is dear. Aggregated up to a whole basket of goods, the law yields an "equilibrium" exchange rate: the one that equalizes the purchasing power of the two currencies. If the basket costs $P^*$ in quote currency and $P$ in base currency, the PPP rate is $P^*/P$, homogeneous with the BASE/QUOTE rate of chapter 1.
</Callout>

The most famous illustration fits inside a sandwich. Since 1986, *The Economist* has published the **Big Mac index**: the same burger, produced locally with the same ingredients, sold in dozens of countries — a basket of goods all by itself, deliberately simplistic and deliberately playful. It is the example interview panels love, precisely because it forces you to unroll the reasoning cleanly. Suppose a Big Mac at \$5.80 in the United States and €5.00 in the euro area:

$$
\text{PPP}_{\text{EUR/USD}} = \frac{5.80}{5.00} = 1.16
$$

The rate that equalizes the price of the burger is 1.16 dollars per euro. If spot EUR/USD trades at 1.10, the gap to parity is:

$$
\left(\frac{1.10}{1.16} - 1\right) \times 100 = -5.17\ \%
$$

Mind the direction — this is where exam scripts go astray: spot is **below** PPP, so 1 euro buys only 1.10 dollars where the basket would justify 1.16 — it is the **base** currency, the euro, that is **undervalued** by 5.17%; and the dollar, symmetrically, that is overvalued. Verification reflex: convert €5.00 at spot and you get \$5.50 — the European Big Mac is *cheaper* than the American one at \$5.80, the signature of a cheap currency.

Why does PPP not hold at the horizon of a quarter, or even a year? First because goods prices are sticky while the exchange rate moves by the millisecond. Second because a large part of the basket is **not tradable**: the haircut, the rent, the service component of the Big Mac itself cannot be arbitraged by container. That is the heart of the **Balassa-Samuelson effect** (named after the two economists who formalized it in the 1960s): in rich countries, the high productivity of the exposed sector pulls up wages across the whole economy, hairdressers included — services there are therefore structurally more expensive, and rich-country currencies look durably "overvalued" in PPP terms without any arbitrage ever coming to correct the gap. The classic studies estimate that a deviation from PPP takes several years to close by half — the commonly cited order of magnitude is three to five years. Practical verdict: PPP is a very-long-run compass, precious for saying whether a currency is expensive or cheap, just about silent on what it will do between now and Christmas.

## Uncovered parity: the theory the data contradict

Second parity, more ambitious. **Uncovered interest parity** (UIP) transposes the forward's logic onto the *realized* future spot: if one currency pays 5% and the other 1%, investing in the first should provide no advantage in expectation — otherwise, everyone would do it. For returns to equalize, the high-rate currency must therefore **depreciate**, on average, by roughly the differential:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{depreciation of the high-rate currency}] \approx i_{\text{high}} - i_{\text{low}}
$$

The difference with the previous chapter's covered parity is fundamental: there, the forward is locked by a riskless arbitrage — the relationship holds to the pip. Here, nothing is locked: UIP is a bet on the future spot, not an arbitrage.

<Callout type="important" titre="The forward premium puzzle: the most documented anomaly in FX">
And that bet, the data lose. Over decades of history and across the major pairs, high-rate currencies have **not** depreciated by the differential at horizons of a few months or years — they have tended to depreciate less than predicted, or even to **appreciate**. This is the *forward premium puzzle*, brought to light by Eugene Fama in 1984: the empirical relationship comes out not only weaker than the theory, but often with the **wrong sign**. Market translation: pocketing the rate differential has historically paid on average, when the theory promised the exchange rate would claw it all back. At very long horizons, UIP regains some colour; at the horizon where desks live, it is contradicted. This anomaly is not an academic curiosity: it is the fuel of the carry trade.
</Callout>

## The carry trade: harvesting the differential

<Callout type="definition" titre="Carry trade">
The **carry trade** consists of borrowing a low-rate currency — the **funding currency** — to invest in a high-rate currency, and pocketing the differential as long as the exchange rate does not move too much. The canonical example: borrow yen at 0.5%, convert, invest in Mexican pesos at 10% — nearly 9.5 points of carry per year, at the mercy of a peso sell-off.
</Callout>

The mechanics fit on one line. On a notional $N$, with an investment at $r_{\text{target}}$, funding at $r_{\text{fund}}$ and a move $\Delta$ in the target currency (in %, positive if the target appreciates):

$$
\text{Result} = N \times \left( \frac{r_{\text{target}} - r_{\text{fund}}}{100} + \frac{\Delta}{100} \right)
$$

Run it on \$1m of notional, investment at 8%, funding at 2%. Calm year, stable exchange rate ($\Delta = 0$): 1,000,000 × 0.06 = **+\$60,000** — six points of carry collected while nothing happens. Year that turns bad, the target currency drops 10%: 1,000,000 × (0.06 − 0.10) = **−\$40,000**. A single bad year erases two thirds of a good one — and sell-offs in high-yield currencies often exceed 10%.

You recognize the profile: it is, line for line, module 2's "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" — lots of small regular gains, rare massive losses. History has confirmed it for every generation. In 2008, yen-funded carry trades unwound in panic: emblematic pairs like the Australian dollar against the yen lost more than 30% in a few months, and years of carry evaporated in a quarter. In August 2024, the same again at fast-forward: the Bank of Japan had just raised rates, disappointing American data did the rest — a brutal episode of unwinding in yen-funded carry, the yen appreciating sharply within weeks and the Nikkei losing more than 12% in the single session of August 5. The mechanism is self-reinforcing: losses force unwinds, which push the funding currency up, which worsens the losses of the remaining carriers — the steamroller accelerates precisely when everyone runs for the exit.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The carry trade's negative skewness: historical volatility lies">
Measure a carry trade's performance over three calm years: steady returns, modest volatility, flattering Sharpe ratio. All of it is true — and all of it is misleading. The distribution of carry returns has **negative skewness** and fat tails (module 2): the risk is not in the observed variance, it is in the rare jump that has not yet happened in your sample. Worse: that jump arrives in *risk-off* regimes, exactly when your other positions are hurting too. Presenting the carry as "a 6% return for 8% volatility" without mentioning the asymmetry is the misreading that disqualifies — mean and variance are not enough once the distribution is asymmetric.
</Callout>

## An over-determined price: the forecaster's humility

Let us sum up the FX trader's dashboard. At very long horizons, the exchange rate gravitates around its economic anchors: PPP, current accounts (a country in persistent deficit eventually sees its currency under pressure), the terms of trade. Day to day, it lives on something else: rate differentials and central bank surprises, macro releases, and the *risk-on/risk-off* pendulum (appetite for or aversion to risk) that lifts the safe-haven currencies — yen, Swiss franc — on bad days. The exchange rate is thus **over-determined**: too many legitimate forces pull in different directions, at different horizons, for a single model to settle the matter.

Hence a finding of humility every candidate should know: forecasting exchange rates is notoriously difficult — the FX equivalent of the SPIVA scorecards encountered in module 3. The founding academic result (Meese and Rogoff, 1983) shows that at horizons of a few quarters, structural exchange-rate models do not beat a simple random walk. Professionals draw the right conclusion: you do not "predict" FX, you reason in scenarios, market positioning and risk asymmetries.

<GoFurther suffixe="the UIP test in numbers and the wrong-sign beta">
How is UIP tested? With module 2's tool: a regression. You regress the realized change in the exchange rate on the rate differential observed at the start of the period:

$$
\Delta s_{t+1} = \alpha + \beta\,(i_{\text{high}} - i_{\text{low}})_t + \varepsilon_{t+1}
$$

If UIP holds, $\beta = 1$: one point of differential is paid for, on average, by one point of depreciation. Yet the classic studies on the major pairs, following Fama (1984), find $\beta$s that are often **negative** — on the order of −1 in the most cited historical estimates. Let us put a number on what that means: with a 3-point differential and $\beta = -1$, the high-rate currency does not lose 3% — it *gains* roughly 3, and the carry holder pockets nearly 6%: the carry plus the currency move. That is the "wrong-sign beta", the anomaly that has spilled the most ink in international economics. Two guard-rails before getting excited: the $R^2$ of these regressions are tiny and the coefficients unstable from one decade to the next — and above all, a line fitted on calm periods says nothing about the tails: the crash of 2008 or of August 2024 is precisely what extrapolating a regression does not see coming.
</GoFurther>

PPP, UIP, carry: this whole chapter assumed the currency's price was free to gravitate toward its parities. Yet for a large share of the world's currencies, that is false: their rate is administered, contained, defended. The next chapter opens the menu of exchange rate regimes — and tells the story of what happens when a defended parity gives way.
