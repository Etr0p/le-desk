This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the essentials chapter by chapter, the formula sheet, the traps and the reflexes. To reread the day before an oral — and to complete with the jury mode of the practice section. The guiding thread fits in one sentence: a bond is not a stream of cash flows, it is a **promise** — and this whole module teaches you to weigh that promise. The market displays it in basis points (the **spread**), the agencies in letters (the **rating**), and both are only disguises of the same underlying quantity: a probability of default married to a recovery rate — $EL = PD \times LGD$. The rest is engineering: lodging the doubt in a bond price ($y = r + s$), detaching it from the paper (the **CDS**), slicing it into floors (**securitisation**) — and knowing what time it is on the **cycle**'s clock.

## The essentials, chapter by chapter

1. **The price of the promise (ch. 1).** M4 managed the "when" (certain cash flows, moving rates); this module manages the "whether" — the very realisation of the flows. You do not lend to a company, you lend to a **rank** of its liabilities: the default queue — secured (R ≈ 60-70%), **senior unsecured (R ≈ 40%, the convention)**, subordinated (~20% or less), hybrids, equity. The thermometer: $s = (y - r) \times 100$ — 5.5% against 3.5% = **200 bps**. The benchmarks: euro IG 80-150 bps, HY 300-500 bps, distressed above 1,000 bps; iTraxx Main ~50-80, Crossover ~250-400. The spread pays MORE than the average default: expected loss + risk premium (defaults come in clusters, in recessions) + liquidity premium — the *credit spread puzzle*: a BBB "deserves" 18 bps and quotes at 120-150. And the founding asymmetry: upside capped at the promised yield, downside −60% — credit is a business of **avoiding losers**, where diversification is not a choice but the business model itself.
2. **Ratings (ch. 2).** Three houses (S&P and Moody's ~40% each, Fitch ~15%), an alphabet from AAA to D, notches — and ONE line that matters: the **BBB− / BB+** border, a contractual wall ("IG only" mandates, indices, ECB collateral, Basel) built on a continuous slope. The cumulative 5-year defaults to recite: AAA ~0.1%, BBB ~1.5%, BB ~6-8%, B ~15-20%, CCC ~40-50% — the ordinal works, the scale is violently non-linear. A rating is a **rule, not information**: the spread moves before the agency, but the letter is what triggers mechanical selling — the **fallen angel** cliff (Ford, March 2020: \$36bn switched in one day, the biggest in history, the Fed including fallen angels in its purchases). The fair trial of 2008: corporate ratings held up, it is the **structured** AAA that lied. And **notching**: same issuer, secured above, subordinated below — same PD, different LGDs.
3. **PD × LGD (ch. 3).** The central formula: $EL = PD \times LGD$, with $LGD = 100 - R$. The textbook case: PD 2%, R 40% ⇒ EL = **1.2%/year = 120 bps**. It is an EXPECTATION: the actual year gives 0 or −60, never −1.2 — hence spreads at 5-15 times the EL on IG. The inverse reading, the desk's: $PD_{implied} = s / LGD$ — 300 bps, R 40% ⇒ **5%/year**, a **risk-neutral** PD that overstates the historical frequency (factor of 2 to 5): the market prices stress, it does not forecast. And THE trap of the module: PDs do not add up, they compound — survival $(1-PD)^n$: PD 2% over 5 years = **9.61%** cumulative default, not 10%; PD 5% over 10 years = 40.13%, not 50%. You must be alive to die. (GoFurther: the intensity $\lambda = -\ln(1-PD)$, survival $e^{-\lambda t}$, and the credit triangle $s \approx \lambda \times LGD$.)
4. **Pricing the risky bond (ch. 4).** The desk's method: contractual flows INTACT, discounted at $y = r + s$ — the default is in the rate, not in the flows. The running example: coupon 5, r 3, s 200 bps ⇒ y = 5% = coupon ⇒ **par, exactly 100**; coupon 4 ⇒ 95.67, and 104.58 without the spread — the spread "costs" 8.91 price points without a single default having occurred. **Spread duration**: $\Delta P/P \approx -D \times \Delta s$ — D 4.5 and +50 bps: −2.25%; D 7 and +300 bps: **−21%**, a bond book losing like an equity book. A corporate differentiates TWICE — rates and spread, sometimes opposed (*flight to quality*). The decision criterion: **net yield = gross − PD × LGD** — 7% at PD 5% returns 4% net, beaten by a 4.5% risk-free: compare the NET, never the gross. And the grammar of curves: IG steep, distressed **inverted**; below 50-60% of par, quote in **price** — a bet on recovery.
5. **CDS (ch. 5).** JPMorgan, 1994: detach credit risk from the paper. Two legs: the premium — notional × s/10,000, i.e. **€200,000/year for 10M at 200 bps**, in quarterly instalments — and the protection at default: **notional × (1 − R) = 6M**, the LOSS, never the notional. The credit event (bankruptcy, failure to pay, restructuring) is declared by an ISDA committee, and the **auction** sets R for all contracts: Lehman, October 2008 — **8.625%**, sellers paid 91.375%; net settlements only ~\$5.2bn. The **basis** = s_CDS − s_cash (180 − 200 = −20 bps): the negative basis trade is a "no credit risk" position that dies of margin calls — the LTCM lesson. After the 2009 Big Bang: fixed coupons (100/500 bps), **upfront** ≈ Δspread × risky duration (~4.5), central clearing. The indices: **iTraxx Main** (125 IG), **Crossover** (75 HY), CDX on the US side. Three uses: hedge without selling, short without borrowing the paper, take exposure without balance sheet — the AIG position, now under margins.
6. **Securitisation (ch. 6).** Pool thousands of receivables, sell them in a **true sale** to an **SPV/FCT** (insulation in both directions), and **tranche**: tranche loss = clamp$((L-A)/(D-A))$ × 100. The anchors on equity 0-3 / mezzanine 3-6 / senior 6-100: at L = 5%, the mezzanine has lost **66.67%** of its notional — **leverage without borrowing**, 100/(D−A) = 33 to 1 inside the band. Enhancement (subordination, overcollateralisation, excess spread, reserve) manufactures AAA — on paper: everything rests on **correlation**. Independent defaults ⇒ law of large numbers, an impassable cushion; correlation → 1 ⇒ **bimodal** distribution, senior destroyed at once with the expectation unchanged. The senior is **short correlation**: a structured AAA survives every scenario but one. The bestiary: ABS, RMBS, **CLO** (managed leveraged loans — AAA without one default in 2008) versus **ABS CDO / CDO²** (re-securitising correlated cliffs). After 2008: **5% retention**, the STS label, and the covered bond — dual recourse, pool on balance sheet — which is NOT a securitisation.
7. **The cycle and the desk (ch. 7).** Four phases: expansion (tight spreads, cov-lite dominant — tomorrow's LGD), the turn (the real signal is **quantity**: the primary market closes), defaults (peak **12-18 months** later — death by refinancing: fixed-rate debt is rolled, and the maturity due in front of a closed window kills), the purge (the best vintages are bought in fear). HY spreads: a **leading indicator** — two-three quarters ahead of the statistics. The cycle's arithmetic: 7% nominal at PD 3% returns 5.2% net; at PD 8%, **2.2%** — less than a 3% government bond. The primary: syndication, book-building, new issue premium, windows. Who carries the risk: funds and ETFs (daily liquidity on an illiquid asset), insurers (mechanical sellers of fallen angels), CLOs (two thirds of loans), **private credit** (~\$1.5tn, unmeasured volatility — *volatility laundering*). The desk: market making by **RFQ** and inventory, hedged with the index; analyst, sales, syndication. The jury's question — "where are we?": spread levels, default rate, state of the primary.

## The traps that cost points

1. **Confusing spread and PD** (ch. 1, 3): the implied PD is computed by DIVIDING by the LGD — 300 bps at R 40% price 5%/year, not 3%; you only lose 60% of face value, not all of it.
2. **Adding PDs** (ch. 3): 5 × 2% ≠ 10% — survival compounds: $1 - (0.98)^5 = 9.61\%$. The gap explodes with the horizon (PD 5% over 10 years: 40.13%, not 50%).
3. **Taking the risk-neutral PD for a forecast** (ch. 3): "the market prices 5%" is correct, "the market forecasts 5%" is wrong — the spread contains risk and liquidity premia, the implied PD overstates the real frequency by a factor of 2 to 5.
4. **The big coupon** (ch. 4, 7): 7% gross at PD 5% returns 4% net — beaten by a 4.5% risk-free. The coupon was not yield but an advance repayment of losses. Compare the NET, never the gross.
5. **300 bps = 0.3%** (ch. 4): no — 300 bps = 3%. The conversion error that turns −21% into −2.1% in a spread duration calculation.
6. **"The CDS seller repays the notional"** (ch. 5): they pay the LOSS — notional × (1 − R), i.e. 6M on 10M at R 40%. The CDS compensates, it does not pay out a jackpot.
7. **"R ≈ 40% is a law"** (ch. 3, 5): it is a calm-times convention for senior unsecured — the Lehman auction returned 8.625%, and deep subordinated debt recovers almost nothing. Seniority sets R.
8. **Misreading the tranche's bounds** (ch. 6): the attachment is where the tranche STARTS losing, the detachment where it is destroyed — neither "wiped out at attachment" nor "intact until detachment": at L = 5%, the 3-6% has lost 66.67%, not 0 nor 100.
9. **"An AAA is an AAA"** (ch. 2, 6): the corporate AAA survives almost every scenario; the structured AAA survived every scenario EXCEPT one — correlation jumping. It is not the same letter.
10. **Confusing CLOs and ABS CDOs** (ch. 6): companies across varied sectors do not share the correlation of a single housing market — zero CLO AAA defaults in 2008, a massacre on the ABS CDO side. Judge the pool, not the structure.
11. **"The negative basis is free money"** (ch. 5): the trade is funded in overnight repo and marked every evening — if the basis moves from −20 to −80 bps, margin calls land before convergence. No credit risk ≠ no risk.
12. **"Widening spreads immediately choke the issuer"** (ch. 7): its existing debt is FIXED-rate — what kills is the maturity due in front of a closed primary, hence the default peak 12-18 months AFTER the turn.
13. **"The covered bond is a securitisation"** (ch. 6): pool on balance sheet, dual recourse, zero tranching — the exact opposite of the true sale.

## The twelve reflexes for the oral

1. Credit reads in two coordinates: **the name AND the rank** — "is this Renault bond risky?" calls for the counter-question "which one?": same PD, very different LGDs.
2. Facing any rating, place it first relative to **the BBB− / BB+ border** — the rest is detail. And remember: a rating is a **rule**, not information — the spread votes before it, but the letter triggers the mandates.
3. Facing any spread, decompose it: expected loss + risk premium + liquidity premium — then compute the implied PD ($s/LGD$) and call it risk-neutral.
4. The three letters by heart: $EL = PD \times LGD$ — 2% × 60% = 1.2% = 120 bps. The order of magnitude a jury expects without a calculator.
5. **You must be alive to die**: any PD cumulates by compounding survival, never by adding.
6. Compare the **net of expected losses** to the risk-free rate, never the gross — a big coupon can hide a negative net yield at the bottom of the cycle.
7. A corporate has **two legs** — rates and spread, same duration, sometimes in opposite directions: "is your portfolio long or short rates?" requires separating them.
8. Below 50-60% of par, switch languages: think in **price and recovery**, no longer in spread — and an inverted spread curve is the entrance sign of the distressed regime.
9. Selling protection is **selling options** (m8): collecting small and regular against a rare, massive loss — AIG is the \$500bn version.
10. Judge a securitisation by its **pool's correlation**, never by its structure — and be wary on principle of any RE-securitisation.
11. The cycle's real signal is **quantity, not price**: the primary market closing announces the defaults 12-18 months ahead — HY spreads see before the statistics do.
12. "Where are we in the cycle?" — three readings, not a prediction: **spread levels** against their history, the current and expected **default rate**, and the **state of the primary** (who can issue, at what premium, with what cov-lite share).

## Bridges to the rest of the course

- **Module 4 — rates and duration.** A corporate is a government bond **plus a spread**: same discounting, same duration — applied twice, to the rate and to the spread. And survival compounding is compound interest, in probabilities.
- **Module 7 — futures and clearing.** The post-2009 CDS lives under m7's infrastructure: central clearing, initial margins, daily calls — and the negative basis trade is m7's basis trade gone wrong, funded overnight against a gain at maturity.
- **Module 8 — options.** The protection seller has the option seller's exact profile: regular premium against a rare, massive loss — the market's psychological key, and the AIG position in miniature.
- **Module 9 — structured products and correlation.** The tranche is the worst-of at scale: subordination manufactures AAA as long as correlation stays low, and correlation jumps towards 1 in the exact scenario where you were counting on it.
- **Module 10 — central banks.** Credit spreads sit on central bankers' dashboards: a leading indicator of the cycle, two-three quarters ahead of the statistics — and the March 2020 Fed treated the fallen angel cliff as a market-plumbing problem.
- **Module 11 — the crises.** The loop closes: you saw spreads on fire — the 2007 interbank market, Greece's 3,350 bps, AIG, haircuts jumping — and you now know how to read them in peacetime. The thermometer before the fire: a Main at 60 bps describes a world asleep, at 120 a world that worries — it is by knowing how to read 60 that you understand what 600 means.

Each one-line answer is an entry point, not a complete answer: train yourself to develop them over two minutes in jury mode, and to put numbers on them with the module's exercises. One last word for the road: this module gave you the grammar of doubt — a price (the spread), an institutionalised opinion (the rating), an expectation linking them (PD × LGD), a pricer (y = r + s), an instrument that detaches (the CDS), a machine that slices (securitisation) and a tempo (the cycle). Further down the road, risk management (module 12) will take this credit risk into VaR, Basel III and stress tests — module 11's scars codified into rules. But the next step is **module 6 — FX, commodities, crypto**: the rest of the cash journey, where you leave borrowers' promises for assets that promise nothing — a currency pays no coupon, a barrel does not redeem at par, and new tools will be needed to price them. You now know how to weigh a promise; it is time to learn to weigh what gives none. On to module 6.
