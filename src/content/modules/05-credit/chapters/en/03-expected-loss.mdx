Chapter 1 set up the thermometer — the spread, the price of credit risk in basis points — and chapter 2 showed that the agencies' letters are just default probabilities in disguise: an A is one default per thousand issuer-years; a B, a few percent per year. It remains to do what the desk does every morning: connect the two. How much is a given default probability *worth*, in basis points? The answer fits in a three-letter multiplication — the most profitable formula of the module in an interview, because it reads both ways: from the probability to the spread, and from the spread to what the market believes.

## The three letters that sum up the job

<Callout type="definition" titre="PD, LGD, R">
The **PD** (*probability of default*) is the probability that the issuer defaults over **one year** — the quantity that chapter 2's ratings encode. The **recovery rate R** is what you get back after default, as a % of **notional** — market convention: **R ≈ 40%** for *senior unsecured* debt. The **LGD** (*loss given default*) is the complement, what you actually lose: $LGD = 100 - R$, i.e. 60% of notional in the standard case. PD says *if*, LGD says *how much* — and you need both to quantify anything.
</Callout>

The 40% R is not a law of nature: it is the historical average for senior unsecured debt, and it is the **seniority** from chapter 1 that moves it. Debt secured by collateral (covered bond, senior secured) recovers far more — its LGD collapses; deep subordinated debt comes after everyone else and often recovers next to nothing — LGD close to 100%. Same issuer, same PD: the rank in the bankruptcy queue changes the whole price.

## The central formula: EL = PD × LGD

How much does carrying this risk for a year cost, *in expectation*? The expected loss:

$$
EL = PD \times LGD
$$

On the module's textbook case: PD 2%/year, R 40% hence LGD 60%, and $EL = 2\% \times 60\% = 1.2\%$ of notional **per year**. Converted into desk language, this expected loss is an "actuarial" spread: the spread that exactly compensates for the average loss, i.e. **120 bp** here. Three letters, one multiplication, and you know what any signature "deserves" — the order of magnitude a jury expects off the top of your head, no calculator.

<Callout type="important" titre="EL is an expectation — nobody ever loses 1.2%">
An interview answer to polish: the actual year **never** delivers −EL. It delivers 0 (no default, by far the most frequent case) or −LGD (default: −60% at a stroke). The EL is the mean of that very asymmetric lottery, not a scenario. That is precisely why observed spreads sit **systematically above** the actuarial spread: in investment grade, the market pays **5 to 15 times the EL** — an issuer with a 0.1%/year PD "deserves" 6 bp and quotes at 80 to 120 bp. The gap has a name, the *credit risk premium puzzle*, and an explanation: the spread remunerates the stress (tail risk, illiquidity, tax), not the average loss. You earn almost nothing for being right, you lose 60% for being wrong.
</Callout>

## The reverse reading: what the market is pricing

The desk uses the formula far more often the other way round. The spread reads off the screen; the unknown is the PD the market is currently paying:

$$
PD_{implied} = \frac{spread}{LGD}
$$

A bond quoting **300 bp** with an assumed recovery of 40%: $PD = 3\% / 0.60 = 5\%$ per year. The market is pricing one default every twenty issuer-years — and you can set that number against chapter 2's historical ratings table.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Risk-neutral PD ≠ default forecast">
This implied PD is called **risk-neutral**: it is the probability that makes the spread actuarially fair, not a forecast. Since the spread contains the risk premium from the previous Callout, the implied PD mechanically **overstates** the historical default frequency — often by a factor of 2 to 5. It is not that the market is wrong: it prices the stress, not the mean. In an interview, saying "the market prices 5% default per year" is correct; saying "the market forecasts 5% of defaults" is the trap — the nuance between risk-neutral and historical probability is exactly the difference between an M2 exam paper and a desk answer.
</Callout>

## You have to be alive to die: compounding

A 2% PD is annual. What does the risk become over 5 years, a bond's horizon? The additive reflex — 5 × 2% = 10% — is wrong, and it is THE trap of the chapter. To default in year 3, you must have survived years 1 and 2: defaults do not add up, they **compound**, exactly like the interest in module 4 — but in survival probabilities:

$$
P(\text{survival over } n \text{ years}) = (1 - PD)^n \qquad P(\text{cumulative default}) = 1 - (1 - PD)^n
$$

On the textbook case: $(0.98)^5 = 90.392080\%$ survival, hence **9.607920%** cumulative default — not 10%. The gap looks cosmetic? It grows with the horizon and with the PD: at PD 5% over 10 years, the additive rule gives 50% — a coin flip — while compounding yields **40.126306%**. Ten points of difference, purely because you have to be alive to die: each year of survival shrinks the population still exposed to default.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Take the expected loss into your own hands">
Three experiments. **One**: set PD 2%, recovery 40% — recover the 1.2%/year EL and the 120 bp from the text, then drop the PD to 0.1% to see the 6 bp an investment grade name "deserves", and re-read the warning at the bottom of the screen: the gap versus the 80-120 bp quoted IS the risk premium. **Two**: PD 5%, 30-year horizon — the additive rule promises 5 × 30 = 150% of "probability" (the screen charitably caps it at 100%) while the compounded curve plateaus toward 78.5%: the gap between the two grows with the horizon, and you can see it. **Three**: push the recovery to the extremes — at R = 0% every default costs everything (the deep subordinated assumption), at R = 75% even a big PD costs almost nothing: that is the whole logic of secured debt, and proof that chapter 1's seniority is a pricing variable, not a legal footnote.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="default intensity λ: the continuous version">
Professional credit models do not work in discrete annual PD but in **default intensity** $\lambda$: default is the first jump of a Poisson process, and survival becomes an exponential, $P(\text{survival to } t) = e^{-\lambda t}$ — the exact analogue of moving from annual to continuous compounding in module 4. The link to the discrete version: one-year survival equals $e^{-\lambda} = 1 - PD$, so $\lambda = -\ln(1 - PD)$ — for PD 2%, $\lambda = 2.0203\%$, nearly identical, since $\ln(1-x) \approx -x$ for small $x$. The payoff of the detour is the **credit triangle**, the most compact relationship in the business: under constant intensity, the equilibrium spread is $s \approx \lambda \times LGD$ — the formula EL = PD × LGD was just its discrete version. All the CDS pricing in chapter 5 (bootstrapping survival curves from quoted spreads) rests on this $\lambda$. But in an interview, the discrete annual version is enough: the numbers agree to the second decimal for realistic PDs, and the examiner wants to check that you compound survival — not that you can integrate an exponential.
</GoFurther>

You now know what default costs *in expectation*: 1.2% per year for the textbook signature, convertible into 120 bp, and cleanly cumulable over any horizon provided you compound. But an expected loss is not yet a price. A risky bond is dated coupons, a final redemption, and a risk-free curve in the background — the credit risk now has to be housed inside module 4's discounting machine. That is exactly what the desk does, with a move of disarming simplicity: chapter 4.
