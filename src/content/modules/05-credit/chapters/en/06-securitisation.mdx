Chapter 5 showed you how to detach credit risk from **one** bond: the CDS isolates one issuer's default and sells it separately. Securitisation performs the reverse operation, and at far larger scale: it takes the risk of an entire **portfolio** — thousands of auto loans, credit card balances, mortgages — and slices it vertically, into hierarchical layers of risk that different investors buy at different prices. It is the most powerful and most misunderstood financial technology in the credit market: it funds a real share of the economy, it nearly destroyed the system in 2008, and it rests entirely on a single variable this chapter will dissect — default correlation. Module 11 showed you the film; here, you take the projector apart.

## Why securitise: making the illiquid liquid

A €15,000 car loan to a household in Clermont-Ferrand is a perfectly illiquid asset: no investor wants to analyse it, let alone buy it on its own. But 50,000 identical loans put in a pool become a statistical object: predictable monthly cash flows, a documented historical default rate, a granularity that dilutes each individual borrower. Securitisation turns that illiquid mass into **tradable securities**, with three motivations for the selling bank:

- **Free up the balance sheet.** The transferred loans leave the asset side: the regulatory capital they immobilised is released to lend again. The bank moves from a business of *storing* risk to a business of *flow*.
- **Fund itself.** The pool is sold today for cash, instead of waiting thirty years of instalments.
- **Transfer the risk.** The borrowers' default becomes the investor's problem — that is the stated purpose, and that is where the shoe pinches.

<Callout type="piege" titre="Originate-to-distribute: the incentive flaw">
When whoever grants the loan keeps it for thirty years, he vets the borrower — his balance sheet depends on it. When he sells it on within the week for securitisation, he is paid **on flow**: origination fee, volume, next. Every link in the chain — broker, arranger, rating agency — is paid as the asset passes through, and the final risk lands with an investor who has never seen the borrower. Credit quality becomes everyone's business, that is to say, no one's. This **originate-to-distribute** model is the behavioural fuel of 2007 (the full story is in module 11, chapter 5); post-crisis regulation attacks precisely this, and you will see how at the end of the chapter.
</Callout>

## The plumbing: true sale and the dedicated vehicle

The legal mechanics matter as much as the financial mechanics. The bank does not sell the loans to the investor: it transfers them to a **dedicated vehicle** — an SPV (*special purpose vehicle*), an FCT (fonds commun de titrisation) under French law — created for this single transaction. The transfer must be a **true sale**: the loans leave the bank's balance sheet for good and become the property of the vehicle, which issues, on the other side, securities backed by the pool's cash flows.

<Callout type="definition" titre="SPV / FCT: watertight in both directions">
The vehicle is a shell with no employees and no other activity, whose sole reason for existing is **legal separation** — and it works in both directions. Direction one: the investor has recourse **only** to the pool — if the borrowers default en masse, the selling bank owes nothing, it has sold. Direction two: if the **bank** goes bankrupt, its creditors cannot seize the transferred loans — they belong to the vehicle, not to it. The investor is therefore not buying a bank's signature: he is buying pure exposure to a portfolio of borrowers, and the whole analysis shifts from the seller's balance sheet to the pool's statistics.
</Callout>

## The reactor core: pooling, then tranching

Pooling alone is not enough: a pool of BBB loans is still a BBB asset. The magic — treat the word with suspicion — comes from **tranching**: the vehicle's liability side is cut into hierarchical tranches, defined by their **attachment** point $A$ and **detachment** point $D$ in % of the pool. A tranche starts losing when the portfolio's cumulative losses $L$ reach its attachment, and it is wiped out entirely when they reach its detachment:

$$
\text{tranche loss} = \min\!\left(100,\ \max\!\left(0,\ \frac{L - A}{D - A} \times 100\right)\right)
$$

This is the `perteTranchePct` function from the module's library, and you need to feel it in your fingers on the canonical structure — **equity** 0-3% (the first loss), **mezzanine** 3-6%, **senior** 6-100%:

- Pool losses $L = 2\%$: the equity has lost $2/3$ of its notional, the mezzanine is **intact** (0%), so is the senior.
- $L = 3\%$: the equity is **wiped out** — and it was already half gone (50%) at $L = 1.5\%$.
- $L = 5\%$: the mezzanine has lost $(5-3)/(6-3) = 66.666667\%$ of its notional. The pool has lost "only" 5%; the tranche, two thirds.
- $L = 8\%$: the mezzanine is wiped out (100%), and the senior — intact up to exactly 6% — starts to be eaten into.

Look at what the formula does: a **continuous** loss on the pool becomes a **cliff** for the tranche investor. Between 3% and 6% of pool losses, every additional point destroys a third of the mezzanine — a 33-to-1 sensitivity, without borrowing a cent. It is **leverage without borrowing**: subordination concentrates risk exactly as debt would, but it is housed in the structure, invisible in the classic ratios. Handle it yourself:

## The cash flow waterfall and credit enhancement

The simulator warns you honestly: it applies losses in one shot, as a % of the pool. Reality is temporal. Every quarter, the pool's cash flows — interest, then principal — run down a **waterfall**: the senior is served first, then the mezzanine, then the equity picks up what is left. **Coverage tests** stand guard: if the collateral value relative to the tranches' notional (the OC test, *overcollateralisation*) or the interest received relative to the interest due (the IC test) falls below its threshold, the waterfall is **re-routed** — the flows destined for the lower tranches are diverted to pay down the senior early. The simulator's subordination order is exact; the timing is a simplification.

Subordination is in fact only one floor of **credit enhancement** — the art of manufacturing AAA out of a BBB pool. The full arsenal: **subordination** (the thickness of the tranches below), **overcollateralisation** (the pool holds 105 of loans for 100 of securities issued — the surplus absorbs the first losses), **excess spread** (the pool earns 6% while the tranches cost 4% — the annual margin mops up current defaults before they touch anyone), and a **reserve account** fed along the way. Stack it all up, and the probability of the senior taking a loss becomes — on paper — infinitesimal. On paper.

## Correlation: what makes a structured AAA different

Here is the chapter's real subject, the one that separates a candidate who recites from a candidate who understands. All the senior's protection rests on a **distributional** assumption: the shape of the pool's loss distribution. If defaults are **independent**, the law of large numbers works for you: on 10,000 loans with a 2% PD, realised losses will almost surely land close to 2% — the distribution is tight around its mean, and a 6% cushion is a rampart. If correlation **jumps toward 1**, the distribution turns **bimodal**: almost nobody defaults, or everybody does at once. The expected loss has not moved; the rampart has become a Maginot line.

The senior is therefore structurally **short correlation**: it has sold insurance against the scenario where all the borrowers die together, and its rating rested on the assumption that this scenario did not exist — in 2007, literally: "house prices have never fallen nationally". A **national** housing downturn is precisely the event that synchronises every default in the pool. That is the fine mechanics behind module 11's story: a CDO AAA could quote at 30 **without a single additional default being realised**, because the market was repricing the correlation — hence the shape of the distribution — and not the current losses. A corporate AAA survives almost every scenario; a structured AAA survives every scenario except one. It is not the same letter.

<GoFurther suffixe="the bimodal distribution, on the back of an envelope">
Pool of 100 loans of 1 each, PD 2%, zero recovery for simplicity, senior tranche attached at 6%. **Case 1, independent defaults**: the number of defaults follows a binomial with mean 2 and standard deviation $\sqrt{100 \times 0.02 \times 0.98} \approx 1.4$; the probability of exceeding 6 defaults is below 0.5% — the senior is virtually untouchable, and that is what the 2006 models computed. **Case 2, perfect correlation**: a single draw for the whole pool — losses of 0% with probability 98%, of 100% with probability 2%. The pool's expected loss is **identical** in both cases: 2%. But the senior does not live the same life: untouchable in the first world, it is destroyed once every fifty in the second — and destroyed *whole*, not dented. Interview moral: securitisation does not reduce aggregate risk by one iota, it **redistributes it along the distribution**; the price of a tranche is a bet on the shape of that distribution, that is, on correlation. The so-called "correlation desks" do nothing but trade that parameter.
</GoFurther>

## The bestiary: knowing who is who

- **ABS** (*asset-backed securities*): the generic term, and in practice pools of auto loans, card balances, consumer loans. Granular, short, statistically docile — the best-behaved segment.
- **RMBS / CMBS** (*residential / commercial mortgage-backed securities*): residential and commercial real estate. Subprime RMBS are the raw material of 2008.
- **CLO** (*collateralized loan obligations*): pools of **leveraged loans** — loans to indebted companies, often under LBO — selected and actively managed by a manager. **Never** to be confused with the CDO of ABS: companies across varied sectors are not 50,000 mortgages backed by the same housing market. AAA CLO tranches came through 2008 **without a single default** — the argument every CLO salesperson will put to you, and it is factually correct.
- **CDO of ABS and CDO²**: re-securitisation — mezzanine tranches of RMBS put back into a pool and re-tranched, then CDO tranches re-pooled in their turn. Each floor re-manufactures AAA out of the unsold inventory of the floor below, and each floor **stacks the correlations**: the incoming mezzanines, already cliffs, all die together in the same housing scenario. The CDO² has been essentially extinct since 2008; know what it was, and why.

## After 2008: securitisation disciplined

The regulatory response targets the incentive flaw at the root. **Mandatory 5% risk retention** (*skin in the game*): the originator must keep at least 5% of the risk of each transaction on its balance sheet — no more full distribution, every link stays exposed. The European **STS label** (simple, transparent, standardised, 2019): no re-securitisation, documented default histories, loan-by-loan data for the investor. And a distinction to know for the interview: the **covered bond** (the German Pfandbrief, the French obligation foncière) is **not** a securitisation — the pool stays on the bank's balance sheet and the investor has **dual recourse**, on the bank AND on the pool; zero tranching, zero risk transfer, the prudent two-century-old version. Today, securitisation quietly funds autos, consumer credit and SME loans, and the European agenda is trying to **revive** it — a market still several times smaller than America's, when Europe would like to unclog bank balance sheets that carry 80% of the financing of its economy. The tool was not guilty; the use was.

You now know how to read a spread (ch1-3), price default (ch4), detach it (ch5) and slice it (this chapter). What remains is understanding when all of this breathes together: why spreads grind tighter for years and then all widen the same month, what a credit desk concretely does with these instruments, and how to position within the cycle. That is chapter 7.
