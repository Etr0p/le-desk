Your toolbox is complete. You can read a spread (chapter 1), a rating (chapter 2), convert them into default probability and expected loss (chapter 3), price the bond that carries them (chapter 4), detach the risk from the paper (chapter 5) and slice it into tranches (chapter 6). What remains is what no tool provides: the **tempo**. Credit is not a stationary market oscillating around a mean — it is a market that breathes in long cycles, where the same spreads are "expensive" one year and "given away" three years later, and where careers are made on a single good reading of the moment. This last substantive chapter answers the two questions a jury always chains together: how does the credit cycle turn — and who, on the desk and across from it, makes a living from it.

## The cycle in four beats

Module 11 showed you the cycle through its catastrophes; in normal regime, it unfolds in four beats, always in the same order:

- **The expansion.** Spreads are tight, the primary market wide open, refinancing easy — and discipline melts along with fear: lender protections get lighter year after year. Calm manufactures audacity: it is Minsky's instability hypothesis (module 11, chapter 1) — stability is destabilising, because every year without a default justifies one more notch of leverage.
- **The turn.** Spreads widen — but the real signal is not the price, it is the **quantity**: the primary market shuts. Nobody wants to underwrite a high yield issue any more, at any reasonable price.
- **The defaults.** The default peak follows the turn by **12 to 18 months**. Default is a *delayed* event: an issuer does not die when its spread widens, it dies when its debt matures in front of a closed window.
- **The purge and the restart.** Defaults clean out the most fragile balance sheets, the survivors refinance expensively, spreads overcompensate the coming losses by a wide margin — and the best high yield vintages are built exactly there, in the fear. Then spreads tighten, memory fades, and the expansion begins again.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Cov-lite (covenant-lite)">
A **covenant** is a contractual clause protecting the lender: a leverage cap, a minimum interest coverage ratio, a ban on moving assets out — with early repayment or renegotiation at stake if the borrower breaches it. A **cov-lite** loan has none, or almost none. The cov-lite share of loan issuance is the thermometer of excess: close to zero in the early 2000s, a majority at the top of recent cycles. A delayed and under-appreciated consequence: without covenants, the lender cannot step in *early* — the company keeps deteriorating all the way to a payment default, and the **recoveries** of the following cycle are lower. Today's cov-lite is tomorrow's LGD.
</Callout>

<Callout type="important" titre="Death by refinancing, not by prices">
The point candidates miss: a high yield issuer almost never repays its debt — it **rolls** it. Its existing debt is fixed-rate: a spread that doubles changes nothing about its current coupons. What kills it is the issue that matures in a quarter when the primary is closed: no re-issuance possible, no cash for redemption, default — even while the business itself was still running. Hence the 12-18 month lag between the spread turn and the default peak: it is the time the maturities take to catch up with the issuers. The credit market kills by asphyxiation, rarely by bullet.
</Callout>

## Spreads see before the statistics

This mechanism makes spreads a **leading indicator** of the economy — one of the best on module 10's dashboard (chapter 6). GDP and employment are published weeks late and revised for months; the high yield market, for its part, votes in real time with the marginal lenders' money: at the first serious doubt about the cycle, they withdraw, the HY primary seizes up and spreads widen — often two or three quarters before the recession shows in the numbers. And the sensitivity is brutal: a crisis widening of 300 bp on a portfolio with a spread duration of 7 costs $-7 \times 300/100 = -21\%$ — chapter 4's arithmetic, which explains how a HY book can lose like an equity book. You spent module 11 watching spreads on fire; keep the loop in mind: in normal regime, they become the thermometer again — the one module 10's central bankers themselves watch to gauge financial conditions.

## The refinancing wall: when the carry no longer pays

The tool for dating the danger is called the **maturity wall**: the year-by-year profile of the amounts of HY debt to be re-issued. A tall wall right in front of a closed primary is next year's default list. And the arithmetic of the investor who decides — or not — to hold through the wall is chapter 3's:

$$
\text{net yield} = \text{nominal yield} - \text{PD} \times \text{LGD}
$$

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The big coupon tested by the cycle">
A high yield portfolio delivers 7% nominal yield, with the conventional 40% recovery (LGD 60%). Mid-cycle, expected PD 3%: net yield $= 7 - 3 \times 0.6 = 5.2\%$ — comfortably above an OAT at 3%, the risk is paid. The cycle turns, expected PD rises to 8%: net yield $= 7 - 8 \times 0.6 = 2.2\%$ — **less than the risk-free**, for infinitely more risk. Nothing changed in the portfolio: only the expectation of defaults moved, and the big coupon became an optical illusion. The reflex to recite: you always compare the yield **net of expected losses** to the risk-free, never the gross — the "big coupon" trap promised in chapter 3, here dismantled.
</Callout>

## The primary: syndication, book-building, windows

The primary market deserves a stop, because it is what opens and closes the cycle. A bond issue is manufactured like module 3's IPO, only faster: the issuer mandates a **syndicate** of banks, which sounds out investors, builds the **order book** (*book-building*) in a few hours, and sets the final spread according to demand. To convince investors to leave their existing positions, the new paper offers a **new issue premium** of a few basis points over the secondary — the NIP, whose size is itself an indicator: near zero in an expansion, gaping when the market doubts. And all of this lives by **windows**: a few favourable days after results or between two macro releases, when issuers pile in — then weeks of closed doors. A HY issuer's treasurer does not manage a rate, he manages a calendar.

## Who carries the risk

Facing the primary, four big families of buyers — and knowing *who* holds the risk tells you how the next crisis will propagate:

- **The IG and HY funds**, mutual funds and ETFs: the bulk of the market. Their structural fragility: they promise their holders daily liquidity on an asset that does not have it.
- **The insurers**, big natural holders of duration — but rating-constrained: chapter 2's BBB− cliff makes them *mechanical* sellers of fallen angels, at the worst moment.
- **The CLOs**, structural buyers of two thirds of the loans (chapter 6): as long as the structure's arbitrage works, they buy — a powerful and procyclical support for the loan market.
- **Private credit**, the rising force of the current cycle: about \$1.5 trillion of unlisted direct lending, extended by closed-end funds to companies too small or too levered for the bond market. The extra yield remunerates the illiquidity — but the absence of a market price has a flip side: nobody knows the true value until they need to sell.

<GoFurther suffixe="private credit, or the volatility no one measures any more">
The asset class was born of regulation: Basel III and Dodd-Frank made risky lending more expensive for banks after 2008, and specialised funds occupied the ground — *direct lending*. The sales pitch: returns of 10-12%, a tiny displayed volatility. The second half of the pitch deserves the scalpel: these loans are quoted nowhere, their valuation is quarterly and expert-based — the volatility has not disappeared, it is simply **not measured**. The Anglo-Saxons call this *volatility laundering*. The defenders offer two serious replies: the funds are closed-end (no holder to redeem tomorrow, hence no forced selling — the structure is more stable than a daily HY fund), and a sole lender can restructure fast, without a committee of 200 creditors. The honest point for the interview: the asset class has not yet been through a full default cycle at its current size — \$1.5 trillion of credit risk has migrated from supervised balance sheets to vehicles that publish no prices, and we will find out at the next default peak what the "marks" are really worth. Saying that — neither panic nor marketing — is exactly the nuance expected.
</GoFurther>

## The credit desk: liquidity by appointment

It remains to place the jobs — sell-side first. The core is **bond market making**, and it looks nothing like module 1's equity order book (chapter 5): tens of thousands of issues most of which do not trade every day, no central order book — liquidity happens *by appointment*. The client sends an **RFQ** (*request for quote*) to a few banks; the flow trader quotes, takes the bonds into his **inventory**, and carries spread risk in stock while looking for the other side — hedging with chapter 5's CDS index. Next to him: the **CDS and indices desk**, which trades protection and tranches; and the world of **distressed** — specialised funds that buy troubled issuers' debt at 30 and live off restructurings, where the analysis becomes as much legal as financial. Around the traders, three entry-level jobs: the **credit analyst**, who does the fundamentals — net leverage/EBITDA, interest coverage, covenants, maturity schedule; **credit sales**, who knows his clients' portfolios better than they do; and **syndication**, at the hinge between issuers and the market, pricing the primary's windows.

## "Where are we in the cycle?" — the jury's question

Facing the jury, the question will land in one form or another: "where are we in the credit cycle?". Nobody expects a prediction — they expect a **positioning**, in three readings: the **level of spreads** against their history (Main around 50-80 bp and Crossover around 250-400 bp in calm times — chapter 5; double that in stress); the current **default rate** and its 12-month forecast; and the **state of the primary** — who manages to issue, at what premium, with what share of cov-lite. Three public numbers, one structured answer: that is the difference between reciting a course and reading a market.

The module's thread is complete: a price of risk — the spread (chapter 1) —, an institutionalised opinion — the rating (chapter 2) —, an expectation linking them — PD × LGD (chapter 3) —, a bond price embodying it (chapter 4), an instrument detaching the risk (chapter 5), a machine slicing it (chapter 6), and a cycle setting the tempo for the whole. All that is left is to fold the map: the summary gathers the formulas, the orders of magnitude and the interview reflexes of the entire module.
