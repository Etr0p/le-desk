Chapter 4 closed on a promise: a theorem that connects the binomial tree to Black-Scholes, and yesterday's sample to tomorrow's inference. Here it is. But to gauge its reach, you first have to face an uncomfortable truth: every figure computed since chapter 2 — means, volatilities, correlations — was computed on **samples**. And a sample is only one draw among all the draws that could have happened.

## Sample versus population: why you will never observe μ

The **population** is the complete set we would like to know; the **sample** is the fragment we actually observe. In finance, the nuance is subtler than in an election poll: the "population" of a strategy's returns is not a finite list sitting somewhere, it is the random process that generates them — its true mean $\mu$ and true standard deviation $\sigma$ are parameters of the model, unobservable by nature. Last year's 252 daily returns are only one realization of that process: a single history drawn from all the histories that could have unfolded.

Faced with an out-of-reach parameter, we build an **estimator** (a computing recipe that turns the sample into an approximate value of the parameter): the sample mean $\bar{x}$ estimates $\mu$, the sample standard deviation $s$ — with its $n-1$ denominator justified in chapter 2 — estimates $\sigma$.

The decisive conceptual point, the one that separates descriptive statistics from inference: **an estimator is itself a random variable**. Replay the year with different randomness, and $\bar{x}$ would take a different value. The real question is therefore not "what is $\bar{x}$?" — you have computed it — but "how much does $\bar{x}$ move from one sample to the next?". If it moves little, your figure is solid; if it moves a lot, you have measured noise to three decimal places.

## The central limit theorem: the shape and the speed

The answer fits in one theorem — and chapter 2 had already delivered half of it without saying so. In the "why $n-1$" box, we used the variance of the sample mean: $\sigma^2/n$. Its standard deviation is therefore $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, and that number has a name: the **standard error** (the standard deviation of an estimator — here, the dispersion of $\bar{x}$ around $\mu$ from one sample to the next).

The **central limit theorem** (CLT) adds the missing information, the shape:

$$
\bar{x} \;\approx\; N\!\left(\mu,\; \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \quad \text{for } n \text{ large enough}
$$

In plain words: whatever the starting distribution — flat, discrete, violently skewed — the **distribution of the sample mean** becomes a normal bell centered on $\mu$, with width $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, as soon as $n$ is sufficient (the substantive conditions: independent draws from the same distribution, and a finite variance). Two miracles in one: the *shape* is universal, and the *speed* of tightening is known — in $\sqrt{n}$.

Why is this THE theorem of finance? Because finance is saturated with sums and averages. Log returns add up — hence the lognormal prices of chapter 4. The suitably rescaled binomial becomes normal — hence the bridge between the binomial tree and Black-Scholes. Every sample mean becomes normal — hence this chapter's confidence intervals and chapter 6's tests. And every simulation-based estimator is an average — hence Monte Carlo convergence in chapter 7. Four pillars, one theorem.

## The 95% confidence interval

The CLT turns uncertainty into geometry. If $\bar{x}$ is normal around $\mu$, then — chapter 4's rule, $\Phi(1.96) = 0.9750$ — in 95% of samples, $\bar{x}$ falls within $1.96$ standard errors of $\mu$. Flipped around: the 95% **confidence interval** around the observed mean,

$$
CI_{95\,\%} = \left[\;\bar{x} - 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\;,\; \bar{x} + 1.96\,\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\;\right]
$$

is built to capture $\mu$ in 95% of cases (in practice, the unknown $\sigma$ is replaced by $s$ — legitimate without remorse once $n$ exceeds a few dozen).

A complete, and instructive, example. Over one year ($n = 252$ days), a stock shows an average daily return $\bar{x} = 0.08\,\%$ with a standard deviation $s = 1.2\,\%$. The standard error is:

$$
\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1.2}{\sqrt{252}} = \frac{1.2}{15.8745} = 0.0756\,\%
$$

hence the margin $1.96 \times 0.0756 = 0.1482$ points, and the interval:

$$
CI_{95\,\%} = [\,0.08 - 0.15\;,\; 0.08 + 0.15\,] = [\,-0.07\,\%\;,\; +0.23\,\%\,]
$$

The reading stings: $0.08\,\%$ a day is roughly 20% a year ($0.08 \times 252 = 20.16\,\%$) — a spectacular performance. And yet the interval **contains zero**: a full year of daily data is not even enough to assert that this stock's mean return is positive. Daily volatility (1.2%) dwarfs the daily mean (0.08%) so thoroughly that the signal drowns. Estimating a mean return is one of the most thankless problems in finance — remember this contrast: volatility is estimated quickly and well, the mean slowly and badly.

<Callout type="piege" titre='"There is a 95% chance that μ is in the interval" — the sentence that gets you eliminated'>
This is THE trick oral question, and the natural phrasing is wrong. Within this chapter's framework, $\mu$ is not random: it is a fixed number, unknown but frozen. What is random is the **interval** — it depends on the sample that was drawn. Once computed, $[-0.07, +0.23]$ either contains $\mu$ or it does not; there is no probability left in play, only ignorance. The correct statement is about the *method*: "across repeated sampling, 95% of the intervals built this way would contain μ". The confidence qualifies the interval-making machine, not the interval it made. In an oral exam, drawing this distinction unprompted is worth a great deal — missing it after being asked is worth a great deal too, in the other direction.
</Callout>

## Sample size: the price of precision

The standard error shrinks as $1/\sqrt{n}$, and that square root has a brutal economic consequence: **halving the error requires quadrupling n**. Going from 252 days to 1,008 days — four years — only doubles the precision on the mean. And for our example's interval to finally exclude zero (holding mean and volatility constant), you would need $n$ such that $1.96 \times 1.2/\sqrt{n} < 0.08$, i.e. about 864 days: nearly three and a half years of data to validate statistically what the 20% annualized figure seemed to shout.

Hence a rule of professional lucidity: a six-month **backtest** (the retrospective simulation of a strategy on historical data) showing a handsome average proves close to nothing — its standard error is so wide that the result is compatible with a strategy possessing no skill at all. Before getting excited about a performance, the reflex is always the same: divide $\sigma$ by $\sqrt{n}$, and check whether the mean survives its own error bars. Chapter 6 will turn that reflex into a decision protocol: the hypothesis test.

<GoFurther suffixe="an honest sketch of the CLT">
Honesty first: the clean proof requires a tool — characteristic functions, cousins of the Fourier transform — that lies beyond this course. But the sketch can be told. Set $S_n = x_1 + \cdots + x_n$ (independent variables, same distribution, mean $\mu$, finite variance $\sigma^2$) and standardize: $Z_n = (S_n - n\mu)/(\sigma\sqrt{n})$. To each distribution we attach its characteristic function $\varphi(t) = E[e^{itX}]$, which has two virtues: a sum of independent variables has as its characteristic function the **product** of the individual ones, and near $t = 0$, $\varphi$ expands as $1 - t^2/2 + \cdots$ for a standardized variable — the higher-order terms carrying the skewness and the tails. Raising to the power $n$ after standardization, each factor is evaluated at $t/\sqrt{n}$: the terms beyond $t^2$ are crushed at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$ or faster, and what remains is $(1 - t^2/2n)^n \to e^{-t^2/2}$ — precisely the characteristic function of the standard normal. All the individuality of the starting distribution (its skewness, its kurtosis) evaporates in the passage to the limit: only $\mu$ and $\sigma$ survive. The sketch also says where the theorem cracks: if the variance is infinite — tails so fat that $\sigma$ does not exist — the expansion collapses, and the limit is no longer normal but belongs to the so-called stable distributions, with heavy tails. The fat tails of chapter 4 are therefore not an anomaly of the CLT: they mark the exact boundary of its territory.
</GoFurther>
