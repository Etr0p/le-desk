This page condenses the seven chapters of the module into a final-stretch revision: the formulas, the reflexes, the numbers and the traps. To reread the day before an oral — and to complete with the jury mode of the practice section to test yourself under real conditions.

## The key formulas

| Formula | When to use it |
| --- | --- |
| $FV = PV\,(1+r)^n \qquad PV = \dfrac{FV}{(1+r)^n}$ | Move a single cash flow through time — compound toward the future, discount back to today |
| $PV = F \times \dfrac{1-(1+r)^{-n}}{r}$ | Value a stream of constant cash flows (loan payments, rents, coupons); inverted, it gives the payment on a loan |
| $P = \dfrac{F}{r}$ | Price a perpetuity, first flow one period away — the first building block of dividend models |
| $NPV = -I_0 + \sum_{t=1}^{n} \dfrac{F_t}{(1+r)^t}$ | Decide whether to invest: positive NPV, accept — and when NPV and IRR conflict, NPV decides |
| $r_g = \left[\prod_{i=1}^{n}(1+r_i)\right]^{1/n} - 1$ | The compound performance actually achieved over several periods; the arithmetic mean estimates the expectation for one period |
| $s^2 = \dfrac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(r_i - \bar{r})^2$ | Measure the dispersion of a sample — $n-1$ because the mean is itself estimated on the same data |
| $\sigma_{\text{ann}} = \sigma_{\text{daily}} \times \sqrt{252}$ | Annualise a volatility: variances add over time, so the standard deviation grows with the square root of time |
| $\rho_{AB} = \dfrac{s_{AB}}{s_A\,s_B}$ | Normalise the covariance to make it readable: always between −1 and +1, linear relationships only |
| $P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B \mid A)\,P(A) + P(B \mid \bar{A})\,P(\bar{A})}$ | Reverse a conditioning — from "positive given sick" to "sick given positive"; in practice, think in headcounts |
| $P(X = k) = \dbinom{n}{k}\,p^k\,(1-p)^{n-k}$ | Count $k$ successes out of $n$ independent trials — the very structure of the binomial tree used to price options |
| $z = \dfrac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$ | Measure a distance to the mean in standard deviations, to read the probability off the normal table |
| $SE = \dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$ | The standard error: how much a sample mean moves from one draw to the next — the unit of measure of uncertainty |
| $CI_{95\%} = \bar{x} \pm 1.96\,\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$ | Bracket $\mu$: the method catches the true mean in 95% of samples |
| $t = \dfrac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}$ | Test a hypothesis: the observed gap counted in standard errors — beyond 1.96 (large $n$), significant at 5% |
| $b = \dfrac{\text{cov}(x,y)}{\text{var}(x)} \qquad a = \bar{y} - b\,\bar{x}$ | Estimate the least-squares line — regressing a stock on the market gives its beta |
| $\sigma_p^2 = w_A^2\,\sigma_A^2 + w_B^2\,\sigma_B^2 + 2\,w_A w_B\,\rho_{AB}\,\sigma_A\sigma_B$ | The variance of a two-asset portfolio: the correlation is what decides the diversification gain |

## The rules to remember

- **Arithmetic ≥ geometric, always.** The gap is about $\sigma^2/2$: the *volatility drag* — at equal average, volatility erodes compound performance. +10% then −10% is not 0 but −0.50% per year.
- **1% daily vol ≈ 16% per year.** Variances add over time, so the standard deviation grows with the square root of time: ×√252 ≈ ×16 from daily to annual.
- **Bayes = think in headcounts.** "Out of 10,000 people…": the tree in headcounts makes the result almost obvious and keeps the conditioning straight — $P(A \mid B) \neq P(B \mid A)$.
- **"At least one" = 1 − P(none).** The complement turns an enumeration nightmare into three lines: at least one six in four rolls = $1 - (5/6)^4 \approx 51.8\%$.
- **Twice the precision = four times the data.** The standard error shrinks as $1/\sqrt{n}$: precision is quadratically expensive — going from 252 to 1,008 days only halves the error.
- **Significant ≠ important.** Significance is a matter of √n, importance a matter of cents: with a million observations, 0.01% a year becomes significant — and invisible after transaction costs.
- **Small sample → Student.** With $\sigma$ estimated by $s$ on $n = 25$, the two-sided 5% threshold is 2.06 (24 degrees of freedom), not 1.96: the "significant" fund at $t = 2.0$ slips back under the bar.
- **Correlation is not causation.** Ice creams and drownings: the hidden variable is summer. And you regress returns, never prices — two trending series always share the hidden "time" factor.
- **The normal lies about the extremes.** Real kurtosis > 3: fat tails make the "impossible" happen — 19 October 1987, −22.6% in one session, more than 20 standard deviations. The bell is good at the centre, not in the tails.
- **Every estimator is a mean, and every mean carries a $1/\sqrt{N}$ error.** Backtests, confidence intervals, Monte Carlo: same law — one extra decimal of precision costs a hundred times more simulations.

## The orders of magnitude to know by heart

| Value | What it says |
| --- | --- |
| 68.3% / 95.4% / 99.7% | The mass of a normal between $\mu \pm 1\sigma$, $\pm 2\sigma$, $\pm 3\sigma$ |
| 1.96 | The two-sided 95% z: $\Phi(1.96) = 0.9750$ — confidence intervals and the 5% significance threshold |
| 1.645 | The one-sided 95% z: a single 5% tail |
| 2.06 | The two-sided 5% Student threshold for 24 degrees of freedom — small sample, a higher bar than 1.96 |
| √252 ≈ 15.87 ≈ 16 | The annualisation factor for daily volatility: 1% a day ≈ 16% a year |
| Kurtosis 3 | The normal-distribution benchmark — real daily returns score well above it (fat tails) |
| Rule of 72 | Capital invested at r% doubles in roughly 72/r years (6% → 12 years) |
| 15% / 30% | The annualised volatility of a calm equity index / of a market under stress |

## The classic traps

1. **The IRR seduces, the NPV decides** (ch. 1): non-conventional cash flows = multiple IRRs or none; a percentage is blind to the size and length of the project — in case of conflict, NPV wins, because it counts in euros.
2. **+10% then −10% ≠ 0** (ch. 2): the loss applies to a higher base — €100 ends at €99, i.e. −0.50% per year geometric. A zero "average of returns" can hide money actually lost.
3. **Intuition is the trap** (ch. 3): 1/2 on the two children, "it changes nothing" on Monty Hall, "99%" on the medical test — the reflex answers are wrong; what saves you is the protocol: sample space, conditioning, tree, headcounts.
4. **Assuming normality without saying so** (ch. 4): the bell is excellent at the centre and wrong in the tails — a Gaussian VaR underestimates precisely the losses it claims to bound.
5. **"There is a 95% chance that μ is in the interval"** (ch. 5): μ is a fixed number, the interval is what is random — the confidence qualifies the interval-making machine, never the interval made.
6. **The p-value is not the probability that H₀ is true** (ch. 6): $p = P(\text{data} \mid H_0)$, not $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$ — reversing it is the prosecutor's fallacy, desk version.
7. **Regressing prices** (ch. 7): two series that rise with time are mechanically correlated — overwhelming R², zero information. You regress returns (changes), never levels.

## What the jury asks most

| Standard question | The one-line reflex |
| --- | --- |
| "A fund made +10% then −10%: its performance?" | −0.50% per year, not 0: geometric mean √(1.10 × 0.90) − 1 — the 10% loss applies to a higher base than the gain |
| "A 99%-reliable test comes back positive: probability of being sick (1% prevalence, 5% false positives)?" | Think in headcounts: out of 10,000 people, 99 true positives against 495 false ones — 99/594 = 1/6 ≈ 16.67%; rarity crushes reliability |
| "What does a 95% confidence interval mean?" | The method catches μ in 95% of samples; the computed interval either contains μ or it does not — the confidence qualifies the machine, not the result |
| "2% outperformance a year for 25 years: skill or luck?" | t = 2/(5/√25) = 2.0: significant by a hair under the Gaussian (p ≈ 4.6%) — and not at all under Student with 24 df (threshold 2.06); twenty-five years for so fragile a conclusion, that is the order of magnitude to remember |
| "Your backtest is significant at 5%: convinced?" | Ask how many tests were run: out of 100 worthless strategies, P(at least one "works") = 1 − 0.95¹⁰⁰ = 99.4% — demand out-of-sample validation, then ask whether the effect is worth money |

Each one-line answer is an entry point, not a complete answer: train yourself to develop them over two minutes in jury mode, and to put numbers on them with the module's exercises.
