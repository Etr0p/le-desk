Last chapter of the module, and a change of register: after the concepts, the production tools. **Linear regression** turns chapter 2's correlation into a prediction machine — it manufactures betas and factor models. **Monte Carlo** turns chapter 5's central limit theorem into a computation machine — it is what prices whatever has no formula.

## Simple linear regression: the best line through the cloud

Two paired series — the market's returns $x_i$ and a stock's returns $y_i$, week by week — form a scatter of points. Regression looks for the line $y = a + bx$ that best summarizes the cloud. "Best" in the sense of **least squares** (OLS, for ordinary least squares): $a$ and $b$ are chosen to minimize the sum of squared vertical errors $\sum_i (y_i - a - b x_i)^2$. The squares penalize large errors heavily and, above all, make the solution explicit (the derivation fits in six lines — end-of-chapter box):

$$
b = \frac{\text{cov}(x, y)}{\text{var}(x)} \qquad a = \bar{y} - b\,\bar{x}
$$

The slope is a normalized covariance — chapter 2's tools, recycled as they stand — and the line always passes through the mean point $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

The textbook case first: the three points $(1, 2)$, $(2, 4)$, $(3, 6)$. Means $\bar{x} = 2$, $\bar{y} = 4$; covariance $\big((-1)(-2) + 0 + (1)(2)\big)/2 = 2$; variance of $x$: $1$. Hence $b = 2/1 = 2$ and $a = 4 - 2 \times 2 = 0$: the line $y = 2x$, which passes exactly through all three points.

Reality is less docile. Five weeks of returns (in %):

| Week | Market $x$ | Stock $y$ | $x - \bar{x}$ | $y - \bar{y}$ | Product | $(x-\bar{x})^2$ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | −2 | −3 | −3 | −4.2 | 12.6 | 9 |
| 2 | +1 | +2 | 0 | 0.8 | 0 | 0 |
| 3 | +3 | +5 | 2 | 3.8 | 7.6 | 4 |
| 4 | −1 | −2 | −2 | −3.2 | 6.4 | 4 |
| 5 | +4 | +4 | 3 | 2.8 | 8.4 | 9 |

Means: $\bar{x} = 1$, $\bar{y} = 1.2$. The sums: $\sum (x-\bar{x})(y-\bar{y}) = 35$ and $\sum (x-\bar{x})^2 = 26$, hence:

$$
b = \frac{35}{26} = 1.3462 \approx 1.35 \qquad a = 1.2 - 1.3462 \times 1 = -0.1462 \approx -0.15
$$

The estimated line: $\hat{y} = -0.15 + 1.35\,x$. When the market gains 1%, the stock gains 1.35% on average; but the points no longer sit *on* the line — in week 5, it predicted (with unrounded coefficients) $5.24\,\%$ where the stock only delivered 4%: a miss of −1.24 points.

## Reading a regression: β, R² and residuals

Three numbers sum up the professional reading of a regression.

**The slope, alias β.** Regressing a stock's returns on the market's is exactly the definition of the CAPM *beta* (the capital asset pricing model of module 12): the stock's sensitivity to market moves. Our $\beta = 1.35$ reads "the stock amplifies the market by 35%" — an aggressive profile; $\beta = 0.5$ would dampen. The intercept $a$ prefigures *alpha*, the performance the market does not explain — here −0.15% a week, and chapter 6 supplied the reflex question: significantly different from zero, or noise?

**The R-squared.** The coefficient of determination (the share of the variance of $y$ explained by the line) equals, in simple regression, $\rho^2$ — the square of chapter 2's correlation. Over our five weeks, $\rho = 0.9631$ and $R^2 = 0.9275$: 93% of the stock's variations are explained by the market, the remaining 7% are its specific risk. In the perfect example, $R^2 = 1$ — a score that, on real data, should trigger suspicion rather than enthusiasm.

**The residuals.** The **residual** $e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i$ (the vertical gap between the point and the line) is what the model does not see. Residuals sum to zero by construction, but their *structure* is a diagnostic: a curve, a trend, clusters of volatility — any of these means the line is missing something. The quant's reflex: never deliver a regression without having looked at the residual plot.

## The pitfalls: when the line lies

<Callout type="piege" titre="Price regressions, the spurious-correlation factory">
Regress the price of the S&P 500 on the cumulative number of films produced since 1990: a crushing $R^2$. Why? Two series that **rise with time** are mechanically correlated, with no economic link whatsoever — this is **spurious correlation** (*spurious regression*: two trends share a "hidden factor", the passage of time, and the regression mistakes that common drift for a relationship). The desk's absolute rule: regress **returns** (changes), never **prices** (levels). An R-squared of 0.95 between two prices says close to nothing; an R-squared of 0.3 between two returns is already genuine information.
</Callout>

Three further pitfalls complete the kit. **Extrapolation**: the line only exists over the range of the cloud — our β = 1.35, measured on ±4% weeks, says nothing about a −20% crash, precisely where correlations deform (chapter 2: "in a crisis, everything goes to 1"). ***Outliers***: the squares give enormous weight to extreme points — remove week 5, and the slope jumps from 1.35 to 1.66. And the short sample: an estimated slope carries a standard error, chapter 5 applies word for word — a β computed on five points is a five-person poll. Finally, chapter 2's eternal safeguard: even a clean regression establishes an association, never a causation.

## Monte Carlo: simulating what you cannot compute

Change of tool. When a quantity — a complex option price, a probability of loss — has no closed-form formula, the desk computes it by the brute force of chance: the **Monte Carlo** method (estimating a quantity by simulating a large number of random scenarios and averaging the results — named after the casino). Three steps: model the randomness, simulate $N$ scenarios, count.

Switch to "2 — Monte-Carlo". The model: a price starts at €100, drifts at 5% a year, 20% volatility, lognormal dynamics (chapter 4). Target: the probability of ending the year above €110, whose exact value — 37.2%, computable via $\Phi$ — is the dashed line on the convergence chart. Click "Relancer" (rerun) several times at fixed N: the dispersion of the estimates from one run to the next is chapter 5's standard error, in flesh and pixels.
</Callout>

Why does it converge, and how fast? Because a Monte Carlo estimator **is a sample mean** — here, the mean of $N$ draws worth 1 if the path ends above €110, 0 otherwise. Chapter 5 therefore applies in full: the standard error is $\sqrt{p(1-p)/N}$ and shrinks as $1/\sqrt{N}$. The loop is closed: the "curse of the square root" that haunts backtests haunts simulators too. Quadrupling $N$ only halves the error; one extra decimal of precision costs **one hundred times** more simulations — at $N = 1{,}000$, the standard error is about 1.5 points; for 0.15 points, you need $N = 100{,}000$.

The desk's uses follow. Pricing **exotic options** (whose payoffs depend on the underlying's entire path — Asians, barriers), with no closed-form formula: simulate thousands of paths, discount the average payoff (module 9). **VaR** (*Value at Risk*: the loss a portfolio should exceed only with a given probability): simulate, sort the losses, read off the quantile (module 12). With a lucidity inherited from chapter 4: the simulation reproduces *your model*, not the world — a million lognormal paths will never invent the fat tails the lognormal ignores. Monte Carlo solves the computation problem, never the model problem.

## The module's five quantitative reflexes

This module boils down to five reflexes:

1. **Returns compound.** Geometric mean for realized performance, arithmetic for expectation; variances add across time, hence volatility in $\sqrt{252}$ (chapters 1-2).
2. **Keep the direction of conditioning straight.** $P(A \mid B) \neq P(B \mid A)$: think in headcounts, unroll Bayes — from the medical test to the p-value (chapters 3 and 6).
3. **The normal at the center, suspicion at the extremes.** Bell and z-scores for the everyday, fat tails and the lognormal for prices — know where the model cracks (chapter 4).
4. **Every mean carries a standard error.** $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, precision in root n: backtests, intervals, Monte Carlo simulations — one law for all (chapters 5 and 7).
5. **Significant is neither true nor important.** Before believing a result, hunt for the bias: how many tests, which survivors, what out-of-sample — then ask whether the effect is worth money (chapter 6).

These five reflexes are the quantitative bedrock of everything that follows in the course: the trees and bells of pricing, the simulations behind exotic products, the betas and VaR of portfolio management will be nothing but ever more ambitious applications of them.

<GoFurther suffixe="the least-squares derivation in six lines">
We seek $a$ and $b$ minimizing $S(a, b) = \sum_i (y_i - a - b x_i)^2$ — a quadratic function: the minimum sets both partial derivatives to zero. Derivative in $a$: $\partial S/\partial a = -2 \sum_i (y_i - a - b x_i) = 0$ — the sum of the residuals is zero, hence $a = \bar{y} - b\bar{x}$: the line passes through the mean point. Derivative in $b$: $\partial S/\partial b = -2 \sum_i x_i (y_i - a - b x_i) = 0$. Substituting $a$ and recentering the variables, this condition becomes $\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})\big[(y_i - \bar{y}) - b (x_i - \bar{x})\big] = 0$, hence:

$$
b = \frac{\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \frac{\text{cov}(x, y)}{\text{var}(x)}
$$

(numerator and denominator divided by the same $n-1$, which cancels). The geometric reading of the two conditions: the residuals have zero mean and are orthogonal to $x$ — the least-squares line is the projection of $y$ onto the information contained in $x$, and that reading carries over unchanged to multiple regression.
</GoFurther>
