Chapter 5 taught how to bracket uncertainty; this one teaches how to decide. The typical situation: a fund has beaten its benchmark index by 2% a year on average for 25 years. Skill, or luck? All of quantitative finance runs into this question — is a manager good, does a strategy work, does a signal predict anything at all — and statistics answers it with a codified protocol, the **hypothesis test**. A double-edged protocol: well understood, it is a noise detector; misunderstood, it is a machine for validating anything.

## H₀, H₁ and the threshold α: the statistical presumption of innocence

Every test pits two hypotheses against each other. The **null hypothesis** $H_0$ is the no-effect hypothesis: the fund has no skill, the coin is fair, the signal predicts nothing — the observed gap is mere sampling noise. The **alternative hypothesis** $H_1$ asserts the effect: the fund genuinely outperforms.

The logic is exactly that of a courtroom. $H_0$ plays the defendant: presumed true as long as the data do not bring strong evidence to the contrary. The burden of proof rests entirely on $H_1$ — and that is deliberate: we would rather let a few talented managers go undetected than label the first lucky streak "skill". A consequence of this asymmetry, one that exam panels love to make you spell out: you never **prove** $H_0$. A test that fails to reject the null does not demonstrate that the fund lacks skill — it records that the data are not sufficient to assert it. Acquitted does not mean innocent.

It remains to set the standard of proof: the **significance level** $\alpha$ (the probability of judicial error we accept — convicting an innocent, that is, rejecting $H_0$ when it is true). The dominant convention: $\alpha = 5\,\%$. Nothing sacred about that figure — it is a legacy of Fisher turned industry reflex; 1% for the cautious, and we will see that the exact borderline can change everything.

## Test statistic and p-value: measuring surprise

The protocol takes three steps. First, count the gap in units of uncertainty: the **test statistic** scales the observed gap by chapter 5's standard error —

$$
t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}
$$

where $\mu_0$ is the value posited by $H_0$. You will recognize a z-score from chapter 4, applied not to a data point but to the *mean*: "how many standard errors away does the result sit from what luck alone would produce?"

Application to the fund. Average excess return over the index: $\bar{x} = 2\,\%$ a year; standard deviation of the annual excess returns: $s = 5\,\%$; track record: $n = 25$ years. Under $H_0$, the true excess return is $\mu_0 = 0$. The standard error is $5/\sqrt{25} = 1\,\%$, hence:

$$
t = \frac{2 - 0}{5/\sqrt{25}} = \frac{2}{1} = 2.0
$$

The outperformance sits two standard errors away from zero. Is that a lot? That is the second step: the **p-value** — the probability, *if $H_0$ is true*, of observing a gap at least as extreme as the one recorded. The CLT makes $t$ approximately standard normal under $H_0$; counting extremes on both sides (two-tailed test — a fund can underperform too):

$$
p = 2 \times \big(1 - \Phi(2)\big) = 2 \times (1 - 0.97725) = 4.55\,\%
$$

Third step, the decision: $p \approx 4.6\,\% < 5\,\%$, so $H_0$ is rejected — the outperformance is said to be **statistically significant** at the 5% level. But look at the margin: the exact 5% borderline sits at $t = 1.96$ (the totem number returns: rejecting at 5% two-tailed means exceeding 1.96 standard errors). Our fund clears it at $t = 2.0$ — significant... by a whisker. At the 1% level, it would fail outright. Twenty-five years of a steady 2%-a-year outperformance are barely enough to rule out pure luck: that is what calibrates the humility owed to any shorter track record.

**A point of precision that would land well with an exam panel:** with $\sigma$ estimated by $s$ on $n = 25$, the exact test is a Student's t with **24 degrees of freedom** — the critical value is 2.06 (not 1.96) and $p \approx 5.7\,\%$: the whisker snaps. The 1.96 borderline assumes large $n$; here, statistical honesty concludes "not significant, by a hair".

<Callout type="piege" titre="The p-value is NOT the probability that H₀ is true">
Reread the definition: $p$ = the probability of observing data this extreme **if** $H_0$ is true — that is $P(\text{data} \mid H_0)$, not $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$. Saying "p = 4.6%, so there is a 95.4% chance the fund has skill" reverses the direction of the conditioning: it is, word for word, the prosecutor's fallacy of chapter 3, desk edition. To obtain $P(H_0 \mid \text{data})$ you would need Bayes — and therefore a prior probability that the fund is skilled, which the p-value ignores entirely. Yet genuine skill is rare: as with the medical test, a 5% "positive" drawn from a population where almost all funds lack skill remains, more often than not, a false positive. The p-value measures surprise under the luck hypothesis; it does not measure the probability of skill.
</Callout>

## Two ways to be wrong: type I and type II

A binary decision facing a binary reality: four cells, two of them errors.

| | $H_0$ true (no effect) | $H_0$ false (real effect) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Reject $H_0$** | Type I error — false positive (probability $\alpha$) | Correct decision (power $1-\beta$) |
| **Fail to reject** | Correct decision | Type II error — missed effect (probability $\beta$) |

The analogy that fixes the ideas: an air-defense radar. Tuned too sensitive, it goes off at flocks of birds — false alarms, type I. Tuned too deaf, it lets aircraft through — missed detections, type II. The threshold $\alpha$ is the sensitivity dial: lowering it from 5% to 1% cuts the false alarms, but mechanically makes the radar deafer — $\beta$ rises, and the **statistical power** (the probability $1-\beta$ of detecting an effect that really exists) drops. At a given setting, you cannot win on both fronts; the only way to improve both at once is to enlarge the antenna — increase $n$, which shrinks the standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ and separates signal from noise more cleanly. Chapter 5's square root, always the same one.

In finance, where you set the dial depends on the relative cost of the two errors: when allocating capital to a strategy, the false positive is the costly one — you tighten $\alpha$; for a risk warning signal, it is the missed detection that kills — you accept false alarms.

## The quant's traps: when the testing machine becomes a mirage machine

The hypothesis test assumes one thing never written in the formula: that you ran **only one test**. The entire pathology of finance stems from violating that clause.

*Data snooping* (mining the data: testing dozens of strategies and keeping only those that "work") is its industrial form. Test 100 strategies with no predictive power at the 5% level: by construction of the threshold, about 5 will look significant — by pure luck. And the probability that at least one "works" is $1 - 0.95^{100} = 99.4\,\%$: the discovery is guaranteed in advance, even when there is strictly nothing to discover. Twenty tests are already enough to push that probability to 64%.

*Survivorship bias* (dead funds vanish from the databases) rigs the sample before the test even starts: measuring the average performance of *existing* funds means averaging the winners while forgetting the departed — our fund at $t = 2.0$ impresses less once you remember that its deceased contemporaries are no longer around to testify. *p-hacking* (torturing the analysis — windows, sub-periods, excluding "outliers" — until $p$ slips below 5%) completes the picture: with enough degrees of freedom in the analysis, you always end up finding a cut of the data that "works".

<Callout type="important" titre="The question to ask before believing a backtest">
"How many tests were run before the one I am being shown?" A strategy significant at 5% is worth nothing if it is the survivor of 100 attempts — it is exactly what luck promised. The professional defenses: declare the hypotheses before seeing the data, tighten the threshold as tests multiply, and above all demand *out-of-sample* validation — on data never touched during the research. And one final distinction exam panels cherish: **significant ≠ important**. With a million observations, an excess return of 0.01% a year becomes statistically significant — and economically invisible, devoured by the smallest transaction cost. Significance says "this is probably not noise"; it never says "this is worth money". One is a question of √n, the other a question of cents.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="one-tailed, two-tailed, and the bridge to the confidence interval">
Our test was two-tailed: $H_1$ said "the true excess return is non-zero", and the p-value counted extremes on both sides. Had the hypothesis of interest been "the fund outperforms" (one-tailed), only the right tail would count: $p = 1 - \Phi(2) = 2.28\,\%$ — half as much, significant with more room to spare. Tempting? Precisely: choosing the one-tailed test *after* seeing that the gap is positive is a discreet form of p-hacking. The rule: the direction of the test is declared before looking at the data; when in doubt, two-tailed.

Second bridge, an elegant one: the test and the confidence interval are the same object seen from two sides. Rejecting $H_0 : \mu = \mu_0$ at the 5% two-tailed level means observing $|t| > 1.96$, i.e. that $\mu_0$ lies more than $1.96\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ away from $\bar{x}$ — in other words, that $\mu_0$ sits **outside** the 95% confidence interval. Check on the fund: $CI_{95\,\%} = 2 \pm 1.96 \times 1 = [\,0.04\,\% \,,\; 3.96\,\%\,]$ (with a Student's t at 24 df: $[\,-0.06\,\% \,,\; 4.06\,\%\,]$, zero included — consistent with the exact non-rejection). Zero is excluded — by a breath of 4 hundredths of a point — exactly as $p = 4.55\,\%$ slipped under 5% by a breath. Same information, two languages: the CI additionally shows the whole range of values compatible with the data, which often makes it the more honest of the two.
</GoFurther>
