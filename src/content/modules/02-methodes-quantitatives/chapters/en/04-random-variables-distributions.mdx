Chapter 3 handled random variables one outcome at a time: a die, a test, a door. **Distributions** give the overall view: the complete shape of randomness, all the outcomes and their probabilities in a single glance. Three laws structure market finance — the binomial for counting successes, the normal for modelling returns, the lognormal for modelling prices. Plus one compulsory piece of lucidity: knowing where these models crack.

## The binomial: counting successes

Repeat the same two-outcome experiment $n$ times — success with probability $p$, failure otherwise — independently. The total number of successes $X$ follows a **binomial** distribution $B(n, p)$:

$$
P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k}\,p^k\,(1-p)^{n-k}
$$

The formula reads like a compacted tree: $p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$ is the probability of one specific path with $k$ successes, and $\binom{n}{k}$ counts the number of paths containing exactly $k$ of them. Its expected value is $E[X] = np$.

Example: over 10 trading sessions in which the market rises each day with probability 0.5 (and sessions assumed independent), the probability of observing exactly 3 up sessions is:

$$
P(X = 3) = \binom{10}{3} \times 0.5^3 \times 0.5^7 = \frac{120}{1{,}024} = 0.1172 \approx 11.72\%
$$

You recognise the $\binom{10}{3} = 120$ from chapter 3, and the expected value of $np = 5$ up sessions. Remember this repeated up/down mechanics well: it is exactly the structure of the **binomial tree** with which you will learn to price options in module 8 — at each step, the underlying moves up or down, and the option's value is computed by working back up the tree.

## The normal distribution: the universal bell

The **normal** (or Gaussian) distribution is continuous, bell-shaped, entirely described by two parameters: its mean $\mu$, which positions the centre, and its standard deviation $\sigma$, which sets the width. Its density reads:

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}
$$

Symmetric around $\mu$, it concentrates its mass at the centre in proportions to know by heart — the **68-95-99.7** rules: about 68.3% of the mass between $\mu \pm 1\sigma$, 95.4% between $\mu \pm 2\sigma$, 99.7% between $\mu \pm 3\sigma$.

To compute any probability, you reduce to the **standard** normal ($\mu = 0$, $\sigma = 1$) via the **z-score** $z = (x - \mu)/\sigma$: the distance to the mean, counted in standard deviations. The tables (or the function $\Phi$) give the cumulative probability. Two values to know: $\Phi(1.96) = 0.9750$, i.e. 97.5% of the mass below $\mu + 1.96\sigma$ — hence the 95% interval of $\mu \pm 1.96\sigma$ — and, by the symmetry $\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$, $\Phi(-1) = 0.1587$.

Direct application: an index has an annual return with mean $\mu = 6\%$ and standard deviation $\sigma = 15\%$, assumed normal. Probability of a year worse than −9%? The z-score is $z = (-9 - 6)/15 = -1$, so $P = \Phi(-1) = 15.87\%$ — about one year in six.

## The lognormal: the law of prices

Why not model **prices** directly with a normal? Two disqualifying reasons. First, a normal extends from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$: it would assign a non-zero probability to a negative share price, which makes no sense — limited liability caps the loss at zero. Second, returns **compound**: going from 100 to 110 then from 110 to 121 means multiplying by 1.10 twice, not adding. And sums are easy to handle, products are not. The solution: take the logarithm, which turns products into sums — compounded returns **add up in logs**:

$$
\ln\!\frac{P_T}{P_0} = \ln(1+r_1) + \ln(1+r_2) + \cdots + \ln(1+r_T)
$$

If that sum of log returns is normal, then $\ln P_T$ is normal — and the price $P_T$ is said to follow a **lognormal** distribution: $X$ is lognormal if $\ln X$ is normal. Its support is strictly positive, and it is asymmetric: tail stretched to the right, mass squeezed near zero.

Now flip the explorer above into "Lognormal" mode: same $\mu$ and $\sigma$, but applied to the logarithm. Observe three things. The support: the curve never crosses zero. The asymmetry: the $E[X]$ marker shifts to the right of the peak — the mean exceeds the mode, pulled by the right tail. And the invariance: the bounds keep their z-score through the toggle, so "±1σ" still contains 68.3% of the mass — probabilities pass through the logarithm intact. Increase $\sigma$: the asymmetry sharpens spectacularly.

This is precisely the assumption of the **Black-Scholes** model you will meet in module 8: lognormal prices, that is, normal log returns. The entire option-pricing machinery rests on the bell you have just handled.

## Where the normal cracks

The normal is elegant, tractable — and wrong in the tails. Real returns display **fat tails** (extreme events are far more frequent than under the normal) and a *skew* (asymmetry: violent falls are more frequent than equivalent rises).

The canonical example: on 19 October 1987, the Dow Jones loses 22.6% in a single session. With a daily volatility of around 1% at the time, that is a move of more than **20 standard deviations**. Under a normal distribution, such an event has a probability so infinitesimal that it should not be observed even once, even replaying every trading day since the age of the universe. Yet it happened — and 2008, the 2010 flash crash and March 2020 have since reminded everyone that "statistical impossibilities" have an unfortunate tendency to recur. The correct conclusion is not "the market suffered cosmic bad luck", but "the normal model massively underestimates the tails".

<Callout type="piege" titre="“Assuming normality is the original sin of risk management”">
The line is famous — and to be handled with nuance, which a panel will appreciate. The normal remains an excellent approximation at the **centre** of the distribution, where 95% of market days live; that is why it survives in pricers and quick calculations. The sin is not using it, it is **forgetting that you are using it**: a Gaussian 99% VaR that ignores fat tails underestimates precisely the losses it claims to bound. Hence the modern toolkit: stress tests, historical scenarios, fat-tailed distributions. The right posture at the oral: "the normal, yes — knowing that it lies about the extremes".
</Callout>

## From one distribution to the next

One last thread to pull: go back to the binomial from the start. Sketch $B(10;\,0.5)$ in your head: already the silhouette of a bell. Increase $n$: the binomial, suitably centred and scaled, becomes indistinguishable from a normal — that is the de Moivre-Laplace theorem, a special case of a far more general result. Why are so many real-world quantities approximately normal? Because they are **sums** of a large number of small independent effects — and a sum of random variables, under broad conditions, tends to the normal. That result, the **central limit theorem**, is the subject of chapter 5: it is what links the binomial pricing tree to Black-Scholes, and yesterday's sample to tomorrow's inference.

<GoFurther suffixe="why prices are lognormal">
The full sketch of the argument fits in four lines. A price evolves by successive multiplications: $P_T = P_0 \times (1+r_1) \times (1+r_2) \times \cdots \times (1+r_T)$, where the $r_t$ are the returns of each period. Taking logarithms: $\ln P_T = \ln P_0 + \sum_t \ln(1+r_t)$ — a sum of a large number of small, roughly independent random variables. The central limit theorem (chapter 5) drives that sum towards a normal distribution: $\ln P_T$ is therefore approximately normal, which is the very definition of a lognormal price $P_T$. Exponential of a normal sum: positive support guaranteed (the exponential never produces a negative value) and mechanical asymmetry (the exponential stretches high values and compresses low ones). Note the chain of assumptions: independence of returns, smallness of the shocks, finite variance — each one is debatable in real markets, and that is exactly where fat tails come in.
</GoFurther>
