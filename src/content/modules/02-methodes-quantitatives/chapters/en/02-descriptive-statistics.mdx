A year of daily quotes is 252 returns. Nobody reads 252 numbers: the desk compresses them into a handful of statistics — an average level, a dispersion, co-movements, a shape. This chapter builds that toolkit and, above all, teaches you to pick the right tool: most panel questions on statistics test not the computation, but the choice of tool and the reading of the result.

## Three means, three questions

The **arithmetic mean** is everyone's mean: $\bar{r} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i$. It answers the question "how much does a *typical* period earn?" — it is the right estimate of the return to expect over *one* future period, and the basic building block of all expected-value calculations.

The **geometric mean** answers a different question: what constant rate, compounded period after period, would have produced the same final wealth?

$$
r_g = \left[\prod_{i=1}^{n}(1+r_i)\right]^{1/n} - 1
$$

It is the geometric mean, and it alone, that measures the annual performance actually achieved over several periods — because it respects the compounding of returns.

<Callout type="piege" titre="+10% then −10% ≠ 0 — THE panel trap">
A fund gains 10% the first year, loses 10% the second. Arithmetic mean: 0%. Reality: €100 becomes €110, then $110 \times 0.90 = \text{€}99$. The 10% loss applies to a higher base (€110): it destroys €11, where the gain had created only €10. The geometric mean says it exactly: $\sqrt{1.10 \times 0.90} - 1 = \sqrt{0.99} - 1 = -0.5013\%$, i.e. roughly −0.50% a year. Variant: −50% then +50% leaves you at $0.50 \times 1.50 = 0.75$, i.e. −25%. A fund can post a zero "average return" and have lost money.
</Callout>

This trap illustrates a general inequality: **the arithmetic mean is always greater than or equal to the geometric mean**, with equality only if all returns are identical. The gap is roughly $\sigma^2/2$: it grows with volatility. On ±10%, the gap is 0.50 points; on +20% then −10%, the arithmetic mean gives 5.00% but the geometric 3.92%, a 1.08-point gap. Practitioners call this *volatility drag*: at equal arithmetic mean, the more volatile an asset, the more its compounded performance crumbles.

That leaves the **harmonic mean**, $H = n / \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{x_i}$, with a more targeted use: it is the average purchase price when you invest constant *amounts*. Put in €100 when the unit is worth €10, then €100 when it is worth €20: you hold $10 + 5 = 15$ units for €200, i.e. €13.33 per unit — the harmonic mean of 10 and 20 ($2/(\frac{1}{10}+\frac{1}{20}) = 13.33$), not the arithmetic mean of €15. That is the mechanics of dollar cost averaging (DCA, mechanically buying for a fixed amount at regular intervals): you automatically acquire more units when the price is low. Same tool for averaging ratios with a constant numerator, such as P/Es.

| The question asked | The right mean |
| --- | --- |
| Return to expect over one future period | Arithmetic |
| Compounded performance achieved over several periods | Geometric |
| Average purchase price with constant amounts invested | Harmonic |

And always in that order: arithmetic ≥ geometric ≥ harmonic.

## Dispersion: from variance to volatility

A mean without a dispersion is half a piece of information. The **sample variance** measures the average squared deviation from the mean:

$$
s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(r_i - \bar{r}\right)^2
$$

and the **standard deviation** $s = \sqrt{s^2}$ brings it back into the units of the data. On the toy sample $\{2, 4, 6\}$: mean 4, deviations −2, 0, +2, sum of squares 8, variance $8/2 = 4$, standard deviation 2.

Why divide by $n-1$ and not by $n$? Because dispersion is measured around $\bar{r}$, which is *estimated on the same data*. By construction, $\bar{r}$ is the value that fits the sample best: deviations measured around it are systematically smaller than deviations around the true, unknown mean. Dividing by $n-1$ corrects exactly that bias — the proof is sketched in the box at the end of the chapter. You divide by $n$ only when you observe the entire population, a vanishingly rare case in finance.

In finance, the standard deviation of returns has a name of its own: **volatility**. And the market convention is to annualise it. If daily returns are independent, *variances* add up over time: the annual variance is 252 times the daily variance (252 trading days in a year), so the standard deviation grows like the **square root of time**:

$$
\sigma_{\text{ann}} = \sigma_{\text{daily}} \times \sqrt{252}
$$

A daily volatility of 2% gives $2 \times \sqrt{252} = 2 \times 15.8745 = 31.75\%$ annualised. Same logic for the other frequencies: ×√52 for weekly data, ×√12 for monthly. Desk rule of thumb: √252 ≈ 16, so 1% of daily vol ≈ 16% annualised.

On the reading side: if returns are roughly normal, about two thirds of days fall within ±1σ and 95% within ±2σ. A 2% daily vol therefore means an index that moves by more than 2% — one way or the other — about one day in three. Orders of magnitude to know: a calm equity index lives around 15% annualised vol; above 30%, the market is in stress.

## Covariance and correlation: two assets together

No asset lives alone. The **covariance** measures whether two series move together:

$$
s_{AB} = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(r_{A,i}-\bar{r}_A\right)\left(r_{B,i}-\bar{r}_B\right)
$$

Positive if A and B deviate from their means on the same side at the same time, negative if they pull in opposite directions. Its flaw: an unreadable unit (%²) and a magnitude that depends on the volatilities. The **correlation** normalises it:

$$
\rho_{AB} = \frac{s_{AB}}{s_A \, s_B}
$$

with $\rho \in [-1, 1]$ always: +1 for a perfect linear relationship, −1 for a perfect opposition, 0 for no linear relationship.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Full calculation by hand: four days, two assets">
Daily returns (in %): A gives +2, −1, +3, 0; B gives +1, 0, +4, +3. Means: $\bar{r}_A = 1$%, $\bar{r}_B = 2$%.

| Day | A | B | Deviation A | Deviation B | Product |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | +2 | +1 | +1 | −1 | −1 |
| 2 | −1 | 0 | −2 | −2 | +4 |
| 3 | +3 | +4 | +2 | +2 | +4 |
| 4 | 0 | +3 | −1 | +1 | −1 |

Sum of products: 6, hence $s_{AB} = 6/3 = 2$%². The squared deviations sum to 10 on each side: $s_A^2 = s_B^2 = 10/3 \approx 3.33$%², i.e. $s_A = s_B = 1.83$%. Finally:

$$
\rho = \frac{2}{1.8257 \times 1.8257} = \frac{2}{3.3333} = 0.60
$$

The two assets broadly move together (days 2 and 3), without being perfectly in phase: days 1 and 4, when they diverge, hold the correlation back from 1.
</Callout>

Two safeguards, which every panel expects. First, **correlation is not causation**: ice-cream sales and drownings are correlated — the hidden variable is summer. A high correlation can reflect a common factor, reverse causality, or a mere coincidence over a short sample; and $\rho$ only captures *linear* relationships.

<Callout type="important" titre="“In a crisis, all correlations go to 1”">
Correlations are not physical constants: estimated in calm times, they shoot up in periods of stress, when everyone sells at the same time. In 2008, supposedly uncorrelated assets plunged in unison. The brutal consequence: diversification built on historical correlations evaporates precisely when you need it most. You will meet this limit again at the heart of portfolio management (module 12).
</Callout>

## Skewness and kurtosis: the shape of the distribution

Mean and variance are the first two *moments* of a distribution. The next two describe its shape — and that is where finance stops being well-behaved.

*Skewness* (the coefficient of asymmetry) is 0 for a symmetric distribution. **Negative**, it signals a stretched left tail: many small gains, and rare massive losses. It is the signature of option-selling strategies — collecting regular premiums and paying out rarely, but very dearly; the business traders describe as "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller". **Positive**, it describes the opposite profile, lottery-ticket style or option buying: frequent small losses against rare huge gains.

*Kurtosis* (the coefficient of tail weight) measures how heavy the tails are. The benchmark: the normal distribution, whose kurtosis is exactly 3. Beyond that, the distribution is called *leptokurtic* — fat-tailed (*fat tails*): extreme events are more frequent there than under the Gaussian. Yet daily financial returns almost always display a kurtosis well above 3. The canonical example: on 19 October 1987, the Dow Jones loses 22.6% in a single session — more than twenty standard deviations against the volatility of the time, an event to which the normal distribution assigns a probability so minute that it should never occur within the age of the universe. It happened. Finance is not Gaussian; the normal remains a precious working tool, whose use — and limits — chapter 4 will pin down.

## Summarising a series in four numbers

Faced with a history of returns, the professional reflex fits in four numbers: **μ** (the mean — geometric for realised performance, arithmetic for expectations), **σ** (the volatility, annualised), the **skewness** and the **kurtosis**. A reading example: "8% mean, 12% vol, skew −0.8, kurtosis 6" translates into one sentence — regular gains, but exposure to rare and severe losses, with extremes far more frequent than under the normal: an insurance seller's profile, not a quiet investment.

That is exactly what a panel expects: not formulas recited, but translation — saying what the four numbers tell about the risk profile, and pointing out unprompted that mean and variance are no longer enough once the distribution is asymmetric or fat-tailed: two strategies with the same μ and the same σ can hide radically different risks.

<GoFurther suffixe="why n − 1?">
The starting point: $\bar{x}$ is not the true mean $\mu$. It is computed to fit the sample as closely as possible — among all constants $c$, it is $\bar{x}$ that minimises $\sum (x_i - c)^2$. So, mechanically:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2 \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2
$$

Deviations measured around $\bar{x}$ are too small. By how much? The exact decomposition is:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2 - n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2
$$

Take expectations. The first term on the right is $n\sigma^2$ by definition of the variance. The second involves the variance of the sample mean, $\mathbb{E}[(\bar{x}-\mu)^2] = \sigma^2/n$, hence $n \times \sigma^2/n = \sigma^2$. In total:

$$
\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2\right] = n\sigma^2 - \sigma^2 = (n-1)\,\sigma^2
$$

To obtain an unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$, you must therefore divide the sum of squares by $n-1$, not by $n$. Another reading, in terms of degrees of freedom: the $n$ deviations from the mean are not free, they sum to zero by construction — the last one follows from the other $n-1$. There are only $n-1$ independent pieces of information about dispersion. The effect is negligible over 252 observations (a 252/251 factor), decisive over 5 — and at the oral, the short answer "because the mean is itself estimated on the same data, which understates the deviations" is more than enough.
</GoFurther>
