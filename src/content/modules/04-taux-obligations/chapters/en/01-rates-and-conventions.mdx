Before touching a single bond, you need to master the raw material it is made of: time. An interest rate is nothing more than the price of time. And like any price, it can be read, decomposed and computed according to precise — and often treacherous — conventions that professionals handle without a second thought and that exam panels love to test.

## Why interest rates exist

Lending €100 today against the promise of getting €100 back in a year is not a neutral transaction. The lender provides several distinct services, and each one carries a charge:

- **Giving up liquidity.** For the life of the loan, the lender can neither consume nor seize another opportunity. Economists call this time preference: for the same amount, a euro available now is worth more than a euro promised later. The rate pays for that wait — it is the "rent on money".
- **Default risk.** The promise to repay may not be kept. The weaker the borrower, the more compensation the lender demands: a risk premium is added on top of the base rent.
- **Monetary erosion.** If prices rise by 2% over the year, the €100 returned buys less than the €100 lent. The lender therefore demands protection against the inflation they expect.

<Callout type="definition" titre="Decomposing a nominal rate">
The nominal rate observed in the market breaks down into three stacked layers: a real rate (the "pure" reward for time), expected inflation, and risk premia (default, liquidity, term). The Fisher relation reads exactly $(1+i) = (1+r_{\text{real}})(1+\pi^{e})$, which for low rates yields the approximation $i \approx r_{\text{real}} + \pi^{e}$, on top of which come the premia specific to each borrower and each horizon.
</Callout>

This decomposition can be read straight off market levels. In early 2026, overnight money lends at around 2% in the euro area (the level of the €STR), while the French State borrows at around 3 to 3.5% over ten years on the OAT (*Obligation assimilable du Trésor*, the French government bond). The gap is the price of long time: uncertainty about future inflation and rates, a term premium, capital locked up for a decade.

## Compounding and discounting

Two calculation regimes coexist. Under **simple interest**, interest is proportional to time and does not itself earn interest: this is the money market convention, for transactions of under one year. Under **compound interest**, each period's interest is added to the principal and earns interest in turn: this is the convention beyond one year, and therefore the convention of the entire bond universe.

Compounding carries a sum forward in time. A present value $PV$ invested at rate $r$ for $n$ years becomes:

$$
FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n
$$

Discounting makes the reverse journey: it is finance's time machine. It answers the central question of all pricing: how much is a cash flow promised tomorrow worth today?

$$
PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}
$$

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The full round trip: €10,000 at 3% over 5 years">
**Outbound (compounding).** €10,000 invested at 3% compounded for 5 years grows to $10{,}000 \times 1.03^5 = 10{,}000 \times 1.159274 = \text{€}11{,}592.74$. Under simple interest, you would only get $10{,}000 \times (1 + 0.03 \times 5) = \text{€}11{,}500$: the €92.74 gap is the interest earned on interest.

**Return (discounting).** Symmetrically, receiving €10,000 in 5 years, discounted at 3%, is worth $10{,}000 / 1.159274 = \text{€}8{,}626.09$ today. Check: €8,626.09 invested at 3% for 5 years grows back to exactly €10,000.
</Callout>

Everything else in this module — bond prices, the yield curve, duration — is nothing but the repeated application of that second formula to streams of cash flows.

## Day-count conventions: the basis drives the amount

In the money market, the interest on a deposit accrues pro rata to the number of days elapsed:

$$
I = N \times r \times \frac{\text{days}}{\text{basis}}
$$

The whole question is: how do you count the days, and how many days does the year have? The answer is pure convention, specific to each market segment — but it changes the amount actually paid.

| Basis | Days counted | Year | Typical use |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Actual/360 | actual calendar days | 360 | Euro money market (Euribor, deposits, BTFs) |
| Actual/365 | actual calendar days | 365 | Sterling money market |
| 30/360 | months standardised to 30 days | 360 | US corporate bonds |
| Actual/Actual | actual calendar days | actual length of the period | Government bonds (OATs, accrued interest) |

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Same deposit, three different interest amounts">
A treasurer places €1,000,000 at 3% from 15 February to 17 May 2026, i.e. 91 actual calendar days.

- **Actual/360** (the euro money market rule): 1,000,000 × 3% × 91/360 = **€7,583.33**.
- **Actual/365**: 1,000,000 × 3% × 91/365 = **€7,479.45**.
- **30/360**: with February standardised to 30 days, the convention counts 92 days, hence 1,000,000 × 3% × 92/360 = **€7,666.67**.

Three conventions, three amounts, up to €187 apart — for the same deposit, at the same quoted rate.
</Callout>

<Callout type="piege" titre="A rate without its basis means nothing">
Bluntly comparing a money market rate on Actual/360 with a rate on a 365 basis is comparing apples and oranges: at an equal quoted rate, the 360 basis pays roughly 1.4% more interest in relative terms (365/360 ≈ 1.014). A 3% Actual/360 rate is thus equivalent to about 3.04% on a 365 basis. Before comparing any two rates, the professional reflex is to ask: "on what basis?"
</Callout>

## Nominal rate and effective rate

A quoted rate of 4% "compounded semi-annually" does not earn 4% over the year: it earns 2% per half-year, and the first half's interest itself earns interest in the second. A nominal rate $r$ compounded $m$ times a year produces an effective annual rate of:

$$
r_{\text{eff}} = \left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{m} - 1
$$

Example: 4% nominal compounded semi-annually gives $(1 + 0.02)^2 - 1 = 0.0404$, i.e. an effective rate of 4.04%. With monthly compounding, you would get about 4.07%. The more frequent the compounding, the higher the effective rate — but the effect tops out very quickly.

<GoFurther suffixe="continuous compounding">
What happens if you compound ever more often — every day, every hour, every instant? The limit exists, and the exponential appears:

$$
\lim_{m \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{r}{m}\right)^{mt} = e^{rt}
$$

At 4%, the effective rate under continuous compounding is $e^{0.04} - 1 \approx 0.0408$, i.e. 4.08%: barely more than monthly compounding (4.07%). The race to ever-higher frequency converges.

If quants work in continuous time, it is not for those few basis points, but for a structural property: **the additivity of log returns**. The log return over a period, $\ln(FV/PV) = rt$, is exactly the sum of the log returns of its sub-periods — whereas simple returns compound multiplicatively. This additivity simplifies all continuous-time modelling: zero-coupon curves written as $e^{-rt}$, diffusion models, Black-Scholes.
</GoFurther>

## The money market

The money market is the compartment for lending and borrowing at under one year. It is the plumbing of finance: unspectacular, but everything else feeds off it. It brings together banks (balancing their cash positions day by day and managing their reserves with the ECB), governments (for their day-to-day cash management), large corporates, money market funds investing short-term savings — and the central bank, which steers short rates through its policy rates.

Two reference rates structure the euro area:

| | €STR | Euribor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Horizon | Overnight | 1 week to 12 months |
| Method | Computed every day by the ECB from actual unsecured borrowing transactions reported by banks | A fixing published every day by EMMI from the contributions of a panel of banks |
| Nature | An observed rate, essentially risk-free | A term interbank offered rate: embeds credit and term risk |
| Level in early 2026 | Around 2% | Around 2 to 2.5% for the 3-month |

The spread between Euribor and expected €STR over the same period is, incidentally, a closely watched barometer of interbank confidence: it blew out in 2008.

On the instrument side, four main families of short-term paper:

- **BTFs** (*Bons du Trésor à taux fixe et à intérêts précomptés*, French fixed-rate discount Treasury bills): the French State's sub-one-year paper, auctioned every week;
- **T-bills** (Treasury bills): their American equivalent, the reference "risk-free" investment in dollars;
- **certificates of deposit**: short-term paper issued by banks;
- **commercial paper**: short-term paper issued by corporates. In France, certificates of deposit and commercial paper have been grouped since 2016 under the single label NEU CP (*Negotiable EUropean Commercial Paper*).

To fix the orders of magnitude in early 2026: French public debt is approaching €3,500 billion, of which around €2,800 billion is negotiable State debt — funded for the most part by OATs, the instrument we dissect in the next chapter, with the short end covered by BTFs.
