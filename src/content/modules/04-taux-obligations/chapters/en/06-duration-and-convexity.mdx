Chapter 3 left an observation hanging: on our running-example bond, a 1-point fall in rates gains €28.82, but a 1-point rise loses only €27.75. Neither the amounts nor their asymmetry are accidents. This chapter builds the instruments that measure a bond's sensitivity to rates — duration, modified duration, convexity — and that explain, along the way, this asymmetry. It is the daily toolkit of every fixed-income desk, and one of examiners' favourite hunting grounds.

## Measuring sensitivity: why maturity is not enough

First intuition, inherited from chapter 3: the longer the maturity, the more sensitive the price to rates, since distant cash flows are divided by $(1+r)^t$ factors with high exponents. True, but incomplete. Compare two bonds of the same name and the same 10-year maturity, in a 4% market, hit by the same 1-point rise:

| Bond (10 years, face value €1,000) | Price at 4% | Price at 5% | Change |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Zero-coupon | €675.56 | €613.91 | **−9.13%** |
| 8% coupon | €1,324.44 | €1,231.65 | **−7.01%** |

Same maturity, same shock, clearly different sensitivities. The reason lies in the timing of the cash flows: the zero-coupon concentrates all its value in year 10, the year most exposed to discounting, while the large coupons of the second bond bring part of the value back to the early years, which are almost insensitive. Maturity only dates the last flow; we need a measure that weighs them all.

On a desk, the question is in fact asked directly in euros: "if rates rise 1bp, how much does my position lose?" That quantity has a name — the **DV01** (*dollar value of a basis point*) — and it is what traders quote to each other all day long. The two sections that follow build the tools it derives from.

## Macaulay duration

<Callout type="definition" titre="Macaulay duration">
The Macaulay duration of a bond is the average of its cash-flow dates, each date weighted by the share of the discounted flow in the total price:

$$
D_{Mac} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{n} t \cdot PV(F_t)}{P}
$$

It is expressed in **years**: it is the time barycentre — the centre of gravity — of the discounted cash flows. The classic image: lay the discounted flows on a plank graduated in years; duration is the point where the plank balances.
</Callout>

Full calculation on the running-example bond (face value €1,000, 5% coupon, 3 years, market rate 4%), whose price we have known since chapter 3: P = €1,027.75:

| Year $t$ | Cash flow $F_t$ | $PV(F_t)$ at 4% | $t \times PV(F_t)$ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | €50 | €48.0769 | 48.0769 |
| 2 | €50 | €46.2278 | 92.4556 |
| 3 | €1,050 | €933.4462 | 2,800.3385 |
| **Sum** | — | **€1,027.7509** | **2,940.8711** |

$$
D_{Mac} = \frac{2{,}940.8711}{1{,}027.7509} = 2.8615 \approx 2.86 \text{ years}
$$

2.86 years for a 3-year maturity: the barycentre is pulled toward the present by the two intermediate coupons, but only barely — chapter 3 noted that the final flow accounts for more than 90% of the price, and duration confirms it. Three limiting cases illuminate the mechanics:

- **The zero-coupon.** A single flow, at maturity: the barycentre coincides with it, $D_{Mac} = n$ exactly. A 10-year zero-coupon has a duration of 10 years, full stop.
- **Coupon up, duration down.** Larger coupons bring more value back to the early years and pull the barycentre toward the present. Over 3 years at 4%: a duration of 3.00 years with no coupon, 2.86 years at 5%, 2.79 years at 8%.
- **The perpetuity.** A bond that pays a coupon forever has infinite maturity... but finite duration — the result, an elegant one, is proved in the box at the end of the chapter.

## Modified duration

Macaulay duration is a length of time. To turn it into an instrument for measuring sensitivity, one tweak is enough:

$$
D_{mod} = \frac{D_{Mac}}{1+y}
$$

On the running example: $D_{mod} = 2.8615 / 1.04 = 2.7514$. This unitless factor reads as follows: a 1-point rise in the rate loses roughly 2.75% of the price. Hence the formula every desk applies in its head:

$$
\Delta P \approx -D_{mod} \times \Delta y \times P
$$

with $\Delta y$ in decimal form (50bp = 0.005). The minus sign encodes the inverse price-rate relationship of chapter 3. Example on the running bond, for a 50bp rise:

$$
\Delta P \approx -2.7514 \times 0.005 \times 1{,}027.75 = -\text{€}14.14
$$

that is, −1.38% of the price. The same formula gives the DV01 directly by setting $\Delta y$ = 1bp = 0.0001: here $2.7514 \times 1{,}027.75 \times 0.0001 = 0.28$ euros per basis point — and for a position of 10,000 bonds, about €2,828 per bp: that is the number the trader watches.

<Callout type="important" titre="The formula the desk recites">DV01 ≈ modified duration × market value × 0.0001 — in euros per basis point.</Callout>

<Callout type="piege" titre="Three measures, three units — THE interview trap">
**Macaulay** is measured in **years** (a weighted average time), **modified duration** is a unitless **sensitivity factor** (% of price lost per point of yield), the **DV01** is in **euros** per basis point. "The duration of this portfolio is 7." — 7 what? Answering "7 years" about a modified duration, or quoting a DV01 in years, is the classic slip that betrays recited understanding. The reflex: always name the unit — it says which measure you are using.
</Callout>

## Convexity, or why the tangent lies

Apply the desk formula to chapter 3's ±1-point shock: $D_{mod} \times 0.01 \times P = 2.7514 \times 0.01 \times 1{,}027.75 = 28.28$ euros. Duration therefore predicts −€28.28 for a rise and +€28.28 for a fall: a perfectly symmetric response. Yet the exact values, recomputed in chapter 3, are **−€27.75** and **+€28.82**. Duration is wrong twice, and both times on the pessimistic side: it overstates the loss (28.28 versus 27.75) and understates the gain (28.28 versus 28.82).

The reason is geometric. The price-yield relationship is not a straight line: it is a **convex** curve, bowed upward. Modified duration is nothing but the slope of the tangent to that curve at the current rate — the box at the end of the chapter proves it properly. Approximating the price change with duration means replacing the curve by its tangent: excellent very close to the point, increasingly wrong as the shock grows. And since a convex curve always stays **above** its tangent, the error always works in the same direction: the actual price always ends up above duration's prediction.

The second-order correction has a name: **convexity** $C$, which measures the curvature of the price-yield relationship. The full formula becomes:

$$
\frac{\Delta P}{P} \approx -D_{mod}\,\Delta y + \tfrac{1}{2}\,C\,(\Delta y)^2
$$

On the running bond, convexity is $C = 10.41$. The corrective term for ±1 point: $\tfrac{1}{2} \times 10.4127 \times (0.01)^2 \times 1{,}027.75 = +0.54$ euros — **in both directions**, since $(\Delta y)^2$ is positive whatever the sign of the shock. Rise: −28.28 + 0.54 = −€27.74 (exact: −€27.75). Fall: +28.2775 + 0.5351 = +€28.81 (unrounded values — summing the rounded figures would give 28.82) (exact: +€28.82). Chapter 3's asymmetry is fully explained: it was nothing but convexity at work.

Widen the shock to watch the tangent's error swell. A 200bp rise on the running bond (rate from 4% to 6%):

| Estimate | Price at 6% | Gap vs exact |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Exact price (chapter 3) | €973.27 | — |
| Duration alone | €971.20 | −€2.07 |
| Duration + convexity | €973.34 | +€0.07 |

The error of duration alone grows as the **square** of the shock: invisible at 10bp, €2 at 200bp on this short bond — and far more at long maturities. Convexity recovers most of it: the residual gap falls to 7 cents.

<Callout type="important" titre="Convexity is a quality">
The term $\tfrac{1}{2}\,C\,(\Delta y)^2$ is positive whether rates rise or fall: convexity **cushions losses** when rates rise and **amplifies gains** when they fall. At equal duration and yield, the more convex bond is strictly preferable — it behaves better in both scenarios. That is why portfolio managers speak of "buying convexity", and that is why it costs: all else equal, the market accepts a slightly lower yield for a more convex bond.
</Callout>

## Using it day to day

**Portfolio duration.** The modified duration of a portfolio is the average of the durations of its holdings, weighted by their market values. A portfolio 60% invested in a line with modified duration 2.5 and 40% in a line with duration 7 shows $0.6 \times 2.5 + 0.4 \times 7 = 4.3$. A single multiplication then summarizes the rate risk of the whole — which is what makes the measure so handy.

**Immunization.** An insurer or a pension fund must pay benefits on known dates: its liabilities have a duration. By matching the duration of its bond assets to that of its liabilities — **asset duration = liability duration** (at equal present values — matching durations alone is not enough) — it neutralizes the effect of a rate move: if rates rise, its bonds lose value, but the coupons reinvest at better rates, and at the duration horizon the two effects offset. This mechanism, immunization, is at the heart of asset-liability management; you will build it step by step in the level-4 problem of the practice section.

**DV01 hedging.** To neutralize the rate risk of a portfolio with futures, equalize the DV01s:

$$
\text{number of contracts} = \frac{DV01_{\text{portfolio}}}{DV01_{\text{contract}}}
$$

A portfolio whose DV01 is €4,000 per bp, hedged with a futures contract whose DV01 is €80 per bp: sell 50 contracts. After the trade, a 1bp rise costs €4,000 on the bonds and earns 4,000 on the contracts sold — a position insensitive, to first order, to rate moves.

**The limits.** This whole chapter assumes a **parallel** shock: the entire chapter 5 curve shifts as one block. But as you have seen, it steepens, flattens, humps — and two portfolios with the same DV01, one concentrated in the 2-year, the other in the 30-year, react very differently to a *bear steepening*. Managing those deformations requires maturity-by-maturity measures (partial durations) and dedicated curve strategies, beyond our syllabus. But knowing how to name the limit — "duration hedges the level, not the slope" — is exactly the kind of sentence that sets a candidate apart.

<GoFurther suffixe="duration as a derivative, and the perpetuity">
The rigorous formulation of modified duration is differential: it is the relative sensitivity of the price to the yield,

$$
D_{mod} = -\frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dy}
$$

Proof, starting from the price $P(y) = \sum_{t=1}^{n} F_t\,(1+y)^{-t}$. Each term differentiates to $-t\,F_t\,(1+y)^{-t-1}$, hence:

$$
\frac{dP}{dy} = -\sum_{t=1}^{n} t\,F_t\,(1+y)^{-t-1} = -\frac{1}{1+y}\sum_{t=1}^{n} t\,\frac{F_t}{(1+y)^t}
$$

$$
-\frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dy} = \frac{1}{1+y} \cdot \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{n} t \cdot PV(F_t)}{P} = \frac{D_{Mac}}{1+y} = D_{mod}
$$

The $1/(1+y)$ factor separating Macaulay from modified duration is therefore not a convention: it falls out of the differentiation. (Convexity is obtained the same way, as the second derivative — the next term of the Taylor expansion.)

Immediate application: the **perpetuity**, which pays a coupon $c$ every year forever. Its price is the geometric sum $P = c/y$, hence $dP/dy = -c/y^2$ and $D_{mod} = 1/y$, that is:

$$
D_{Mac} = \frac{1+y}{y}
$$

At 4%: $1.04/0.04 = 26$ years. An infinite maturity, but a duration of only 26 years: beyond a certain horizon, discounted flows weigh almost nothing in the barycentre.
</GoFurther>

Duration and convexity measure rate risk: how much the price moves when the curve moves. There remains the other half of bond risk — the possibility that the borrower pays badly, late, or not at all. Chapter 7 opens the file on the name: spreads and sovereign risk.
