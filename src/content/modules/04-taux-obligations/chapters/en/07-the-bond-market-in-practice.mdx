## The repo, the market's invisible engine

Let us start with the most mundane and least known operation in finance. A repo (*repurchase agreement*) consists of selling securities for cash while committing, in the same contract, to buy them back at an agreed date and price. Legal packaging aside, it is a cash loan secured by securities: the "seller" borrows money, the "lender" holds the securities as collateral for the duration of the trade.

| | Bond desk (cash borrower) | Treasurer (cash lender) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Day T | Delivers €10m of OATs, receives €9.8m in cash | Pays out €9.8m, receives the OATs as collateral |
| Day T+7 (maturity) | Repays €9.8m plus interest, gets its OATs back | Returns the OATs, collects €9,805,335.56 |

Two numbers in this table deserve a pause. First, why €9.8m of cash against €10m of securities? Because the lender applies a *haircut*: it lends only a fraction of the value of the collateral, here 98%, to protect itself if the securities fall during the trade and the borrower defaults. A 2% haircut on €10m of securities: €9.8m of cash raised.

Next, the interest. The repo is a money-market operation: simple interest, Actual/360 basis — the conventions of chapter 1. For 7 days at a repo rate of 2.8%:

$$
I = 9{,}800{,}000 \times 2.8\,\% \times \frac{7}{360} = \text{€}5{,}335.56
$$

One vocabulary subtlety remains, one that desks handle daily. When any security from a good-quality basket will do as collateral — the lender just wants to invest its cash — the trade is GC (*general collateral*): the GC repo rate then tracks policy rates; it is the price of cash. But when one specific security is in high demand — typically because participants want to borrow it in order to sell it short — its holder is in a position of strength: it raises cash at a rate below GC. The security trades "special", and the gap between its repo rate and GC measures the intensity of the demand for that particular security.

<Callout type="important" titre="Why the repo is vital">
Three functions make the repo the central plumbing of the bond market. **Funding**: a desk does not pay for its positions out of its own capital — it buys the security, repos it out, and thereby finances most of the position day to day. **Short selling**: to sell a security you do not own, you must source it — the reverse repo provides it, against cash. **Monetary-policy transmission**: short-term market rates form in the repo market; when the central bank moves its policy rates, this is the channel through which the impulse irrigates the prices of all assets. When the repo seizes up, the whole market coughs — the box at the end of the chapter gives two spectacular illustrations.
</Callout>

## Sovereign debt in practice

Who orchestrates France's borrowing? The AFT (*Agence France Trésor*, the French debt management office), met in chapter 2: it publishes at the end of each year the following year's funding programme — on the order of €300bn of medium- and long-term issuance in recent years — then rolls it out through regular auctions, BTFs every week, OATs every month. This predictability is a deliberate policy: a regular, transparent and liquid issuer borrows more cheaply.

The orders of magnitude to know as of early 2026: French public debt is approaching €3,500bn, of which about €2,800bn is negotiable central-government debt — mostly OATs, with the short end covered by BTFs. Who holds this stock? Just over half (≈ 54% per AFT) is in the hands of non-residents (foreign central banks, funds, international insurers) — a sign of confidence as much as a source of vulnerability, because these holders constantly compare the OAT with its competitors. The rest is split between French insurers (life insurance is structurally a large holder of OATs), banks, funds — and the Eurosystem.

For the ECB has become, through the QE met in chapter 5, a major holder of euro-area sovereign debt: at its peak, the Eurosystem carried on the order of a quarter of France's negotiable debt. Since 2023 the movement has reversed — the central bank is letting its portfolio run off without reinvesting; this is quantitative tightening. Concretely, a price-insensitive buyer is withdrawing: the market must absorb more paper, and the judgement of private investors on each name regains weight. Which brings us to spreads.

## Sovereign spreads

Chapter 4 set the definition: a spread is a difference of yields to maturity, never of prices. Applied to governments, it produces the most watched thermometer of the Paris market: the **OAT-Bund spread**, the gap between the 10-year OAT yield and the 10-year Bund yield, expressed in basis points. OAT at 3.30%, Bund at 2.55%: 3.30 − 2.55 = 0.75 points, that is a 75bp spread. It is the premium the market demands for lending to France rather than to Germany — a blend of fiscal risk, political risk and liquidity difference.

The historical record speaks for itself. Before 2022, the spread lived peacefully between 30 and 40bp. The rise in rates, then French political instability — the 2024 dissolution, repeated budget censure motions — shifted it into a new regime: it exceeded 80bp at the tension peaks of 2024-2025, levels not seen since the euro-area crisis. Each political episode can be read in real time in this number: it has become, quite literally, France's political thermometer.

Italy offers the comparison point: the **BTP-Bund**, structurally wider than the French spread — heavier debt, weaker growth. But nothing is set in stone: buoyed by Italian political stability, it tightened sharply in 2024-2025, to the point of crossing, at moments, a French spread in full fever. And to measure what "wide" really means, you have to remember 2011-2012: at the paroxysm of the euro-area crisis, the BTP-Bund exceeded 500bp, Spain was on the same trajectory and markets were openly testing the reversibility of the euro. It took Mario Draghi's "whatever it takes", in July 2012, to break the spiral. A spread is not an abstraction: at those levels, it is the solvency of a state that is at stake.

<Callout type="piege" titre="A spread has two legs">
"The OAT-Bund widens from 75 to 90bp" does not tell you what happened: did the French yield rise, or did the German yield fall? Both moves widen the spread, and their readings are opposite. If the OAT rises with the Bund stable, the market is sanctioning France. If the Bund falls with the OAT stable — flight to quality on a day of panic —, France has done nothing wrong: it is Germany that is more in demand. The professional reflex before any spread move: decompose the two legs before interpreting.
</Callout>

## Sovereign vs corporate: the credit spread

The same reading grid extends to companies. The yield of a *corporate* bond decomposes into the sovereign rate of the same maturity plus a **credit spread**, which compensates for the issuer's default risk. To rank these risks, the market relies on the rating agencies, whose scale splits into two worlds: investment grade (IG — from AAA to BBB−) and high yield (HY — below). The typical orders of magnitude:

| Segment | Indicative spread over the sovereign |
| --- | --- |
| Investment grade (IG) | ~80 to 150bp |
| High yield (HY) | ~300 to 500bp |

These are orders of magnitude, highly variable across the cycle: credit spreads compress when all is well and blow out in crises. Default probability, recovery rate, ratings, the mechanics of the spread: all of that is the subject of module 5.

## Market miscellany

Three practices complete the picture of the market at work.

**Stripping.** A 10-year OAT is a bundle of eleven cash flows — ten coupons and one principal. The market knows how to separate them into eleven independently tradable zero-coupons: stripped OATs, met in chapter 2. The benefit is direct for anyone with a commitment on a fixed date: a zero-coupon maturing in 2040 perfectly matches a benefit due in 2040, with a duration exactly equal to its maturity (chapter 6).

**Sovereign green bonds.** France was a pioneer among major issuers (Poland printed the first sovereign green bond, in 2016) with the OAT verte (green OAT), launched in 2017: same cash flows, same name as a standard OAT, but issuance proceeds earmarked for documented environmental spending. The outstanding stock now runs to tens of billions of euros — sustainable finance and its debates (what is the green promise worth? is there a *greenium*?) await you in module 12.

**Liquidity in the secondary market.** The SVTs (*Spécialistes en Valeurs du Trésor*, France's primary dealers) of chapter 2 do not serve only in the primary market: they also commit to quoting French debt continuously in the secondary market. Liquidity concentrates on the benchmark issues — the most recent 10-year OAT trades within a few cents of bid/ask, while an old stripped line can sit for days without a trade. Even within the "risk-free" world, liquidity remains a quality that is paid for.

<GoFurther suffixe="repo and leverage: LTCM and the 2019 squeeze">
The repo is also the machine for manufacturing leverage. The recipe of LTCM, the star *hedge fund* of the 1990s: buy a security, repo it out, use the cash raised to buy another security, repo that one out in turn — and repeat. With negotiated haircuts close to zero, the fund carried roughly 125 billion dollars of positions on less than 5 billion of capital, leverage on the order of 25×. When spreads widened after the 1998 Russian default, margin calls cascaded and the New York Fed had to orchestrate a rescue to avoid a disorderly liquidation — full autopsy in module 11. Another reminder that this plumbing can break: in September 2019, a mere conjunction of tax deadlines and Treasury settlements drained the cash available in the US repo market — the overnight rate touched 10% intraday, five times its normal level, forcing the Fed to inject liquidity overnight, a first since 2008.
</GoFurther>
