Chapter 3 started from a rate and computed a price. In the market, the problem almost always comes the other way round: the price is there, displayed on the screen, and the question is "what does it earn?". This chapter builds the right answer in two steps: a naive but immediate measure, the current yield, then the reference measure of the entire bond market, the yield to maturity.

## Current yield: quick but short-sighted

The most intuitive measure divides the annual coupon by the price paid:

$$
r_{\text{current}} = \frac{C}{P}
$$

On the running-example bond of Chapter 3 (€1,000 face value, 5% coupon, 3 years), bought at its market price of €1,027.75: 50 / 1,027.75 = **4.86%**. Better than reading the 5% face coupon, which would ignore that the bond is paid for above par.

But this measure is short-sighted, for two reasons:

- **It ignores redemption.** The buyer pays €1,027.75 and will recover only €1,000 at maturity: a capital loss of €27.75, certain and dated, of which the current yield knows nothing. Symmetrically, on a discount bond, it ignores the gain to come.
- **It ignores timing.** A coupon due in one year and a coupon due in three years carry the same weight in its calculation, in head-on contradiction with all of Chapter 1: a distant euro is worth less than a near one.

The current yield remains useful as an instant order of magnitude — the bond's annual "rent" relative to its value. For a complete measure, time and redemption must be brought back in.

## Yield to maturity: the reference measure

<Callout type="definition" titre="Yield to maturity (YTM)">
The yield to maturity of a bond, or YTM, is THE rate $y$ that equates the observed price with the sum of the discounted cash flows:

$$
P = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{F_t}{(1+y)^t}
$$

It is Chapter 3's price formula read in reverse: instead of fixing the rate and deriving the price, you observe the price and extract the rate. The YTM is the internal rate of return (IRR) of the bond investment: it incorporates the coupons, their timing, the purchase price and the redemption.
</Callout>

On the running example, the answer is immediate: we built the price of €1,027.75 by discounting the flows at 4% — the rate that equates price and discounted flows is therefore **4% by construction**. And the comparison with the current yield is instructive: 4% versus 4.86%. The gap corresponds essentially to the €27.75 capital loss, spread over three years, that the current yield could not see. On a premium bond, the YTM is always below the current yield; on a discount bond, it is the other way round.

**How is it computed in practice?** There is no direct formula once the bond has more than a few cash flows: the equation is polynomial in $y$ and cannot be inverted algebraically. But the numerical solution is trivial, because we know from Chapter 3 that the price is a strictly decreasing function of the rate. You proceed by bracketing trials: try a rate, compute the corresponding price, compare it with the observed price — too high, raise the rate; too low, lower it. That is exactly what a solver (spreadsheet, financial calculator) does in a few iterations.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="2-year bond, 4% coupon, price €990: find the YTM">
Before any computation, the Chapter 3 reflex: the bond trades below par, so its YTM exceeds its 4% coupon.

- Try 4%: the price would be €1,000 (coupon = rate, hence par). Too high: a higher rate is needed.
- Try 5%: $40/1.05 + 1{,}040/1.05^2 = 38.10 + 943.31 = \text{€}981.41$. Too low: the rate we are after lies between 4% and 5%.
- The solution is **y ≈ 4.53%** (4.534% more precisely): at that rate, the discounted flows give back exactly €990.00.

Reading: the buyer earns a 4% coupon and, on top, a €10 gain between the purchase price (€990) and redemption at par — hence a total return above the coupon.
</Callout>

That is the whole practical point of the YTM: it makes bonds with different profiles comparable. High coupon bought at a premium, low coupon bought at a discount, zero-coupon with no intermediate flows at all: the YTM reduces every price-flow combination to a single annual rate, comparable from one bond to the next — for comparable maturities and credit quality. The coupon alone tells you nothing: our 2-year bond with its 4% coupon (YTM 4.53%) earns more than the running-example bond with its 5% coupon (YTM 4%) (an indicative comparison: the maturities differ, and Chapter 5 shows why that matters).

## What the YTM really assumes

The YTM is a rate of return **promised under conditions**, not a guaranteed return. Reading it as the return actually realised rests on two assumptions:

1. **Holding to maturity.** Sell before redemption, and your return will depend on the resale price, hence on the rates of the moment — it can deviate sharply from the YTM at purchase.
2. **Reinvesting the coupons at the rate $y$ itself.** The YTM only equals your actually realised growth rate if every coupon is reinvested at the rate $y$ — strictly speaking, the pricing equation itself assumes nothing: it is its interpretation as a realised return that requires it. If rates have fallen in the meantime, the coupons are reinvested on worse terms, and the actually realised return will be below the YTM displayed at purchase — that is reinvestment risk. If rates have risen, it will be above.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The YTM is not a guaranteed return">
"I buy at a 4% YTM, I hold to the end, so I earn 4% a year": wrong, unless rates stay at exactly 4% for the entire life of the bond. Holding to maturity locks in the cash flows, not their reinvestment. The only bond whose return to maturity is rigorously locked in at purchase is the zero-coupon: no coupons, hence nothing to reinvest. This nuance — realised return versus promised return — is typically what sets a candidate apart at the orals.
</Callout>

## Price and yield: two ways of writing the same object

For given characteristics (cash flows, maturity), price and YTM map into each other unambiguously: a bijective dictionary. The market draws a practical consequence: sovereign debt is **quoted in yield**. When a screen, a newspaper or a trader announces "the 10-year OAT is at 3.2%", read: the YTM of the French State's 10-year benchmark line works out at 3.2% at the current price. On the screens, you *trade* in price; in market conversation, you *talk* in yield. Yield is the universal unit of account, because it compares instantly across lines, maturities and countries.

This common language enables the queen of measures in bond finance: the **spread**, the difference in YTM between two bonds. 10-year OAT at 3.2% versus 10-year German Bund at 2.7%: a France-Germany spread of 0.5 point, i.e. 50 basis points, which measures the premium required on the French name. Corporate bond versus the sovereign curve: a credit spread. Chapter 7 will come back to it in practice on sovereign spreads — remember from now on that a spread is a difference in yields to maturity, never a difference in prices.

One last language conversion: "rates are rising" and "bond prices are falling" are one and the same sentence, said in the two units of the dictionary.

<GoFurther suffixe="the YTM, an average that flattens the curve">
Strictly speaking, each cash flow should be discounted at the rate of its own horizon: the zero-coupon rate $z_t$, that of a single investment of maturity $t$. The "true" price is then written with the whole curve, and the YTM is the single rate $y$ that reproduces that same price:

$$
P = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{F_t}{(1+z_t)^t} = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{F_t}{(1+y)^t}
$$

The YTM is therefore a complex average of the zero-coupon rates, weighted by the bond's discounted cash flows. Two subtle consequences. First, two bonds with the same maturity and the same credit quality but different coupons do not have exactly the same YTM on a steep curve: the high-coupon bond puts more weight on the short maturities, hence on short rates. Comparing their raw YTMs means comparing two differently weighted averages. Second, the same YTM can hide very different curves — flat at 4%, or steep, rising from 2% to 5%: the single number flattens the information away. To recover the pure $z_t$ from the prices of coupon bonds, there is a step-by-step extraction technique, bootstrapping — the subject of Chapter 5.
</GoFurther>
