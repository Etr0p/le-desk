Chapter 1 laid out the mechanics of time and rates. Here now is the instrument that industrialises those mechanics on a planetary scale: the bond. It is the most heavily traded financial instrument in the world, the one that funds governments, sets the reference price for every other asset — and the one on which an exam panel expects flawless command of the vocabulary.

## Anatomy of a bond

<Callout type="definition" titre="Bond">
A bond is a negotiable debt security: the issuer borrows a principal amount (the face value $N$), pays a periodic interest in exchange (the coupon $C$) and repays the face value on an agreed date (the maturity). Unlike a bank loan, this security can be freely bought and sold on a market.
</Callout>

Four parameters are enough to describe the contract: the face value (the unit of debt the security represents), the coupon rate (the annual interest as a percentage of face value), the maturity (the redemption date) and the price, quoted not in euros but **as a percentage of face value**. Learn to read a screen line like a professional:

> OAT 3.00% 25/05/2036 — price 98.45%

| Field | How to read it |
| --- | --- |
| OAT | The issuer: the French State (*Obligation assimilable du Trésor*, a French government bond) |
| 3.00% | The coupon rate: €30 paid every year per €1,000 of face value |
| 25/05/2036 | The maturity: face value repaid on 25 May 2036; the coupon falls due every 25 May |
| 98.45% | The market price as a percentage of face value: €984.50 per €1,000 of face value |

The bond trades below par (below 100%): the buyer pays less than the face value they will collect at maturity. Its yield is therefore above the 3.00% coupon — here around 3.2%. The exact link between price and yield is the subject of Chapter 3.

<Callout type="piege" titre="The quoted price is not the price paid">
98.45% does not mean the buyer pays exactly €984.50 per €1,000 of face value. The quoted price is a clean price: you must add the interest accrued since the last coupon date to get the amount actually settled (the dirty price). Confusing the two is a classic mistake — the full mechanics are detailed in Chapter 3.
</Callout>

## Who issues, and why

Three big families of issuers share the market:

- **Governments**, by far the largest: French OATs, German Bunds, US Treasuries, Italian BTPs, UK Gilts. Each major sovereign name lends its name to its reference curve.
- **Agencies and supranationals**: the EIB, development banks, the European Union — quasi-public borrowers with very high ratings.
- **Corporates** (corporate bonds), from utilities to banks, from the best-rated names down to high yield.

Why does a large corporate issue bonds rather than borrow from its bank? Four structural reasons:

1. **Size**: a bond issue routinely raises €500m to several billion in a single transaction, beyond the capacity of any single bank balance sheet.
2. **Maturities**: the bond market lends at 7, 10, 30 years, where bank credit rarely goes beyond a few years.
3. **Diversification**: issuing spreads the funding across hundreds of investors and reduces dependence on bank credit.
4. **Market pricing**: for a good name, the market is often more competitive than a banking pool, and the issuer builds a public credit curve that serves as a reference for its future funding.

This underlying shift — funding through securities rather than loans — has a name: disintermediation.

## The bond family

The basic contract comes in several variants, each answering a specific need.

**The fixed-rate bond** is the standard: a coupon known in advance, identical every year until maturity. It concentrates most of the interest rate risk studied in this module.

**The floating-rate note** (FRN) pays a coupon reset periodically off a money market rate, typically 3-month Euribor plus a margin set at issue. Example on a Euribor 3M + 0.60% note, for €100,000 of face value on an Actual/360 basis:

| Fixing date | 3M Euribor observed | Margin | Annualised coupon | Days in period | Coupon paid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 15 Jan | 2.15% | +0.60% | 2.75% | 91 | €695.14 |
| 16 Apr | 2.20% | +0.60% | 2.80% | 92 | €715.56 |
| 17 Jul | 1.95% | +0.60% | 2.55% | 90 | €637.50 |

The coupon hugs the money market: if rates rise, the following coupons rise too. The essential consequence: an FRN's price stays close to par, since the note "resets to current conditions" at each fixing. An FRN's price stays close to par as long as the issuer's credit quality is stable; if the *credit margin* demanded by the market changes, the price moves away from par — and a slight rate sensitivity remains between two fixings.

**The zero-coupon bond** pays nothing before maturity: a single cash flow, the face value, repaid at the end. It therefore sells well below par, and the entire return sits in the gap between purchase price and redemption. BTFs work this way, as do stripped OATs (strips).

**Inflation-linked bonds** protect purchasing power: the face value is multiplied by an indexation coefficient that tracks the price index, and the coupon — a fixed real rate — applies to that indexed face value. France issues OATi (linked to French inflation excluding tobacco) and OAT€i (linked to euro-area inflation excluding tobacco).

Finally, some bonds embed options: redeemable early at the issuer's discretion (callable) or at the investor's (puttable) — their treatment belongs to Module 9.

| Type | Coupon | Sensitivity to the level of rates | Typical example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Fixed-rate | Fixed, known in advance | High (Chapter 6) | OAT 3.00% 25/05/2036 |
| Floating-rate (FRN) | Reset at each fixing | Very low: price close to par | Corporate Euribor 3M + 0.60% |
| Zero-coupon | None | The highest for a given maturity | BTFs, stripped OATs |
| Inflation-linked | Fixed real rate on indexed face value | High, to real rates | OATi, OAT€i |

*In practice, Euribor is set two business days before the start of each interest period (fixing in advance).*

## Where bonds are born: the primary market

A bond is born on the primary market, where the issuer meets its first investors. Two mechanisms dominate.

**The auction** is the route for governments. In France, the Agence France Trésor (AFT, the French debt management office) issues on a regular, pre-announced calendar: BTFs every week, OATs every month. Participation is restricted to the SVTs (*Spécialistes en valeurs du Trésor*, France's primary dealers), some fifteen accredited banks committed to bidding and to making markets in the secondary. The French technique is the **multiple-price** auction — *adjudication à prix demandés* in AFT parlance: bids are filled from the highest price down to the lowest until the target amount is reached, and each successful SVT pays the price it bid itself — not a uniform price.

The multiple-price format lets the State capture the surplus of the most aggressive bidders — each SVT pays the price it bid, at the risk of a winner's curse for whoever overbids.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="An OAT auction, step by step">
Late in the morning, the AFT auctions around €11bn across three lines, including the benchmark 10-year OAT. The SVTs submit their bids: one bank asks for €800m at 98.50%, another for €600m at 98.46%, and so on. The AFT fills the bids starting from the highest price until it reaches the amount retained on the line — say €5.2bn. Results are published a few minutes later: stop price 98.44%, total demand of 2.4 times the amount allotted (the bid-to-cover ratio). Each SVT pays the price of its own filled bids: the one that bid 98.50% pays 98.50%.
</Callout>

**Syndication** is the normal route for corporates — and for governments on certain new or very long lines. The issuer mandates a syndicate of banks (the sell-side) which sounds out investors, builds the order book (book-building), sets the issue price in light of demand, then allocates the bonds. The syndicate's fees and the premium offered to investors are part of the cost of issuance.

## Life in the secondary market

Once issued, the bond lives its own life: it trades over the counter (OTC), mostly through market makers who continuously quote a bid and an ask price. The gap between the two pays the market maker — and measures the liquidity of the bond.

That liquidity is anything but uniform. On a benchmark sovereign line, such as the most recent 10-year OAT, the bid/ask spread is measured in hundredths of a percentage point and volumes are near-continuous. On a mid-sized corporate line, the spread widens and days can go by without a trade: the holder who wants to sell fast pays the price of that illiquidity. Liquidity is a quality that costs money — remember the liquidity premium from Chapter 1.

<Callout type="important" titre="The biggest market in the world">
The global bond market represents an outstanding amount in the order of $140 trillion — of the same order as the global equity market capitalisation; long larger, the gap has narrowed. Equities make the headlines; bonds set the funding conditions for the whole economy. It is on the sovereign yield curve that the prices of nearly all other assets are built, which justifies the nickname of king of instruments.
</Callout>

<GoFurther suffixe="capital structure and the creditor hierarchy">
Where does a bond stand when the issuer defaults? The answer lies in the hierarchy of the liability side, from best protected to most exposed:

1. **Senior secured debt**: backed by specific assets (security interests, collateral) — first served in a liquidation;
2. **Senior unsecured debt**: the great mass of ordinary bonds, sovereign and corporate alike;
3. **Subordinated debt**: repaid only after the senior creditors — typically bank Tier 2 instruments;
4. **Hybrids**: instruments halfway between debt and equity (bank AT1s, corporate perpetuals), designed to absorb losses;
5. **Equity**: served last, which in a bankruptcy most often means not at all.

The further down the hierarchy, the lower the expected recovery rate and the higher the required yield. That mechanism — default probability, recovery, credit spread — is the heart of Module 5.
</GoFurther>
