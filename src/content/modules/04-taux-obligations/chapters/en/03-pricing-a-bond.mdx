Chapter 2 ended on a question left hanging: where does that 98.45% price on the screen come from? The answer fits in one sentence, and it is the most important sentence of the module: **the price of a bond is the sum of its discounted future cash flows**. Everything else — premium, discount, rate sensitivity, accrued interest — is nothing but the logical consequence of that definition.

## A price = discounted cash flows

A fixed-rate bond is a promise of cash flows known entirely in advance: a coupon $C$ every year, then the face value $N$ repaid along with the final coupon. To find out what that promise is worth today, you apply Chapter 1's time machine — discounting — to each flow, then add up:

$$
P = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{F_t}{(1+r)^t}
$$

where $F_t$ is the cash flow of year $t$ (the coupon $C$ in the intermediate years, $C + N$ in the final year) and $r$ the rate required by the market for this issuer and this maturity. That rate is the only input that does not appear in the contract: the market sets it, and through it the price moves.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="The module's running example: €1,000, 5% coupon, 3 years, 4% market rate">
The bond pays a €50 coupon per year (5% of €1,000) and repays €1,050 in year three. The market requires 4%.

| Year $t$ | Cash flow $F_t$ | Discount factor $1/1.04^t$ | Present value |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | €50 | 0.961538 | €48.08 |
| 2 | €50 | 0.924556 | €46.23 |
| 3 | €1,050 | 0.888996 | €933.45 |

$$
P = 48.08 + 46.23 + 933.45 \approx \text{€}1{,}027.75
$$

More precisely, summing the unrounded values (48.0769 + 46.2278 + 933.4462) gives **P = €1,027.75**. In practice, round only at the very end: here, adding the rounded amounts would give €1,027.76, one cent too many.
</Callout>

Two observations on this result. First, the final cash flow alone accounts for more than 90% of the price (€933.45 out of €1,027.75): most of the value of an ordinary bond is concentrated in the final repayment — remember this, the duration of Chapter 6 follows directly from it. Second, the price exceeds the face value: €1,027.75 for a bond that will repay only €1,000. That is not an anomaly, it is information.

## Par, premium, discount

Why pay €1,027.75 for a bond redeemed at €1,000? Because its 5% coupon is more generous than the 4% the market offers: the buyer pays for that advantage. The position of the price relative to face value can be read entirely from the comparison between the coupon and the market rate:

| Situation | Price | Vocabulary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Coupon > market rate | P > face value | The bond trades **above par** (at a premium) |
| Coupon = market rate | P = face value | The bond trades **at par** |
| Coupon < market rate | P < face value | The bond trades **below par** (at a discount) |

Check it on 5-year bonds when the market requires 4%: a 2% coupon gives a price of €910.96, a 4% coupon gives exactly €1,000, a 6% coupon gives €1,089.04.

The intuition is competition between the old and the new. Picture a bond issued a few years ago with a 2% coupon, while today's new issues, of the same name and the same maturity, pay 4%. Nobody would pay par for the old one: at €1,000, it earns half as much as the new. Its holder only finds a buyer by cutting the price, down to the exact level where the meagre coupon, topped up by the gain between the purchase price and redemption at €1,000, becomes competitive again with the 4% on the new issues. The discount is not a punishment: it is the mechanism that equalises yields.

## Why prices and rates move in opposite directions

This is THE bond market question at the orals, and it admits two answers — learn both, they complement each other.

**The mechanical answer.** In the price formula, the cash flows $F_t$ are carved into the contract: they will never move. The rate $r$, however, sits in the denominator, inside the factor $(1+r)^t$. If $r$ rises, every denominator grows, every present value shrinks, and the sum — the price — falls. With the cash flows fixed, the price is the only adjustment variable.

**The market answer.** If rates rise, new issues offer higher coupons. Older bonds, whose coupons are now below the going rate, become less attractive: their price must fall until their total return catches up with the new issues. It is the previous section's argument, set in motion.

<Callout type="important" titre="If you remember only one thing">
The cash flows of a fixed-rate bond are contractual and immutable; when the rate required by the market changes, the only thing that can adjust is the price. **Rates up = prices down, rates down = prices up.** All of bond management — and a good share of oral exam questions — rests on this reflex.
</Callout>

Let us put numbers on it with the running-example bond (€1,000, 5% coupon, 3 years), priced under three rate scenarios:

| Market rate | Price | Reading |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3% | €1,056.57 | The 5% coupon is worth a lot: large premium |
| 4% | €1,027.75 | Our reference case |
| 5% | €1,000.00 | Coupon = rate: exactly back to par |

The table also confirms the rule of the previous section: at a 5% market rate, the 5% coupon is exactly in line with the market, and the price falls to par. Note a discreet asymmetry too: the 1-point fall in rates gains €28.82, the 1-point rise loses only €27.75. The price-rate relationship is not a straight line — that curvature has a name, convexity, and we will come back to it with duration.

## Accrued interest, clean price, dirty price

One subtlety remains, promised in Chapter 2: the quoted price is not the amount paid. Between two coupon dates, interest accrues day after day for the holder's benefit. If you sell your bond 146 days after the last coupon, you have "earned" 146 days of interest — but it is the buyer who will collect the next coupon, in full. To restore fairness, the buyer compensates you by paying, on top of the quoted price, the **accrued interest**: the fraction of the coupon accumulated since the last payment.

Hence two prices for the same bond:

- the **clean price**: the price quoted on screens, excluding accrued interest;
- the **dirty price**: the amount actually settled, i.e. clean price + accrued interest.

<Callout type="exemple" titre="Buying an OAT 146 days after the coupon">
Take Chapter 2's OAT again: 3% coupon, €1,000 face value, quoted price 98.45%. The purchase takes place 146 days after the last coupon date, on an Actual/Actual basis (non-leap year, 365-day coupon period):

$$
\text{accrued} = 1{,}000 \times 3\% \times \frac{146}{365} = \text{€}12.00
$$

Amount settled: €984.50 (clean price) + €12.00 (accrued) = **€996.50** per €1,000 of face value.
</Callout>

<Callout type="piege" titre="The price on the screen is not the price paid">
On a screen, an OAT is always quoted clean: that is a pure display convention. Answering "€984.50" to the question "how much do you settle?" is the classic mistake. The reflex: quoted price × face value, **plus** the accrued interest, computed in the bond's own day-count basis (Actual/Actual for OATs — and beware, a coupon period containing a 29 February counts 366 days).
</Callout>

Why quote clean rather than dirty? Because the accrued interest grows mechanically every day, then falls back to zero on the coupon date: quoted dirty, the price would climb in a sawtooth pattern without anything changing in the market. The clean price strips out that calendar mechanics and expresses only what the market cares about: the value of the bond in terms of rates, credit quality, liquidity.

<GoFurther suffixe="discounting at fractional dates">
Between two coupons, the cash flows no longer fall due in exactly 1, 2, 3 years, but at fractional horizons: if $j$ days have elapsed since the last coupon (365 basis), the next flow is due in $1 - j/365$ years, the one after in $2 - j/365$, and so on. The dirty price is then written with non-integer exponents:

$$
P_{\text{dirty}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{F_i}{(1+r)^{\,t_i - j/365}}
$$

This formulation explains why the price "rolls" mechanically between two coupons: at a constant rate $r$, each passing day reduces every exponent by $1/365$, which amounts to multiplying the dirty price by $(1+r)^{1/365}$. The dirty price thus climbs steadily — by roughly one day's compounding at the market rate — then drops by the coupon amount on the payment date. The linear accrued interest (146/365 of the coupon) is only a conventional approximation of that compounding, close enough to serve as a universal settlement rule. The clean price, defined as dirty price minus accrued, stays roughly stable from one day to the next: which is exactly the point.
</GoFurther>

We now know how to go from a rate to a price. The next chapter makes the reverse journey: start from the price observed in the market and extract the yield.
